# OAK-T4.002 — Compromised Front-End Permit Solicitation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition), OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM
**First documented:** 2022 (Curve Finance DNS hijack)
**Aliases:** "frontend hijack permit", "DNS-takeover permit", "BGP-hijack frontend"

## Description

A pattern in which a legitimate protocol's front-end is temporarily replaced or modified — via DNS takeover, BGP hijack, supply-chain compromise of a JS dependency, hosting compromise, or insider action — to solicit a permit or approval from users that exceeds the scope of any normal interaction with that protocol. From a defender's perspective, T4.002 is characterized by the **mismatch between the protocol's stated canonical interaction patterns and the permit being solicited** during the compromise window. The compromise window is typically short (minutes to hours) but observable on-chain by an anomalous concentration of permit-grantees from a known protocol's user base.

T4.002 frequently chains into T4.001 (Permit2 misuse) or T4.004 (Allowance drainer) — the front-end is the entry vector; the actual extraction uses standard signature- or approval-pattern flows. The defender's separable signal is the protocol-domain-level baseline drift, not the per-victim wallet-side signal.

## Observed indicators

- Spike in permit-grant or `Approval` events from a known-protocol's user base, granted to a non-protocol address within a narrow time window.
- Hosting/DNS change-control events in the protocol's front-end infrastructure (registrar nameserver swap, DNS A-record diff, IPFS content-hash drift on ENS resolver) correlating with the spike.
- Frontend-served bytecode targeting a different router/spender than the protocol's canonical contract list.
- Public incident disclosure from the affected protocol confirming a compromise window.
- Drainer-router contract addresses on the receiving end matching ScamSniffer / SlowMist threat-feed entries (Angel Drainer infrastructure was the receiving end of the September 2023 Balancer compromise per public reporting).

## Detection signals

- Per-protocol baseline: maintain a baseline of expected permit-grantee distribution for major protocols (Uniswap, Curve, Balancer, Aave, Lido, etc.); alert on deviations.
- Off-chain telemetry (OAK-M30 adjacent): DNS canary monitoring for protocol domains — record canonical A/AAAA records, content-hash for ENS-resolver-pointed dApps, registrar lock state; alert on diff.
- Content-hash diff at the IPFS gateway: legitimate dApps pinned via ENS+IPFS should produce a stable content hash; mid-window drift is high-precision.
- Cross-reference: cluster anomalous grantees against known-adversarial allowlists (drainer-router contract addresses are public).
- BGP route-monitoring feeds (RIPEstat, BGPmon): hijacks targeting the IP space of dApp hosting providers (Vercel, Cloudflare, Netlify) provide a pre-on-chain signal.

## Real-world examples

- **Curve Finance frontend DNS hijack — Ethereum — 2022-08** — Approximately \$575K drained from users who interacted with a cloned Curve UI served from compromised DNS (the underlying nameserver at registrar `iwantmyname` was the compromise vector, not the Curve registrar account). Users on the cloned UI were prompted to approve a malicious contract; proceeds were subsequently moved to centralized exchanges and Tornado Cash. Curve responded by redirecting `.fi` to neutral nameservers and operating from `curve.finance` until stabilised. Forensic write-up: `[rektcurve2022]`.
- **Balancer frontend BGP/DNS hijack — Ethereum — 2023-09-19/20** — A social-engineering attack on EuroDNS (the registrar for the `.fi` TLD) allowed nameserver records for `balancer.fi` to be updated; a forked phishing front-end then prompted users to approve token transfers via Angel Drainer phishing contracts. SlowMist also identified BGP hijacking against the upstream IP space as a parallel vector. Total loss approximately \$238K before Balancer regained domain control at 17:45 UTC on September 20. The Balancer DAO subsequently announced `.fi` domains as structurally unsafe given the EuroDNS exposure. <!-- TODO: cite https://medium.com/balancer-protocol/dns-security-incident-post-mortem-1b1feb735aca -->
- **Galxe DNS hijack — multi-chain — 2023-10** — A social-engineering attack on Galxe's DNS provider replaced the canonical front-end with a phishing surface; users were prompted to sign approvals/permits to attacker-controlled spenders. Per-incident on-chain trace and post-mortem: `[galxepostmortem2023]`, `[peckshieldgalxe2023]`, `[rektgalxe2023]`. Loss in the low single-digit millions; the incident is the canonical 2023 reference for "registrar account compromised, dApp domain repointed" and is structurally identical to the Curve and Balancer cases.
- **CoinStats Snap supply-chain — multi-chain — 2024-06** — `[coinstats20240622]`, `[slowmistcoinstats2024]` document a supply-chain compromise of the CoinStats portfolio-tracker mobile/desktop dApp surface; signing prompts originating from inside the trusted-app surface led approximately 1,590 wallets to sign malicious payloads. T4.002-adjacent: the compromise was at the dApp client distribution layer rather than DNS, but the structural signal (trusted-surface mismatch with canonical interaction pattern) is the same.
- **DEXX exchange-extension drainer — multi-chain — 2024-10/11** — `[dexxstatements2024]`, `[slowmistdexx2024]`, `[zachxbtdexx2024]` document the DEXX trading-extension compromise: signing prompts originating from inside the DEXX surface authorised attacker-controlled token transfers across the user base. Per `[peckshielddexx2024]`, the compromise sat at the operator-infrastructure layer rather than user-side phishing, making it a T4.002-class signal at the cohort-attribution level.

## Reference implementations

- MG `mg-detectors-rs` — partial coverage (link TBD): per-protocol grantee-baseline drift detection.
- Wallet vendors — partial coverage via grantee allowlists; OAK-M30 per-dApp-domain allowlists are the highest-leverage user-side intervention.
- DNS/BGP monitoring: Cloudflare Radar, RIPE NCC route monitors, Spaceshift / DNSSEC enforcement, and registry-lock at registrar level.

## Mitigations

- **OAK-M30 (Per-dApp Domain and App-Store-Package Allowlist):** highest-leverage user-side layer — pin canonical dApp domains and content hashes; warn on signing prompts originating from non-allowlisted origins.
- **OAK-M18 (Out-of-Band Destination Verification):** verify destination contract addresses against canonical protocol-published lists before signing any permit.
- **OAK-M31 (EIP-712 Permit Display and Signing Risk Heuristics):** surface signed-payload spender vs canonical-protocol-spender mismatch.
- **OAK-M19 (Air-Gap Cold Wallet Signing):** for treasury-class wallets, route signing through air-gapped device that displays full permit scope independently of the dApp surface.
- **OAK-M40 (Supply-Chain Package Integrity):** for the dApp-distribution-layer subclass (CoinStats, DEXX), package-integrity verification covers the same signal at the build-and-distribute layer.
- **Protocols:** publish canonical contract addresses in machine-readable form; integrate with wallet allowlists; apply registry-lock and DNSSEC on critical protocol domains; pin frontend via ENS+IPFS content hash with multi-sig update authority.

## Discussion

T4.002 sits at the intersection of on-chain telemetry and traditional infosec; coverage requires inputs from outside the on-chain surface. Coordination with traditional CTI feeds (DNS/BGP/registrar change feeds) is an open integration item.

False-positive reasoning: a single grantee anomaly is noisy because protocols routinely deploy new router contracts (e.g., Uniswap V4 deployment); the high-precision signal is grantee-anomaly + DNS/content-hash drift in the same window. Edge case: a protocol intentionally migrating its front-end during a planned redeploy will trigger the same DNS/hash-drift signal — defenders need an out-of-band confirmation channel (protocol's GitHub release tag, signed announcement) before alerting downstream consumers.

v0.x deferred items: cross-chain frontend-compromise observability (a single dApp's compromise affects users across all chains it supports — current detection is largely per-chain), and frontend-attestation standards (signed-frontend manifests, akin to subresource integrity but at the dApp-bundle level) — adoption is nascent and not yet standardised across the dApp ecosystem.
