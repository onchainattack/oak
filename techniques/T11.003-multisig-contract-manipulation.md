# OAK-T11.003 — In-Use Multisig Smart-Contract Manipulation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure), OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit) — partial cross-classification because the on-chain artefact is a contract modification
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM
**First documented:** 2024 (WazirX canonical case)
**Aliases:** "multisig hijack", "in-flight multisig modification"

## Description

A class of attacks in which an attacker, having obtained access to one or more signers of an in-use custodial multisig wallet, uses that access not merely to withdraw funds but to **modify the smart contract controlling the multisig itself** — for example, by changing the signer set, replacing the multisig's logic with a copy that requires only the attacker's signature, or upgrading a proxy to point at attacker-controlled implementation logic. Once the contract has been modified, subsequent extractions require no further signatures from the legitimate signer set. T11.003 is a sub-case of T11.001 (the entry vector is typically a third-party-vendor compromise that gives the attacker partial signing authority) but is documented as a standalone Technique because the on-chain artefact and the post-event mitigation are distinct.

The defender's signal lives at the *contract-modification event* on-chain: a multisig that was operating with a 3-of-5 + 1 threshold and then suddenly accepts unilateral signatures has been modified, and that modification event itself is detectable as a contract-state change.

## Observed indicators

- A multisig contract upgrade or signer-set modification event that is *not* preceded by a publicly-announced governance / operational-change rationale from the operator.
- Subsequent extraction transactions that satisfy the new (attacker-modified) authorisation predicate but that would not have satisfied the prior predicate.
- Off-chain disclosure from the affected operator that the multisig was hijacked rather than merely drained — typically lagging the on-chain artefact by hours.

## Detection signals

- Real-time on-chain: monitor for multisig-modification or proxy-upgrade events at known custodial-multisig contracts; alert on modifications that are not paired with the operator's published change-control announcements.
- Pre-event: maintain an inventory of high-value custodial-multisig contracts with their published authorisation predicates; treat any modification event as a candidate T11.003 incident pending operator-side disclosure.
- Cross-reference: combine T11.003 modification-event monitoring with T7.003 cross-chain bridge laundering monitoring to surface end-to-end attack chains within hours of the contract-modification event.

## Real-world examples

- **WazirX / Liminal multisig — Ethereum — 2024-07-18** — Loss \~\$234.9M. WazirX's multisig used a 3-of-5 + 1 authorisation predicate (5 WazirX signers + 1 Liminal Custody signer; 3 WazirX + 1 Liminal required to authorise transactions). The attackers obtained partial multisig access via the third-party custody surface (T11.001 entry vector), then **altered the smart contract controlling the multisig** so that further extractions did not require additional WazirX signatures. Once the contract was modified, the attackers drained the wallet without further WazirX involvement. OAK-G01 Lazarus Group attribution via global analysis. WazirX subsequently froze trading and entered restructuring; users received \~85% recovery via the restructuring scheme by October 2025. See worked example at `examples/2024-07-wazirx.md` (pending v0.x). Sources: `[wazirxwiki2024]`, `[crystalwazirx2024]`.

## Mitigations

- **Custodial multisig operators:** governance / change-control review for any contract-modification event; mandatory delay between modification proposal and modification execution; runtime-invariant monitoring that pauses operations on modification-event detection pending out-of-band verification.
- **Third-party custody vendors:** the T11.003 entry vector is typically T11.001 (vendor compromise giving partial multisig access); mitigations at the vendor layer are the same as T11.001 mitigations.
- **Risk teams:** cross-reference custodial-multisig modification events against the operator's published change-control history; treat any unannounced modification as a P0 incident pending operator-side disclosure.

## Discussion

T11.003 is structurally important because it changes the defender's reasoning about *what an in-use multisig protects against*. A naive risk model treats a multisig as protecting against any single-signer compromise via the threshold predicate; T11.003 demonstrates that a sufficient-signer compromise plus contract-modification capability collapses the threshold predicate entirely. Contributors writing future T11.003 examples should preserve the contract-modification event as the canonical detection-and-attribution surface — once the contract is modified, the on-chain transaction record reads as legitimate, and only the modification-event itself is the unambiguous attribution signal.

A live design question for custodial-multisig vendors is whether modification authority should be removed from the multisig itself and delegated to a separate, slower-pace governance contract with a mandatory delay. Several post-WazirX custody-vendor architectures have adopted this pattern; OAK does not endorse a specific architecture but documents the design choice as a primary T11.003 mitigation surface.
