# OAK-T6.002 — Fake Audit-Claim

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion); modifier on T1 (Token Genesis), T2 (Liquidity Establishment), T3 (Holder Capture)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (the failure mode lives off-chain; the underlying Technique it modifies may be on any chain)
**First documented:** widespread from approximately 2021 onward
**Aliases:** "audit fraud", "fake CertiK audit", "audit-affiliation impersonation"

## Description

A class of OAK-T6 evasion patterns in which a token, protocol, or service falsely claims security-audit coverage by a recognised audit firm (CertiK, Halborn, Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, etc.) — either by fabricating an audit certificate that the firm did not issue, or by claiming audit affiliation with a firm that has not engaged with the project. The attack surface is the user's reasonable trust that an audit-claim implies professional security review; T6.002 defeats this trust by exploiting the lack of reliable verification infrastructure for audit claims at the user-decision layer.

T6.002 frequently combines with T1.001 / T1.003 / T1.004 (T1 underlying Techniques whose detection static analysis would otherwise surface) by lending the underlying scam contract a defensive-baseline-claim that delays user-side scepticism. The detection signal lives at the audit-firm-side verification layer: the firm itself can confirm or deny the audit claim; the user's burden is to perform that verification before treating the claim as load-bearing.

## Observed indicators

- A project's marketing or smart-contract front-end claims audit by a recognised firm without linking to a verifiable per-firm audit registry entry.
- The named firm publishes a public denial or non-affiliation statement regarding the project (e.g., CertiK's "not conducting an audit and are not affiliated with this project" pattern).
- Discrepancy between the project's claimed audit date / scope and any verifiable artefact at the firm's published audit registry.

## Detection signals

- Per-firm registry verification (highest-leverage): each major audit firm maintains a public registry of audited projects (CertiK Skynet, Halborn published audits, OpenZeppelin Defender audit-trail, Trail of Bits public-audits archive). User-side verification consists of looking up the project in the firm's registry and confirming the audit's existence and scope.
- Per-incident: monitor audit-firm public denial statements; treat any project that has been publicly disclaimed by a named audit firm as elevated risk for any underlying T1 / T2 / T3 Technique it might combine.
- Cross-cohort: per-project audit-claim cluster analysis — projects making audit claims that resolve via the same fabricated-certificate template are typically related at the operator level.

## Real-world examples

- **ATMA case — 2024** — A project claimed it was undergoing CertiK audit; CertiK publicly clarified "not conducting an audit and are not affiliated with this project in any way." Following the public callout, the project deleted its social media presence. Source: `[certikfakeaudit]`.
- **Lymex exit scam — 2024** — Loss \~\$300K. The project falsely claimed CertiK audit coverage; per CertiK's public statement, no audit services were rendered (failed KYC verification at the engagement-attempt stage). The project subsequently exited. Source: `[certikfakeaudit]`.
- **Cohort context** — `[certikfakeaudit]` documents the broader pattern of CertiK-brand misuse including phishing sites with counterfeit security-audit certifications and direct impersonation of audit-firm employees on Telegram.
- **Adjacent — Swaprum, 2023** — \~\$3M rug pull on a CertiK-audited project. This is *not* a T6.002 case (the audit was real); it illustrates that real audit coverage does not bound post-launch operator behaviour. Cited as a defensive-baseline reference. Source: `[dlnewsswaprum2023]`.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T6.002 detection lives at the off-chain audit-registry verification layer; not in MG's on-chain scope).
- Vendor coverage: audit firms themselves (CertiK Skynet, Halborn published-audit registry, etc.); third-party audit-claim verification tooling is sparse at v0.1.

## Mitigations

- **Audit firms (highest-leverage):** maintain machine-readable public registries of audited projects, with cryptographically-verifiable certificates where possible; publish public denial statements promptly when brand misuse is detected; integrate registry-verification APIs that wallet vendors and risk teams can integrate.
- **Wallet vendors / risk teams:** integrate audit-firm registry-verification APIs at the user-warning layer; do not surface "audited" labels based on project-self-claim alone.
- **Standardisation:** machine-readable audit-attestation formats (similar to Sourcify-style cryptographic source-verification) would meaningfully reduce T6.002 attack surface.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M16, OAK-M23, OAK-M24, OAK-M25.

## Discussion

T6.002 is OAK's first audit-related T6 Technique. The choice to scope it tightly around *fake* audit claims (rather than including weak / shallow / partial audits) reflects the cleanly-binary verification surface: the named firm can confirm or deny the audit claim, and that confirmation/denial is operationally actionable. Adjacent failure modes — real audit + post-audit operator action (Swaprum), shallow audit, audit of a contract version different from the deployed version — are documented as adjacent observations but are not classified as T6.002 because the failure mode and mitigation differ.

A live discussion in the audit-firm community concerns whether audit firms should bear more contractual or regulatory obligation to maintain machine-readable public registries; OAK does not opine but documents that the registry-verification gap is the primary defensive surface that T6.002 exploits.
