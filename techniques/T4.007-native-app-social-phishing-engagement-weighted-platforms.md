# OAK-T4.007 — Native-app Social Phishing on Engagement-Weighted Platforms

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (Polygon-resident Polymarket canonical at v0.1; cross-platform analogues across Friend.tech, Pump.fun, Farcaster, and any platform with engagement-weighted in-platform distribution)
**First documented:** 2025-10/11 (Polymarket comment-section phishing campaign exploiting comment-pinning mechanic)
**Aliases:** "comment-section phishing", "engagement-weighted phishing", "in-platform paid-pinning phishing", "native-app social phishing"

## Description

Phishing campaigns whose distribution surface is a paid-for or engagement-weighted *in-platform mechanic* that the attacker manipulates to occupy visibility (e.g., comment-pinning, reply-ranking, engagement-weighted feed ranking). The platform's own mechanics fund or amplify the distribution surface; the attacker buys visibility through a legitimate platform mechanic rather than through external advertising or DNS-level compromise.

T4.007 is **structurally distinct** from sibling T4 Techniques:

- **T4.001 (Permit2 Authority Misuse)** — defines the on-chain extraction primitive. T4.007 is a *distribution-surface* sub-Technique; the on-chain extraction primitive may be Permit2 misuse (T4.001), allowance-drainer (T4.004), setApprovalForAll-drainer (T4.005), or — distinctively for the canonical case — a *non-on-chain* credential-capture (email-magic-link verification code captured by a fake login + fake captcha, exploiting embedded-wallet identity-provider auth flow).
- **T4.002 (Compromised Front-End Permit Solicitation)** — DNS-hijack / supply-chain JS / BGP / registrar compromise of a real platform's user-facing surface. T4.007 does not compromise the legitimate platform's surface; the attacker uses the platform's own legitimate engagement-weighted mechanic to anchor phishing visibility.
- **T4.008 (Fake-DEX Clone-Frontend Phishing)** — paid-for inventory on a *legitimate ad platform* (Google Search ads, X promoted posts, Telegram bot traffic, App Store) distributing counterfeit DEX UIs. T4.007 distributes phishing through the *target platform's own* engagement mechanic.

The structural feature is that the platform's own mechanics fund the distribution surface. The attacker's spending is denominated in the platform's native asset (Polymarket positions in the canonical case; potential extensions to Friend.tech keys, Pump.fun bonding-curve buys, Farcaster casts) and the distribution-surface visibility is the return on that spending — independent of any external ad-platform enforcement surface.

## Observed indicators

- **Platform mechanic exploited:** comment-pinning ranks comments by the commenter's combined position rather than by community signals; reply-ranking on engagement-weighted platforms; feed-ranking algorithms that reward in-platform spending.
- **Symmetric position-taking:** attacker buys both YES and NO shares (or equivalent symmetric exposure) on a high-volume market specifically to anchor pinning visibility; the position is not directional and the platform-native asset spend is the cost-of-distribution rather than a directional bet.
- **High-volume / high-engagement target markets:** attacker selects markets with high cumulative volume to maximise the eyeball-count downstream of the pinned comment slot.
- **Phishing-comment content pattern:** "private markets with better odds" / "exclusive trading group" / "claim airdrop here" framings that direct users to an obfuscated URL — typically a tracking / redirect domain that resolves to a cloned platform login page.
- **Credential-capture stage:** the credential-capture flow may exploit the platform's identity-provider integration directly (fake login + fake captcha capturing email-magic-link verification code in the canonical case) or chain into a standard T4 on-chain authority grant.

## Detection signals

- **Platform-side comment-ranking audit:** detect symmetric position-taking patterns (buying YES + NO at sizes calibrated to anchor pinning) as a discrete signal independent of comment content moderation; surface elevated-risk pinned-comment slots for human review.
- **Cross-vendor credential-monitoring:** when phishing campaigns target an embedded-wallet identity-provider (Magic Labs / Privy / Web3Auth), independent vendors (CoinSpot, Phemex, BingX, Cryptopolitan in the canonical case) often surface campaign existence and scale before platform-side moderation catches up.
- **User-side `we-will-never-DM-you-first` invariant:** platform-published invariants (the canonical Polymarket statement: "we will never DM you first; do not click external links from comments") form the user-side mitigation but the operational signal is *user-attention rather than detection automation*.
- **DaaS-kit-fingerprinting absence as a finding:** the canonical case did not exhibit a publicly-named DaaS-kit fingerprint (Inferno / Angel / Pink / Monkey / Venom / Vanilla / Chick); operator opsec consistent with DaaS affiliate behaviour but no kit confirmed. The absence-of-kit-fingerprint is itself a finding for cohort-level attribution analysis.

## Real-world examples

- **Polymarket comment-section phishing campaign — Polygon — 2025-11 (canonical T4.007 case)** — ≥ $500,000 cumulative loss across affected users; per-incident losses individually unlogged but disclosed in aggregate by Polymarket support and corroborated by independent vendors (CoinSpot, Phemex, BingX, Cryptopolitan). Through October-November 2025, attackers ran a coordinated phishing campaign exploiting Polymarket's native comment-section mechanic. The campaign worked as follows: (1) attacker selected high-volume, high-engagement Polymarket markets (election trackers, sports outcomes, geopolitical events); (2) attacker bought *both* YES and NO shares on the target market in size sufficient to anchor a "top comment" — Polymarket's comment-pinning mechanic ranks comments by the commenter's combined position, not by reaction count, so the phishing comment remained pinned regardless of community downvotes; (3) the pinned comment offered "private markets with better odds" and linked to an obfuscated URL — typically a tracking/redirect domain that resolved to a cloned Polymarket login page; (4) the cloned login page presented a fake Cloudflare turnstile challenge and a Polymarket-branded login form that captured the user's email and the email-magic-link verification code; (5) with email + code, the attacker authenticated to Magic Labs as the user, gained access to the user's Polymarket-derived non-custodial wallet, and transferred USDC to attacker-controlled addresses. Independent disclosure on November 11, 2025 (user handle "25usdc" on X) and follow-up reporting from CoinSpot, Phemex, and BingX placed cumulative losses above $500,000. Attribution: `inferred-strong`; Polymarket and independent vendors confirmed campaign existence and scale; specific operator-cluster attribution not published — operator pattern is consistent with OAK-G02 (Drainer-as-a-Service) but no specific DaaS-kit fingerprint has been confirmed in this campaign — itself a finding. The chain into T11.008 (Embedded-Wallet Identity-Provider Compromise) is the load-bearing custody-substrate axis: the phishing captures the email-magic-link credential at the T4.007 layer and exploits Polymarket's Magic Labs auth path at the T11.008 layer. See [`examples/2025-11-polymarket-comment-section-phishing.md`](../examples/2025-11-polymarket-comment-section-phishing.md).

## Mitigations

- **Platform-side mechanic redesign (highest-leverage):** comment-pinning and reply-ranking algorithms should incorporate community-signal weighting (downvotes, flag rates, account-age) alongside in-platform-spending signals so that paid pinning cannot persist against community downvotes. The canonical Polymarket case is structurally significant *because* the mechanic ranked solely by combined position with no community-signal override.
- **Platform-side phishing-content moderation:** automated detection of obfuscated URLs, tracking-redirect domains, and known typosquat / near-miss domains in pinned-comment slots; human review for pinned-comment content on high-volume markets.
- **Platform-side user-education invariants:** publish in-product warnings (the canonical Polymarket "we will never DM you first; do not click external links from comments" invariant) at the points of maximum user attention — login flow, deposit flow, market-detail page.
- **Embedded-wallet identity-provider hardening (T11.008 chain):** because T4.007 in the canonical case chains into T11.008 (email-magic-link credential capture exploiting Magic Labs auth), platforms should require hardware-backed multi-factor authentication for transaction-signing flows; rate-limit magic-link verification-code attempts; surface unusual-login alerts to users in real time.
- **Independent-vendor cohort-coordination:** the canonical case was first surfaced through independent-vendor disclosure (user handle "25usdc" on X) before platform-side acknowledgment. Industry-press cohort coordination accelerates platform-side response and informs the user base across competing platforms.

OAK Mitigation cross-references at v0.1 are a **gap** — no existing OAK-M* entry directly maps to engagement-weighted-platform mechanic redesign. v0.x candidate Mitigation entries: "OAK-M-x community-signal-weighted ranking on engagement mechanics"; "OAK-M-x platform-side phishing-content moderation in pinned slots"; "OAK-M-x platform-published user-education invariants at high-attention points".

## Discussion

T4.007 closes a structural gap in OAK v0.1's T4 coverage. The dominant v0.1 T4 sub-Techniques (T4.001-T4.006) cover specific on-chain extraction primitives (Permit2, allowance, setApprovalForAll, address-poisoning, WalletConnect-session-hijack) and the compromised-frontend distribution surface (T4.002). T4.007 introduces a **distribution-surface-by-platform-mechanic-exploitation** failure surface that is structurally distinct from compromised-frontend distribution and from external-ad-platform distribution (T4.008).

The structural OAK lesson is that **engagement-weighted platforms create paid-distribution surfaces denominated in the platform's native asset, with attacker-controllable visibility independent of any external ad-platform enforcement**. The platform's own mechanics fund the distribution surface; the attacker's spending is a *cost-of-distribution* rather than a directional bet.

The pattern generalises beyond Polymarket. Any platform where engagement-weighted ranking determines visibility — Friend.tech-class apps (key-purchase ranking), Pump.fun (bonding-curve buy ranking), Farcaster (cast engagement weighting), and any future platform with similar mechanics — is exposed to the same attack class. The canonical Polymarket case is structurally significant because the comment-pinning mechanic ranked solely by combined position with no community-signal override; platform-mechanic redesigns that incorporate community-signal weighting bound the attack surface but do not eliminate it.

The credential-capture stage in the canonical case is structurally distinctive within T4. Most T4 sub-Techniques target an on-chain authority grant (Permit2 signature, allowance, setApprovalForAll). The Polymarket case targets the *embedded-wallet identity-provider auth credential* (email-magic-link verification code), chaining into T11.008 (Embedded-Wallet Identity-Provider Compromise). The combined T4.007–T11.008 attack chain is the canonical illustration of how phishing distribution surfaces and embedded-wallet custody surfaces interact in the prediction-market and broader embedded-wallet ecosystem.

The DaaS-kit-fingerprinting absence is structurally informative. The canonical case did not exhibit a publicly-named DaaS-kit fingerprint despite operator opsec consistent with DaaS affiliate behaviour. The absence-of-kit-fingerprint pattern suggests either (a) custom kit development for the embedded-wallet credential-capture sub-pattern (which is structurally distinct from on-chain authority-grant kits), or (b) a kit that is not yet publicly named at v0.1 reporting horizon. Future T4.007 worked examples should preserve the kit-fingerprint dimension explicitly.

The platform-side surveillance-partnership response (Polymarket–Chainalysis announced April 2026) is the canonical platform-level mitigation deployment for the broader Polymarket cohort. T4.007 mitigation specifically benefits from comment-ranking algorithm redesign (community-signal weighting) and pinned-comment content moderation (URL / domain reputation analysis at the moderation layer).

Promotion to `stable` will follow once additional cohort cases land — Friend.tech-equivalent engagement-weighted-platform phishing cohorts; Pump.fun comment-section phishing cohorts; Farcaster engagement-weighted-distribution phishing cohorts. The class anchor at v0.1 (Polymarket comment-section campaign) is sufficient for `emerging` status but the multi-platform anchor pattern that warrants `stable` is still forming.
