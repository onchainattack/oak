# OAK-T4.006 — WalletConnect Session Hijack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (primary), Solana, multi-chain (any chain WalletConnect supports)
**First documented:** systematic 2023 onward; mobile-app-impersonation cases at scale 2024
**Aliases:** "WalletConnect phishing", "fake-dApp pairing", "QR-code wallet hijack"

## Description

A class of phishing flows in which a victim is induced to establish a WalletConnect session with a malicious dApp (or with software masquerading as a legitimate dApp), after which the attacker uses the session to solicit signing of malicious payloads. Distinct from OAK-T4.001 (Permit2 Signature-Based Authority Misuse) because the entry vector is the **session-establishment** step rather than the signing-flow step inside an already-trusted dApp. Distinct from OAK-T4.002 (Compromised Front-End) because the attacker controls a separate fake-dApp / malicious-app surface rather than compromising a legitimate dApp's distribution channel.

Real-world cases combine three sub-vectors: (a) malicious mobile apps masquerading as legitimate WalletConnect-using dApps (App Store / Google Play distribution), (b) QR-code phishing on social media or in physical-world contexts that establishes a WalletConnect session with attacker infrastructure, and (c) deep-link-based malicious dApp invocation that bypasses traditional URL inspection. The per-session payloads themselves typically chain into T4.001 or T4.004 — i.e., once the session is hijacked, the attacker uses standard signature-phishing or approve-pattern flows.

## Observed indicators

- Mobile-app distribution channels (App Store, Google Play) hosting apps with names mimicking WalletConnect-using dApps (e.g., "Mestox Calculator", "WalletConnect — DeFi & NFTs", "WalletConnect — Airdrop Wallet").
- Session-establishment events with peer dApps whose URL or deep-link is structurally inconsistent with the user's intended interaction.
- Per-session signing-payload patterns matching OAK-T4.001 or OAK-T4.004 signatures (the session is the entry vector; the actual extraction is a chained Technique).

## Detection signals

- Wallet-side (highest-leverage): integrate per-dApp domain / app-store-package allowlists; warn at session-establishment time when the peer dApp is not on the allowlist; surface session-history with operator-clustering alerts.
- App-store moderation: detect impersonation patterns at app-store level; remove malicious apps following user reports.
- On-chain: post-session, the resulting signing payloads chain into T4.001 / T4.004 — detection at the chained-Technique layer applies.

## Real-world examples

- **Fake "WalletConnect" mobile-app campaign — Google Play — 2024-09** — A malicious app distributed under multiple names ("Mestox Calculator", "WalletConnect — DeFi & NFTs", "WalletConnect — Airdrop Wallet") achieved over 10,000 downloads via Google Play, ranking via fake reviews and brand impersonation. Approximately \$70K stolen across over 150 victims. The malicious app used smart contracts and deep links to silently drain assets once users were tricked into using the app. Sources: industry reporting (The Hacker News) and follow-up CTI write-ups.
- **WalletConnect protocol-level vulnerability disclosure — 2022** — A Trail of Bits audit identified HTML5 local-storage usage for session data as a medium-severity vulnerability (CVE-2022-28843) due to susceptibility to cross-site scripting attacks. The vulnerability is at the protocol-implementation layer rather than at the user-interaction layer, but is documented for completeness.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T4.006 detection lives at the wallet-UX layer + app-store-moderation layer, not at the on-chain layer where MG operates).
- Vendor coverage: wallet vendors with rich session-history and peer-dApp warnings (Trust Wallet, Coinbase Wallet, Rabby); WalletConnect protocol-side telemetry where exposed.

## Mitigations

- **Wallets (highest-leverage):** maintain per-dApp domain / app-store-package allowlists; warn at session-establishment time on non-allowlisted peers; surface session-history with operator-clustering alerts; require explicit user confirmation for high-value signing payloads regardless of session trust state.
- **App stores:** detect impersonation patterns; require WalletConnect-using apps to verify domain / dApp affiliation as a publishing precondition.
- **End users:** establish WalletConnect sessions only from canonical dApp URLs (read directly, not via QR code from untrusted source); prefer session-by-session connections over persistent sessions for high-value wallets.

## Discussion

T4.006 is the entry-vector-specific framing for what frequently chains into T4.001 / T4.004 / T4.005. The defender's analytical question is whether a session-establishment-time warning would have prevented the chained extraction; in essentially all documented cases, the answer is yes — the failure mode is consent-to-session, not consent-to-payload. Contributors writing future T4.006 examples should expect the chained-Technique pattern and link explicitly to whichever T4 sub-Technique handles the actual extraction.

The mobile-app-distribution sub-vector (Google Play / App Store impersonation) is the most operationally-scalable T4.006 entry vector at v0.1 and is expected to grow as mobile-wallet usage grows. Contributors should monitor app-store moderation policy changes as a defensive-baseline signal — improvements in app-store impersonation detection move the T4.006 attack surface across mobile platforms.
