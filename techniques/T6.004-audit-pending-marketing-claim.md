# OAK-T6.004 — Audit-Pending Marketing Claim

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion); modifier on T1 (Token Genesis), T2 (Liquidity Establishment), T3 (Holder Capture)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (the failure mode lives off-chain at the marketing-claim layer; the underlying Technique it modifies may be on any chain)
**First documented:** widespread from approximately 2021 onward
**Aliases:** "audit pending", "audit in progress", "audit forthcoming", "working with [firm]", "audit Q[X]"

## Description

A class of OAK-T6 evasion patterns in which a project markets itself as having an audit *in progress*, *forthcoming*, *pending*, or *being arranged with a named audit firm* — without an audit engagement actually being in progress, contracted, or planned at the named firm. The attack surface is the user's reasonable inference that "audit pending" implies an active or imminent professional security review; T6.004 defeats this inference by exploiting the asymmetry between forward-looking claims (which have no canonical verification surface) and backward-looking claims (which can be checked against the audit firm's published registry).

T6.004 is distinct from OAK-T6.002 (Fake Audit-Claim) along the temporal dimension. T6.002 falsely claims a *completed* audit and is defeated by per-firm registry verification: the audit either exists in the registry or it does not. T6.004 claims an audit *in progress* or *forthcoming* — a claim about future state, against which a registry-of-completed-audits lookup is structurally inconclusive (the audit not being in the registry yet is consistent with both an honest pending engagement and a fabricated one). The defender's analytical question shifts from "is this audit in the registry?" to "is the cited engagement actually in progress at the named firm?", and the verification surface shifts from the firm's audit-completed registry to the firm's engagement-tracking layer — which most firms do not expose publicly at v0.1.

T6.004 is distinct from OAK-T6.003 (Audit-of-Different-Bytecode-Version) in that T6.003 has a real, registry-listed audit whose audited bytecode does not match the deployed bytecode; T6.004 has no audit at all — only a forward-looking claim. T6.003's failure mode is scope drift between audited and deployed code; T6.004's failure mode is fabricated or never-materialising future engagement.

T6.004 is also distinct from "soft" project-side audit-language failures in which a project did engage a firm but missed its self-promised timeline — those cases have a real engagement contract or quote behind the marketing claim, even if the claim's timeline anchoring is unrealistic. T6.004 specifically covers cases where no engagement, no contract, and no quote exist behind the "pending" framing.

Sub-patterns observed in the wild:

- (a) Claim of pending audit that never materialises; project rugs / fails / disappears before any audit lands, with the "audit pending" claim having served only as a launch-window defensive-baseline assertion.
- (b) "We're working with [firm]" framing with no engagement contract, no quote, and no acknowledgement from the named firm — frequently combined with logo-borrowing on the project's marketing site.
- (c) Audit timeline pushed indefinitely as the project progresses, with user expectations anchored to a never-arriving artefact ("audit Q3" → "audit Q4" → "audit early next year" → silence).

## Observed indicators

- A project's marketing or smart-contract front-end claims an audit is "in progress", "pending", "forthcoming", "scheduled", or "being arranged" with a named audit firm — without linking to an engagement-tracking entry, public quote, or firm-side acknowledgement.
- A project displays an audit-firm logo or brand mark adjacent to "coming soon", "Q[X]", or similar language with no firm-side artefact backing the affiliation.
- The named firm publishes a public denial or non-engagement statement regarding the project (analogous to T6.002's denial pattern but applied to forward-looking rather than backward-looking claims).
- The project's stated audit timeline slips repeatedly across launch, liquidity establishment, and holder-capture phases with no public engagement artefact ever surfacing.
- The "audit pending" claim is removed from project marketing material around the same time the project rugs or stalls, leaving no archival trace of the original engagement assertion.

## Detection signals

- **Per-firm engagement-verification (highest-leverage):** for any "audit pending with [firm]" claim, query the named firm directly (or via its engagement-tracking surface, where one is published) to confirm that an engagement is in progress. Most major firms will respond to direct inquiries about whether they have engaged with a named project; absence of confirmation is the canonical T6.004 signal.
- **Per-firm registry-absence consistency check:** confirm the project does not appear in the firm's completed-audit registry. This is necessary but not sufficient — registry-absence is consistent with both an honest pending engagement and a fabricated one — but it bounds the question and rules out misclassification as T6.002 (claim-of-completed-audit) versus T6.004 (claim-of-pending-audit).
- **Per-incident:** monitor audit-firm public denial statements specifically about *engagement claims* (as distinct from completed-audit claims). Treat any project that has been publicly disclaimed by a named firm at the engagement layer as elevated risk for any underlying T1 / T2 / T3 Technique it might combine.
- **Timeline-slip analysis:** track project marketing claims over time; repeated audit-timeline slips with no public engagement artefact between slips is a strong T6.004 indicator. The pattern is most visible in archival snapshots (Wayback Machine, Discord logs) rather than at any single point in time.
- **Cross-cohort:** projects making "audit pending" claims that share fabricated-engagement-template language, identical firm-logo placements, or identical timeline-slip cadences are typically related at the operator level. Cohort-level pattern matching surfaces the class even where per-incident verification is lossy.

## Real-world examples

- **Cohort scale — multi-chain — 2021 onward** — T6.004 is most visible at the cohort level rather than the per-incident level, because "audit-pending" promises are typically made in marketing material (project websites, Discord pinned messages, Twitter threads) that disappears with the project. Industry retrospectives (`[chainalysis2025rug]`, `[slowmist2024report]`) document the pattern at cohort scale: forward-looking audit claims are a recurring component of commodity-scam-token launch playbooks, paired with T1 / T2 / T3 underlying Techniques to delay user-side scepticism through the holder-capture phase.
- **Audit-firm denial cohort — 2024** — `[certikfakeaudit]` documents the broader pattern of CertiK-brand misuse. While the primary cases cited (ATMA, Lymex) are T6.002 (completed-audit claims), the same cohort includes engagement-claim impersonation: projects framing themselves as "currently undergoing CertiK audit" or "in discussions with CertiK" without an engagement actually being in progress. CertiK's public-denial pattern ("not conducting an audit and are not affiliated with this project in any way") explicitly covers both backward-looking and forward-looking framings.
- **Per-incident named cases** — OAK does not at v0.1 name a single canonical T6.004 incident with public forensic disclosure of the engagement-claim-vs-engagement-record gap. The class is documented at industry-retrospective cohort scale; per-incident write-ups that specifically document a fabricated "audit pending" claim (with archival evidence of the original marketing material and firm-side denial of engagement) are encouraged via PR.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T6.004 detection lives at the off-chain engagement-verification layer; not in MG's on-chain scope).
- Vendor coverage: audit-firm engagement-tracking transparency is **emerging** rather than mature at v0.1. Most major firms publish completed-audit registries (the T6.002 verification surface) but do not publish in-progress-engagement registries (the T6.004 verification surface). Firms respond to direct inquiries about engagements but the response is not machine-readable and does not scale to cohort-level monitoring.
- Adjacent: project-marketing-claim archival infrastructure (Wayback Machine, Discord-log archives) provides the timeline-slip side of the detection signal; the firm-side engagement-acknowledgement surface is the gap.

## Mitigations

- **Audit firms (highest-leverage):** publish a machine-readable engagement-tracking surface — projects currently under engagement, with engagement-start date and expected-completion window — alongside the existing completed-audit registry. This converts T6.004 verification from a manual direct-inquiry exercise into a registry-API lookup that wallet vendors and risk teams can integrate. Where commercial confidentiality concerns prevent public engagement disclosure, an opt-in attestation API (firm confirms "yes, this project is under engagement" against a queried project identifier) provides a privacy-preserving alternative.
- **Standardisation:** machine-readable cryptographic engagement attestations binding the engagement to the firm — analogous to the audit-attestation format proposed for T6.003 but at the engagement layer rather than the completed-audit layer. An attestation a project can display on its marketing surface, that wallets and explorers can verify against the firm's public key, would reduce T6.004 to a deterministic signature check.
- **Marketplaces / launchpads / DEX aggregators:** require firm-side engagement attestation before surfacing any "audit-pending" label in the listing UI; treat unverified pending-audit claims as project-self-claim with no defensive weight, and surface this distinction explicitly to the user.
- **Wallet vendors / risk teams:** treat "audit pending" project-self-claims as a marketing assertion, not a defensive baseline; do not surface "audit-pending" labels at the user-warning layer based on project-self-claim alone; where a firm-side engagement attestation is available, surface it; where it is not, surface the absence explicitly rather than echoing the project's framing.
- **Project teams:** publish the engagement contract reference, firm-side quote artefact, or engagement-acknowledgement letter alongside any "audit pending" marketing claim; for engagements that have not yet been finalised, frame the claim as "exploring audit with [firm]" rather than "audit pending with [firm]" to preserve the distinction between active engagement and prospective engagement.
- **User-side:** treat any "audit pending" or "audit forthcoming" claim as carrying zero defensive weight until firm-side engagement confirmation is obtained; the asymmetry between fabrication-cost (a marketing-line assertion) and verification-cost (a direct firm inquiry) makes user-side scepticism the only viable defence at v0.1 absent engagement-tracking infrastructure.

## Discussion

T6.004 closes the fourth corner of the OAK-T6 audit-trust failure-mode space, completing the temporal axis of audit-claim evasion: T6.002 covers fabricated *backward-looking* claims (audit said to have happened, did not), T6.003 covers technically-true backward-looking claims that fail at the bytecode-scope layer (audit happened, but not on this code), and T6.004 covers fabricated *forward-looking* claims (audit said to be in progress, is not). The three audit-related Techniques together describe the failure modes of the user-facing assertion "this project has audit coverage" across both temporal directions and bound the corresponding mitigation surfaces: completed-audit registry verification (T6.002), audit-firm-side bytecode attestation (T6.003), and engagement-tracking transparency (T6.004).

The choice to scope T6.004 around *engagement-claim fabrication* (rather than including over-promised audit timelines on real engagements) reflects the same cleanly-binary-verification standard applied across T6.001–T6.003: the question "is there an engagement contract or firm-side acknowledgement behind this claim?" is determinate given the firm-side artefact. Adjacent failure modes — real engagement with slipped timeline, real engagement that the project later cancelled before audit completion, real engagement on a contract version that will not be the deployed contract — are documented as adjacent observations but are not classified as T6.004 because the failure mode is qualitative rather than binary and the mitigation is editorial rather than mechanical.

The forward-looking-vs-backward-looking asymmetry is the structural reason T6.004 is harder to defend against than T6.002. A backward-looking claim has a canonical verification surface (the completed-audit registry); a forward-looking claim has no such surface at v0.1 because audit firms generally do not publish in-progress-engagement registries. The asymmetry is not a property of the underlying engagements — both are facts the firm knows — but a property of the firm's published surfaces. Closing the asymmetry is a coordination problem at the audit-firm-industry layer rather than a technical problem at the protocol layer.

A live discussion in the audit-firm community concerns whether engagement-tracking transparency should be a default deliverable, balanced against commercial-confidentiality concerns of clients in early-engagement phases. OAK does not opine but documents that the engagement-tracking gap — the absence of a machine-readable in-progress-engagement registry — is the primary defensive surface that T6.004 exploits, and that closing this gap (whether via opt-in attestation API, public engagement registry, or cryptographic engagement-attestation standard) is the canonical mitigation. Until that infrastructure matures, T6.004 detection at scale remains a per-project manual exercise, and forward-looking audit claims at the user-decision layer should be treated as carrying zero defensive weight.
