# OAK-T4.013 — Endpoint Infostealer Wallet-Material and Credential Exfiltration

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** cross-chain (commodity infostealers enumerate every browser-extension wallet and desktop wallet application present on the host regardless of chain — MetaMask, Phantom, Trust Wallet, Exodus, Electrum, and dozens more — plus raw key/keystore files)
**First documented:** 2011-06 (allInVain `wallet.dat` theft from a Windows PC — the earliest documented host-malware wallet-file extraction); commodity malware-as-a-service scale 2016 onward (Azorult), with the modern crypto-targeting stealer families RedLine (2020), Raccoon, Vidar, and Lumma / LummaC2 (2022 onward); trusted-platform game/content delivery anchors 2025–2026 (Steam game Chemia, July 2025; Steam Workshop / Wallpaper Engine campaign, June 2026)
**Aliases:** "infostealer", "info-stealer", "stealer malware", "crypto stealer", "wallet stealer", "RedLine / Vidar / Lumma stealer", "wallet-data exfiltration malware"

## Description

Host-resident malware that scrapes **wallet key material and authentication state at rest** from a victim's own endpoint and exfiltrates it to the attacker. Where a clipper (T4.012) redirects a single in-flight transfer, an infostealer takes the **keys themselves** — granting full, standing control of every wallet whose material lived on the device. A single successful infection commonly harvests, in one sweep:

- **Browser-extension wallet vaults** — the encrypted vault stores (LevelDB / IndexedDB) of MetaMask, Phantom, Trust Wallet, Rabby, Coinbase Wallet, Keplr, and dozens of others, for offline password brute-forcing or direct seed/key recovery.
- **Desktop-wallet files** — `wallet.dat` (Bitcoin Core), Electrum / Exodus / Atomic / Ledger Live data directories, and any keystore / mnemonic files on disk.
- **Saved browser credentials, autofill, and crypto-exchange logins** stored in the browser password store.
- **Session cookies and authentication tokens** — enabling **live session hijacking** of exchange, Steam, and webmail sessions, which bypasses passwords and MFA entirely for the lifetime of the stolen session.
- **The clipboard and local files** matching seed-phrase / private-key patterns.

The crypto-relevant primitive OAK classifies here is the **harvesting and exfiltration of wallet material and authentication state from the endpoint at rest**. The surrounding malware facets — the loader/dropper that stages the stealer (RenEngine, Hijack Loader), packing and anti-analysis, persistence, and C2 (frequently Telegram-bot or HTTP exfiltration) — are general-malware delivery/operational context that ride alongside the primitive and are not minted as separate crypto-attack Techniques, consistent with how OAK scopes endpoint-malware incidents elsewhere (cf. T4.012).

T4.013 is **structurally distinct** from the adjacent classes:

- **T4.012 (Clipboard-Hijacker / Clipper Address Substitution).** A clipper substitutes the *destination address* at copy time and harvests secrets *as they transit the clipboard*; the victim still authorises the transfer, and only funds sent during the infection window are at risk. T4.013 extracts the *stored key material itself* directly from disk / browser stores, so the attacker gains independent, durable control of the wallet and can drain it at any later time without the victim transacting at all. Modern commodity malware frequently ships both arms; OAK maps the dominant value-extraction primitive of a given campaign and cross-references the other.
- **T11.006 (Cold-storage Seed-phrase Exfiltration at Rest).** T11.006's substrate is seed material in a *third-party storage service* (cloud backup, password-manager Secure Notes) compromised server-side, where the user did not realise they had created a custody surface. T4.013's substrate is the *victim's own endpoint*, compromised by host-resident malware; no third-party storage service is involved.
- **T15.003 / T15.004 (Operator-Endpoint / Operator-Credential Compromise).** Those Techniques scope the *operator / enterprise* endpoint — developer workstations, signing machines, employee laptops whose state controls a protocol or exchange signing/auth surface. T4.013 is the **commodity consumer-endpoint** shape: the victim is an individual wallet holder, the malware is mass-distributed malware-as-a-service rather than a targeted operator implant, and the value extracted is the individual's own holdings. (The 2011 allInVain `wallet.dat` case is historically mapped to T15 "broadly construed" only because no consumer-endpoint home existed; T4.013 gives that shape its own home.)
- **T4.010 (Fake Security-Tool / Browser-Extension Phishing).** There the *malicious browser extension is itself* the exfiltration tool, published to an extension store and installed voluntarily. T4.013 is standalone malware that scrapes the victim's *existing, legitimate* wallets; it is delivery-channel-agnostic and does not require the user to install a counterfeit wallet.

**Delivery is malware-class-typical and varies independently of the stealer logic**: trojanised games and content on trusted platforms (Steam games; Steam Workshop executable "application" wallpapers), cracked/keygen software bundles, fake-tool and fake-update downloads, malvertising and SEO-poisoned download pages, phishing attachments, fake CAPTCHA / "ClickFix" paste-and-run lures, and loader/dropper chains. The **trusted-platform content-channel** delivery shape — abusing a legitimate first-party platform's user-generated-content or game-distribution feature so the payload inherits the platform's trust — is tracked as forward candidate **OAK-T4.013.001** in TAXONOMY-GAPS (anchors: Steam Workshop / Wallpaper Engine, June 2026; Steam game Chemia, July 2025).

## Observed indicators

- A short-lived process that reads browser-profile directories (`Local Storage`, `IndexedDB`, `Login Data`, `Cookies`) and known wallet-application data directories shortly after execution, then exfiltrates a compressed archive.
- Enumeration of browser-extension IDs corresponding to wallet extensions (MetaMask `nkbihfbeogaeaoehlefnkodbefgpgknn`, Phantom, Trust Wallet, etc.) and copying of their vault stores.
- Collection of `wallet.dat` / keystore / `*.mnemonic` / seed-phrase-shaped files by filename and content pattern across user directories.
- Theft of session cookies / auth tokens followed by attacker logins from a new device/geo that reuse the session without triggering a password or MFA prompt (session-hijack signature).
- Exfiltration to a Telegram bot API endpoint, a paste service, or a short-lived HTTP C2; staged by a separate loader (RenEngine, Hijack Loader) that fetches the stealer second-stage.
- Delivery artefacts: payload bundled inside a downloaded game/app, or hidden in a **password-protected archive** whose password is embedded in the archive name or a bundled JSON/config so AV cannot scan the contents pre-extraction.

## Detection signals

- **Endpoint (highest leverage):** EDR/AV behavioural detection of processes touching multiple browser-profile and wallet-application data paths in rapid succession; the major commodity families carry stable AV signatures (Vidar, Lumma/LummaC2, RedLine, Fickle; Microsoft / Kaspersky / ESET stealer families).
- **Browser/wallet store access:** flag non-browser processes reading extension `Local Storage` / `IndexedDB` vault stores or the browser `Login Data` / `Cookies` databases.
- **Session-hijack telemetry (exchange / platform side):** anomalous reuse of a session token from a new device fingerprint or geo without a corresponding authentication event — the value-realisation half of a cookie-theft infection.
- **Network:** exfiltration to Telegram bot endpoints / paste services / freshly-registered C2 from a short-lived child process of a loader; archive POSTs immediately after browser-data reads.
- **Delivery-side:** password-protected archives with the password in the filename/config; executable content delivered through a trusted platform's UGC channel (Steam Workshop "application" wallpapers, trojanised Early Access games) — treat auto-executing community content as an active-code trust boundary.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2026-06-steam-workshop-wallpaper-engine-infostealer.md`](../examples/2026-06-steam-workshop-wallpaper-engine-infostealer.md) — Kaspersky "Dozens of malicious wallpapers found on Steam Workshop" — Windows / cross-chain — disclosed 2026-06-16, active since ≥ August 2025. Dozens of trojanised Wallpaper Engine "application" wallpapers on Steam Workshop auto-executing **Lumma** and **Vidar** infostealers (plus RenEngine loader, DarkKomet backdoor, miners, ransomware) that steal Steam credentials + live sessions, browser data, and crypto-wallet material; thousands–tens-of-thousands of downloads per package; 89% of attempts in China. **Canonical anchor**, and the **trusted-platform content-channel delivery** sub-shape (T4.013.001).
- **allInVain `wallet.dat` theft — Bitcoin — 2011-06 (historical class anchor).** ~25,000 BTC stolen from a single user's Windows PC via extraction of the unencrypted `wallet.dat` file — the earliest documented host-malware wallet-material theft, establishing the "your coins are only as safe as the machine your keys live on" lesson. Historically mapped to T15.003 / T15.004 "broadly construed" for lack of a consumer-endpoint home; see [`examples/2011-06-allinvain-first-major-bitcoin-theft.md`](../examples/2011-06-allinvain-first-major-bitcoin-theft.md).
- **Steam game Chemia (EncryptHub / LARVA-208) — 2025-07 (trusted-platform delivery precedent).** Prodaft reported the Early Access game Chemia trojanised to distribute **Hijack Loader + Fickle Stealer + Vidar Stealer** targeting crypto wallets and browser data, with Telegram-fetched instructions — the independent prior anchor for the trusted-platform game-distribution delivery shape (T4.013.001) and part of the FBI's 2026 Steam-malware investigation (Chemia, PirateFi, BlockBlasters, and others).

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. T4.013's actionable detection lives at the endpoint (EDR / browser-and-wallet-store access behaviour) and at the platform-account layer (session-hijack telemetry), not at the on-chain runtime risk-engine surface MG operates on.
- Vendor coverage: endpoint AV/EDR stealer detections (Microsoft Defender, Kaspersky, ESET, SentinelOne stealer families for Vidar / Lumma / RedLine / Fickle); browser-side phishing/extension protections; exchange-side session-anomaly and device-binding controls; wallet-hardware isolation that keeps key material off the general-purpose endpoint.

## Mitigations

- **OAK-M19 (Air-Gap Cold-Wallet Signing):** highest-leverage — key material that never resides on an internet-connected general-purpose endpoint cannot be scraped by an endpoint stealer; hardware wallets and air-gapped signers keep the seed/key off the surface T4.013 harvests.
- **OAK-M22 (Rotate-on-Disclosure Discipline):** any seed phrase, private key, keystore, or session that lived on a host suspected of stealer infection must be treated as compromised — migrate funds to freshly-generated keys on a clean device and invalidate all active sessions.
- **OAK-M30 (Per-dApp Domain and App-Store-Package Allowlist) / OAK-M40 (Supply-Chain Package Integrity):** counter the delivery shape — install games, tools, and content only from verified first-party sources; treat auto-executing community/UGC content (Steam Workshop "application" wallpapers, trojanised game builds) and password-protected download archives as adversarial.
- **OAK-M21 (Anti-Phishing Training for Privileged Staff):** endpoint hygiene against the dominant lures — cracked software, fake updates, paste-and-run / fake-CAPTCHA prompts, and password-protected-archive downloads.
- **OAK-M18 (Out-of-Band Destination Verification):** mitigates the co-resident clipper/substitution arm many stealers also carry (cross-ref T4.012).

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M19, OAK-M22, OAK-M30, OAK-M40.

## Discussion

T4.013 fills a long-standing structural gap. Commodity infostealers are among the highest-volume crypto-theft vectors in the wild — RedLine, Vidar, and Lumma logs are traded at industrial scale and routinely include wallet vaults and exchange sessions — yet OAK previously had no consumer-endpoint home for the primitive. The closest mappings were awkward: T11.006 scopes itself to *third-party storage*, T4.010 to *counterfeit extensions*, and the operator-endpoint Techniques (T15.003 / T15.004) to *enterprise* hosts, forcing the canonical 2011 allInVain case into T15 "broadly construed." T4.013 makes the value-extraction primitive explicit: **stealer malware harvests the keys and sessions off the endpoint at rest, and the attacker drains on its own schedule** — there is no on-chain authorisation event at compromise time and no protocol interaction, so the attack is invisible to allowance/permit/signature detection and is defeated only at the endpoint and by keeping key material off it.

The defining contrast with the clipper sibling (T4.012) is **what is taken and when value moves**. A clipper needs the victim to transact and only captures that transaction's destination (plus whatever secrets transit the clipboard); an infostealer captures the standing key material and authentication state, so a single infection can compromise *every* wallet and session present on the device and can be monetised long after the malware is gone. Many 2025–2026 campaigns carry both arms, which is why OAK maps the dominant primitive per incident and cross-references the other rather than double-counting.

The class is marked `emerging` rather than `stable` because per-campaign operator attribution is overwhelmingly `unattributed` / `pseudonymous` — the families are sold as malware-as-a-service and operated by many independent affiliates, and individual campaigns (the Steam Workshop wallpapers were assessed as **multiple distinct threat actors**, not one group) rarely resolve to a named operator. The **trusted-platform content-channel** delivery shape — abusing a legitimate platform's UGC or game-distribution feature so the payload inherits the platform's trust, as in the Steam Workshop / Wallpaper Engine campaign and the trojanised Chemia game — is tracked as forward candidate **T4.013.001**; it is the load-bearing *delivery* innovation across the 2025–2026 anchors and is the natural companion to T4.012.002 (reputation-laundered fake-software distribution), differing in that the trust channel here is a single legitimate first-party platform rather than a synthetic cross-platform reputation network. Promotion of T4.013 to `stable`, and of T4.013.001 to a first-class sub-Technique, will follow as the per-incident worked-example anchor set expands.
