# OAK-T11.010 — Off-chain Counterparty-Risk Insolvency

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (deposit substrate is BTC / ETH / stablecoins on-chain; the failure mode is off-chain operator credit decision → counterparty default → customer-asset shortfall)
**First documented:** 2020-11-07 (Cred Inc. Chapter 11, D. Del. Case No. 20-12836)
**Aliases:** "counterparty-risk insolvency", "yield-without-due-diligence failure", "custodial-lending default cascade", "CeFi yield-platform collapse", "re-lending concentration risk"

## Description

A custodial yield platform extends **unsecured or under-collateralised credit** to a small set of offshore or high-leverage counterparties, re-lending customer-deposited assets to fund the platform's yield product. When the counterparty defaults — or when the counterparty's own exposure cascade triggers a solvency crisis — the operator absorbs the counterparty's default as a customer-facing solvency event: customer withdrawals are suspended, the platform files for bankruptcy, and customer assets are trapped in the bankruptcy-claims process for multi-year distributions at cents-on-the-dollar recovery rates.

The structural feature is that the **platform was not fake** — unlike T11.005 (Operator-Side Fake-Platform Fraud), the operator built a real custodial platform, accepted real customer deposits, and deployed them in real (but disastrously under-diversified) re-lending strategies. The failure mode is an **off-chain credit decision**: the operator chose a small number of concentrated, high-leverage counterparties, extended unsecured credit without adequate risk controls, and failed to disclose the counterparty concentration to depositors. When the counterparty defaulted, the platform's customer-asset coverage gap materialised as a solvency event.

Distinct from sibling Techniques:

- **T11.005 (Operator-Side Fake-Platform Fraud)** — the platform itself is the fraud vehicle: fake exchange, fake custodian, fake yield product. T11.010's platform was genuine; the failure was in the counterparty credit decision, not in the platform's existence.
- **T11.001 (Third-Party Signing-Vendor Compromise)** — the vendor surface is breached by an external attacker. T11.010 has no external attacker; the failure is operator-side credit-risk management.
- **T5.005 (Treasury-Management Exit)** — operator-side exit from a DeFi protocol's treasury. T11.010's operator does not exit with the funds; the funds are lost to a third-party counterparty default, and the operator enters bankruptcy proceedings.
- **T9.x (Smart-Contract Exploit)** — on-chain exploit of a protocol. T11.010 has no on-chain exploit; the failure is entirely in the off-chain credit relationship between operator and counterparty.

The cohort spans a continuous lineage from 2020 through 2022–2023:

- **Cred Inc. (November 2020, ~$140M+)** — canonical anchor. MoKredit (Chinese-domiciled crypto-asset borrower) defaulted on obligations to Cred; Cred filed Chapter 11. The earliest cleanly-documented case of the counterparty-risk insolvency pattern in OAK's corpus.
- **Celsius (July 2022, ~$1.2B+)** — counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital, FTX/Alameda, and others; Chapter 11 filed July 2022.
- **Voyager Digital (July 2022, ~$1B+)** — counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital; Chapter 11 filed July 2022.
- **BlockFi (November 2022, ~$1B+)** — counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital and FTX/Alameda; Chapter 11 filed November 2022.
- **Genesis Global (January 2023, ~$2B+)** — counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital and FTX/Alameda; Chapter 11 filed January 2023.
- **FTX (November 2022, ~$8B+)** — structurally adjacent: the counterparty was Alameda Research, a sibling entity under common control; the credit relationship was not arm's-length.

The structural commonality across all six cases is: (1) a custodial yield product whose yield is funded by re-lending customer deposits; (2) a small, concentrated set of counterparties receiving the re-lent assets; (3) insufficient operator-side risk controls, insufficient external disclosure of counterparty composition, and insufficient regulatory capital-adequacy or segregation requirements; (4) counterparty default triggering a customer-facing solvency event; (5) customer recovery via bankruptcy-claims process at cents-on-the-dollar rates over multi-year proceedings.

## Observed indicators

- **Counterparty-concentration fingerprint:** the yield platform's customer-deposit flow funnels through a small number of destination addresses controlled by a single or small set of counterparties. On-chain, this appears as a large fan-in from customer-deposit addresses to a concentrated set of re-lending destination addresses — structurally different from the diversified operational flow of a genuine exchange or custodian.
- **Yield-product sustainability fingerprint:** the platform's advertised yield rate is materially above prevailing DeFi and CeFi market rates, implying either (a) a genuine alpha source (unlikely at scale), (b) a Ponzi structure (T11.005.002), or (c) undisclosed counterparty concentration risk (T11.010). The absence of public disclosure of counterparty composition is the discriminating signal between (c) and the other two.
- **Withdrawal-suspension fingerprint:** the platform suspends customer withdrawals citing "market conditions" or "liquidity stress" — not a security breach, not a smart-contract exploit, not a regulatory action. The suspension is the operator's acknowledgement that the counterparty default has materialised as a customer-asset coverage gap.
- **Bankruptcy-filing fingerprint:** the platform files for Chapter 11 (or equivalent jurisdiction-specific insolvency proceeding) within days to weeks of the withdrawal suspension. The filing discloses the counterparty exposure as the proximate cause.
- **Recovery-channel fingerprint:** customer recovery is via the bankruptcy-claims process — a multi-year judicial proceeding producing cents-on-the-dollar distributions to customer creditors. This recovery channel is structurally distinct from on-chain partial recovery (Harvest), negotiated full recovery (Lendf.me), operator-funded compensation (Akropolis), and token-based compensation (Akropolis).

## Detection signals

- **Counterparty-concentration analysis (highest-leverage pre-default signal).** For every custodial yield platform, map the on-chain flow from customer-deposit addresses to re-lending destination addresses. Compute the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) of re-lending destination concentration. Platforms whose re-lending flow is concentrated in fewer than 5 destination addresses, or whose largest single counterparty receives >30% of re-lent assets, are exposed to T11.010.
- **Yield-rate sustainability cross-reference.** Compare the platform's advertised yield rate against contemporaneous DeFi benchmark rates (AAVE supply APY, Compound supply APY, stETH staking yield). A sustained premium >200 bps above the highest DeFi benchmark without a disclosed and independently verifiable alpha source is a counterparty-concentration risk signal.
- **Proof-of-reserves with counterparty-composition disclosure.** Custodial yield platforms should disclose not only that assets exist (proof-of-reserves) but also the composition and concentration of the counterparties to whom those assets have been re-lent (proof-of-counterparty-diversification). Absence of the latter while yield is above market is a T11.010 risk signal.
- **Regulatory insolvency-proceeding monitoring.** Monitor bankruptcy court dockets (US Bankruptcy Court, Delaware and Southern District of New York being the highest-volume venues for crypto-platform insolvencies) for Chapter 11 filings by custodial yield platforms. The filing itself is the definitive T11.010 confirmation signal.
- **Cross-platform counterparty-exposure graph.** Construct a bipartite graph of custodial yield platforms ↔ counterparties. When a counterparty (e.g., Three Arrows Capital) defaults, trigger an alert for all platforms with exposure to that counterparty. This is the structural analogue of the T9.007 fork-relationship graph applied to the CeFi counterparty-risk surface.

## Real-world examples

- **Cred Inc. — multi-chain (custodial) — 2020-11-07 (canonical T11.010 anchor).** Cred's CredEarn yield product re-lent customer-deposited BTC, ETH, and stablecoins to MoKredit (Chinese-domiciled crypto-asset borrower) and other counterparties. MoKredit defaulted on its obligations to Cred during 2020; Cred suspended withdrawals in October 2020 and filed Chapter 11 on 2020-11-07 (D. Del. Case No. 20-12836). Aggregate customer-asset shortfall: ~$140M+. Customer recovery via multi-year bankruptcy-claims process at cents-on-the-dollar rates. The case is the earliest cleanly-documented instance of the T11.010 pattern in OAK's corpus. See [`examples/2020-11-cred.md`](../examples/2020-11-cred.md).

### Additional incidents (pipeline anchors for promotion to `observed`/`stable`)

- **Celsius Network — 2022-07-13.** Counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital (~$40M+ loss on 3AC default), FTX/Alameda, and others. Suspended withdrawals June 2022; filed Chapter 11 July 2022 (S.D.N.Y.). Customer-asset shortfall: ~$1.2B+. Pipeline anchor for T11.010 as the largest non-fraudulent CeFi yield-platform insolvency.
- **Voyager Digital — 2022-07-05.** Counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital (~$650M+ unsecured loan to 3AC). 3AC defaulted; Voyager filed Chapter 11 July 2022 (S.D.N.Y.). Customer-asset shortfall: ~$1B+. Pipeline anchor.
- **BlockFi — 2022-11-28.** Counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital and FTX/Alameda. Filed Chapter 11 November 2022 (D.N.J.). Customer-asset shortfall: ~$1B+. Pipeline anchor.
- **Genesis Global — 2023-01-19.** Counterparty exposure to Three Arrows Capital and FTX/Alameda. Filed Chapter 11 January 2023 (S.D.N.Y.). Customer-asset shortfall: ~$2B+. Pipeline anchor.
- **FTX — 2022-11-11.** Structurally adjacent: the counterparty was Alameda Research, a sibling entity under common control — the credit relationship was not arm's-length, making FTX a hybrid between T11.010 (counterparty-risk insolvency) and T11.005 (operator-side fraud). Customer-asset shortfall: ~$8B+. Pipeline anchor for the fraud-adjacent boundary case.

## Reference implementations

- No runtime detection reference implementation at v0.1. The detection surface is at the counterparty-concentration analysis, yield-rate sustainability cross-reference, and bankruptcy-docket monitoring layers — off-chain analytical and regulatory-monitoring surfaces outside `mg-detectors-rs`'s current runtime risk-engine scope.
- Vendor-ecosystem coverage: Chainalysis Reactor (counterparty-exposure graph construction from on-chain flow data); Nansen (exchange and custodian wallet labelling enabling re-lending flow mapping); Arkham Intelligence (entity-level wallet attribution enabling counterparty identification); bankruptcy-court docket monitoring (PACER for US bankruptcy courts; competing commercial docket-monitoring services).

## Mitigations

- **Proof-of-counterparty-diversification disclosure (highest-leverage).** Custodial yield platforms should disclose, in user-facing language and with verifiable on-chain evidence, the composition and concentration of the counterparties to whom customer-deposited assets have been re-lent. The disclosure should include the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index of counterparty concentration and the identity (or at minimum the jurisdictional domicile and regulatory status) of each counterparty receiving >10% of re-lent assets.
- **Regulatory capital-adequacy and segregation requirements.** Jurisdictions that license custodial yield platforms should impose capital-adequacy requirements proportional to counterparty-concentration risk and mandatory segregation of customer assets from re-lending assets. The absence of these requirements in the 2018–2022 period structurally enabled the T11.010 cascade.
- **Counterparty-exposure monitoring infrastructure.** Third-party risk-monitoring services (Chainalysis, TRM Labs, Elliptic) should maintain a live bipartite graph of custodial yield platforms ↔ counterparties and trigger alerts when a counterparty defaults or shows on-chain distress signals (large outflows, collateral drawdown, margin-call activity).
- **User-side yield-rate sustainability scrutiny.** Depositors evaluating a custodial yield product should cross-reference the advertised yield rate against contemporaneous DeFi benchmark rates. A sustained premium >200 bps above the highest DeFi benchmark without a disclosed and independently verifiable alpha source is a counterparty-concentration risk signal — not a free lunch.
- **Bankruptcy-claims-process transparency.** Bankruptcy courts and Trustees handling crypto-platform insolvencies should publish customer-asset-shortfall figures, counterparty-exposure breakdowns, and per-creditor-class distribution percentages in machine-readable formats. The current manual-extraction burden from PACER dockets is a transparency gap that slows community-side risk analysis.

OAK Mitigation cross-references at v0.1: gap. T11.010's mitigation surface is at the regulatory-capital, counterparty-disclosure, and bankruptcy-transparency layers. No existing OAK-M* entry directly maps to proof-of-counterparty-diversification or counterparty-concentration monitoring. v0.x candidate Mitigation entries.

## Discussion

T11.010 occupies a structurally distinct slot in OAK's T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure) Tactic. Existing T11 sub-Techniques (T11.001–T11.009) all share the assumption that the load-bearing primitive is a **security failure** — a signing-vendor compromise, a wallet-software distribution compromise, a multisig manipulation, an entropy failure, a fake platform, a seed-phrase exfiltration, a hardware-wallet supply-chain compromise, an identity-provider compromise, or a trader-tooling supply-chain compromise. T11.010's load-bearing primitive is a **credit-risk-management failure** — the operator made real but disastrously under-diversified counterparty credit decisions, and the counterparty default triggered a customer-facing solvency event.

The Cred case is the canonical anchor because it is the earliest cleanly-documented case in OAK's corpus where (a) the platform was genuine (not a fake-CEX or Ponzi), (b) the failure mode was counterparty default (not operator theft or external breach), (c) the judicial record is public and detailed (US Bankruptcy Court docket, civil litigation against named officers, criminal indictment against a named individual former officer), and (d) the structural pattern — concentrated re-lending to a small set of offshore counterparties, counterparty default, withdrawal suspension, Chapter 11 filing, customer recovery via bankruptcy-claims process — recurs at larger scale across the 2022–2023 CeFi yield-platform collapse wave.

The 2022–2023 wave (Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi, Genesis, FTX) is structurally lineal from Cred. The same counterparty (Three Arrows Capital) appears as the proximate default trigger across four of the six cases (Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi, Genesis), demonstrating that T11.010 is not an idiosyncratic failure of one platform's credit department but a systemic feature of the custodial-yield-platform model when counterparty-concentration risk is not disclosed, monitored, or regulated. The FTX case is structurally adjacent — the counterparty was a sibling entity under common control — and represents the fraud-adjacent boundary of T11.010 where the technique blurs into T11.005 (Operator-Side Fake-Platform Fraud).

The relationship to T11.005 is the most important taxonomic distinction. T11.005's platform was a fiction — the operator built a database-backed UI that looked like an exchange/custodian but had no real trading backend, no real custody, and no real yield generation. T11.010's platform was real — the operator built a genuine custodial platform, accepted real deposits, and deployed them in real re-lending strategies — but the operator's credit decisions were disastrously concentrated, and the counterparty default destroyed the platform's solvency. The distinction matters for defender tooling: T11.005 is detected via OSINT (regulatory warnings, corporate-registry verification, "test withdrawal" rule); T11.010 is detected via counterparty-concentration analysis and yield-rate sustainability cross-reference. Both produce customer-asset losses and both are CeFi failure modes, but the detection surface and mitigation surface are different.

Promotion to `observed` requires at least one additional independent worked-example anchor beyond Cred — Celsius (2022) is the pipeline target as the largest non-fraudulent CeFi yield-platform insolvency. Promotion to `stable` requires cross-platform counterparty-exposure graph construction and at least three independent worked-example anchors demonstrating the pattern across different counterparties (3AC, Alameda, and at least one additional). The pipeline is well-populated; the bottleneck is example-file write-up bandwidth, not incident scarcity.

The technique is published at `emerging` maturity with Cred as the single canonical anchor. The 2022–2023 wave cases (Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi, Genesis, FTX) are pipeline targets listed in the "Additional incidents" section; each is a well-documented public-record event with judicial-record attribution and on-chain flow traceability, and each would independently anchor T11.010 at `observed` maturity once written up as a dedicated OAK worked example.
