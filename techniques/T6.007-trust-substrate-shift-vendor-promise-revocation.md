# OAK-T6.007 — Trust-substrate Shift / Vendor-side Promise Revocation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (the substrate-of-revocation is the vendor / regulator / infrastructure-policy claim; the realised effect is on user-side threat-model construction across whichever chains the affected product operates)
**First documented:** 2023-05-16 (Ledger Recover seed-recovery service announcement collapsing the "seed never leaves the device" trust-substrate claim that had informed Ledger users' threat-model construction since 2016)
**Aliases:** "vendor-policy promise revocation", "trust-substrate revocation event", "non-attack defender-credibility event", "vendor-policy-as-defense-evasion"

## Description

A **non-attack defender-credibility event** in which a vendor-policy decision (or, in adjacent variants, a regulatory action, infrastructure-level policy change, or oracle-resolution-policy change) revokes a load-bearing trust-substrate claim that previously informed user-side threat-model construction. The realised loss is **non-financial** (trust-substrate revocation; cohort-level effect on the broader category's trust posture; competing-vendor user-acquisition opportunities) but structurally significant — the user's effective security posture depends on both (a) the absence of attacks and (b) the persistence of vendor-side trust-substrate claims.

T6.007 is **structurally distinct** from sibling Defense Evasion Techniques:

- **T6.001 (Source-Verification Mismatch)** — defense-evasion-by-attacker-action; the attacker constructs a verifiable-source surface that does not match deployed bytecode. T6.007 is defense-evasion-by-vendor-policy-change; no attacker is involved.
- **T6.002 (Fake-Audit Claim)** — attacker-action; false-audit-claim surface. T6.007's structural feature is *legitimate vendor* policy change that revokes a previously-load-bearing trust-substrate claim.
- **T6.003 (Audit of Different Bytecode Version)** — attacker-action; audit pertains to a different bytecode than what is deployed. T6.007 has no audit-mismatch dimension.
- **T6.004 (Audit-Pending Marketing Claim)** — attacker-action / operator-action; marketing claim of pending audit that does not materialise. T6.007's vendor announcement is *executed* — the policy change is real, not marketing.
- **T6.005 (Proxy-Upgrade Malicious Switching)** — attacker-action / operator-action; mutable-implementation surface used to switch to malicious bytecode. T6.007 is the *non-malicious* analogue: legitimate vendor-policy change that nonetheless revokes user trust.
- **T6.006 (Counterfeit Token Impersonation)** — attacker-action; counterfeit token branded as legitimate. T6.007's vendor is the legitimate vendor performing legitimate corporate action.

The structural feature is **defense-evasion-by-vendor-policy-change** rather than defense-evasion-by-attacker-action. "Evasion" is reframed at the framework level: not the attacker evading the defender's controls, but the **vendor policy change evading the defender's mental model of what the product does**. The framework's credibility depends on documenting how user trust gets revoked even in the absence of a deployed attack.

The class structurally generalises beyond hardware-wallet vendor-policy:

- **Vendor-policy decisions** that revoke trust-substrate claims (Ledger Recover at v0.1; potential future custodial-services policy changes; potential future wallet-as-a-service policy changes).
- **Regulatory / legal-process events** that change the operational threat model (sanctions enforcement against mixers; KYC / AML rule changes affecting custody surface).
- **Infrastructure-level changes** that reshape the defender's mental model (L2 sequencer policy changes; oracle-resolution-policy changes at the platform-override / oracle-dispute-resolution layer).

## Observed indicators

- **Vendor-policy announcement:** the vendor publicly announces a policy decision (firmware update, terms-of-service change, custodial-flow introduction) that materially changes the product's trust-model surface.
- **Public-controversy magnitude:** unusually-high public response from the affected user base — security researchers, competing vendors, crypto-press outlets, and security-conscious users producing a coordinated critical response within days of the announcement.
- **Vendor-side communication mismanagement:** deleted-and-clarified social-media posts; CEO-level walk-back statements; emergency CTO interviews. The Ledger May 18, 2023 deleted tweet ("Technically speaking it is and always has been possible to write firmware that facilitates key extraction") is the canonical example.
- **Competing-vendor user-acquisition campaigns:** Trezor, GridPlus, Cypherock, Tangem, Coldcard ran user-acquisition campaigns capitalising on the Ledger Recover controversy — a category-level secondary indicator that the trust-substrate shift is structurally significant.
- **User-side threat-model invalidation:** users publicly articulating that the previously-load-bearing trust-substrate claim is now operationally false — "the seed-never-leaves-the-device claim is invalidated"; "the non-custodial claim is qualified by the auth-provider dependency"; "the censorship-resistance claim is qualified by the sequencer-policy".

## Detection signals

- **Vendor-policy-change-monitoring as part of operational-security workflow:** users with material holdings on a vendor's product should subscribe to the vendor's official communications and monitor for trust-substrate-relevant policy announcements. The signal is qualitatively different from per-incident attack-detection signals — it is a corporate-policy-tracking surface.
- **Industry-press cohort tracking:** crypto-press outlets (CoinDesk, The Block, Decrypt, Wired) typically surface trust-substrate-relevant policy changes within 24–72 hours of the vendor announcement; cohort-level coverage is the operational signal for users without direct vendor-communication-feed subscription.
- **Competing-vendor messaging:** when a vendor in a category announces a trust-substrate shift, competing-vendor messaging often surfaces the structural-significance dimension before the affected vendor's own communication catches up.
- **Vendor-side communication-quality anomaly:** deleted-and-reposted communications; ambiguous CEO statements; rapid-fire clarification cycles. These are signals that the policy change is being received as a trust-substrate revocation rather than as a routine product update.

## Mitigations

- **User-side vendor-policy-change-monitoring (highest-leverage):** subscribe to the vendor's official communications feed for any product whose trust-substrate claim is load-bearing in your threat-model construction; treat trust-substrate-relevant policy announcements as first-class operational-security events.
- **Vendor-side commitment-stability:** vendors should treat trust-substrate claims as load-bearing operational commitments, not marketing language. Material changes to the trust-substrate surface should be communicated with the same care as security-incident disclosures, including public-comment windows for the affected user base before final policy adoption.
- **Vendor-side communication discipline:** during trust-substrate-shift events, vendor-side communications should avoid ambiguous claims about prior product capabilities (the Ledger May 18, 2023 deleted tweet is the canonical anti-pattern); all communications should be prepared in advance and reviewed for trust-substrate-implications.
- **Conditional-trust framing in user education:** vendor trust-substrate claims should be framed as **conditional on the vendor's continued policy adherence** in user-onboarding documentation, not as absolute properties. Users with non-trivial holdings should include vendor-policy-change-monitoring as part of their operational-security workflow.
- **Source-code release as trust-substrate-restoration primitive:** Ledger's commitment to release the relevant firmware-components source code prior to Ledger Recover launch is the canonical recovery-side primitive for vendor-side trust-substrate revocation events. Source-code release does not fully restore the prior trust substrate but it is the closest operational analogue for partial restoration.
- **Competing-vendor accountability:** the category-level competitive surface (Trezor, GridPlus, Cypherock, Tangem, Coldcard messaging during the Ledger Recover controversy) is a market-driven accountability primitive that disciplines vendor-side trust-substrate changes through user-acquisition opportunity-cost.

## Discussion

T6.007 closes a structural gap in OAK v0.1's T6 coverage. The dominant v0.1 T6 sub-Techniques (T6.001-T6.006) all share a *defense-evasion-by-attacker-action* shape — the attacker constructs a surface that defeats the defender's controls. T6.007 introduces a **defense-evasion-by-vendor-policy-change** surface where no attacker is involved but the user's effective security posture is nonetheless reduced.

The framework's credibility depends on documenting how user trust gets revoked even in the absence of a deployed attack. A defender vocabulary that only catalogues attacker-action events misses the structural fact that vendor-policy changes can revoke load-bearing trust-substrate claims unilaterally, with realised non-financial effects on the affected user cohort.

The structural OAK lesson is that **vendor trust-substrate claims are first-class defender-side mitigation surfaces** that can be invalidated by vendor-policy changes without any adversarial action. The defender's mental model is constructed partly from the vendor's commitments; revocation of those commitments is a defender-mental-model invalidation event regardless of whether on-chain extraction occurred.

The Ledger Recover case is the canonical anchor at v0.1. Pre-Recover, Ledger's "seed never leaves the device" claim was a load-bearing user-side mental-model anchor that informed Ledger users' threat-model construction for nearly a decade (2016 Nano S launch to 2023 Recover announcement). Post-Recover, the claim is operationally false for users who opt into the service; users who do not opt in are still exposed to the *capability* surface that the firmware update introduced (the device can extract the seed if instructed; trust now depends on the firmware-release change-control process).

The Ledger CEO's framing — "the trade-off, I think, is acceptable" — did not restore the prior trust substrate. The structural lesson is that vendor-side trust-substrate claims, once revoked, cannot be fully restored through assurance language; partial restoration through source-code release is the closest operational analogue.

The class generalises beyond hardware-wallet vendor-policy. The Polymarket / UMA platform-override pattern (Polymarket's public characterisation of the resolution as "unprecedented governance attack" is itself a defender-credibility-event signal at the platform layer) is adjacent to T6.007. Future T6.007 worked examples should preserve:

- The non-attack structure (no on-chain extraction)
- The realised-non-financial-loss dimension (trust-substrate revocation; cohort-level effect on category trust posture; competing-vendor user-acquisition opportunities)
- The vendor-communications-during-trust-substrate-shift dimension (the deleted-tweet pattern)
- The custodian-set jurisdiction footprint dimension where third-party-custodian shapes are introduced
- The cohort-level effect on the broader category's trust posture

Promotion to `stable` will follow once additional worked-example anchors land — additional vendor-policy-change events with material trust-substrate revocation implications; regulatory / legal-process events that change the operational threat model; infrastructure-level changes (L2 sequencer policy, oracle-resolution-policy) that reshape defender mental models. The class anchor at v0.1 (Ledger Recover) is sufficient for `emerging` status but the multi-anchor pattern that warrants `stable` is still forming.
