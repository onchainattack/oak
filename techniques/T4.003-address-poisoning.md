# OAK-T4.003 — Address Poisoning

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition), OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM (primary), Solana, Tron
**First documented:** 2022 (early reports); industrial-scale 2023 onward (Tsuchiya et al. 2025 USENIX Security cohort)
**Aliases:** "zero-value transfer scam", "lookalike-address phishing", "wallet-history poisoning"

## Description

A class of attacks in which an attacker injects transactions involving a lookalike address (visually similar to one of the victim's known counterparties) into the victim's transaction history, with the goal that the victim will subsequently copy-paste the lookalike address from their wallet history and send funds to the attacker rather than the intended counterparty. From a defender's perspective, T4.003 is the largest-volume class of T4-category attacks in production today: `[tsuchiya2025poisoning]` (USENIX Security 2025) characterises approximately 17.3M poisoning transfers observed on Ethereum since April 2023 across three sub-classes (tiny-value transfers, zero-value transfers, counterfeit-token transfers).

T4.003 is structurally distinct from the signature-based T4.001 Permit2 and the allowance-based T4.004 approve-pattern drainers because the attacker does **not** acquire any on-chain authority over the victim's wallet — the attacker only manipulates the victim's wallet history display, relying entirely on the victim's wallet UX (truncated address display, copy-paste from history) and the victim's own subsequent send action. This makes T4.003 a textbook OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion) modifier of T4: the victim authorises the transfer themselves and there is no on-chain authorisation event to detect at the moment of compromise.

## Observed indicators

- Sustained inflow of small-value or zero-value transfers from previously-unseen addresses into a high-balance wallet, often arriving in clusters that match the victim's recent legitimate counterparty pattern.
- Lookalike-address generation pattern: attacker addresses share the first 4–6 characters and last 4 characters with one of the victim's known counterparties (truncated-display attack surface). Vanity-address generators (`profanity`, `vanity-eth`) are the canonical operator tooling.
- Zero-value `Transfer(from, to, 0)` events emitted by ERC-20 contracts naming the victim's address as sender or receiver — used to plant fake history when zero-value direct transfers do not propagate to the wallet UI.
- "Counterfeit token" sub-class: ERC-20 contracts deploying a `Transfer` event with attacker-controlled `from`/`to` parameters that mimic a stable-token symbol (USDT, USDC) but originate from an unaffiliated contract.
- Persistent campaign-cluster funding: Tsuchiya 2025 documents that 17.3M poisoning transfers across the cohort cluster into a small number of operator-funder graphs traceable via OAK-T8.001 (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) heuristics.

## Detection signals

- Wallet-side (highest leverage, OAK-M29): detect inbound transfers whose source address is structurally similar (first/last-N-character match) to addresses already in the user's send history; surface them as suspicious in the wallet UI rather than mixing them silently into history. Trezor Suite's address-poisoning filter and Rabby / MetaMask "address book mismatch" warnings are reference implementations.
- Per-wallet: monitor for the canonical "tiny-value-transfer / zero-value-transfer / counterfeit-token-transfer" pattern targeting active high-balance wallets over a rolling window.
- Pre-send (OAK-M18 / OAK-M29 intersection): wallet UX intervention — require explicit verification of the *full* destination address (not just the first/last six characters) for sends above a configurable threshold.
- Counterfeit-token detection: cross-reference token contract address against canonical token registries (CoinGecko, Etherscan token lists) at display time; any `Transfer` event from a token claiming a popular symbol but residing at a non-canonical contract is a high-precision flag.
- Post-event forensic: trace the lookalike address back to the campaign cluster and apply OAK-T8.001 (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) heuristics to identify other potentially-targeted wallets.

## Real-world examples

- **\$68M WBTC poisoning case — Ethereum — 2024-05-03** — A single victim wallet (0x1E227…) initiated a legitimate transfer to counterparty 0xd9A1b at 09:14 UTC; later in the day the victim copy-pasted what they believed was the same address from their wallet history but was in fact a poisoned lookalike (0xd9A1c…), sending approximately \$68M in WBTC to the attacker. The attacker subsequently returned \$68M of the original transfer following a public bounty negotiation; the operator had nonetheless netted approximately \$1.49M from the broader 66-day campaign of which this victim was the most prominent target — a 1,147% ROI on campaign cost per Chainalysis's published forensic analysis. See worked example at `examples/2024-05-address-poisoning-68m.md`. Source: `[chainalysis2024poisoning]`.
- **Cohort context — Ethereum — April 2023 onward** — `[tsuchiya2025poisoning]` characterises approximately 17.3M poisoning transfers across the three sub-classes; the academic cohort is the canonical reference for class scale and per-sub-class detection-methodology calibration. Targeting profile: more active wallets with larger balances, longer transaction histories, and recurring counterparty patterns are disproportionately represented in victim selection.
- **Trezor hardware-wallet user advisory — multi-chain — 2024 onward** — Trezor published an explicit address-poisoning advisory and shipped a Suite-level filtering mechanism that blurs and marks-as-unverified inbound transactions matching the poisoning pattern. The advisory is the canonical hardware-wallet vendor T4.003 acknowledgement and reference implementation of the OAK-M29 mitigation. <!-- TODO: cite https://trezor.io/support/troubleshooting/coins-tokens/address-poisoning-attacks -->

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T4.003 detection lives at the wallet-UX layer, not at the on-chain detection layer where MG operates).
- Vendor coverage: Trezor Suite address-poisoning filter (reference for hardware-wallet UX layer); Rabby and MetaMask "address book mismatch" warnings; Coinbase Wallet, Trust Wallet poisoning-pattern detection. Revoke.cash and similar tools are *not* relevant here because no allowance is granted.

## Mitigations

- **OAK-M29 (Full Address Verification and Lookalike Detection):** highest-leverage layer — wallet vendors flag inbound zero-value or near-zero-value transfers from previously-unseen senders whose addresses are structurally similar to addresses in the user's send history; require explicit full-address verification for sends above a configurable threshold; surface address-book labels prominently in send flows.
- **OAK-M18 (Out-of-Band Destination Verification):** verify the *full* destination address before sending, not the first/last six characters; maintain an address book of known counterparties and prefer the address-book entry over wallet-history lookup.
- **OAK-M19 (Air-Gap Cold Wallet Signing):** air-gapped signing devices that render the full destination address with checksum (rather than truncated) defeat the truncated-display attack surface for treasury-scale transactions.
- **OAK-M04 / OAK-M07 (Funder-Graph and Cross-Chain Attribution):** maintain campaign-cluster watchlists; cross-reference outbound transfers from organisational treasury wallets against known-poisoning-campaign attacker addresses before broadcast.

## Discussion

T4.003 is one of the highest-leverage Techniques in OAK for the wallet-UX-layer mitigation argument. Effectively all on-chain detection happens after the fact; the defender's actionable layer is the wallet UI showing the full address, the address-book pattern matching, and the user's habit of verifying the full address. Contributors writing future T4.003 examples should preserve the on-chain-vs-UX-layer distinction explicitly — it is the most actionable single takeaway from the Technique class, and it is what differentiates T4.003 from T4.001 (where on-chain detection of the permit-grantee cluster is meaningful).

False-positive reasoning: legitimate exchange-deposit address rotation can produce inbound transfers from previously-unseen addresses that share prefix characters with prior counterparties (CEX hot-wallet hashing patterns happen to collide); the precision-improving signal is the value-near-zero filter combined with the structural-similarity threshold. Edge case: airdrop campaigns intentionally seed many low-value transfers; legitimate airdrops typically originate from contracts with public registry entries, allowing the OAK-M29 implementation to whitelist them.

A separate consideration: the Bybit case (`examples/2025-02-bybit.md`) involved a *different* class of "destination address swap at sign time" — the attacker swapped the address inside the signing UI rather than poisoning the user's history. OAK does not classify Bybit as T4.003 because the entry vector is software supply-chain compromise of the signing infrastructure, not wallet-history poisoning. The structural similarity (victim signs / approves a transfer to an attacker address they did not intend) is a useful teaching point but the underlying defense lives in different places.

v0.x deferred items: cross-chain poisoning observability (some campaigns operate identical operator clusters across Ethereum, Tron, and Solana — current detection feeds are largely per-chain), and ERC-4337 / smart-account name-resolution semantics where the destination is resolved from a named identifier rather than a raw address (poisoning shifts to name-registration squatting and is structurally adjacent rather than identical).
