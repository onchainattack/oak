# OAK-T7.007 — DEX Aggregator Routing Laundering

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T7 (Laundering)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (primary); DEX aggregator deployments (1inch, 0x/Matcha, Paraswap, CowSwap, Odos, KyberSwap aggregator) are predominantly EVM-native; cross-chain aggregators (Li.Fi, Socket/Bungee, Across) compound the surface with T7.003 bridging
**First documented:** The aggregator-as-laundering-rail pattern was operationalised systematically post-2022 Tornado Cash sanctions, as laundering operators sought non-mixer obfuscation primitives; cohort-scale documentation 2023+
**Aliases:** "aggregator-hop laundering", "split-route laundering", "multi-hop-routing obfuscation", "aggregator-churn laundering"

## Description

A laundering technique in which the operator routes proceeds through a DEX aggregator (1inch, 0x/Matcha, Paraswap, CowSwap, Odos) configured to split the trade across multiple liquidity venues through multi-hop intermediate-token paths, exploiting the aggregator's routing complexity to defeat single-venue chain-analysis clustering. The aggregator itself is a legitimate price-discovery and execution-optimisation service — the operator's trade executes at a market rate — but the operator selects routing parameters (slippage tolerance, intermediate tokens, split-route fraction, gas-price tier) designed not for best execution but for maximum trail-fragmentation. The defender's analytical question is whether a given aggregator-routed trade's path complexity, intermediate-token selection, and venue-splitting pattern are consistent with a cost-minimising trader or with a fragmentation-maximising launderer.

T7.007 is structurally distinct from T7.001 (Mixer-Routed Hop): T7.001 uses a service whose explicit purpose is obfuscation (Tornado Cash, Railgun, zk.money), so the mere fact of interaction carries a defender signal; T7.007 uses a legitimate trading infrastructure whose purpose is price execution, so the interaction carries no inherent illicit-signal weight. T7.007 is distinct from T7.003 (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering): T7.003 obfuscates by crossing chain boundaries; T7.007 obfuscates within a single chain by fragmenting the trade path across venues — the two are frequently chained, with T7.007 on chain A, then T7.003 to chain B. T7.007 is distinct from T7.006 (DeFi Yield-Strategy Laundering): T7.006 obfuscates temporally through the yield-user crowd; T7.007 obfuscates structurally through routing complexity — the holding period is sub-block rather than weeks.

The technique exploits three aggregator design properties: (a) split-routing decomposes a single trade into N sub-trades across M venues, multiplying the number of on-chain trace edges; (b) multi-hop routing introduces intermediate tokens whose liquidity pools sit on different venues, each hop producing a fresh transaction-log entry with a different `pool` address; (c) solver/intent-based architectures (CowSwap, 1inch Fusion, Uniswap X) introduce an off-chain matching layer where the solver's on-chain settlement transaction does not trivially trace back to the originating wallet's swap intent.

## Observed indicators

- Route-path entropy exceeding the per-asset-pair per-aggregator typical complexity: a USDC→ETH trade routing through 4+ intermediate tokens when a 1-hop path exists and the aggregator's default route for that pair is widely known to be simpler.
- Split-route fractions that do not reflect liquidity-depth-optimal execution: the operator uses an even split (50/50 or 33/33/33) across venues with materially different liquidity depth, producing worse execution than a depth-weighted split.
- Source-cluster attribution: the aggregator-interaction address belongs to a cluster attributed to a known illicit source; the post-aggregator output cluster differs from the input cluster, with no economic rationale for the address change beyond the aggregator interaction itself.
- Intent/solver-architecture settlement: the operator's swap-intent submission and the settlement transaction are attributed to different addresses, and the gap between intent-submission-time and settlement-time is consistent with solver-matching delay rather than operator-side custody — this is a structural T7.007 indicator specific to solver-based aggregators.
- Cross-aggregator churn: the operator routes the same capital through multiple aggregators in sequence (1inch → Matcha → CowSwap) within a short block window, each step fragmenting the trace against a different aggregator contract and a different set of venue contracts.

## Detection signals

- Per-trade route-path entropy: compute the number of hops, intermediate tokens, and split-venue count for each aggregator-routed trade attributed to a watchlisted source-cluster; compare against the per-aggregator per-asset-pair baseline distribution; alert on trades above a configurable entropy threshold.
- Split-route efficiency analysis: for each split-route trade, compute the execution price vs. the optimal single-venue or minimal-hop execution price at the same block; trades that sacrifice more than a configurable basis-point margin to routing complexity are T7.007-suspect.
- Aggregator-contract interaction graph: treat aggregator-router contracts as nodes; edges are same-cluster sequential interactions; alert on clusters that interact with N≥3 different aggregator-router contracts within a short window carrying the same notional.
- Post-aggregator cluster divergence: for each aggregator trade attributed to a watchlisted source-cluster, compare the input-cluster to the output-cluster; cases where the input-cluster is watchlisted and the output-cluster is previously unseen are structural T7.007 signals.
- Venue-fragmentation graph: represent the set of liquidity venues visited during a single aggregator trade as a subgraph; alert on subgraphs whose venue count and hop count exceed the per-aggregator per-asset-pair baseline by a configurable multiple.

## Real-world examples

- **Lazarus Group / OAK-G01 post-Tornado-Cash DEX aggregator routing — EVM — 2023–2025.** Per `[chainalysis2024laundering]` and `[chainalysis2024dprk]`, DPRK-attributed laundering chains during 2023–2025 have included DEX aggregator stages as a within-chain fragmentation rail, typically chained with T7.003 cross-chain bridge hops and T7.006 yield-protocol cover stages. The aggregator stage sits between the extraction event and the bridge off-ramp, fragmenting the single-chain trail before the cross-chain jump. Specific aggregator-router contracts used include 1inch v5 router, 0x Exchange Proxy, and Paraswap AugustusSwapper — all of which are publicly verifiable on Etherscan.
- **Chainalysis 2024 Laundering Report — cohort-level.** The report documents aggregator-integration laundering as a measurable share of post-2022 sophisticated laundering flows, noting that aggregator-router contracts have become a standard stage in multi-stage laundering chains alongside bridges and mixers. The report does not name individual aggregator-router contracts as "laundering infrastructure" — they are legitimate services — but characterises the routing-complexity exploitation pattern as the operator-side technique.
- **Cohort-scale framing caveat.** At v0.1, T7.007 is most usefully framed at the cohort scale because the routing-complexity-vs-execution-quality distinction is a per-trade behavioural inference; forensic providers (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic) maintain the per-cluster attribution internally, and OAK documents the technique surface without overclaiming public incident attribution.

## Mitigations

- **Aggregator-side routing-transparency (highest-leverage):** aggregator protocols exposing per-trade route-path selection and split-venue execution detail as public API endpoints enable forensic providers to compute per-protocol routing-complexity baselines and flag anomaly-class trades. This is a protocol-level mitigation that requires no per-trade operator action.
- **Per-cluster aggregator-interaction monitoring:** forensic providers monitoring aggregator-router contract interactions attributed to watchlisted source-clusters; alerting on trades with elevated route-path entropy, sub-optimal split-route execution, or cross-aggregator churn.
- **Downstream venue source-of-funds attribution:** CEXes and fiat off-ramps that receive post-T7.007 flows are the canonical attribution surface; per-deposit source-of-funds attribution supported by forensic-provider cross-aggregator attribution graphs is where the T7.007 trail is typically closed in practice.
- **Risk teams (organisational):** treat aggregator-router contract interactions from watchlisted source-clusters as elevated risk regardless of the aggregator's legitimate-function posture; maintain per-aggregator per-cluster interaction monitoring rather than treating aggregator contracts as neutral infrastructure.

## Discussion

T7.007 is structurally one of the harder T7 sub-techniques to detect cleanly because aggregator routing is the dominant legitimate execution path for DeFi traders. Legitimate traders use 1inch and Matcha for gas optimisation and price improvement — routing complexity is a feature, not a bug. The defender signal is consequently relative to the per-aggregator per-asset-pair baseline: a trade that routes through 4 hops when the baseline for that pair is 1–2 hops is suspect; a trade that routes through 4 hops when the baseline is 3–5 hops is not. This makes T7.007 detection heavily dependent on forensic providers' internal routing-complexity baselines, which are not publicly available at v0.1.

A separate observation: T7.007 is more effective on aggregators with solver/intent-based architectures (CowSwap, 1inch Fusion, Uniswap X) because the solver's settlement transaction breaks the direct on-chain link between the user's swap-intent submission and the output tokens. The solver layer is a legitimate gas-abstraction and MEV-protection primitive, but it also functions as a structural obfuscation layer when the solver's settlement address differs from the user's intent-submission address. Contributors writing future T7.007 examples should preserve the routing-complexity-vs-execution-quality distinction; the technique is not "use an aggregator" but "use an aggregator's routing complexity specifically to fragment the chain-analysis trail at the cost of execution quality."

False-positive considerations: the vast majority of aggregator-routed trades have elevated route-path entropy for legitimate reasons — the aggregator's routing algorithm may select a multi-hop path because the direct pool lacks liquidity, not because the user requested fragmentation. The detection signal is specifically the delta between the chosen route's entropy and the aggregator's default/recommended route for the same asset pair at the same block, combined with source-cluster attribution. A trade with high route-path entropy from a non-watchlisted cluster that matches the aggregator's optimal-route output is a legitimate trade; a trade with high entropy from a watchlisted cluster whose route deviates from the aggregator's default is a T7.007 candidate. Forensic providers that do not maintain per-aggregator per-asset-pair routing baselines will produce elevated false-positive rates on T7.007 alerts.

v0.x deferred items: per-aggregator per-asset-pair route-path-entropy baselines (a dataset-construction and maintenance item, not a one-off analysis); solver-matched intent flow attribution (mapping the solver's settlement transaction back to the intent-submission address — data available in solver APIs but not yet integrated into standard forensic-provider pipelines at v0.1); cross-aggregator churn quantification (how many operators chain aggregators, at what notional, across which aggregator pairs).

Attribution-strength considerations: T7.007 attribution is typically `pseudonymous-cohort` rather than `pseudonymous-attacker` — the cluster that routes through aggregators with elevated route-path entropy is identifiable at the funder-graph-cluster layer, but per-trade attribution to a named attacker requires additional off-chain intelligence. The routing-complexity pattern itself identifies the laundering pattern, not the launderer. T7.007 composes heavily with T7.003 (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering): a laundering chain that uses DEX aggregator routing (T7.007) on chain A, then bridges (T7.003) to chain B for the off-ramp leg, is the dominant multi-rail laundering architecture observed in post-2022 sophisticated laundering operations. Forensic providers that monitor aggregator-router contracts on chain A and bridge contracts on the egress side should correlate the two surfaces: a watchlisted cluster that exhibits T7.007 routing complexity on chain A and T7.003 bridging to chain B should trigger a compound T7.007 + T7.003 alert.

T7.007 is also adjacent to T7.006 (DeFi Yield-Strategy Laundering) in the temporal domain: T7.007 fragments the trail within a single block (sub-block timescale via routing); T7.006 fragments the trail across weeks (multi-week timescale via yield-user cover). The two techniques operate at different temporal layers but compose readily: the launderer may route through a DEX aggregator (T7.007) to fragment the immediate post-extraction trail, deposit the fragmented output into a yield protocol (T7.006) for the holding stage, and bridge to another chain (T7.003) for the off-ramp. The composition of T7.007 → T7.006 → T7.003 is the dominant post-2023 sophisticated laundering architecture observed in the Chainalysis and TRM forensic record.
