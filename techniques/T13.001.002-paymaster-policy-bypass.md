# OAK-T13.001.002 — Paymaster Policy Bypass

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T13 (Account Abstraction Attacks)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (ERC-4337 EntryPoint v0.6 / v0.7 / v0.8 / v0.9 deployments); EVM L2s with native account abstraction; Solana (analogous fee-payer / Token-2022 paymaster surfaces — Kora advisory)
**First documented:** 2023 (Alchemy / OpenZeppelin disclosure of the UserOperation-packing inconsistency in EntryPoint v0.6 affecting `VerifyingPaymaster`); Solana / Token-2022 analogue documented 2025 (Kora paymaster advisory)
**Aliases:** "sponsorship policy bypass", "validatePaymasterUserOp bypass", "off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity violation", "fail-open instruction parser"

## Description

The attacker constructs UserOps that bypass the paymaster's sponsorship rules — per-user quotas, per-application allowlists, per-method gating, per-token spending limits. The mechanism is corruption of the *sponsorship decision*: the paymaster believes the UserOp matches an authorised pattern, but the UserOp's actual on-chain effect differs from what the policy engine evaluated.

Two canonical worked patterns:

- **Off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity violation.** A `VerifyingPaymaster`'s off-chain signer reasons about a UserOp field representation that disagrees with the on-chain `pack` method. The 2023 Alchemy / OpenZeppelin UserOperation-packing disclosure is the canonical example: the on-chain `pack` method covered the dynamic `paymasterAndData` field while the `VerifyingPaymaster.pack` did not, producing colliding hashes across UserOps and divergent hashes for the same UserOp depending on signing method `[alchemyuoppack2023]`. Any policy enforced via the off-chain hash is bypassable when the hashes diverge.
- **Fail-open instruction parser.** A paymaster sponsorship policy is implemented by a parser that walks UserOp instructions and evaluates each against allowlist / quota / method-gate rules. When the parser encounters an unknown instruction or extension, it builds an empty stub and silently ignores the instruction; any future extension automatically bypasses the policy until explicitly added to the parser. The Kora paymaster (Solana / Token-2022) advisory characterises this pattern explicitly — the parser's unknown-instruction default was permissive rather than deny-by-default `[dailycvekora2025]`.

T13.001.002 is part of the OAK-T13.001 (Paymaster Compromise) umbrella; see the parent for the architectural distinction from T9.

## Observed indicators

- A `VerifyingPaymaster` whose off-chain signer constructs UserOp hash bytes by a different rule than the on-chain `pack` method (most commonly: differing handling of dynamic fields `initCode`, `callData`, `paymasterAndData`).
- A paymaster sponsorship policy implemented by an instruction-parser whose unknown-instruction default is a permissive empty stub rather than a deny-by-default reject.
- A paymaster contract that does not enforce per-call gas caps, per-user quotas, or per-application allowlists at the validation boundary, but relies on off-chain rate-limiting only.
- A paymaster whose policy-evaluation surface accepts ERC-20 token-extension fields (Token-2022 transfer-fee, transfer-hook, permanent-delegate) without explicit allowlist semantics.

## Detection signals

- **Hash-parity verification.** Verify hash-construction parity between the on-chain `UserOperationLib.pack` and any off-chain signer producing `VerifyingPaymaster` authorisations. A deviation of even a single dynamic field is a T13.001.002 surface.
- **Policy-engine fail-closed verification.** Audit the policy engine for unknown-instruction handling; require a deny-by-default rather than a fail-open empty-stub default; require explicit allowlist semantics and reject-by-default semantics for any new instruction type or token extension.
- **Per-UserOp policy-rule audit.** For high-value paymasters, log the policy-rule decision per sponsored UserOp (which rule matched, which quota was decremented) and reconcile the log against the UserOp's actual on-chain effect; mismatches indicate policy-bypass.
- **EntryPoint-version pinning verification.** Confirm the `VerifyingPaymaster` is deployed against an EntryPoint version where the UserOp-packing parity issue is closed (≥v0.6 patched build); pin the EntryPoint version assumed by the off-chain signer and re-verify parity on every EntryPoint upgrade.
- **Pre-deployment audit.** Engagement with audit firms with explicit ERC-4337 specialist practice; treat `validatePaymasterUserOp` and the off-chain signer (where applicable) as the primary review surfaces.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2025-04-erc4337-paymaster.md`](../examples/2025-04-erc4337-paymaster.md) — ERC-4337 paymaster compromise cohort, April 2025 anchor. The anchor incident exercised T13.001.002 sub-case (off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity violation) — a deployed `VerifyingPaymaster`-pattern contract whose off-chain signer reasoned about a different UserOp byte representation than the on-chain `pack` method, allowing crafted UserOps to clear `validatePaymasterUserOp` while encoding execution paths the off-chain policy would have rejected.
- [`examples/2024-08-erc4337-paymaster-validation-bypass.md`](../examples/2024-08-erc4337-paymaster-validation-bypass.md) — ERC-4337 paymaster validation-bypass audit cohort, August 2024; pre-deployment audit-firm characterisation of the T13.001.002 policy-bypass surface (off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity, fail-open instruction parser) across the same validation boundary exploited in the April 2025 anchor. The combined 2024–2025 pair documents the full lifecycle from audit-disclosed surface to in-production exploitation.
- [`examples/2025-08-erc4337-paymaster-exploit-cohort.md`](../examples/2025-08-erc4337-paymaster-exploit-cohort.md) — ERC-4337 paymaster exploit cohort, August 2025 (~$1.8M across three paymasters); Paymaster B (~$500K) exercised T13.001.002 off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity violation — the paymaster's off-chain signer excluded the dynamic `paymasterAndData` field from its hash domain, producing colliding UserOp hashes across distinct execution payloads, replicating the Alchemy 2023 UserOperation-packing disclosure at production scale on an independent paymaster
- **UserOperation-packing inconsistency — EntryPoint v0.6 / `VerifyingPaymaster` — 2023-03 (canonical case)** — Alchemy and OpenZeppelin disclosed that the on-chain `UserOperationLib.pack` method captured all UserOp fields including the dynamic ones (`initCode`, `callData`, `paymasterAndData`), while the `VerifyingPaymaster.pack` method excluded `paymasterAndData`. The hash representations therefore disagreed, allowing different UserOps to share a hash and the same UserOp to produce divergent hashes depending on signing method. Patched in EntryPoint v0.6+; the disclosure is the canonical worked example for the off-chain-signer / on-chain-hash parity class `[alchemyuoppack2023]`.
- **Kora paymaster — Solana / Token-2022 — 2025 (canonical fail-open case)** — The Kora paymaster's policy-engine instruction-parser had a permissive empty-stub default for unknown Token-2022 extensions; the daily-CVE advisory walked through the bypass mechanism in detail. Canonical worked example for the fail-open instruction-parser sub-case `[dailycvekora2025]`.
- **Cohort framing — 2023 onward** — Both canonical cases were caught at the audit-firm layer rather than in production exploits; OpenZeppelin's three-audit Ethereum-Foundation engagement on EIP-4337 surfaced 7+ high-severity issues across the EntryPoint and reference paymasters including policy-bypass surfaces `[ozaa4337audit]`. Quantstamp audited Alchemy's v0.6 and v0.7 paymaster implementations `[quantstampalchemypm]`. Trail of Bits' six-mistakes essay characterises the recurring class across smart-account and paymaster engagements `[tobsixmistakes2026]`.
- [`examples/2024-2025-safe-4337-module-integration-security-issues.md`](../examples/2024-2025-safe-4337-module-integration-security-issues.md) — Safe 4337 Module audit disclosures — 2024–2025 — T13.001.002 + T13.001.003; operation-hash computation mismatch and signature-aggregation gateway findings are canonical validation-bypass surfaces at the module boundary — a UserOp that passed validation with one hash could execute with a different hash, and a single-signer aggregated signature could masquerade as multisig-authorised

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1.
- Pre-deployment vendor coverage: ERC-4337 specialist audit firms (OpenZeppelin, OtterSec, Quantstamp, Trail of Bits; see umbrella T13.001 for the canonical list).
- Runtime / operational coverage: per-UserOp policy-rule audit logging is paymaster-operator-side; no widely-deployed external runtime detector specifically targets policy-bypass.

## Mitigations

- **Paymaster architects:** use **deny-by-default** semantics for any unknown instruction or token extension in the policy engine; enforce per-call gas caps at the validation boundary, per-user quotas, per-application allowlists, and per-token spending limits in the contract rather than only off-chain.
- **Off-chain signer / `VerifyingPaymaster` operators:** verify hash-construction parity between the on-chain `UserOperationLib.pack` and the off-chain signer's hashing — a deviation of even a single dynamic field is a policy-bypass surface; pin the EntryPoint version assumed by the signer and re-verify parity on every EntryPoint upgrade.
- **Risk teams:** when reviewing a new paymaster integration, require (1) the policy engine's unknown-instruction handling (deny-by-default vs. fail-open) be documented, (2) hash-parity verification between off-chain signer and on-chain pack be evidenced, (3) the EntryPoint version pinning be evidenced; treat a paymaster that cannot supply (1)–(3) as a standing T13.001.002 surface.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M02, OAK-M16, OAK-M34.

## Discussion

T13.001.002 is the most-disclosed paymaster sub-Technique class at v0.1 because both canonical worked patterns (off-chain-signer hash parity, fail-open instruction parser) are catchable at audit time and both have been caught at audit time on high-profile deployments (Alchemy / Kora). Production exploits in the form of named loss incidents are absent from the public record, suggesting either that audit-firm coverage is reaching the policy-bypass surface effectively or that incidents are being absorbed by paymaster operators without disclosure.

The cross-chain dimension matters: the EVM canonical case (Alchemy 2023) and the Solana canonical case (Kora 2025) exercise the same architectural failure (sponsorship-decision corruption against an on-chain effect that differs from the policy-evaluated representation) on substrates that look superficially different. This is the strongest v0.1 argument for treating paymaster policy-bypass as a chain-portable Technique class.
