# OAK-T11.001 — Third-Party Signing-Vendor UI / Signing-Flow Compromise

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Solana, cross-chain
**First documented:** systematic 2022 onward; canonical large-scale case Bybit / Safe{Wallet} 2025-02
**Aliases:** "signing-vendor UI compromise", "Safe{Wallet}-class supply-chain compromise", "UI-payload substitution at sign time"

## Description

A class of attacks in which the attacker compromises a third-party signing-vendor's **user-interface or signing-flow surface** — typically a web UI, signing-platform front-end, or signing-platform build pipeline — and uses that compromise to substitute the transaction payload that a customer's signers see at sign time, while the cryptographic-signing layer continues to operate normally. The customer's authorised signers approve what they believe is a legitimate transaction at the vendor's UI; the on-chain transaction submitted carries an altered destination, calldata, or instruction set. Signatures are valid; intent is not.

T11.001 is **scoped to vendor-UI / signing-flow compromise only** at v0.1. It does **not** cover:

- **In-use multisig smart-contract modification** (Liminal / WazirX-class, where the on-chain multisig contract's signer set or threshold is manipulated such that future drains require no further signatures) — that case lives at OAK-T11.003 (In-Use Multisig Smart-Contract Manipulation), which is its canonical home.
- **Validator-set / bridge signer-key compromise** (Ronin-class) — that case lives at OAK-T10.001 (Validator / Signer Key Compromise) under the Bridge Tactic.
- **Wallet-software distribution compromise** (malicious wallet binary / extension) — that case lives at OAK-T11.002.

From a defender's perspective, T11.001 is identifiable through two complementary signals: an *off-chain* signal (vendor-side disclosure of supply-chain compromise of the UI build / hosting / DNS surface, malicious-payload reporting, internal-IR escalations from the affected vendor or its customers) and an *on-chain* signal (a single large outflow whose signature is valid but whose destination diverges from what the customer's signing personnel report having approved at the vendor's UI).

The dominant entry vector across documented T11.001 incidents is social engineering of vendor-side personnel with access to UI build / hosting / signing-flow code (engineering staff, signing-flow developers, customer-support staff with privileged UI access) — typically via LinkedIn fake-job-offer payloads, supply-chain compromise of the vendor's development tooling, or compromise of the vendor's hosting / DNS infrastructure. The customer organisation is rarely the proximate victim of the entry vector; the customer is the on-chain victim downstream.

## Observed indicators

- Off-chain: vendor-side disclosure of supply-chain compromise (workstation breach, malicious code injection, DNS / hosting compromise) at a signing-platform or custody-service vendor.
- On-chain: a single large outflow from a customer's cold or hot wallet to an unfamiliar destination, where the customer's signing personnel report having approved a different (legitimate-seeming) transaction at the vendor's UI.
- Pattern continuity: T11.001 incidents typically chain forward into T7.003 (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering) within hours; the speed-from-extraction-to-bridge-deposit metric is one of the cleanest off-chain signals when wallet-cluster forensic providers have a watchlist on the affected operator.

## Detection signals

- Off-chain telemetry: monitor disclosed supply-chain compromise events at major signing-infrastructure and custody-service vendors; treat customer organisations of an affected vendor as elevated risk for a calibrated post-disclosure window.
- Transaction-destination verification: require out-of-band verification of the destination address (read from a hardware wallet display, not from the web UI) before high-value transfers; this is the single most actionable customer-side mitigation.
- Wallet-cluster-attribution: per-customer cold-wallet outflow monitoring against known illicit-cluster watchlists; alert on large outflows whose destination clusters with known operator watchlists (especially OAK-G01).

## Real-world examples

- **Bybit / Safe{Wallet} — Ethereum — 2025-02-21 (canonical T11.001 case)** — Loss \~\$1.46B in ETH equivalent. Safe{Wallet} (Bybit's third-party multisig signing platform) was compromised earlier in February 2025 via social-engineering targeting of a Safe{Wallet} developer's workstation; malicious JavaScript was planted in the Safe{Wallet} signing UI to alter the destination address of routine cold-to-warm transfers at sign time. The cryptographic-signing layer was not compromised — Bybit's signers signed exactly what their UI displayed; the UI displayed a payload different from the one submitted on-chain. FBI IC3 PSA confirmed OAK-G01 attribution within five days. The single largest crypto-theft event on the public record and the canonical worked example for the T11.001 UI-payload-substitution class. See worked examples at `examples/2025-02-bybit.md` (extraction event) and `examples/2025-02-bybit-thorchain-laundering.md` (downstream T7.003 chain).
- **Cross-reference — WazirX / Liminal — Ethereum — 2024-07-18 — NOT T11.001.** Loss \~\$234.9M. The WazirX case is **not** a T11.001 instance and is cross-referenced here only to disambiguate scope: the failure mode was in-use modification of the multisig smart-contract (signer-set / threshold manipulation), not signing-flow UI compromise. Future drains required no further signatures because the on-chain contract had been altered. The canonical home for this case is **OAK-T11.003 (In-Use Multisig Smart-Contract Manipulation)**; see that Technique page for the worked example. Earlier OAK drafts grouped Bybit and WazirX under the same Technique; v0.1 separates them because the mitigation surfaces are structurally different (UI-build-pipeline integrity for Bybit-class; on-chain change-control delay between proposal and execution for WazirX-class).
- Aggregate context: per `[chainalysis2024dprk]`, T11.001-class incidents (UI / signing-flow compromise) plus the adjacent T11.002 (wallet-software distribution) and T11.003 (in-use multisig manipulation) cases together account for a substantial fraction of OAK-G01-attributed annual losses (\$1.34B in 2024, 61% of all attacker-stolen value globally per Chainalysis).

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T11 is custody-and-signing-infrastructure layer; out of MG scope).
- Vendor coverage: limited at v0.1. Wallet-cluster-attribution providers (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic) provide post-event laundering tracking; pre-event detection for signing-infrastructure compromise lives primarily at the affected vendor's internal SOC.

## Mitigations

- **Customer organisations (highest-leverage):** out-of-band verification of high-value transaction destinations at sign time (hardware-wallet display, multi-channel confirmation); air-gap signing for cold-wallet transfers; vendor-relationship contractual requirements for breach notification SLA; redundancy across signing-infrastructure vendors for the highest-balance accounts.
- **Signing-infrastructure vendors:** anti-phishing training (especially for engineering staff routinely contacted via LinkedIn / messaging platforms); supply-chain controls on UI deployment artefacts; runtime integrity verification of deployed UI against signed reference; mandatory breach-disclosure SLA in customer contracts.
- **Off-chain CTI integration:** subscribe to vendor-disclosure feeds; cross-reference with on-chain attribution providers when vendor disclosures land.

## Discussion

T11.001 is structurally distinct from three adjacent classes that earlier OAK drafts conflated with it:

- vs. **T10.001 (bridge validator-key compromise; Ronin-class):** T10.001 attacks bridge-protocol-specific validator sets that cross-chain operators directly control; T11.001 attacks signing platforms used by independent customer organisations to authorise on-chain actions. Both involve "third-party signing infrastructure" but the chain-architecture layer differs.
- vs. **T11.002 (wallet-software distribution compromise):** T11.002 substitutes the signing software the customer runs (malicious wallet binary / extension distribution channel); T11.001 substitutes the payload the customer-controlled signer signs at the vendor's UI / signing-flow. The signing software in T11.001 may itself be uncompromised.
- vs. **T11.003 (in-use multisig smart-contract manipulation; Liminal / WazirX-class):** T11.003 modifies the on-chain multisig contract such that future drains require no further signatures from the customer; T11.001 retains the customer's signing-set and threshold intact but corrupts what gets signed at sign time. The Bybit Feb-2025 incident is canonical T11.001; the WazirX Jul-2024 incident is canonical T11.003. Contributors writing future T11.001 examples should preserve this distinction explicitly — if the on-chain multisig contract was modified, the case belongs at T11.003.

Contributors writing future T11.001 examples should preserve the customer-vs-vendor and UI-vs-on-chain distinctions explicitly — the breach happens at the vendor's UI / signing-flow layer; the customer is the on-chain victim; the multisig contract itself remains unmodified.

The dollar-loss-prevented metric for T11.001 is most sensitive to vendor-side operational security on the UI / signing-flow surface; on-chain detection arrives too late in essentially all documented cases. The OAK position is that T11.001 mitigation is primarily an off-chain CTI + procurement-controls problem (vendor-build-pipeline integrity, UI-payload runtime verification, out-of-band destination verification at sign time), and that on-chain telemetry is the forensic-attribution layer rather than the prevention layer.
