# OAK-T10.004 — Optimistic-Bridge Fraud-Proof Gap

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T10 (Bridge and Cross-Chain)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM, cross-chain (any optimistic-message-verification or optimistic-rollup-bridged architecture)
**First documented:** 2022 (concept characterised in Connext / Nomad architecture write-ups; recurring as architecture-review finding)
**Aliases:** "watcher-liveness failure", "challenge-window inadequacy", "fraud-proof system gap", "1-of-N honest-verifier assumption failure"

## Description

A class of bridge-infrastructure attacks specific to optimistic-rollup-style or optimistic-message-verification-style bridges, where the bridge architecture relies on a fraud-proof challenge window to detect invalid messages, but the challenge mechanism is incompletely operationalised. The attack surface is the **gap between the architectural assumption (a 1-of-N honest, live, economically-motivated challenger exists) and the deployed reality** (challengers may be absent, censored, under-incentivised, or technically blocked from filing a valid proof).

Sub-cases:

- **(a) Absent challenger network.** No economically-incentivised challenger ("watcher", "verifier", "fraud-prover") actually monitors the bridge and submits fraud proofs. The 1-of-N honest-verifier assumption reduces to 0-of-N in operational terms.
- **(b) Inadequate challenge window.** The challenge window is too short for realistic challenger response time given the chain's reorg depth, mempool latency, and human escalation time, or — for cross-chain optimistic bridges — too short to permit cross-chain proof construction.
- **(c) Fraud-proof system bugs.** The fraud-proof contract itself contains bugs (incorrect state-transition reproduction, gas griefing, missing dispute-game branches, incorrect bond-slashing logic) that prevent valid challenges from being processed even when a challenger is present and timely.
- **(d) Censorship / liveness defeat.** The challenger-side liveness can be defeated by attacker-controlled censoring — e.g., a sequencer or block-builder excluding fraud-proof transactions, a DoS against challenger infrastructure, or economic griefing that exhausts the challenger's bond capital before the window closes.

T10.004 differs from T10.001 (key compromise) and T10.002 (verification bypass) in that the on-chain code is *behaving as designed* — the bridge accepts the message because no valid challenge arrived in time. The failure is at the **defensive-mechanism layer**, not at the authorisation layer (T10.001) or the verification-predicate layer (T10.002). This is why T10.004 is detected primarily by architecture review and operational-readiness audit rather than by transaction monitoring.

## Observed indicators

- Bridge documentation specifies a challenge window but does not name a funded, public, contractually-bonded challenger set.
- Challenge window expressed in minutes (e.g., 30 min) without a published analysis of challenger response-time budget across reorgs, mempool delays, and cross-chain proof-construction time.
- Fraud-proof contract has been deployed but has never processed a real or simulated challenge in production (a "cold" fraud-proof path).
- Operator post-mortems or status-page disclosures referring to "watcher downtime" or "single-watcher" operation.
- Dispute-game contract code contains operator-controllable parameters (window length, bond size, allowed-prover set) without timelocks or governance constraints.

## Detection signals

- Pre-deployment / pre-integration: review the bridge's challenger-set economics (bond size vs. potential-loss size; reward-vs-cost for honest challenge); verify that at least one independent challenger operator is funded and live, not just specified in documentation.
- Architecture review: verify that the challenge window is justified by a written response-time-budget analysis covering chain-reorg depth, transaction-inclusion latency under congestion, and cross-chain proof-construction time (where applicable).
- Fraud-proof exercise: require the operator to demonstrate, on testnet or via an adversarial drill, that a *valid* fraud proof is processable end-to-end within the configured window. A fraud-proof system that has never processed a successful challenge is a T10.004 surface regardless of code review.
- Runtime: monitor challenger-set liveness as a first-class metric (last-heartbeat, last-bond-balance, last-successful-challenge-or-no-fault-confirmation). Alert when a single challenger has been live for fewer than expected rotations.
- Censorship-resistance review: identify whether fraud-proof transactions can be censored by the same operator whose messages they would dispute (the canonical self-censorship gap).

## Real-world examples

- **Optimistic-bridge fraud-proof gap as a class — multiple architectures — 2022 onward** — OAK does not at v0.1 name a single canonical T10.004 incident in the same league as Ronin (T10.001) or Nomad (T10.002), because optimistic-bridge fraud-proof gaps are documented at the architecture-review layer rather than via headline loss incidents. The class is well-characterised in academic and industry literature: Halborn's analysis of the Nomad bridge documents the optimistic-verification mechanism and its dependence on watcher liveness `[halbornnomadoptimistic2022]`; the original Connext / Nomad architecture write-ups by Arjun Bhuptani describe the 1-of-N honest-verifier assumption explicitly `[bhuptanioptbridges2022]`; recent academic work characterises liveness-incentive insufficiency in dispute games as a strategic-exploit surface `[hollowvictory2025]`. Note that the Nomad 2022-08 incident itself is catalogued under T10.002 because its proximate failure was a verification-predicate bug (`0x00` trusted-root initialisation), not a fraud-proof-window failure — the optimistic mechanism was bypassed entirely rather than out-raced. Contributors with public forensic write-ups of specific named incidents whose primary failure mode is T10.004 are encouraged to submit them via PR.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T10.004 is a bridge-infrastructure / architecture-review class, out of MG runtime-detector scope).
- Pre-deployment vendor coverage: audit firms with bridge-specialist practice (Halborn, Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin) reviewing fraud-proof contract correctness and challenger-set economics; bridge-specialist analysts (L2BEAT) publishing per-bridge "stage" classifications that flag fraud-proof maturity. No widely-deployed runtime detector specifically observes challenger-liveness as of v0.1.

## Mitigations

- **Bridge architects:** size the challenge window from a written response-time-budget analysis (reorg depth + inclusion latency + cross-chain proof construction + human escalation), not from convention. Treat the challenger set as a first-class operational dependency: name it, fund it, bond it, and require liveness telemetry. Prefer designs where multiple independent challenger operators run by default, not as an aspirational future state.
- **Bridge operators:** publish challenger-set membership, bond balances, and last-heartbeat metrics on a public dashboard; commission periodic adversarial drills that exercise the fraud-proof path end-to-end on mainnet (or a high-fidelity testnet) and publish the results. Implement censorship-resistance for fraud-proof submissions — at minimum, ensure the entity producing messages cannot also exclude fraud-proof transactions.
- **Risk teams:** treat any optimistic bridge whose challenger set is the operator themselves, or whose fraud-proof contract has never processed a successful challenge, as elevated risk regardless of audit reputation. Review L2BEAT-equivalent stage classifications and require the operator to provide a response-time-budget analysis as part of integration due diligence.
- **Cross-comparison:** unlike T10.001, raising thresholds does not help — the 1-of-N assumption is structurally distinct from M-of-N validator authorisation. Unlike T10.002, post-deployment smoke-testing of the verification function does not cover this surface; the relevant smoke test is a fraud-proof drill, not a verification-predicate test.

## Discussion

T10.004 is included in v0.1 partly as a **completeness move** for the Bridge Tactic — analogous to T10.003. A Bridge Tactic that catalogues only validator compromise, verification bypass, and replay would leave the optimistic-bridge architecture class uncatalogued, even though the dollar-loss-attribution to T10.004 specifically is presently small relative to T10.001 / T10.002. This may be a reporting artefact rather than a real prevalence statement: a bridge whose fraud-proof system was operationally absent and whose messages were never adversarially probed simply has not been tested.

The Technique is documented because the failure mode is architectural and the question a defender or risk reviewer must ask — *who actually challenges, with what bond, in what window, on what infrastructure?* — is canonical and answerable. Optimistic-bridge designs are gaining adoption (notably as the cross-chain interoperability layer for L2 ecosystems and intent-based bridging), so OAK expects T10.004 to mature from "observed" to "stable" as the architecture deploys at greater scale. Contributors writing future T10.004 entries should not feel obliged to find a comparable-scale named incident; a well-characterised architecture-review finding with verifiable artefacts (audit reports, dispute-game contract code, challenger-set disclosures) is sufficient. Where a future incident's primary failure mode is clearly T10.004 (e.g., a successful invalid-message finalisation due to absent challengers), it should be catalogued here with the standard worked-example treatment.
