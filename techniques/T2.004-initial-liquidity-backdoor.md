# OAK-T2.004 — Initial-Liquidity Backdoor

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T2 (Liquidity Establishment), OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (Ethereum, BSC, Polygon, Arbitrum, Base), Solana
**First documented:** empirical (industry-survey scale; characterised in rug-pull retrospectives and backdoor-code static-analysis research)
**Aliases:** "shadow LP mint", "router-mint backdoor", "creation-time LP backdoor", "privileged pool admin"

## Description

A class of OAK-T2 patterns in which the operator's pool-creation transaction structurally retains backdoor withdrawal authority over the initial LP. The backdoor is embedded at the moment the pool is constructed, rather than being introduced post-creation through a locker-side misrepresentation (T2.002) or a cross-chain receipt mismatch (T2.003). From a defender's perspective, the indicator is *not* the pool's depth profile or any explicit lock claim — there may be no lock claim at all — but the LP-control-authority structure embedded in the pool-creation transaction itself.

T2.004 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T2.001 (Single-Sided Liquidity Plant)** because the depth profile may look entirely normal: quote-side depth can be calibrated to comfortably exceed any reasonable depth/FDV threshold, and the pool can pass T2.001-style static gates while still being fully drainable by the operator through the backdoor path. T2.001's failure mode lives in the *quantitative* shape of the initial pool; T2.004's failure mode lives in the *authority-structural* shape of the creation transaction.

T2.004 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T2.002 (Locked-Liquidity Spoof)** because the issue is not a misrepresented lock. T2.002 assumes a "locked liquidity" claim exists and that the defender's job is to verify whether the on-chain authority structure constrains the deployer the way the marketing claim implies. T2.004 attacks the construction step that *precedes* any locking decision: by the time an LP token reaches a locker (or fails to), the operator-controlled withdrawal path has already been embedded. Even a perfectly-locked LP receipt covers only the LP tokens that were minted to the locker, not whatever shadow LP authority the creation transaction routed elsewhere.

T2.004 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T2.003 (Cross-Chain Locked-Liquidity Spoof)** because the surface is single-chain and creation-local. T2.003 is a verification-path mismatch across chains; T2.004 is a creation-transaction-internal authority retention.

Sub-patterns observed:

- **Router-mediated LP mint to multiple recipients.** A pool-creation router (rather than a direct factory call) is used; the router mints LP tokens to a legitimate-looking LP-provider address *and* to one or more operator-controlled "shadow LP" addresses in the same transaction. Public LP-holder enumerators that filter on the headline LP recipient miss the shadow-LP balance.
- **Upgradeable pool contracts with admin LP-withdraw paths.** The pool itself is deployed behind a proxy whose admin role can withdraw underlying tokens without going through the normal LP-token burn flow. The "LP supply" reported by the pool's LP-token contract is correct on its face but not load-bearing for the actual reserve balance.
- **Concentrated-liquidity (Uniswap V3-style) attacker-controlled tick range.** The pool is initialised with a tick range whose effective trading band excludes the realistic price discovery zone, or whose position NFT is owned by a deployer-clustered address with no constraint on `decreaseLiquidity` / `collect`. Withdrawal is structurally gated by the operator's position-NFT control rather than by an LP-token burn.
- **Mint-recipient indirection.** The LP-mint recipient is an intermediary contract (multisig, vesting wrapper, custodian wrapper) that surface-presents as a third-party custody role but whose authority structure resolves back to the deployer cluster.
- **Per-pool privileged-role retention.** Pool factories that support per-pool roles (fee collector, oracle setter, emergency withdrawer) deploy with one or more such roles assigned to the deployer or a deployer-clustered address, and at least one such role admits an LP-equivalent withdrawal effect.

## Observed indicators

- Pool-creation transaction trace shows LP token mint events to *multiple* recipients, not just the headline LP-provider. Any non-locker, non-LP-provider recipient is a candidate shadow-LP holder.
- Pool contract is deployed behind a proxy (EIP-1967, transparent, UUPS, or factory-managed beacon) whose admin role is held by the deployer cluster or by an upgradeable contract whose admin chain resolves to the deployer cluster.
- Pool contract or its underlying reserve-holding contract enumerates non-trivial admin roles (e.g., `EMERGENCY_WITHDRAW_ROLE`, `RESCUE_ROLE`, `ADMIN_ROLE`, `FEE_COLLECTOR_ROLE`) whose granted-to set overlaps the deployer cluster.
- Concentrated-liquidity position NFTs minted at pool initialisation are held by a deployer-clustered address; the position's tick range is unusually narrow or unusually wide in a way that is inconsistent with the marketed trading band.
- Pool-creation transaction is routed through a non-canonical router contract (i.e., not the canonical factory's documented router) whose source is unverified or whose `mint` / `initialize` flow is not symmetric in the LP-token recipient parameter.
- LP-mint recipient address has no on-chain history before the creation transaction and no transactions after, but is funded from the deployer cluster (a "warmed-up" shadow-LP address).

## Detection signals

- **Static (creation-transaction trace).** For every pool-creation transaction on a watched factory, enumerate all `Transfer` and `Mint` events with the LP token as the source contract; emit one record per LP-mint recipient. Treat any LP supply reaching an address that is not (a) the headline LP-provider, (b) a registry-known locker, or (c) a registry-known custodian as a T2.004 signal.
- **Static (admin-role inventory).** For every pool contract, enumerate all roles via `getRoleMember`/`getRoleAdmin` (OpenZeppelin AccessControl), `owner()` / `pendingOwner()` (Ownable), `proxyAdmin` / EIP-1967 admin slot direct read, and any contract-specific admin getters. Compare every role-holder address against the deployer-cluster set.
- **Static (proxy detection on pool itself).** Many T2.004 instances deploy the pool *implementation* behind a proxy. Read the EIP-1967 implementation slot directly; do not rely on the contract's self-reported interface. A proxy at the pool address with deployer-controlled admin is a strong T2.004 signal regardless of any other indicator.
- **Static (CL position-NFT enumeration).** For Uniswap V3-style pools, enumerate position NFTs minted at or near pool initialisation; resolve owners and tick ranges; flag deployer-cluster ownership and ranges that exclude the marketed trading band.
- **Behavioural (cross-validation).** Reconciling the LP-token total supply against the pool's actual reserve balance flow over the first N blocks: if reserves can move without corresponding LP-burn events, the LP supply is not load-bearing — a definitional T2.004 condition.
- **Workflow.** Treat the pool-creation transaction as the unit of analysis, not the pool address. Many T2.004 setups present cleanly when the pool address is queried in isolation but fail the moment the creation transaction's full event log is inspected.

## Real-world examples

OAK-T2.004 is best characterised at cohort scale rather than via a single named incident at v0.1. Industry retrospectives describe creation-time backdoor patterns recurringly: Chainalysis's annual crypto-crime retrospective frames hard-rug architectures around "hidden withdrawal authority" without isolating the creation-transaction surface as a separate Technique; SlowMist's annual blockchain-security report catalogues the corresponding code patterns in the broader "scam token" cohort; QuillAudits' backdoor-code survey documents the pattern at the per-contract-function level (proxy-upgrade-introduced `withdrawToken` hidden functions, unauthorized ownership-transfer flows, AccessControl-role retention) and is the closest existing public reference to the T2.004 surface in this repository's citation set. The TM-RugPull dataset (~1,028 multi-chain projects spanning 2016–2025) and the SolRPDS dataset (~62,895 suspicious Solana liquidity pools spanning 2021–2024) both contain large sub-cohorts whose primary failure mode is creation-transaction-embedded LP-control retention rather than post-creation lock misrepresentation; per-pool reclassification between T2.002 and T2.004 within these cohorts is a v0.x research item. Recent academic work specifically targeting pool-creation-and-transaction fusion analysis (`[rphunter2025]`) and bytecode-level backdoor pattern detection (`[applsci2025backdoor]`) maps directly onto the T2.004 detection methodology and is the cleanest current academic anchor for the Technique. Cohort sources: `[chainalysis2025rug]`, `[slowmist2024report]`, `[tmrugpull2026]`, `[solrpds]`, `[quillauditsbackdoor]`. Contributors with public forensic write-ups of specific named incidents whose primary failure mode is T2.004 (creation-time LP-authority retention, not post-creation lock spoofing) are encouraged to submit them via PR with the on-chain pool-creation transaction hash, the LP-mint recipient enumeration, and the admin-role inventory at the pool contract.

## Reference implementations

- MG `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. The pool-creation-transaction trace primitive overlaps D02 (used for T2.002 lock-coverage parsing) and D10 (used for T2.001 depth profiling); a unified creation-transaction enumerator that emits per-LP-mint-recipient and per-admin-role records is a v0.x improvement target.
- GoPlus — partial via `lp_holder_count`, `lp_holder_addresses`, and proxy / upgradeable-contract heuristics; LP-mint-recipient enumeration in the *creation transaction* specifically (rather than at query time) is not surfaced.
- RugCheck — partial on Solana for LP-holder distribution; pool-creation-transaction trace parsing as a first-class field is not standardised.
- TokenSniffer / Honeypot.is — partial on contract-level proxy-and-admin-role detection at the *token* contract level; the pool contract itself is not the primary unit of analysis.
- Locker-services side: even a fully-correct lock receipt is defence-in-depth at best for T2.004 — the lock covers only the LP tokens that reached the locker, not the shadow-LP, the proxy-admin path, or the per-pool privileged role.

## Mitigations

- **DEX factory operators:** publish a canonical pool-creation interface and treat any pool created via a non-canonical router as out-of-protocol for the purposes of factory-level safety properties; surface the creation-transaction trace prominently in factory-side block-explorer integrations so that defenders can inspect LP-mint-recipient enumeration without manually walking the event log.
- **DEX factory operators (V3-class):** for concentrated-liquidity factories, surface the initial position-NFT owner and tick range at the factory level as part of the standard pool-listing payload; treat deployer-cluster ownership of the initial position NFT as a first-class warning surface rather than a manual lookup.
- **Listing venues / launchpads / aggregators:** require a creation-transaction-trace-derived LP-mint-recipient enumeration as an explicit listing precondition; reject pools whose creation transaction routes LP supply to addresses outside the announced LP-provider, a registry-known locker, or a registry-known custodian.
- **Locker services:** publish the locker-coverage fraction *of the actual LP token's total supply at lock time*, not just the absolute amount locked, and surface explicitly when the pool contract behind the LP token has admin paths that bypass LP-token burn — i.e., extend T2.002 receipts to flag T2.004 conditions as a defence-in-depth signal even when the locker itself is doing its job correctly.
- **Traders / risk teams:** verify the pool-creation transaction directly; treat "LP locked" claims and depth profiles as necessary-but-not-sufficient. The hard gate is: does the creation transaction admit any LP-equivalent withdrawal path (shadow-LP recipient, proxy admin, privileged role, attacker-owned CL position NFT) that bypasses the LP-token-burn flow?
- **Cross-comparison with T2.001 / T2.002 / T2.003:** T2.001 mitigations (depth thresholds) and T2.002 mitigations (parse lock receipts; verify covered fraction, duration, locker authority) and T2.003 mitigations (chain-binding of lock receipts) all remain necessary; T2.004 adds a *creation-transaction structural* check that none of T2.001–T2.003 require because each of those Techniques implicitly assumes the LP-token burn flow is the only withdrawal path.

## Discussion

T2.004 is the Technique that explains why a pool can pass *all* of T2.001 (clean depth), T2.002 (clean lock receipt), and T2.003 (lock receipt and pool on the same chain) and still be fully drainable by the operator at any moment. The single-chain rug-pull retrospectives of 2021–2022 framed the Liquidity-Establishment failure mode primarily as "thin pool" or "fake lock"; what the post-2023 backdoor-code research adds is that the LP-token burn flow is not, in general, the only withdrawal path — and that defender tooling that conflates "LP supply" with "constrained withdrawal authority" is structurally under-specified.

The CL-pool variant deserves separate emphasis. Concentrated-liquidity pools shift the locus of LP control from the LP-token contract to the position-NFT contract; many T2.002-style locker workflows do not natively cover position NFTs, and many depth-profile gates (T2.001-style) compute depth over the active tick band without surfacing whether the position NFT giving the band its shape is owned by the deployer cluster. The honest defender posture is that V3-class factories require their own creation-transaction trace primitive — position-NFT enumeration alongside LP-token mint enumeration — and that T2.001/T2.002 gates calibrated on V2-class pools do not transfer cleanly.

Coverage gap to track: T2.004 sits at the intersection of T2 (Liquidity Establishment) and T6 (Defense Evasion) because the proxy-admin and per-pool-role sub-patterns are evasion-shaped — the on-chain authority structure is intentionally arranged to evade T2.001 / T2.002 / T2.003 gates rather than to fail them outright. As DEX factories continue to add per-pool admin roles, fee-collector hooks, oracle-setter hooks, and emergency-withdrawer hooks, OAK expects T2.004 to mature from "observed" to "stable" and to acquire named-incident worked examples as forensic write-ups isolate the creation-transaction surface from the broader "rug pull" framing. A factory-side canonical creation-transaction-trace standard (LP-mint-recipient enumeration, admin-role inventory, proxy-presence flag, position-NFT-owner record where applicable) would close most of this surface; absence of such a standard at v0.1 is itself a T2.004 surface.
