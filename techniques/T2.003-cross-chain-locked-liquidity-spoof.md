# OAK-T2.003 — Cross-Chain Locked-Liquidity Spoof

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T2 (Liquidity Establishment), OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (Ethereum, BSC, Polygon, Arbitrum, Base), Solana, cross-chain
**First documented:** 2022 (concept characterised in cohort-scale rug-pull retrospectives; recurring in multi-chain launch venues)
**Aliases:** "off-chain lock claim", "wrong-chain lock receipt", "split-chain LP lock", "lock-on-A pool-on-B"

## Description

A class of OAK-T2 patterns extending OAK-T2.002 (Locked-Liquidity Spoof) to setups where the **lock receipt and the liquidity pool are deployed on different chains**. The deployer publishes a marketing claim of "LP locked on Ethereum" (or some other relatively well-monitored chain), pointing to a transaction or locker-contract entry on chain A, while the actual tradable LP token is held on chain B (BSC, Polygon, Solana, Base, or any chain where defender tooling and locker-registry coverage are weaker).

T2.002 framed the failure mode as a mismatch between the off-chain claim and the on-chain authority structure on a single chain. T2.003 extends that mismatch across the chain boundary: the on-chain authority structure that *would* constrain the deployer is on a chain where no such LP exists, and the LP that *does* exist is on a chain where no such constraint is recorded. From a defender's perspective, the claim is "verifiable" in the most superficial sense — a transaction hash on chain A does exist — but the verification has no semantic relationship to the LP that traders are actually exposed to.

T2.003 is structurally distinct from T2.002 because the defender-side complications are not about parsing locker-contract authority schemas (T2.002's territory) but about reconstructing the cross-chain binding that the marketing claim implicitly asserts and that no canonical tooling currently verifies end-to-end. Per-chain locker-contract registries are not unified; cross-chain lock-receipt verification typically requires manual reconstruction; and per-chain locker-contract address spaces overlap, so a deployer can register a counterfeit "locker contract" on a less-monitored chain that pattern-matches the bytecode or interface of a legitimate locker on the well-monitored chain.

## Observed indicators

- Marketing claim references a lock receipt or locker-contract URL whose chain (per the URL host or block-explorer prefix) differs from the chain on which the project's tradable LP token is held.
- Locker-contract address on chain A matches a known-legitimate locker (Unicrypt / UNCX, Team Finance, PinkLock, Mudra), but the LP token referenced in the lock entry is a freshly-minted token on chain A with no relationship to the chain-B trading pool of the same name.
- Locker-contract address on chain B has the *same address* as a legitimate locker on chain A (a deliberate "address-overlap" choice, easy on EVM chains because address spaces are not chain-bound), but the contract bytecode at that address on chain B is not a deployment of the legitimate locker — it is a deployer-controlled lookalike.
- The "LP locked" announcement points to a chain whose block-explorer UX surfaces lock entries prominently (Etherscan token-locker tab) while the trading venue is on a chain where the same explorer plugin has weaker or no equivalent surfacing.
- Project documentation does not explicitly state which chain the LP itself is on, or states it inconsistently across channels (website vs. Telegram vs. CoinGecko entry).

## Detection signals

- Static (cross-chain): for any "LP locked" claim, retrieve the lock receipt and resolve the LP token referenced by the lock entry to its chain-of-origin; require a hard match against the chain on which the project's tradable pool is deployed. Treat any mismatch as the spoof.
- Static (locker-contract identity): cross-reference the locker-contract address on the *pool's* chain against a per-chain registry of known-legitimate lockers; do not assume that a known address on chain A implies a known deployment at the same address on chain B.
- Static (bytecode comparison): for any locker contract whose address matches a known-legitimate locker on a different chain, compare deployed bytecode against the canonical deployment; flag any divergence as a counterfeit-locker surface (T2.003 + T6 overlap).
- Cross-reference: where the project lists pools on multiple chains, require a separate, chain-local lock receipt for *each* chain's LP supply; a single lock receipt cannot cover multi-chain LP under the current locker model.
- Workflow: treat any lock-claim verification that requires manual chain-selection or manual block-explorer switching as a process failure to harden, not a one-off lookup. Per-chain locker-contract registries that are not unified are themselves a T2.003 surface.

## Real-world examples

OAK-T2.003 is best characterised at cohort scale rather than via a single named incident at v0.1 — the public forensic record on rug pulls describes the cross-chain lock-claim mismatch primarily within larger cohort statistics on misrepresented locks rather than as a separately-named class. The TM-RugPull dataset (\~1,028 multi-chain projects spanning 2016–2025) explicitly spans multiple chains and contains sub-cohorts where the lock claim and the pool location are on different chains; the SolRPDS dataset (\~62,895 suspicious Solana liquidity pools spanning 2021–2024) is a Solana-side counterpart for cases where a marketing claim references an Ethereum or BSC lock for a Solana pool. Industry retrospectives such as Chainalysis's annual crypto-crime reporting describe lock-misrepresentation as a recurring rug-pull enabler at multi-chain launch venues. Cohort sources: `[tmrugpull2026]`, `[solrpds]`, `[chainalysis2025rug]`. Contributors with public forensic write-ups of specific named incidents whose primary failure mode is T2.003 (cross-chain lock-receipt mismatch, not single-chain T2.002 misrepresentation) are encouraged to submit them via PR with the on-chain pool address, the claimed lock-receipt URL, and the chain-A-vs-chain-B mismatch documented.

## Reference implementations

- MG `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T2.002 / D02 covers the single-chain pattern; cross-chain lock-receipt resolution is out of D02's current scope and is a v0.x improvement target).
- GoPlus — partial via per-chain locker heuristics, but cross-chain receipt-to-pool binding is not surfaced.
- RugCheck — partial on Solana; does not currently consume EVM-side lock-receipt claims when validating Solana pools.
- Locker-services side: cross-chain locker-registry standardisation (a unified, signed, multi-chain registry of legitimate locker-contract addresses with per-chain bytecode hashes) is a v0.x improvement target. As of v0.1 no such canonical registry exists; Unicrypt / UNCX, Team Finance, and PinkLock publish per-chain deployments but a unified machine-readable cross-chain manifest with bytecode hashes is not standardised.

## Mitigations

- **Traders / risk teams:** require lock receipts on the *same chain* as the LP itself; treat any "locked on chain A, pool on chain B" claim as unverified by default, regardless of whether the chain-A receipt is otherwise well-formed.
- **Listing venues / launchpads / aggregators:** publish "lock-receipt-must-be-on-same-chain-as-LP" as an explicit listing precondition; reject submissions that present cross-chain lock claims without a chain-local receipt for each chain's LP supply.
- **Locker services:** publish a unified, multi-chain, signed registry of legitimate locker-contract addresses *with per-chain bytecode hashes*, so that defenders verifying a lock receipt on chain B can cryptographically confirm the locker contract there is the same code as the audited deployment on chain A. This is the canonical mitigation for the address-overlap variant of T2.003.
- **Block-explorer tooling:** when surfacing a lock-receipt entry, display the chain-of-origin of the referenced LP token explicitly, and warn when it differs from the chain on which the receipt itself lives.
- **Cross-comparison with T2.002:** the T2.002 mitigations (parse lock receipts, verify covered fraction / duration / authority) remain necessary on each chain; T2.003 adds a *chain-binding* check that T2.002 does not require because T2.002 implicitly assumed single-chain context.

## Discussion

T2.003 is the Technique that makes the **bridge era of token launches** genuinely novel relative to the single-chain era, from a Liquidity-Establishment-Tactic perspective. T2.002 was already well-characterised in the 2021–2022 single-chain rug-pull retrospectives; what changed once token launches went multi-chain is not that the lock-spoof pattern itself became more sophisticated, but that the defender's verification path now crosses a chain boundary that no canonical tooling resolves end-to-end. The address-overlap variant — registering a deployer-controlled lookalike at the *same address* as a legitimate locker on a less-monitored chain — is only available because EVM-chain address spaces are not bound to chains, and is a structural artefact of the multi-chain deployment model rather than a sophistication step on top of T2.002.

Coverage gap to track: T2.003 sits at the intersection of T2 (Liquidity Establishment) and T10 (Bridge and Cross-Chain) without being a Bridge Technique itself — the "cross-chain" element here is not a bridge incident but a verification-path mismatch. As multi-chain launches become the default for new token deployments and as launchpads expand cross-chain listing flows, OAK expects T2.003 to mature from "observed" to "stable" and to acquire named-incident worked examples. A unified cross-chain locker registry with per-chain bytecode hashes (the v0.x mitigation target above) would close most of this surface for the legitimate-locker case; the counterfeit-locker case will remain until block-explorer tooling and aggregator listing flows treat chain-binding of lock receipts as a first-class field rather than a manual lookup.
