# OAK-T9.003 — Governance Attack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T16.001, OAK-T16.002, OAK-T16.003, OAK-T16.005 (T9.003 is the governance-contract-bug case; T16.x are voting-power-abuse-against-working-contract cases — see Description for the scope split)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, cross-chain
**First documented:** 2022 (Beanstalk canonical case)
**Aliases:** "DAO governance exploit", "voting-power attack", "BIP attack", "malicious proposal"

## Description

T9.003 covers the **governance-contract-bug exploitation** case: protocol-layer code-level flaws in the governance contract itself (storage-collision-on-upgrade, hidden-`delegatecall`-to-self-modifying-implementation, missing flash-loan-resistance in the voting-power computation, emergency-execution paths that bypass the proposal lifecycle, malicious-proposal-payload injection at the calldata layer). The defender-relevant invariant is "the governance contract itself is the broken primitive"; the attacker's voting power is mechanical or incidental, but the flaw lies in how the governance contract converts the vote into protocol state. From a defender's perspective, T9.003 attacks the *governance design* at the code-correctness layer.

**Scope split with OAK-T16:** v0.3 introduced T16 (Governance / Voting Manipulation) which covers the **voting-power-abuse-against-working-contract** case — flash-loan vote takeover (T16.001), hostile-vote treasury drain (T16.002), delegation-cluster vote takeover (T16.003), Snapshot / off-chain voting exploitation (T16.004), malicious-proposal snowballing (T16.005). T16.x sub-Techniques assume the governance contract operates as designed and the *outcome* is captured by an attacker who acquires sufficient voting power through legitimate or quasi-legitimate means. T9.003 is the residual case where the governance contract code itself has a bug — the contract does not behave as designed, and exploitation does not require majority voting power against a working primitive. Worked examples that previously mapped to T9.003 alone now also map to one or more T16.x sub-Techniques where the operational sub-pattern is voting-power abuse; T9.003 retains the exploits where the load-bearing surface is the governance contract's code-correctness rather than its voting-power-distribution assumption.

T9.003 is the canonical example of a Technique that is "operationally one transaction long" but "design-wise the protocol's entire governance lifetime": every governance proposal that does not enforce flash-loan-resistance, voting-power timelock, or quorum sufficiency is a standing T9.003 surface from the day the governance contract is deployed.

Sub-patterns observed in the wild:

- **Same-block flash-borrow vote** — flash-loan funds a vote on a single proposal that has no snapshot or timelock (Beanstalk).
- **Malicious proposal with hidden delegatecall** — proposal calldata is published, but a `delegatecall` deep in the execution payload silently grants control of governance state to the attacker (Tornado Cash governance May 2023).
- **Vote-buying / delegate-takeover** — attacker accumulates spot voting power by purchasing or borrowing the governance token via DEX or lending market, then submits a treasury-affecting proposal (Compound proposal-289 cohort).

## Observed indicators

- Sudden acquisition of governance-token voting power by a single address or tightly-related cluster, often funded by a flash loan.
- Voting-power-vs-circulating-supply ratio at vote-submission exceeds typical participation ratio (e.g., a single voter holds >30% of voted weight on a proposal whose median voter holds <1%).
- Submission and rapid execution of a governance proposal without the typical multi-day discussion / voting-period cadence.
- Use of "emergency" or expedited-execution functions in the governance contract that bypass normal proposal lifecycle (`emergencyCommit`, expedited-quorum branches).
- Treasury / privileged-action calls in the executed proposal payload directing funds to attacker-controlled addresses.
- Proposal calldata containing `delegatecall` to an address that has not been a governance target in the protocol's history.

## Detection signals

- **Real-time governance-quorum monitor:** monitor governance-token transfer events for sudden large acquisitions correlated with active proposals; alert on flash-loan-funded acquisitions specifically; cross-reference voted weight against a snapshotted distribution from N blocks before proposal creation.
- **Proposal-payload static analysis (OAK-M02):** before any governance proposal reaches its execution window, decode and statically analyse the calldata; alert on payloads that touch treasury, mint privileged roles, or perform `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets.
- **Pre-deployment governance-design review:** governance-contract audit for emergency-execution paths, quorum-without-timelock voting, and voting power computed from spot balances rather than from time-averaged or snapshotted balances.
- **Per-proposal simulation:** replay-style simulation of any proposal whose execution payload touches treasury or privileged-action surfaces; for cross-chain governance, replay against each target chain's state.

## Real-world examples

- **Beanstalk Farms — Ethereum — 2022-04-17** — Loss \~\$182M. Canonical T9.003 case. The attacker borrowed approximately \$1B via Aave flash loans, used the borrowed stablecoins to acquire approximately 67% of STALK voting power, and used the protocol's `emergencyCommit` function to bypass the normal proposal lifecycle and immediately execute a malicious BIP-18 that drained treasury funds to attacker-controlled addresses. The proceeds were laundered through Tornado Cash. See worked example at `examples/2022-04-beanstalk.md`. The case is the textbook T9.002 (flash loan) + T9.003 (governance attack) chain.
- **Tornado Cash governance — Ethereum — 2023-05-20** — Loss \~\$1.1M (TORN token + governance-control implications outsized relative to dollar loss). The attacker submitted a benign-looking proposal (Proposal 20) but the deployed implementation contained a `selfdestruct` that re-initialised state with attacker-controlled logic on execution; once executed, the attacker held effective unilateral control of the governance contract and minted TORN to themselves. The case is the canonical "malicious proposal with hidden delegatecall / state-replacement" sub-pattern; the attacker subsequently restored the prior implementation via a follow-up proposal, complicating attribution. See worked example at `examples/2023-05-tornado-cash-governance.md`. Sources: `[tornadocomm2023]`, `[peckshieldtornado2023]`, `[blocksectornadogov2023]`, `[slowmisttornadogov2023]`.
- **Compound — Ethereum — 2024-07-29 (Proposal 289)** — Loss: contested; the proposal would have transferred ~5% of Compound's COMP treasury (~\$24M) to a multisig controlled by a delegate consortium ("Golden Boys"). The proposal passed by a narrow margin via accumulated delegated voting power; community pushback led to a follow-up proposal that effectively reverted the transfer. The case demonstrates the **non-flash-loan T9.003 sub-pattern**: spot vote-accumulation by a delegate consortium without governance-timelock-resistance against well-organised whale coordination. Sources: `[compoundforumproposal289_2024]`, `[compoundproposal289_2024]`, `[goldenboyscompound2024]`, `[tallygovernancecompound2024]`.
- **Cohort context** — `[zhou2023sok]` documents governance-attack incidents as a recurring class through 2018-2022; smaller-scale governance-snapshot manipulation events through 2023-2025 are catalogued in industry retrospectives. MakerDAO and Gnosis-related governance-acquisition attempts surfaced in community forum discussions through 2022-2023 but did not result in completed exploits at the scale of Beanstalk; OAK does not treat them as canonical incidents but flags them as evidence the surface is actively probed. <!-- TODO: cite URL — MakerDAO governance attempt 2022 forum discussion -->

## Mitigations

- **Protocols (OAK-M17):** require flash-loan-resistant voting-power computation (snapshot taken at proposal-creation time, multi-block window); enforce minimum delay between proposal submission and execution (timelock); quorum + timelock; remove emergency-execution paths that bypass the normal lifecycle.
- **Protocols (OAK-M02):** static analysis of proposal calldata before execution-window opens; explicit denylist of `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets in proposal payloads.
- **Protocols (OAK-M16):** pre-deployment audit + formal verification of governance contract; specific attention to upgrade paths and emergency branches.
- **Risk teams:** for governance-token holdings, monitor proposal queues for any proposal whose execution payload affects treasury; maintain per-protocol governance-design inventories.

## Discussion

A defensive design observation: many post-Beanstalk governance contracts adopted snapshot-based voting power (e.g., Compound Governor Bravo's checkpoint pattern) and minimum proposal delays as standard. As of 2024-2025, large-scale flash-loan-T9.003 incidents are rarer at the top tier of DeFi protocols precisely because of this design shift; smaller-protocol governance attacks remain common and are typically not published forensically.

**Sub-class structure (deferred).** The three canonical examples partition cleanly:

- T9.003.001 — Flash-loan vote acquisition (Beanstalk) — defended by snapshot-and-timelock.
- T9.003.002 — Malicious proposal with hidden execution-payload semantics (Tornado Cash 2023) — defended by proposal-calldata static analysis (OAK-M02) and execution-target allowlists.
- T9.003.003 — Spot-vote delegate-coordination (Compound proposal 289) — *not* a security flaw in the technical sense; mitigation belongs at the governance-process layer, not the contract layer. Including it under T9.003 acknowledges that "governance attack" has a social dimension that contract-level mitigations alone do not address.

Attribution-strength notes: Beanstalk (high — fully on-chain, attacker known to forensic vendors); Tornado Cash 2023 (medium — restoration proposal complicates intent attribution); Compound 289 (high but contested — participants self-identified, but framing as "attack" vs "legitimate-but-contested governance" is community-dependent). v0.x deferred work: a structured rubric for distinguishing technical-T9.003 from social-T9.003 cases.
