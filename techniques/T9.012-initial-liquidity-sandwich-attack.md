# OAK-T9.012 — Initial Liquidity Sandwich Attack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (primary); any chain with a public mempool and AMM-based token-deployment pattern (Uniswap V2/V3, PancakeSwap, Raydium-equivalent AMMs)
**First documented:** 2020–2021 (the "sniping" pattern emerged with the Uniswap V2 token-deployment wave; the sandwich-at-addLiquidity variant is a refinement characterised by MEV researchers)
**Aliases:** "token-launch sandwich", "addLiquidity snipe", "initial-liquidity front-run", "token-genesis sandwich", "liquidity-addition MEV"

## Description

A MEV-driven attack in which the attacker sandwiches a token deployer's `addLiquidity` transaction — the transaction that establishes the initial liquidity pool for a newly deployed token. The attacker observes the `addLiquidity` transaction in the mempool, front-runs it with a token-purchase transaction that acquires the token at the pre-liquidity zero-or-near-zero-reserve price (the pool does not yet exist, so the purchase occurs against the first block's available liquidity, typically at an extreme price), and back-runs the `addLiquidity` with a token-sale transaction that dumps the acquired tokens at the post-liquidity pool price — inflated because the deployer's `addLiquidity` has just seeded the pool with the pair's quote asset. The attacker extracts the price differential between pre-liquidity and post-liquidity, profiting at the expense of the deployer (who receives fewer LP tokens for the same deposited quote-asset amount, because the pool's reserves were distorted by the front-run buy) and in some cases early buyers who purchase at the inflated post-sandwich price.

T9.012 is structurally distinct from T5.004 (Sandwich MEV Extraction): T5.004 sandwiches a regular user's swap transaction in an established pool, extracting the bid-ask spread through front-run buy + back-run sell. T9.012 sandwiches the **liquidity addition itself** — the defining act of pool creation — and the attacker's profit mechanism exploits the structural price discontinuity between pre-liquidity and post-liquidity state, not the spread of a single trade. T9.012 is distinct from T17.001 (Cross-Venue Arbitrage Price Distortion): T9.012 operates within a single venue (the newly created pool) and exploits the temporal sequencing of liquidity addition, not the price discrepancy between simultaneous venues.

The attack exploits three structural properties of AMM-based token deployment: (a) the `addLiquidity` transaction is broadcast to a public mempool and its execution is predictable (the pool address and token address are computable from the deployer's address and nonce); (b) the first trade after pool creation occurs at a price determined by the constant-product formula with the initial reserves, which may differ dramatically from the post-liquidity equilibrium; (c) the attacker's front-run transaction can be bundled with the back-run via MEV-Boost / Flashbots-style relays, making the sandwich atomic and eliminating inventory risk.

## Observed indicators

- A token-deployment transaction followed in the next block (or same block, same bundle) by a liquidity-addition transaction, where a third-party address submits a buy transaction that lands immediately before the `addLiquidity` and a sell transaction that lands immediately after — the classic sandwich signature at the block-builder layer.
- The attacker's buy transaction acquires tokens at an extreme price (high slippage) relative to the post-addLiquidity pool price — the profit basis is the deployer's quote-asset deposit that inflated the pool reserves.
- The attacker's address has no prior history with the token — the attacker is not a project insider, community member, or legitimate early participant; the address is a MEV bot or sniper address with a transaction history dominated by sandwich bundles.
- The deployer's LP tokens represent a smaller share of the pool than expected given the deposited quote-asset amount — the front-run purchase diluted the deployer's LP share.
- Post-sandwich, the attacker's sell transaction exits the position entirely within the same block (or a small number of subsequent blocks), realising the full profit without ongoing exposure to the token.

## Detection signals

- Pre-deployment (deployer-side): use Flashbots Protect / MEV-Share or equivalent private transaction relay for the `addLiquidity` transaction, preventing mempool observation. Alternatively, deploy and add liquidity atomically within a single transaction (constructor + addLiquidity via factory or deployer contract) so that no external observer can interleave.
- Block-level monitoring: alert on bundles where a buy transaction for a newly deployed token lands immediately before the token's first `addLiquidity` (Mint/Burn LP event) and a sell transaction lands immediately after, with all three attributed to different addresses and the buy-sell pair attributed to the same MEV bot.
- Pool-state analysis: for each newly created pool, compare the deployer's LP-token share to the expected share given the deposited quote-asset amount and the pool's total reserves. An LP share materially below the expected share is a T9.012 indicator.
- Attacker-address profiling: maintain a watchlist of known sniper/MEV addresses; alert on any of these addresses interacting with a newly deployed token's pool in the first N blocks post-creation.
- Token-launch-platform analysis: platforms that facilitate token launches (Uniswap, PancakeSwap, pump.fun, Four.meme) should monitor the first N blocks post-pool-creation for sandwich patterns; a token launch whose initial pool was sandwiched signals to downstream buyers that the early-liquidity price was distorted.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2020-2025-initial-liquidity-sandwich-cohort.md`](../examples/2020-2025-initial-liquidity-sandwich-cohort.md) — Initial liquidity sandwich MEV cohort — 2020–2025
- **Uniswap V2 token-launch sandwiching — Ethereum — 2020–2025 (ongoing).** The pattern has been operational since the Uniswap V2 token-deployment wave of 2020. MEV searchers operating sandwich bots (e.g., jaredfromsubway.eth and equivalents on Ethereum mainnet) routinely monitor the mempool for `addLiquidity` transactions on newly created pools and execute the sandwich: front-run buy at pre-liquidity price, back-run sell at post-liquidity price. The pattern is so prevalent that token deployers are routinely advised to use private transaction relays for the initial liquidity addition. No single headline-grabbing loss figure is typically reported because the individual sandwich extraction is modest per-launch (tens to low hundreds of ETH-equivalent) but the aggregate extraction across thousands of token launches is material.
- **PancakeSwap token-launch MEV — BSC — 2021–2025.** The BSC mempool is transparent and block times are short, making initial-liquidity sandwich attacks economically attractive. Multiple MEV bots specialised in PancakeSwap new-pool sandwiches have been observed; the pattern mirrors the Uniswap equivalent but in the BSC fee-and-gas environment.
- **MEV research literature — 2021–2023.** Academic and industry papers characterising MEV extraction at the token-launch layer document the addLiquidity-sandwich as a distinct sub-class of MEV, structurally separate from trade-sandwich (T5.004) and liquidation-MEV (T17.002). See `[daian2019flashboys]` for the foundational MEV characterisation and `[zhou2023sok]` for the DeFi attack taxonomy that includes the AMM-deployment-layer surface.

## Mitigations

- **Deployers (OAK-M09 — private transaction relay for initial liquidity):** submit the `addLiquidity` transaction via a private relay (Flashbots Protect, MEV-Share, Eden Network, or equivalent) to prevent mempool observation. Alternatively, deploy the token and add liquidity atomically within a single constructor transaction.
- **Deployers (OAK-M02 — pool-creation-time price bounds):** if the protocol exposes a token-launch factory, enforce a price-bound on the first trade after pool creation — e.g., revert if the first trade's execution price exceeds a configurable multiple of the intended intial-liquidity price. This limits the sandwich profit.
- **Deployers (OAK-M11 — post-launch trade delay):** for token launches where the deployer controls the token contract, implement a post-launch block-delay during which only the deployer can trade — the pool's initial N blocks are gated, preventing third-party sandwiches until the pool has reached a non-extreme price.
- **Block builders and relays:** increase transparency of bundle-level sandwich detection for new-pool launches; downstream analytics can consume bundle-level data to flag sandwiched launches for user-facing warnings.
- **Risk teams:** monitor new-pool-launch sandwich rates per chain, per platform, and per time window; treat tokens whose initial pool launch was sandwiched as elevated T9.012 risk for downstream buyers, independent of the token's contract audit posture.

## Discussion

T9.012 sits at the intersection of MEV extraction (T5/T17 classes) and smart-contract exploit (T9 classes) because the attack exploits the **deployment-process timing** rather than a code bug in the deployed contract. The deployed token contract and AMM pool contract are, typically, bug-free — the vulnerability is the mempool-visibility of the `addLiquidity` transaction and the atomic-execution bundling that makes the sandwich risk-free for the attacker. This is why T9.012's mitigation lives at the transaction-relay layer (private mempool) and the deployment-process layer (atomic deploy + addLiquidity) rather than at the contract-code layer.

A structural observation: T9.012 is most profitable when the initial liquidity addition is large relative to the attacker's front-run capital, because the post-liquidity pool reserves are inflated by the deployer's quote-asset deposit. A deployer who seeds the pool with a large quote-asset amount inadvertently funds the attacker's profit — the attacker's back-run sell extracts value from the deployer's quote-asset deposit. This creates an unfortunate incentive alignment where cautious deployers who start with small initial liquidity are less profitable sandwich targets, while ambitious deployers who seed with large liquidity are more profitable — a dynamic that penalises well-capitalised legitimate launches.

False-positive considerations: not every first-block trade that lands around a pool-creation event is a sandwich attack. Legitimate bots that provide liquidity or arbitrage across venues may trade in the first blocks after pool creation without sandwich intent. The distinguishing signal is the **bundle-level ordering**: the attacker's buy and sell straddle the deployer's `addLiquidity`, and the attacker's profit is realised entirely from the price differential across that straddle. A legitimate arbitrageur who trades post-addLiquidity against a different venue's price does not front-run the addLiquidity itself.

T9.012 also composes with T4.003 (Address Poisoning) and T6.006 (Counterfeit Token Impersonation): an attacker deploying a counterfeit token with a symbol and name matching a legitimate project's anticipated token can use T9.012 to sandwich the counterfeit's own `addLiquidity`, extracting from buyers who mistake the counterfeit for the legitimate token. The sandwich extracts from the buyer's mistaken purchase at the inflated post-addLiquidity price, while the attacker front-ran their own pool creation. This is a compound T6.006 (counterfeit) + T9.012 (sandwich) attack that exploits both the brand-impersonation and the MEV-sandwich surfaces.

v0.x deferred items: a structured measurement of initial-liquidity-sandwich prevalence across major AMMs (Uniswap V2/V3, PancakeSwap, Raydium, Orca) and chains (Ethereum, BSC, Solana) for a representative time window; a comparison of public-mempool vs. private-relay initial-liquidity deployments to quantify the mitigation effectiveness of private transaction relays; integration of bundle-level sandwich detection into token-launch platforms (pump.fun, Four.meme, Uniswap) as a user-facing warning at the token-page layer.

Attribution-strength considerations: T9.012 attribution is at the `pseudonymous-attacker` level for operational sandwiches — the attacker's MEV-bot address is on-chain attributable, and the sandwich bundle is identifiable at the block-builder layer. The attacker is typically a MEV searcher operating through a known bot address; the bot address may be attributed to a pseudonymous entity but the underlying human identity requires off-chain intelligence. The deployer (victim) is attributable at the deployer-address layer. The attribution is stronger than many T9 Techniques because the sandwich bundle is a self-contained three-transaction artefact (front-run buy, addLiquidity, back-run sell) that is straightforward to detect and attribute at the bundle level.
