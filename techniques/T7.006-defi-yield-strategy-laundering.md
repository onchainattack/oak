# OAK-T7.006 — DeFi Yield-Strategy Laundering

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T7 (Laundering)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Solana, cross-chain (within-chain laundering rail per chain)
**First documented:** systematic from approximately 2021 onward (DeFi-summer-and-after) as DeFi liquidity-and-yield surface grew large enough to absorb laundering flows; cohort-scale industry attention 2023+
**Aliases:** "yield-farm laundering", "LP-cover laundering", "staking-derivative laundering", "yield-user-persona laundering"

## Description

A class of laundering flows in which proceeds are deposited into legitimate DeFi yield-farming, liquidity-provision, lending, or liquid-staking protocols and withdrawn after a delay — using the "yield user" / "liquidity provider" persona as the cover story. The protocol itself is not designed to obscure flows; on the contrary, deposits and withdrawals are publicly observable and per-position-attributable on the chain. The laundering effect comes from the *crowd* the launderer joins: legitimate yield-seeking users produce a high-volume baseline of deposit-then-withdraw flows, and a laundering deposit-then-withdraw event is statistically indistinguishable from a yield-user event unless the defender resolves the per-position behavioural pattern. The defender's analytical question is whether a given deposit-and-withdraw trajectory matches a real yield-seeking user — long deposit duration, claim of accrued yield, rebalancing across pools or strategies, sensitivity to APY changes — or a laundering pattern — deposit immediately followed by withdrawal at the lockup minimum, no yield-claim, no rebalancing, no APY-sensitivity, withdrawal-recipient differs from deposit-source where the protocol allows it.

T7.006 is structurally distinct from T7.001 (Mixer-Routed Hop): T7.001 uses a service whose explicit purpose is obfuscation, so the mere fact of interaction with the service is itself a defender signal; T7.006 uses protocols whose explicit purpose is yield, so interaction with the protocol carries no inherent illicit-signal weight and the per-position behavioural pattern is the only defender surface. T7.006 is also distinct from T7.003 (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering): T7.003 breaks the single-chain trail by jumping across chains; T7.006 stays within a single chain and obfuscates *temporally and persona-wise* through legitimate-yield-infrastructure cover. The two are frequently chained — T7.006 within chain A as a holding-and-cover stage, then T7.003 to chain B for the off-ramp leg — but each rail addresses a different defender surface.

Sub-patterns observed in practice:

- **Deposit-and-withdraw via different recipient address.** Where the protocol allows the depositor to specify a non-self withdrawal recipient (some lending markets, some LST contracts, some vault standards), the deposit-side cluster and withdrawal-side cluster differ by construction; the protocol acts as a same-asset-class bridge between two operator-controlled addresses without requiring a cross-chain hop.
- **Multi-protocol rotation.** Funds rotate through multiple yield protocols in sequence (e.g., lending market → LP pool → vault aggregator → staking derivative), each step adding a fresh per-position attribution surface and fragmenting the on-chain trail across protocols whose forensic-provider coverage and compliance posture vary widely.
- **Liquid-staking-derivative conversion.** Source asset is converted to a derivative token (stETH, jitoSOL, mSOL, etc.) held for a delay then redeemed; the derivative-token leg sits in a deeply liquid market whose on-chain footprint is dominated by legitimate stakers, providing strong cover-crowd density.
- **Single-sided LP-pool laundering.** Single-sided deposit into an LP pool followed by asymmetric withdrawal of a different pool-asset composition; the pool itself laundromats the asset-mix because the launderer's withdrawal need not match the deposit-side asset proportion.

## Observed indicators

- **Per-position duration distribution:** the per-position deposit-to-withdrawal duration for a flagged source-cluster sits at the lockup minimum or just above it, in contrast with the legitimate-user duration distribution which has a long tail (weeks-to-months for the dominant yield cohort).
- **No yield-claim event:** the position is withdrawn without ever calling the protocol's yield-claim / harvest entrypoint, despite the protocol exposing one. Legitimate yield-seeking users claim or compound; a laundering position ignores accrued yield because the carrying cost is not the laundering operator's optimization target.
- **No rebalancing / no APY-sensitivity:** the position does not move when APYs across competing protocols shift; legitimate yield-seekers (especially the high-volume vault-aggregator cohort) rebalance.
- **Deposit-source and withdrawal-recipient mismatch:** where the protocol permits, the withdrawal recipient differs from the deposit source — a structural T7.006 indicator that has no good legitimate-user explanation in most yield-protocol contexts.
- **Aggregate cluster-side flow shape:** for a deposit-cluster attributed to a known illicit source, the *aggregate* inflow into a given yield protocol over a window matches in amount-class and timing the prior extraction event — consistent with the protocol being used as a holding-and-cover venue rather than an end-state custody venue.

## Detection signals

- **Per-deposit duration analysis:** for each deposit attributed to a watchlisted source-cluster, compute the deposit-to-withdrawal duration and compare against the per-protocol legitimate-user duration distribution; alert on duration sitting at the lockup minimum with no yield-claim event in between.
- **Per-deposit-cluster aggregate flow analysis:** for each source-cluster attributed to a known illicit watchlist, compute the aggregate deposit-then-withdrawal flow into and out of each yield protocol over rolling windows; alert on patterns where aggregate-in matches aggregate-out at high-correlation within a short delay envelope and where the withdrawal-recipient cluster differs from the deposit-source cluster.
- **Yield-claim-rate signal:** per-protocol per-source-cluster ratio of yield-claim events to deposit events, normalised against the legitimate-user baseline; a near-zero claim rate against an active deposit volume is a structural T7.006 signal.
- **Withdrawal-recipient-divergence signal:** per-protocol fraction of positions where the withdrawal recipient differs from the deposit source, segmented by source-cluster risk score; an elevated divergence rate against a watchlisted source-cluster is a structural signal.
- **Cross-protocol rotation graph:** treat the yield-protocol surface as a graph with protocols as nodes and same-cluster sequential deposit-then-withdraw flows as edges; alert on watchlisted-source-cluster trajectories that traverse N≥3 yield protocols within a short window without yield-claim events.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T7.006 is per-position behavioural-pattern + per-cluster aggregate-flow forensic layer; the per-protocol legitimate-user duration distributions and yield-claim-rate baselines required for clean detection are out of MG scope).
- Vendor coverage: Chainalysis Reactor (per-cluster DeFi-protocol attribution), TRM Forensics (per-cluster DeFi-flow tracing), Elliptic Investigator (per-cluster DeFi-laundering-pattern attribution); cross-chain forensic providers are the canonical T7.006 detection access surface.
- Protocol-side compliance: per-protocol compliance integrations (deposit-time screening against illicit-cluster watchlists, withdrawal-recipient screening where permitted by the protocol's design, T7.006-pattern-aware position-level monitoring) are sparse at v0.1 and represent the highest-leverage future mitigation surface.

## Mitigations

- **DeFi protocol-side compliance (highest-leverage):** integrate compliance-provider screening at deposit time and at withdrawal-recipient-specification time; expose per-protocol illicit-cluster inflow and outflow metrics on a recurring cadence; treat positions with deposit-source / withdrawal-recipient divergence and zero-yield-claim trajectories as elevated-risk positions where the protocol governance permits action.
- **Travel-Rule extension to DeFi where regulatorily applicable:** in jurisdictions where DeFi protocols are within Travel-Rule scope (the regulatory boundary varies materially by jurisdiction at v0.1), Travel-Rule-style cross-venue-message infrastructure narrows the T7.006 off-ramp surface at the post-yield-rail stage; OAK documents the mitigation surface without taking a position on the regulatory boundary.
- **Per-deposit source-of-funds attribution at major venues:** downstream venues (CEXes, fiat off-ramps) that receive post-T7.006 outflows are the canonical attribution surface; per-deposit source-of-funds attribution at these venues, supported by forensic-provider cross-protocol attribution graphs, is where the T7.006 trail is typically closed in practice.
- **Risk teams (organisational):** treat post-incident-time-window inflows into yield-protocol deposit addresses as elevated risk, particularly where the inflow source-cluster is watchlisted; maintain per-protocol per-cluster monitoring across the deposit, hold, and withdraw legs rather than monitoring only the deposit edge.

## Discussion

T7.006 is one of the harder T7 sub-techniques to detect cleanly because legitimate yield-seeking-user behaviour overlaps heavily with the laundering-pattern surface at the per-deposit edge. A short-duration deposit with no yield-claim is consistent with both a laundering operation and a legitimate user who decided not to keep the position; a withdrawal-recipient-differs-from-deposit-source flow is consistent with both a laundering operation and a legitimate user moving to a fresh address for non-laundering reasons (treasury operation, multisig migration, custody change). The defender signal is consequently structural-and-aggregate (per-cluster, per-protocol, per-window flow shape) rather than per-event; per-event T7.006 alerts have a much higher false-positive rate than the equivalent T7.001 or T7.003 alert, and OAK contributors writing future T7.006 examples should preserve this asymmetry rather than overclaim per-event attribution.

A separate live observation: T7.006 grows in importance proportionally to the size of the legitimate yield-and-LP cover crowd. The technique is more effective on protocols and chains where the legitimate yield-user baseline is large (mature lending markets, deep LST markets, large LP pools); it is correspondingly weaker on protocols where the legitimate user-base is small enough that any specific position stands out. This shapes operator behaviour toward the deepest-liquidity protocols on each chain — which is also where the protocol-side-compliance-integration mitigation has the highest dollar-loss-prevented leverage. Contributors writing future T7.006 examples should preserve the cover-crowd-density framing; the technique is not a property of an individual protocol design but of the liquidity-baseline against which the laundering position is hidden.
