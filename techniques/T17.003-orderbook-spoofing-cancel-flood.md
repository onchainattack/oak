# OAK-T17.003 — Spoofing / Cancel-Flood Order-Book Manipulation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T17 (Market Manipulation)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T3.002 (Wash-Trade Volume Inflation — T3.002 produces fake *executed* volume; T17.003 produces fake *resting-order* state without execution), OAK-T5.004 (Sandwich / MEV Extraction — adjacent class on sequencer / mempool order-flow but operating on executed transactions rather than on resting limit orders), OAK-T8.001 (Operational Reuse — Cluster-Reuse Signal — load-bearing for spoof-and-cancel cohort attribution)
**Maturity:** draft
**Chains:** EVM-L2 (dYdX / GMX V2 perps / Aevo); Hyperliquid HyperEVM; Solana (Drift, Phoenix, Mango v4); cross-chain order-book-DEX designs broadly inherit the surface
**First documented:** Class anchored conceptually 2010-2015 (CFTC enforcement against Sarao 2015 in equity-futures CME context); crypto-DEX-specific class anchored 2022-2024 (operational deployment of order-book DEXes); no canonical extraction-scale OAK anchor at v0.4
**Aliases:** "spoofing", "cancel-flood", "layering", "phantom liquidity", "spoof-and-cancel", "DEX order-book manipulation", "perp spoofing"

## Description

Order-book manipulation in which the attacker posts large limit orders on a DEX limit-order book or perpetuals order book without intent to fill, then cancels the orders in the seconds before they would otherwise execute. The defining shape is that the **resting-order state itself is the manipulation primitive** — other market participants observe the order book and adjust their own order placement / market-taking decisions on the assumption that the spoof orders represent real liquidity, but the spoof orders are cancelled before they fill. The post-event executed-trade record does not contain the spoof-and-cancel events; the spoof signal lives in the per-order event stream.

T17.003 is **structurally distinct from T3.002 (Wash-Trade Volume Inflation)** in *what gets faked*. T3.002 produces fake *executed* volume — the wash-trader executes circular trades against themselves to inflate the venue's reported trading volume. T17.003 produces fake *resting-order* state — no executions occur, but the order book misleads observers about the depth and direction of liquidity. The mitigation surface differs: T3.002 is closed by venue-side wash-trade detection (cohort-attribution on counterparties of executed trades) and by external volume-attestation (CMC / CoinGecko methodology hardening); T17.003 is closed by venue-side spoof-detection (per-order lifecycle analysis of post-and-cancel patterns) and by order-book-quality reporting standards.

T17.003 is **structurally distinct from T5.004 (Sandwich / MEV Extraction)** in *the surface manipulated*. T5.004 manipulates the sequence of executed transactions in the mempool — front-run and back-run a victim swap to capture slippage. T17.003 manipulates the resting-order book — display fake liquidity to mislead other market participants' order placement / market-taking. T5.004 produces an executed-trade record that can be forensically reconstructed from the on-chain transaction sequence; T17.003 leaves the executed-trade record clean and the manipulation lives in the per-order-event stream that is preserved at the venue layer but rarely exposed at the on-chain layer for non-on-chain order-books.

T17.003 covers four structurally adjacent sub-shapes:

- **Layering.** Multiple spoof orders posted at different price levels to display a false depth structure. Other participants observe the layered book and adjust their own market-taking / market-making decisions; the spoof orders are cancelled before the price reaches them.
- **Cancel-flood / quote stuffing.** High-frequency posting and cancellation of orders to overwhelm other participants' market-data ingestion or to obscure the true order-book state during a manipulation window.
- **One-sided spoof.** Single-side spoof (e.g., large bid posted to push price up, then cancelled before fill while attacker sells from a different account) — the attacker's economically-real trade is on the opposite side of the spoof.
- **Cross-venue spoof.** Spoof orders posted on one venue to influence price discovery that the attacker captures on a different venue (boundary case against T17.001 cross-venue arbitrage; the distinction is whether the manipulation primitive is the spoof order book on venue A — T17.003 — or the cross-venue spread itself — T17.001).

## Why this Technique is `draft` at v0.4

T17.003 is introduced as `draft` rather than `emerging` because OAK does not have a clean dollar-loss anchor for the class against a crypto venue at v0.4. The traditional-finance precedent record at the equity-futures order-book layer is well-anchored — CFTC enforcement against Navinder Sarao (2015 conviction; CME futures spoofing cited in the 2010 flash-crash investigation) is the canonical anchor for the class outside crypto, but the dollar-loss attribution is at the market-microstructure / contributing-factor layer rather than at a discrete-extraction-event layer that maps cleanly onto the OAK worked-example template.

Specifically, the v0.4 documentation gap is:

- **Order-book DEXes are operationally young.** dYdX V3 / V4, Hyperliquid, GMX V2 perps, Drift, Phoenix, Mango V4 are 2022-2024-deployed; the operational record of named-attacker spoof-and-cancel cases against these venues at extraction-scale has not yet matured.
- **Spoof-detection is at the venue layer, not the on-chain layer.** Most order-book DEXes operate the order-book at the venue / sequencer layer (off-chain order matching with on-chain settlement) rather than as on-chain limit-order book primitives; the per-order lifecycle data that would constitute the load-bearing T17.003 forensic signal lives at the venue side and is rarely exposed publicly for the discrete-attacker case.
- **Per-incident named-attacker public-record gap.** Crypto-venue self-reports of spoof-detection enforcement (account bans, position liquidations against detected spoof-and-cancel cohorts) exist at the venue-side compliance layer but rarely produce public post-mortems at the dollar-loss-quantified layer.

Future contributors should anchor T17.003 once a public-record case lands at the dollar-loss scale comparable to T17.002 / T16.001-003 anchors. Promotion to `emerging` requires at least one canonical worked example with a named attacker (or pseudonymous-with-strong-forensic-attribution), a documented spoof-and-cancel pattern, and a quantified extraction outcome.

## Observed indicators

- **Per-order lifecycle:** large limit orders posted at the edge of the spread that are cancelled within milliseconds-to-seconds before they would otherwise fill; cancellation-rate ratio (orders cancelled / orders placed) on the cohort's account approaches 1.0 against a baseline of low cancellation rates for honest market-makers.
- **One-sided economic exposure:** the cohort's *executed* trades are systematically on the opposite side of the cohort's *posted* orders — large posted bids correlated with executed sells, large posted asks correlated with executed buys.
- **Cross-account correlation:** spoof orders posted from one account; economically-real trades executed from a different account whose funder-graph clusters with the spoof account.
- **Forensic record:** the per-order event stream containing the spoof orders, the cancellation events, the executed trades on the opposite side, and the cohort's net-position trajectory across the manipulation window.

## Detection signals

- **Per-order lifecycle analysis at venue layer:** the canonical detection signal lives at the venue / sequencer layer, where the per-order event stream (post / modify / cancel / fill events) preserves the spoof-and-cancel pattern. Order-book DEXes with mature compliance operations (dYdX, Hyperliquid) have venue-side spoof-detection, but public reporting on detected cases is typically aggregated at the cohort-statistics layer rather than the named-attacker layer.
- **Cohort-attribution applied to one-sided economic exposure:** funder-graph clustering applied to accounts whose posted-order direction is systematically opposite their executed-trade direction.
- **Cancellation-rate monitoring:** continuous monitoring of per-account cancellation rates; accounts with cancellation rates approaching 1.0 against a baseline of <0.7 for honest market-makers are spoof-suspect.
- **Order-book-quality reporting:** venue-level metrics on phantom-depth (declared depth at price levels that does not survive a multi-millisecond confirmation window) as a venue-quality signal that downstream consumers (analytics platforms, oracle designers, traders) can incorporate into venue selection.

## Mitigations

- **Venues — per-order lifecycle analysis at sequencer layer:** the canonical mitigation. Venues that operate per-order-lifecycle spoof-detection at the sequencer / matching-engine layer can ban accounts and slash positions of detected spoof-and-cancel cohorts; the operational gap is that compliance enforcement decisions are venue-internal and rarely surface as public deterrent signals.
- **Venues — minimum-resting-time / minimum-fill ratio:** order-book design rules that require orders to rest for a minimum duration or that cap the cancellation-rate ratio per account close the spoof-and-cancel surface by raising the cost of the spoof leg.
- **Venues — order-book-quality reporting:** venue-level metrics on phantom-depth as a transparency primitive that downstream consumers (analytics platforms, oracle designers, traders, index providers) can incorporate into venue selection. Canonical mitigation against the venue-incentive misalignment where venues benefit from inflated apparent-depth signals.
- **Protocols — robust price-reference design:** protocols that consume order-book-DEX prices as oracle inputs should require multi-venue quorum and TWAP-window-randomisation to defeat single-venue-spoof-driven price-discovery distortion (mitigation surface overlaps with T17.001 and OAK-M09).
- **Regulators — crypto-venue spoofing enforcement record:** the equity-futures CFTC enforcement record against Sarao-class spoofing is the canonical deterrent precedent; the operational gap at v0.4 is that the analogous crypto-venue enforcement record at the named-attacker / extraction-quantified layer is sparse.

## Discussion

T17.003's structural lesson is that **the load-bearing forensic signal lives at the per-order event stream, not at the executed-trade record**. Order-book spoofing is invisible in the executed-trade record by construction — no executions occur on the spoof leg. Defenders evaluating T17.003 risk should treat the *per-order lifecycle data* as the load-bearing detection surface, and venue-side compliance operations are the load-bearing enforcement surface. Protocol-side or analytics-side mitigation against T17.003 is structurally weaker because the signal does not propagate to the layers where on-chain analytics operate.

The cohort is structurally distinct from T3.002 (wash-trade) by *what gets faked*: T3.002 fakes executed volume; T17.003 fakes resting-order state without execution. The cohort is structurally distinct from T5.004 (sandwich-MEV) by *the surface manipulated*: T5.004 manipulates executed transaction order; T17.003 manipulates the resting order book.

T17.003's `draft` status reflects the v0.4 documentation gap honestly. The class is real and well-anchored in traditional-finance enforcement record (CFTC / Sarao, CME futures), but the canonical crypto-venue extraction-scale case at the named-attacker / dollar-loss-quantified layer has not yet landed publicly. Order-book DEXes are operationally young and venue-side compliance enforcement decisions are typically venue-internal. Promotion to `emerging` requires at least one canonical worked example with documented per-order-lifecycle forensic signal and a quantified extraction outcome.

Attribution-strength considerations: traditional-finance T17.003 cases are typically `confirmed` (regulatory enforcement actions identify named attackers) — Sarao is the canonical anchor. Crypto-venue T17.003 cases are more often `pseudonymous-cohort` (venue-side compliance enforcement against accounts whose attribution is at the funder-graph-cluster layer) and rarely surface as public extraction-quantified named-attacker cases.

False-positive considerations: legitimate market-making activity involves high cancellation rates by construction — quotes are continuously updated as market conditions change. The detection signal is not "high cancellation rate" but specifically "high cancellation rate combined with one-sided economic exposure" — i.e., the cohort's *executed* trades are systematically opposite the cohort's *posted* order direction. Cohort-level analysis with funder-graph clustering across the spoof account and the economically-real trade account is required to distinguish T17.003 from honest market-making.

v0.x deferred items: a canonical crypto-venue worked example at extraction-scale (the v0.4 documentation gap that promotes T17.003 from `draft` to `emerging`); a structured rubric for distinguishing layering / cancel-flood / one-sided / cross-venue sub-shapes; cohort-level metrics on per-account cancellation-rate distribution across major order-book DEXes.
