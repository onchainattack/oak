# OAK-T14.005 — Builder Censorship MEV Extraction

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T14 (Validator / Staking / Restaking Attacks)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T14.002 (MEV-Boost Relay Attack — T14.002 covers relay-level manipulation of block content; T14.005 covers the builder-level censorship that precedes relay delivery), OAK-T5.004 (Sandwich MEV Extraction — T5.004 covers the per-transaction sandwich against a single trade; T14.005 covers the block-construction-layer censorship that enables sandwiches and other extraction at the builder level), OAK-T17.001 (Cross-Venue Arbitrage Price Distortion — T14.005 is the builder-side censorship that can prevent competing arbitrage from landing)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** Ethereum L1 (canonical, via PBS/MEV-Boost); any chain with a proposer-builder separation (PBS) architecture where block builders can censor transactions at the block-construction layer
**First documented:** 2020–2021 (the Flashbots / MEV-Boost ecosystem formalised PBS and builder-censorship as a surface; the class is characterised in MEV research literature from inception)
**Aliases:** "builder censorship", "PBS censorship", "block-construction censorship", "MEV-Boost builder exclusion", "transaction-suppression MEV", "builder-level sandwich infrastructure"

## Description

A class of MEV extraction attacks in which Ethereum block builders — the entities that construct execution-payload blocks under the Proposer-Builder Separation (PBS) architecture — deliberately exclude specific transactions from their block proposals to benefit their own transaction flows or allied searchers at the expense of competing builders, searchers, or users. The builder censors transactions by refusing to include them in blocks, even when those transactions pay a competitive priority fee and would otherwise be included by an honest builder. The exclusion may target: (a) competing builders' bundles (to prevent rival MEV extraction), (b) specific transaction flows (e.g., a competing searcher's sandwich or arbitrage bundle), (c) user transactions that interact with a protocol the builder (or an allied searcher) intends to exploit, leaving the mempool state unchanged for the builder's own extraction transaction in a subsequent block, or (d) transactions to OFAC-sanctioned addresses (regulatory-compliance censorship, structurally distinct from profit-driven censorship but operating through the same builder-layer infrastructure).

T14.005 is structurally distinct from T14.002 (MEV-Boost Relay Attack): T14.002 covers attacks where the relay — the intermediary between builder and proposer — manipulates block delivery (withholding blocks, unbundling, payload substitution). T14.005 covers attacks where the **builder itself** — the entity constructing the block — censors at the block-content-selection layer. The relay delivers what the builder provides; the builder decides what to include. T14.005 is distinct from T5.004 (Sandwich MEV — per-transaction): T5.004 is the per-trade sandwich pattern (front-run buy, victim trade, back-run sell); T14.005 is the **infrastructure layer** that enables sandwich extraction at scale by allowing the builder to censor competing transactions that would otherwise interfere with the sandwich. A builder who can censor is a builder who can execute sandwiches without competition, increasing the extraction-per-sandwich from the competitive spread to the monopoly spread.

The attack exploits the PBS architecture's concentration of block-construction power: a small number of builders (BeaverBuild, Titan, Rsync, Flashbots Builder) construct the majority of Ethereum blocks. When one builder controls the block-construction pipeline for a material fraction of slots, that builder can selectively censor transactions without meaningfully delaying their inclusion — the censored transaction must wait for an uncensored builder's slot, which may be several blocks away — while the builder's own transactions land in the current slot.

## Observed indicators

- A transaction that pays a competitive priority fee and is seen in the public mempool does not land in a block for N consecutive slots, where N exceeds the expected inclusion delay for the fee level — the transaction is being censored at the builder layer.
- A builder's block consistently contains the builder's own (or a specific allied searcher's) sandwich/arbitrage transactions but excludes similar transactions from competing searcher addresses — the builder is censoring rivals.
- Builder-level transaction-inclusion disparity: a specific builder's blocks contain transactions from searcher addresses A, B, C but never from addresses X, Y, Z that appear in other builders' blocks at comparable priority-fee levels.
- OFAC-compliance censorship: transactions interacting with OFAC-sanctioned addresses (e.g., Tornado Cash contract addresses) are systematically excluded from the blocks of certain builders but included in the blocks of others — the censorship is builder-specific and compliance-driven rather than profit-driven.
- Builder block-template analysis: the builder's block template (observable via `eth_getBlockByNumber` or MEV-Boost relay data) consistently places the builder's allied searcher's extraction transactions at the top of the block, ahead of competing transactions, regardless of relative priority fees.

## Detection signals

- Per-builder transaction-inclusion audit: for each builder, compute the set of searcher/entity addresses whose transactions appear in the builder's blocks; compare across builders; flag builders whose inclusion set materially deviates from the global top-N-searchers-included set — this is the structural T14.005 builder-censorship signal.
- Per-transaction inclusion-delay monitoring: for transactions observed in the public mempool, track the number of slots between first observation and inclusion; alert on transactions that remain un-included beyond a configurable slot-count threshold while paying competitive priority fees — these are censorship candidates.
- Builder-concentration monitoring: track the fraction of blocks constructed by the top-1, top-3, and top-5 builders; a builder above a configurable concentration threshold has the *capacity* to censor at economically meaningful scale regardless of whether censorship is currently observed.
- Builder-allied-searcher correlation analysis: for each builder, identify the searcher addresses that appear exclusively (or disproportionately) in that builder's blocks; alert on builders whose exclusive searcher set accounts for a material fraction of the builder's extracted MEV — this is the builder-allied-searcher T14.005 signal.
- OFAC-compliance censorship monitoring: track the inclusion rate of transactions that interact with OFAC-sanctioned addresses; alert on builders whose inclusion rate is persistently zero while other builders include such transactions — this is the regulatory-censorship T14.005 signal.

## Real-world examples

- **OFAC-compliance builder censorship — Ethereum — 2022–2025 (ongoing).** Following OFAC's August 2022 addition of Tornado Cash to the SDN list, several major Ethereum block builders (including Flashbots Builder, which was the dominant builder at the time) began censoring transactions that interact with Tornado Cash contract addresses, in compliance with U.S. sanctions regulations. Builders that do not censor (e.g., BeaverBuild, Titan, Rsync) continued to include such transactions. The result was a bifurcated builder landscape where censored transactions experienced longer inclusion delays (waiting for a non-censoring builder's slot) but were not permanently excluded. The OFAC-censorship pattern is the most extensively documented and quantitatively measured instance of builder-level censorship — it is structurally T14.005 even though the motivation is regulatory compliance rather than MEV extraction. See `[wahrstatter2023censorship]` for the empirical measurement of inclusion delays under OFAC-compliance builder censorship.
- **Builder-allied searcher exclusivity — Ethereum — 2022–2025 (ongoing).** MEV researchers have documented that specific builders have exclusive (or near-exclusive) relationships with specific searchers: the searcher's bundles are submitted privately to the builder (not through the public MEV-Boost relay auction), and the builder's blocks include that searcher's transactions ahead of competing searchers. The builder benefits from the private order flow (which may include higher-value MEV opportunities than the public auction); the searcher benefits from censorship protection against competing searchers. This is the builder-allied-searcher T14.005 sub-pattern. While no single headline-grabbing incident names a specific builder-searcher pair as "the attacker," the pattern is structurally T14.005 and is documented in MEV research literature (see eigenphi.substack.com for empirical builder-searcher correlation analyses).
- **MEV research literature — 2021–2025.** The PBS censorship surface is extensively characterised in MEV research. Daian et al. (2019, Flash Boys 2.0) established the foundational MEV framing. The Flashbots / MEV-Boost ecosystem documentation explicitly addresses builder censorship as a design consideration, and the MEV-Boost relay specification includes mechanisms (e.g., `eth_sendPrivateTransaction` and bundle-auction transparency) that partially mitigate the surface. See `[daian2019flashboys]` and follow-up MEV-Boost ecosystem documentation.

## Mitigations

- **Protocol layer (inclusion lists — OAK-M09):** Ethereum protocol-level inclusion lists require the proposer to specify a list of transactions that must be included in the block, constraining the builder's ability to censor. Inclusion lists are an active EIP (EIP-7547) as of 2025 and represent the highest-leverage structural mitigation for T14.005.
- **Protocol layer (encrypted mempools):** encrypted mempool designs prevent the builder from observing transaction content before inclusion, eliminating the builder's ability to censor based on content (as opposed to based on submitter address). This is an active research area and partially mitigates the profit-driven censorship arm of T14.005.
- **MEV-Boost relay transparency (builder-level audit):** relays should expose per-builder transaction-inclusion statistics, searcher-correlation metrics, and censorship-pattern data, enabling the community to identify censoring builders and proposers to route blocks to non-censoring builders.
- **Proposer-side builder selection:** proposers (validators) can select builders based on published censorship-resistance metrics; a proposer who routes block construction to a non-censoring builder directly reduces the censored transactions' inclusion delay. This is a proposer-level, not protocol-level, mitigation and relies on proposers valuing censorship-resistance over the marginal MEV-reward difference.
- **Risk teams and protocol integrators:** treat protocol interactions whose transactions are likely to be censored by major builders (OFAC-adjacent interactions, competitive-MEV flows) as subject to T14.005 inclusion-delay risk; price the inclusion-delay into the user-facing UX (longer expected confirmation times, higher required priority fees for uncensored builders).

## Discussion

T14.005 is the builder-side counterpart to T14.002 (relay-side) in OAK's PBS attack taxonomy. The two surfaces are structurally adjacent: T14.002 attacks the relay layer (block delivery and bid integrity); T14.005 attacks the builder layer (block-content selection and transaction censorship). The unifying defender heuristic is: under PBS, a block's content is determined by three independently-acting parties — the builder (constructs content), the relay (delivers content), and the proposer (signs content) — and each party is a censorship and manipulation surface. T14.005 focuses on the builder because the builder is the party that decides what to include.

The class is marked emerging rather than stable because PBS architecture is still evolving: inclusion lists (EIP-7547), encrypted mempools, and proposer-side builder selection are active protocol-development items. As these mechanisms deploy, T14.005 will shift from "a structural property of the current PBS architecture" to "a residual surface under the mitigated PBS architecture." The v0.1 framing captures the current (2025) builder landscape and will need updating as Ethereum's PBS infrastructure evolves.

A structural observation: T14.005 is one of the few OAK Techniques where the "attacker" is not necessarily a malicious entity but an economically rational infrastructure operator (the builder) acting within the PBS protocol's incentive structure. The builder's censorship is not a bug — it is a profit-maximising response to the PBS architecture's builder-concentration dynamic. The mitigation is consequently architectural (inclusion lists, encrypted mempools, proposer-side builder selection) rather than punitive. Contributors writing future T14.005 entries should preserve the distinction between profit-driven censorship (builder excludes rival MEV) and compliance-driven censorship (builder excludes OFAC-sanctioned interactions) — the mitigation surfaces differ even though the builder-layer infrastructure is shared.

False-positive considerations: a builder that consistently excludes a specific transaction or searcher address is not automatically engaging in T14.005 censorship — the builder may have legitimate performance, fee, or bundle-validation reasons for exclusion. The distinguishing signal is that the excluded transaction meets the builder's stated inclusion criteria (pays a competitive priority fee, passes bundle simulation/validation) and appears in other builders' blocks at comparable fee levels but never in the specific builder's blocks. The per-builder inclusion audit against a competitive-fee baseline is the load-bearing forensic artefact; a builder that excludes below-median-fee transactions is selecting on fee, not censoring; a builder that excludes median-or-above-fee transactions from specific addresses while including them from others is a structural T14.005 signal.

v0.x deferred items: EIP-7547 inclusion-list deployment and measurement (what fraction of blocks contain inclusion lists, what fraction of listed transactions are included, what is the builder-compliant rate); encrypted-mempool deployment as a censorship-mitigation layer (quantifying the reduction in builder-observable content); builder-concentration metrics over time as the PBS architecture evolves (e.g., whether the builder landscape becomes more or less concentrated post-inclusion-lists).
