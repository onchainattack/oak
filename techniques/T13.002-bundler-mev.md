# OAK-T13.002 — Bundler MEV

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T13 (Account Abstraction Attacks)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (any chain with deployed ERC-4337 EntryPoint, including Ethereum mainnet, Polygon, Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, BNB Chain)
**First documented:** 2023 (concurrent with EntryPoint v0.6 mainnet deployment and the first vendor-side analyses of the alt-mempool)
**Aliases:** "UserOp MEV", "AA sandwich", "4337 front-run"

## Description

A value-extraction pattern in which the actor is specifically an **ERC-4337 bundler** — the off-chain agent expected to neutrally aggregate `UserOperation` objects from the alt-mempool into a `handleOps` call on the EntryPoint contract — and that actor instead exploits its privileged read of pre-confirmation UserOps (or its position in the bundle-construction pipeline) to capture value from the operations it is supposed to process. T13.002 is the AA-architecture analogue of T5.004 (Sandwich / MEV Extraction): the *economic primitive* (front-run + back-run around a victim swap) is the same, but the **actor role**, **observation surface**, and **mitigation surface** all differ. T5.004 covers a generic mempool searcher; T13.002 covers a bundler whose role definition explicitly assumes neutral aggregation, and whose misbehaviour is a **violation of role**, not merely opportunistic ordering.

Sub-patterns:

- **(a) UserOp front-running.** A bundler observes a profitable UserOp in the alt-mempool (or in its own private intake) before submission, computes that the EntryPoint reimbursement plus extractable value exceeds gas cost, and submits a competing transaction — either a copy of the UserOp's calldata under its own bundle, or an EOA-level transaction that captures the same opportunity. The original bundler's `handleOps` reverts; the user's UserOp is either re-bundled at a worse price or stranded.
- **(b) UserOp sandwiching.** A bundler brackets a victim UserOp inside its own bundle (or across adjacent bundles in the same block) with an opening swap (front-run) and a closing swap (back-run) on the same pool, capturing slippage. Distinct from (a) because both legs are constructed by the bundler itself within the bundle ordering it controls; bundle-internal ordering is not constrained by the EntryPoint, only by the bundler's choice.
- **(c) UserOp censorship.** A bundler refuses to include UserOps that are unprofitable to it (low priority fee, or competitive with its own extraction), forcing those UserOps to either time out, re-broadcast through MEV-protected paths at higher cost, or fall back to a less-decentralised relay. Censorship is observable as a sustained gap between alt-mempool propagation and on-chain inclusion for a specific UserOp class at a specific bundler.
- **(d) Bundler–paymaster collusion.** A bundler and a paymaster operator coordinate so that the paymaster sponsors UserOps which the bundler then extracts value from, splitting the proceeds. Particularly acute for sponsored-gas flows where the user perceives the transaction as "free" and therefore tolerates worse execution price; empirical AA studies report that the overwhelming majority of mainnet UserOps are paymaster-sponsored, expanding this surface.

The architectural distinction from T5.004 matters because the **defender's leverage point is different**: against a generic searcher, traders route through Flashbots Protect or a private mempool; against a misbehaving bundler, the user has typically already chosen that bundler (via the wallet SDK's RPC endpoint) and the alt-mempool's permissionless propagation means *any* listening bundler — not only the one the wallet sent to — can act on the UserOp. Defence therefore shifts to bundler-level controls: per-bundler reputation, MEV-protected bundler routes, allowlists at the paymaster, and pre-bundler UserOp encryption.

## Observed indicators

- A UserOp visible in the public alt-mempool is included in a `handleOps` bundle whose submitter is *not* the originally-targeted bundler, and the inclusion transaction is paired with an opening/closing leg from the same submitter on the same pool.
- A bundler's `handleOps` calls show a non-trivial revert rate concentrated on UserOps that are subsequently included by a competing bundler in the same or next block (signature of (a)).
- A specific bundler exhibits a sustained gap between alt-mempool first-seen time and on-chain inclusion time for UserOps whose execution profile would compete with that bundler's own extraction (signature of (c)).
- Paymaster-sponsored UserOps from a single paymaster cluster disproportionately into bundles from a single bundler, with worse-than-market execution on the swap leg (signature of (d)).

## Detection signals

- **Per-bundler observed-extraction tracking.** For every bundler address known to call `EntryPoint.handleOps`, attribute the bundle's enclosing block-position MEV (sandwich legs, atomic-arb tail) to that bundler. Maintain a rolling window of realised-extraction-per-UserOp and flag bundlers whose extraction profile materially exceeds the cohort baseline.
- **Bundle-content analysis.** For each `handleOps` call, decode the inner UserOps and check for the canonical sandwich shape (opening swap from bundler EOA, victim UserOp, closing swap from bundler EOA) on the same pool inside the same bundle or adjacent bundles in the same block.
- **Front-run revert pattern.** Track, per bundler, the rate of `handleOps` reverts where the same UserOp hash appears in a competing bundler's successful bundle within ±1 block.
- **Censorship signal.** Cross-reference alt-mempool propagation logs (P2P timestamps) against on-chain inclusion; flag bundlers with persistent inclusion delay on a profitable-UserOp class.
- **Paymaster–bundler co-occurrence.** Cluster (paymaster, bundler) pairs by joint share of sponsored-UserOp volume; flag pairs whose victim-side execution-quality distribution differs significantly from the cohort.

## Real-world examples

ERC-4337 bundler MEV is documented at the **cohort level** in vendor research and AA-supply-chain analytics rather than via individual named-operator case studies of the `jaredfromsubway.eth` kind. Public reporting establishes:

- **Alt-mempool MEV exposure characterised, 2023–2025.** Bundler-vendor research (Etherspot, FastLane, BlockPI) documents that any UserOp landing in the canonical alt-mempool is observable by every listening bundler, and that competing bundlers — and downstream block builders — will copy the UserOp into their own `handleOps` and out-bid the original submitter when extractable value exceeds gas cost. The bundler vendors openly position MEV protection as a product feature, indicating that unprotected bundlers are the cohort default. Source: `[etherspot2023bundlermev]`, `[fastlane2024erc4337mev]`, `[blockpi2023bundlermempool]`.
- **Sponsored-flow concentration, mainnet 2024.** Empirical AA-adoption analyses report that ~99% of mainnet UserOperations are paymaster-sponsored and that bundler activity is concentrated in a small set of operators (Coinbase, Alchemy, Pimlico, Biconomy, Particle), establishing the structural conditions for sub-pattern (d). Source: `[gmu2024aaempirical]`.
- **EigenPhi AA supply-chain analytics.** EigenPhi extends its sandwich/arbitrage/liquidation MEV taxonomy to AA flows, providing per-bundle attribution of MEV against bundler addresses; this is the closest analogue to the `[eigenphijared2023]` operator-profile methodology in the AA setting. Source: `[eigenphi2023aamev]`.

No individual bundler operator has been publicly named in the manner of `jaredfromsubway.eth` at v0.1; the cohort framing reflects the present state of disclosure.

## Mitigations

- **Wallets / SDKs:** route UserOps through MEV-protected bundler endpoints (private-relay path or bundler with documented MEV-protection policy) rather than raw alt-mempool broadcast; pin a small allowlist of bundlers per chain.
- **Paymasters:** restrict sponsored UserOps to a vetted bundler allowlist at the paymaster contract level; this is the standard mitigation against (d), at the cost of reduced bundler decentralisation.
- **Protocols / dApps integrating AA:** specify the bundler endpoint at the integration layer and refuse fallback to public alt-mempool for high-value or MEV-sensitive operations (swaps, liquidations, oracle updates).
- **Bundler operators:** publish an MEV-extraction policy and an attestation of non-extraction; submit through MEV-Boost / private builder paths so that bundle-internal ordering is not re-orderable by the block builder.
- **Ecosystem:** develop a per-bundler reputation system on top of the EntryPoint stake (the EntryPoint stake is anti-spam, not anti-MEV); pre-bundler UserOp encryption / threshold-decryption schemes are an active research direction to remove the bundler's pre-inclusion read entirely.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M06, OAK-M11.

## Discussion

T13.002 is rated **developing** rather than **stable** because the public detection toolchain for bundler-attributed MEV lags behind the EOA-mempool case (T5.004): EigenPhi-equivalent per-bundler dashboards exist but are less mature, and no canonical operator-profile case study has been published. The architectural separation from T5.004 is nonetheless load-bearing — defenders who treat AA-mempool MEV as "just sandwiching with extra steps" miss that the natural mitigation surface is *at the bundler* (allowlists, reputation, encryption), not at the trader (Flashbots Protect-style routing), because the alt-mempool propagates UserOps to bundlers the user did not choose. The standalone Technique exists to keep that mitigation surface visible. The Technique should be re-rated to `stable` once at least one publicly-attributed bundler-MEV operator profile lands, in the manner of `[eigenphijared2023]` for T5.004.
