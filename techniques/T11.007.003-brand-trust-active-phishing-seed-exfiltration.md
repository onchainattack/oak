# OAK-T11.007.003 — Brand-Trust-Leveraged Active Phishing for Seed-Phrase Exfiltration

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure); sub-Technique of OAK-T11.007 (Hardware-wallet Supply-chain / Physical-access Compromise)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (phishing campaigns target hardware-wallet users across all chains their devices support)
**First documented:** 2020 (Ledger customer-data-breach-leveraged phishing onset); 2023 Kaspersky spring cohort (85,000+ scam emails in a single quarter); 2023 trojanised companion-app cohort
**Aliases:** "fake firmware update phishing", "hardware-wallet recovery phishing", "brand-impersonation seed solicitation", "Ledger-data-breach-leveraged phishing"

## Description

Active phishing campaigns that impersonate legitimate hardware-wallet vendor communications — email, physical mail, trojanised companion apps, fake firmware-update flows — to solicit the user's seed phrase directly. The attacker leverages the legitimate vendor's brand trust plus personalisation data (from the December 2020 Ledger customer-data breach: ~273,000 email + physical-address + phone-number records) to produce apparent legitimacy.

The structural feature is **channel counterfeiting, not device counterfeiting**: the hardware device itself is legitimate; the *communication* that solicits the seed phrase is attacker-controlled. The user is asked to enter their seed phrase into a fake "recovery" or "verification" or "firmware upgrade" flow, and the seed phrase is exfiltrated directly.

Sub-campaigns documented at v0.1:

- **Ledger-data-breach-leveraged email phishing (2020 onward):** using the December 2020 Ledger customer-data leak for personalisation (addressing recipients by name, referencing their purchase history), increasing per-recipient success rates.
- **Kaspersky spring 2023 cohort:** 85,000+ scam emails detected in a single quarter targeting Ledger and Trezor user bases.
- **Trezor-impersonating physical-mail-letter campaign (2025–2026):** physical letters with QR codes leading to typosquat domains (`trezor.authentication-check.io`, `ledger.setuptransactioncheck.com`).
- **Trojanised companion-app cohort (2023 onward):** infostealers swap fragments of legitimate Ledger Live / Trezor Suite code with trojanised code that fakes an error and initiates a "recovery process" prompting for the seed phrase. The legitimate companion app does *not* request the seed phrase.

Structural distinction from sibling sub-Techniques:

- **T11.007.003** requires no device access at all — the attack is against the user's trust in the vendor communication channel. The device is legitimate; the channel is counterfeit.
- **T11.007.001** (Counterfeit-Hardware Substitution) substitutes the device itself.
- **T11.007.002** (Physical-Access Hardware-Side Seed Extraction) requires physical access to a legitimate device.

## Observed indicators

- Unsolicited communication (email, physical mail, in-app prompt) purporting to be from the hardware-wallet vendor requesting seed-phrase entry for "verification," "firmware update," "security upgrade," or "device replacement."
- QR codes in physical mail leading to typosquat / near-miss domains.
- Companion app that fakes an error and prompts for seed-phrase "recovery." Legitimate companion apps never request the seed phrase.
- Email or letter includes personalisation data consistent with the 2020 Ledger customer-data leak (name, physical address, phone number, purchase history).

## Detection signals

- **Legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase invariant:** the legitimate vendor *never* requests the seed phrase across any communication channel (email, physical mail, in-app prompt, phone call, chat support, firmware-update flow). User-side enforcement of this invariant is the binding mitigation.
- **Vendor-side continuous-update threat-intelligence:** subscribe to vendor-side phishing-campaign tracking (Ledger's "Ongoing phishing campaigns" page; Trezor's parallel advisories).
- **Companion-app integrity verification:** download companion apps exclusively from the vendor's official site or authenticated app-store entry; verify cryptographic checksums where published.
- **Typosquat-domain surveillance:** monitor for near-miss / typosquat domains impersonating vendor domains in phishing campaign infrastructure.

## Mitigations

- **Legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase invariant (highest-leverage):** user-side enforcement that the vendor never requests the seed phrase across any channel. Refuse to enter the seed phrase into any surface other than the legitimate device's own physical input mechanism.
- **Vendor-side continuous-update threat-intelligence subscription:** subscribe to vendor phishing-campaign tracking; treat any unsolicited communication purporting to be from the vendor as a phishing candidate until verified through vendor's authenticated channels.
- **Companion-app integrity:** download exclusively from vendor's official site or authenticated app-store entry; verify checksums; treat companion-app prompts soliciting the seed phrase as definitively malicious.
- **User-side seed-phrase cold-storage invariant:** the seed phrase should be on physical media only; it should never be typed into any digital surface other than the hardware wallet's physical input mechanism.

## Discussion

T11.007.003's structural OAK lesson: **the legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase invariant is the load-bearing mitigation primitive, and it is operationally fragile because phishing campaigns continuously refine their personalisation surface.** The 2020 Ledger customer-data breach (~273,000 records including physical addresses and phone numbers) provides an ongoing operational substrate that produces apparent legitimacy — an attacker who addresses the user by name, references their purchase history, and impersonates the vendor's visual branding exploits the user's learned trust in the vendor.

The invariant is simple to state but hard to operationalise at scale because phishing campaigns continuously adapt — physical-mail letters (2025–2026), trojanised companion apps that swap code fragments with the legitimate app, and multi-channel campaigns that combine email, physical mail, and in-app prompts. The user-side enforcement of the invariant is the only defence that does not require per-campaign updating.
