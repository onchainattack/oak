# OAK-T10.005 — Light-Client Verification Bypass

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T10 (Bridge and Cross-Chain)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM, Cosmos / IBC, Bitcoin SPV consumers, cross-chain (any bridge whose security reduces to a cryptographic light-client or proof-verification primitive)
**First documented:** 2022 (Verichains "Dragonberry" disclosure of an ICS-23 Merkle-proof soundness bug affecting IBC light-client verification across Cosmos-SDK chains; class characterised earlier in zk-bridge academic literature)
**Aliases:** "circuit soundness bug", "trusted-setup compromise", "proof-system bypass", "light-client verifier bug", "zk-bridge soundness failure"

## Description

A class of bridge-infrastructure attacks specific to bridges whose security model reduces to a **cryptographic verification primitive** rather than to a threshold-signing validator set (T10.001) or to an optimistic-fraud-proof window (T10.004). Examples: zk-SNARK / zk-STARK proof bridges (Polyhedra zkBridge, Succinct, Electron, Avail, Polymer), Tendermint / IBC light-client bridges (Cosmos-SDK ICS-23 / `tendermint-light-client`), Bitcoin SPV-style bridges (BTC Relay descendants, drivechain proposals, BitVM-flavoured constructions), and EVM block-header light clients (Telepathy, LightClientBridge variants).

The attack surface is the **cryptographic-primitive layer**: the circuit that encodes the source-chain consensus rule, the proof system that produces and verifies the proof, the trusted setup that parameterises the proof system (where applicable), and the assumed-vs-actual chain-consensus modelling. A successful bypass produces a *valid* proof of a *false* statement — the verifier accepts because, from its perspective, every check passed.

Sub-cases:

- **(a) Circuit-soundness flaw.** The verifier circuit is *under-constrained* — it permits witness assignments that satisfy the constraint system but do not correspond to any valid source-chain state transition. Empirically the dominant failure class: industry analyses of disclosed SNARK bugs from 2018–2024 attribute roughly 96% to under-constrained circuits `[soksnarkvulns2024]`. Examples include missing range checks, ambiguous bit-decomposition, unchecked Fiat-Shamir transcripts ("Frozen Heart" class affecting Plonky2, Halo2, and others), and arithmetic-overflow paths.
- **(b) Trusted-setup compromise.** The structured-reference-string (SRS) for a SNARK with a trusted setup (Groth16, KZG-based PLONK variants) is generated by a ceremony whose toxic-waste destruction cannot be independently verified. A participant who retains the trapdoor can forge proofs for arbitrary statements without breaking the underlying cryptographic assumption.
- **(c) Consensus-rule modelling mismatch.** The light client (cryptographic or otherwise) verifies *block headers* but not the *full consensus state* — e.g., it checks signature thresholds without checking validator-set updates, slashing conditions, or fork-choice rules. The bridge accepts a header that was never finalised on the source chain (or was finalised on a fork that the source chain later abandoned).
- **(d) Reorg / finality-handling bug.** The light client's logic for handling reorgs, equivocations, or unbonding-period transitions is incomplete. The Verichains "Dragonberry" disclosure (2022) is the canonical worked example at the Merkle-membership-proof layer: ICS-23 lacked sufficient encoding constraints, allowing forged membership proofs to be accepted by IBC light clients across Cosmos-SDK chains `[verichainsdragonberry2022]`.
- **(e) Verifier-contract bug.** The on-chain verifier contract that consumes the proof contains an implementation bug independent of the circuit — e.g., precompile mis-use, calldata-encoding mismatch, or a missing public-input binding that decouples the proof from the bridge message it claims to authorise.

T10.005 is **architecturally distinct** from the other T10 Techniques:

- vs. **T10.001 (Validator-Signer Key Compromise):** there is no threshold validator set to compromise; correctness is supposed to come from the cryptographic proof itself.
- vs. **T10.002 (Message-Verification Bypass):** the failure is at the cryptographic-primitive layer (circuit / proof-system / setup), not at the application-logic verification predicate. A T10.002 bypass is typically a logic bug in handwritten Solidity (e.g., Nomad's `0x00` trusted-root); a T10.005 bypass is a logic bug in a circuit, a soundness gap in the proof system, or a trapdoor in the SRS.
- vs. **T10.004 (Optimistic-Bridge Fraud-Proof Gap):** verification is *upfront and cryptographic*, not *deferred and economic*. There is no challenge window because the proof is supposed to be sufficient on its own. Conversely, a working cryptographic light client does not require a funded challenger set.

T10.005 is therefore the third **architectural class** in the bridge taxonomy alongside the validator-multisig model (T10.001 surface) and the optimistic model (T10.004 surface). Each architectural class moves the trust assumption to a different layer; T10.005 concentrates it on the cryptographic primitive and the team's ability to specify, implement, audit, and ceremonially deploy it correctly.

## Observed indicators

- A bridge whose security claim reduces to "a SNARK verifies the source-chain consensus" without a published end-to-end specification of which consensus rule is verified, how validator-set updates are tracked, and how reorgs / unbonding are handled.
- A trusted-setup ceremony with a non-public participant list, no published transcript, or no independently-verifiable attestation that toxic waste was destroyed.
- A verifier circuit deployed without a public formal-verification report or without published artefacts (R1CS / AIR description, public-input layout, verifier-contract bytecode hash matched to the audited circuit).
- Post-deployment circuit upgrades pushed without re-running the trusted-setup ceremony or without re-publishing the formal-verification artefacts.
- Single-prover deployment: only one party can produce proofs, so a prover-side liveness or correctness failure is undetectable until a verifier-side incident.
- Discrepancy between the consensus rule documented in source-chain spec and the consensus rule encoded in the verifier circuit (e.g., the circuit verifies header signatures but not the slashing-aware fork-choice rule the source chain actually uses).

## Detection signals

- **Pre-deployment circuit audit.** Engagement with circuit-audit specialists who can read the constraint system end-to-end (Veridise, Trail of Bits, ZK Security, Zellic, OpenZeppelin's ZK practice) and run automated under-constrained-circuit detectors. A bridge whose SNARK has not been audited by a circuit-specialist firm is a T10.005 surface regardless of the reputation of any non-ZK auditor on the cap table.
- **Formal verification of the consensus-rule encoding.** Independent specification of the source-chain consensus rule, mechanised proof that the circuit's constraint system implements that specification, and published artefacts (proof scripts, SMT outputs, machine-checked theorems) sufficient for a reviewer to reproduce the verification.
- **Trusted-setup transparency.** For setups requiring an MPC ceremony: public participant list, published transcript and contributions, attestation hash chain, and a reproducible verification script. For universal setups (KZG / Powers-of-Tau reuse): pinned version, ceremony provenance, and a published cross-check that the bridge's SRS is a valid extension of the referenced ceremony.
- **Verifier-contract / circuit binding.** Bytecode-pinning of the deployed verifier contract to a specific audited circuit; on upgrade, re-attestation that the new circuit was re-audited and re-set-up. Treat verifier-contract upgradeability without re-audit as a standing surface.
- **Multi-prover redundancy where feasible.** Independent proving stacks (different proof systems or different implementations) producing proofs of the same statement, with the verifier requiring agreement; reduces single-implementation-bug exposure even when the underlying cryptography is sound.
- **Runtime cross-checks.** Where economically feasible, a *secondary* verification path (committee attestation, optimistic-fraud-proof window, or independent light-client run) that catches a circuit-bug-driven false acceptance before withdrawal finalisation. Pure cryptographic verification with no defence in depth is a T10.005 amplifier.

## Real-world examples

- **Verichains "Dragonberry" / ICS-23 Merkle-proof soundness bug — Cosmos-SDK / IBC — 2022-10** — Verichains researchers disclosed that the ICS-23 specification (the Merkle-proof format used by IBC light clients across Cosmos-SDK chains) lacked sufficient encoding constraints on leaf and inner-node prefix/suffix lengths, allowing forged membership proofs to be accepted by IBC verifiers. The flaw was at the cryptographic-primitive layer of the light-client verification stack (Merkle-proof soundness), not at the application-logic layer; affected funds across IBC channels were estimated by the disclosers in the billions of dollars notional. Patched via the Cosmos-SDK "Dragonberry" advisory before exploitation `[verichainsdragonberry2022]`. This is the cleanest verified worked example for T10.005 sub-case (d) and partially (a).
- **Light-client verification bypass as a class — zk-bridge architectures — 2022 onward** — OAK does not at v0.1 name a single canonical zk-bridge T10.005 incident on the same loss-scale as Ronin (T10.001) or Nomad (T10.002), because publicly-disclosed circuit-soundness flaws in deployed zk-bridges have to date been caught at the audit layer rather than via headline loss incidents. The class is well-characterised in academic and industry literature: the SoK on SNARK vulnerabilities catalogues ~96% of documented bugs as under-constrained circuits `[soksnarkvulns2024]`; the 0xPARC zk-bug-tracker maintains a community catalogue of disclosed circuit bugs `[zkbugtracker]`; the original zkBridge construction `[xie2022zkbridge]` and follow-up audits by circuit specialists characterise the threat surface explicitly. As with T10.003 / T10.004, contributors with public forensic write-ups of specific named incidents whose primary failure mode is T10.005 are encouraged to submit them via PR.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T10.005 is a cryptographic-primitive-layer / pre-deployment-audit class, out of MG runtime-detector scope). A runtime detector cannot in general distinguish a forged-but-valid proof from a legitimate one without a redundant verification path.
- Pre-deployment vendor coverage: circuit-audit specialist firms — **Veridise**, **Trail of Bits**, **ZK Security** — as canonical access points for under-constrained-circuit detection, formal-verification of consensus-rule encoding, and trusted-setup ceremony review. Adjacent: Zellic and OpenZeppelin's ZK practice. Tooling: 0xPARC's zk-bug-tracker as a public catalogue of disclosed circuit bugs, plus academic detectors for under-constrained circuits.
- No widely-deployed runtime detector specifically observes light-client-verification correctness as of v0.1; the relevant defences are pre-deployment (circuit audit, formal verification, ceremony transparency) and architectural (multi-prover redundancy, defence-in-depth verification paths).

## Mitigations

- **Bridge architects:** treat the verifier circuit as a high-assurance component on the same tier as a consensus implementation. Specify the source-chain consensus rule formally (machine-checkable), encode it in the circuit, and obtain mechanised proof that the circuit implements the specification. Use proof systems with universal / transparent setup where feasible (PLONK with shared powers-of-tau, STARKs without trusted setup) to eliminate the trusted-setup compromise surface entirely. Avoid single-implementation deployments where the same team writes the spec, the circuit, the prover, and the verifier without independent cross-checks.
- **Bridge operators:** publish, version, and pin all cryptographic-primitive artefacts — circuit description, SRS provenance, verifier-contract bytecode hash, ceremony transcript, formal-verification reports. On any circuit upgrade, treat the new circuit as a new bridge for due-diligence purposes (re-audit, re-ceremony where applicable, re-publish). Run multi-prover redundancy where the proof-cost budget allows; otherwise, deploy a defence-in-depth verification path (committee attestation or short challenge window) until the circuit has matured through real adversarial probing.
- **Risk teams:** when reviewing a new zk-bridge or light-client bridge integration, require: (1) a circuit-specialist audit report from a named firm (Veridise, Trail of Bits, ZK Security, or equivalent), (2) the trusted-setup ceremony transcript and attestation if applicable, (3) a written specification of which source-chain consensus rule is verified and which is *not* (e.g., header-only vs. full fork-choice), and (4) a description of the runtime fallback if the circuit is found to be unsound post-deployment. Treat a bridge that cannot supply (1)–(4) as a standing T10.005 surface independent of TVL or cap-table reputation.
- **Cross-comparison:** unlike T10.001, raising a validator threshold does not help — there is no validator threshold to raise. Unlike T10.002, a smoke test of the verification predicate does not cover this surface — the smoke test passes precisely because the circuit thinks the (forged) proof is valid. Unlike T10.004, a longer challenge window does not help — there is no challenger. The relevant mitigation surface is upstream of deployment (circuit specification, audit, ceremony) plus architectural defence-in-depth.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M12, OAK-M14, OAK-M16.

## Discussion

T10.005 is the third architectural class in OAK's bridge taxonomy alongside the validator-multisig model (T10.001 surface) and the optimistic model (T10.004 surface). Each class places the trust assumption at a different layer: T10.001 trusts a threshold of signers, T10.004 trusts that an honest challenger will appear in time, T10.005 trusts the cryptographic primitive and the team's ability to specify and ship it correctly. The dominant failure mode in v0.1 is **under-constrained circuits** rather than cryptographic-assumption failure — i.e., the proof system itself works, but the circuit encoding the consensus rule does not say everything it should.

The Technique is "observed" rather than "stable" because (a) deployed zk-bridges are still relatively new and have small TVL relative to validator-multisig peers, (b) circuit-specialist audit capacity is the binding constraint on detection coverage, and (c) the public catalogue of disclosed circuit bugs is growing rapidly enough that v0.1's enumeration of sub-cases is likely incomplete. As zk-bridge TVL grows and circuit audits mature, T10.005 may bifurcate into more specific Techniques (e.g., separate entries for trusted-setup compromise vs. circuit-soundness flaws vs. consensus-rule modelling mismatch). Contributors writing future T10.005 entries should not feel obliged to find a comparable-scale named incident; a well-characterised circuit bug with verifiable artefacts (circuit code, audit report, disclosure write-up) is sufficient. Where a future incident's primary failure mode is clearly T10.005 (e.g., a deployed-zk-bridge soundness failure resulting in forged-proof acceptance), it should be catalogued here with the standard worked-example treatment.
