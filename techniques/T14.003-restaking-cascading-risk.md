# OAK-T14.003 — Restaking Cascading Risk

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T14 (Validator / Staking / Restaking Attacks)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** Ethereum L1 (canonical), EVM L2s with restaking-secured AVS, Cosmos-style shared-security analogues
**First documented:** 2023 (concept characterised in Vitalik Buterin's "Don't overload Ethereum's consensus" essay; class formalised through 2024 in restaking risk-analysis literature; mainnet slashing enabled by EigenLayer 2025-04)
**Aliases:** "shared-security cascade", "AVS slashing-cascade", "LRT depeg cascade", "restaking systemic risk", "pooled-security contagion"

## Description

A class of OAK-T14 attacks specific to restaking protocols — EigenLayer (canonical), Symbiotic, Karak, and Cosmos-style shared-security analogues — where the same staked assets simultaneously secure multiple Actively Validated Services (AVS). The attack surface is the **structural coupling between independently-designed AVS that share an operator and a stake pool**: a slashing event on AVS-A reduces the stake pool, which simultaneously reduces the cryptoeconomic security guaranteeing AVS-B and AVS-C, creating a propagation channel that no single AVS designer controls.

Sub-cases:

- **(a) AVS-design slashing-cascade.** AVS-A's slashing condition fires for an operator who is also running AVS-B and AVS-C. The slashed stake reduces the security of AVS-B and AVS-C below their guaranteed thresholds, even though those AVS were themselves operated correctly. The failure mode is *cross-AVS by construction* — the operator did nothing wrong from AVS-B's perspective, but AVS-B's security budget shrank.
- **(b) Liquid-restaking-token (LRT) depeg cascade.** An LRT (e.g., Mellow steakLRT, ether.fi eETH, Renzo ezETH, Kelp rsETH) wraps restaked stake. A partial slashing of the underlying restaked stake — or an oracle disagreement about one — causes the LRT to trade below its peg. Lending protocols accepting the LRT as collateral cascade-liquidate; LP positions in LRT/ETH pools get drained by arbitrageurs; downstream looped-leverage strategies unwind. The slashing event need not be large in absolute terms — leverage and oracle latency amplify it.
- **(c) Operator concentration risk.** A small number of professional operator entities run a majority of AVS by stake-weight. An operator-level compromise (hot-key compromise, infrastructure compromise, jurisdictional seizure, internal fraud) simultaneously fails every AVS that operator secures. This is the restaking analogue of T8.002 (operator continuity) but with the blast radius defined by AVS-set membership rather than by chain.
- **(d) Slashing-as-economic-attack.** An attacker stakes specifically to trigger slashing conditions on competitor restakers — for example, by registering for an AVS, performing the slashable action deliberately, and absorbing the slash as a cost-of-doing-business in exchange for damaging a competitor's operator-reputation surface or LRT peg. The *attacker's stake is a weapon, not a security deposit*.

T14.003 differs from T14.001 (validator-key compromise) and T14.002 (consensus-equivocation slashing) in that no single validator misbehaves in isolation — the failure mode is **systemic, by design**, arising from the legitimate operation of the restaking protocol's stake-reuse mechanism. It differs from T9 (smart-contract exploit) in that the AVS contracts may be bug-free; the cascade emerges from shared-stake economics, not from buggy code.

## Observed indicators

- An AVS specifies a security budget in stake-equivalent terms but does not disclose what fraction of that stake is *unique* to it versus restaked into other AVS.
- LRT issuer publishes a single net-asset-value but does not publish per-AVS stake allocation, per-AVS slashing exposure, or stress-tested depeg curves.
- Lending market accepts an LRT as collateral with the same loan-to-value (LTV) and oracle methodology as the underlying liquid-staking token (LST), without an additional buffer for slashing-tail risk.
- Operator-set page for a major AVS shows a top-5 operator concentration above 60% of delegated stake.
- Restaking protocol governance proposal extends slashing scope to a new class of AVS conditions without an audited cascade-impact analysis on already-deployed AVS.

## Detection signals

- **Per-operator AVS-concentration metric.** For each operator, compute the count and stake-weight of AVS they secure. Alert on operators above a configurable concentration threshold; alert on AVS whose top-N operators overlap with another AVS's top-N by more than a configurable share.
- **Cross-AVS slashing-correlation matrix.** Build a graph where nodes are AVS and edge weights are fraction-of-shared-stake. Identify connected components with high cumulative TVL — these are the cascade-blast-radius units. A single AVS that is heavily connected to a high-TVL component is a systemic node by definition.
- **LRT peg-stability monitoring.** Track LRT/ETH (or LRT/LST) trading peg across DEX venues; alert on persistent (>N hours) divergence beyond a configurable band. Cross-reference with on-chain slashing events on underlying AVS.
- **Lending-protocol LRT-collateral exposure.** For each lending market, track total LRT collateral in absolute and percentage terms; estimate liquidation cascade size for a 5%, 10%, 20% LRT depeg using current oracle, LTV, and liquidity-fragmentation parameters.
- **Operator-key liveness and rotation telemetry.** Track operator-signing-key rotation cadence, infrastructure-attestation status (where AVS provides one), and any indicators that an operator is run as a single point of failure (one cloud region, one bonded entity).
- **Slashing-event change-data-capture.** Subscribe to slashing-event emissions from the restaking protocol; for each slashing, immediately enumerate every AVS the slashed operator secures and every LRT whose stake includes that operator.

## Real-world examples

- **Restaking cascading risk as a class — EigenLayer ecosystem — 2023 onward.** OAK does not at v0.1 name a single canonical T14.003 *loss incident* in the same league as Ronin (T10.001) or Wormhole (T10.002). The class is documented in design-review and risk-analysis literature rather than via a flagship cascade event, in part because mainnet slashing on EigenLayer was disabled at launch and progressively enabled — slashing went live on mainnet on 2025-04-17 with per-AVS Unique Stake Allocation as an explicit cascade-mitigation primitive `[eigenlabsslashinglive2025]`. The design-review cohort is well-characterised: Vitalik Buterin's 2023 essay "Don't overload Ethereum's consensus" identifies social-consensus overloading and too-big-to-fail dynamics as the systemic worry `[vitalikrestaking2023]`; Gauntlet's 2024 restaking-economy analysis develops the cascade-modelling framework and characterises submodular-profit conditions under which strategic operators avoid large-scale cascades — and the bug-driven conditions under which they do not `[gauntletrestaking2024]`; Steakhouse Financial's published LRT methodology documents the depeg-amplification path from underlying-stake slashing to LRT collateral devaluation `[steakhouselrt2024]`; academic work surveys LRT and emerging-restaking risk including leveraged-restaking-of-leveraged-staking liquidation cascades `[alexanderleveragedrestaking2024]`. Specific named incidents whose primary failure mode is T14.003 should be added by PR with verifiable forensic artefacts (slashing-event transactions, AVS-set membership at time-of-slash, LRT depeg traces, lending-liquidation cascades).

## Mitigations

- **AVS designers (stake-isolation):** prefer Unique Stake Allocation primitives (each AVS slashes only stake earmarked for it) over fully-pooled designs `[eigenlabsslashinglive2025]`. Publish a written security-budget analysis in *unique-stake-equivalent* terms, not aggregate-stake-equivalent. Audit slashing conditions against an explicit cascade-impact threat model that enumerates the other AVS the same operator secures.
- **LRT issuers (circuit-breakers):** publish per-AVS allocation and per-AVS slashing exposure; implement deposit / withdrawal circuit-breakers that trigger on underlying-stake slashing events; coordinate oracle methodology with major lending-market integrations so that LRT price discovery and lending-liquidation thresholds are coherent under stress.
- **Lending-market integrators:** treat LRT collateral as a distinct risk class from the underlying LST, with a separate LTV ceiling, isolated-pool listing, and oracle methodology that incorporates slashing-event delay rather than only spot DEX prices. Cap aggregate LRT collateral as a fraction of pool size; require an additional buffer for tail-risk depeg scenarios.
- **Operator-diversity requirements:** restaking protocols and AVS should publish operator-concentration metrics and consider concentration caps (no operator above N% of an AVS's stake; no operator-set top-5 concentration above M% across N largest AVS). Operator-key rotation cadence, infrastructure attestation, and jurisdictional diversity should be auditable, not only self-attested.
- **Risk teams:** before integrating any AVS, LRT, or restaking-collateralised lending market, request the cascade-blast-radius graph and the unique-stake fraction; treat aggregate-stake claims as upper bounds rather than as security budgets. Treat AVS that share the top-5 operators with high-TVL AVS as cascade-correlated regardless of their independent audit posture.
- **Cross-comparison:** unlike T14.001 (validator-key compromise), key hardening alone does not address (a) or (d) — those failure modes coexist with perfectly-secured keys. Unlike T9 (contract exploit), code audits of individual AVS contracts do not surface the cross-AVS cascade — the cascade analysis is a *protocol-economics review*, not a per-contract review.

## Discussion

T14.003 is included as **emerging** maturity rather than **observed** because — at v0.1 — the canonical restaking platform (EigenLayer) only enabled mainnet slashing on 2025-04-17, and the cascade-amplification surface had until then been a design-document risk rather than a tested operational one. This is precisely the situation in which a defender-perspective taxonomy is most valuable: the failure mode is well-characterised by economists and protocol designers, the early-warning signals are observable from on-chain data, and the mitigation primitives (Unique Stake Allocation, LRT circuit-breakers, operator-diversity caps) are deployable now rather than after a flagship cascade event.

Contributors writing future T14.003 entries should not feel obliged to find a comparable-scale named incident. A well-characterised cascade simulation against a real deployed AVS-set, an LRT depeg-and-liquidation cascade trace, or a forensic write-up of an operator compromise that propagated across AVS is sufficient. Where a future incident's primary failure mode is clearly T14.003 (e.g., a real cross-AVS slashing cascade, an LRT-collateralised lending cascade liquidation, or a slashing-as-economic-attack episode), it should be catalogued here with the standard worked-example treatment.

The Technique is documented because the question a defender or risk reviewer must ask — *which AVS share which operators, with what unique-stake fraction, and which LRTs and lending markets sit downstream of those operators?* — is canonical, answerable from on-chain data, and presently asked by too few integrators relative to the systemic exposure already accumulated.
