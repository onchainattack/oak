# OAK-T3.005 — Fake-Validator Staking-Frontend Phishing

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T3 (Social Engineering / Community Manipulation), OAK-T4 (Phishing and User-Deception Operations)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (Lido staking, Rocket Pool); Solana (Marinade, Jito staking); Cosmos (validator delegation scams)
**First documented:** 2021-2022 (Lido stETH staking-interface phishing campaigns; fake validator-delegation portals)
**Aliases:** "staking-frontend phishing", "fake validator portal", "liquid-staking phishing", "validator-impersonation phishing"

## Description

Phishing campaigns targeting staking users with fake staking frontends that impersonate legitimate liquid-staking protocols (Lido, Rocket Pool, Marinade) or validator-delegation portals. The attacker deploys a domain and UI that mirrors the legitimate staking interface. The user connects their wallet, approves a deposit transaction, and the deposited ETH/SOL is routed to an attacker-controlled address rather than to the legitimate staking contract. The user receives no staking receipt token (stETH, rETH, mSOL) in return — the deposit was an outright transfer to the attacker, not a staking contract interaction.

The structural primitive is the **fake staking-interface deposit path**. Unlike swap-interface phishing (T4.008), where the user is lured by a swap quote, the staking user is lured by a yield projection — a displayed APR/APY that the fake frontend presents as the staking return. The user's action is an ETH/SOL deposit approving a spend or executing a transfer to a contract address displayed as the staking contract. The extraction occurs at the deposit step: the destination address is the attacker's wallet, not the protocol's canonical staking contract.

T3.005 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T4.008 (Fake-DEX Clone-Frontend Phishing)**. T4.008 targets swap users with a fake DEX interface where the swap output token or the approval target is attacker-controlled. T3.005 targets staking users with a fake staking interface where the deposit destination is attacker-controlled. The target user demographic differs: swap-interface phishing targets traders seeking a specific token pair at a favorable quote; staking-interface phishing targets yield-seeking depositors who intend to commit capital for a duration. The extraction primitive differs: T4.008 extracts via a drainer contract or a fake-swap-output token; T3.005 extracts via a faked deposit receipt — the user believes they hold stETH/rETH/mSOL but has sent ETH/SOL directly to the attacker.

T3.005 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T4.009 (Pre-Token Brand-Anticipation Phishing)**. T4.009 targets users anticipating a future token (pre-launch airdrop phishing, fake token-claim interfaces). T3.005 targets active stakers depositing to a live, operational protocol whose canonical interface is publicly known. The user already knows about Lido/Marinade/Rocket Pool and is seeking the canonical staking interface; the attacker intercepts that search-and-connect flow with a typosquat domain or a search-engine-advertisement placement.

T3.005 is structurally distinct from **OAK-T3.003 (Coordinated Pump-and-Dump)** and **OAK-T3.004 (Influencer-Amplified Promotion-and-Dump)**. Both are market-manipulation patterns whose distribution surface is social coordination; T3.005 is a phishing pattern whose distribution surface is domain-typosquatting and search-engine-advertisement placement. The extraction mechanism in T3.005 is a faked deposit-receipt (the user's funds go to the attacker directly), not an insider-cluster pump-and-dump exit.

Sub-patterns observed:

- **Liquid-staking protocol impersonation.** The attacker deploys a domain typosquat of a known liquid-staking protocol (lido.fi → lido-stake.com, lidofi.net; rocketpool.net → rocket-pool.org; marinade.finance → marinade-staking.com). The fake frontend mirrors the legitimate staking interface, displays a competitive APR, and routes the deposit transaction to an attacker-controlled address.
- **Validator-delegation portal impersonation.** On Cosmos and other delegation-model chains, the attacker deploys a fake validator-delegation portal that impersonates a high-uptime validator. The user delegates tokens to the fake validator address; the delegated tokens are immediately unbonded and transferred to the attacker.
- **Search-engine-advertisement interception.** The attacker purchases search-engine advertisements for keywords such as "Lido stake ETH," "stake SOL Marinade," or "Rocket Pool staking," placing the fake frontend above the legitimate protocol's organic search result.

## Observed indicators

- Domain typosquat of a known liquid-staking protocol (lido.fi → lido-stake.com, lidofi.net; rocketpool.net → rocket-pool.org; marinade.finance → marinade-staking.com, marinade.fi).
- The deposit transaction routes ETH/SOL to an address that does not match the legitimate staking contract's canonical deployment address.
- No stETH, rETH, mSOL, or equivalent liquid-staking receipt token arrives in the user's wallet after the deposit transaction confirms.
- The frontend's displayed staking APR/APY is higher than the legitimate protocol's current rate — a yield too good to be true, used as bait.
- The fake frontend's domain registration date is recent (within days or weeks of the phishing campaign launch); the legitimate protocol's domain has a multi-year registration history.
- The fake frontend's TLS certificate is issued by Let's Encrypt or a low-assurance CA with domain-validation-only (DV) assurance, while the legitimate protocol typically uses organisation-validation (OV) or extended-validation (EV) certificates.
- Search-engine advertisement placement for staking-protocol keywords where the sponsored result domain differs from the legitimate protocol's canonical domain.

## Detection signals

- **Domain-reputation services (off-chain).** Flag domains that are typosquats of known liquid-staking protocol domains and that serve a staking-interface UI. Domain-reputation services (Google Safe Browsing, Cloudflare Radar, PhishTank, OpenPhish) are the primary off-chain detection layer. The lag between domain registration and reputation-service flagging is the detection window.
- **Wallet-side deposit-destination verification (highest-leverage on-chain-adjacent detection).** Wallet UIs should compare the destination address of a staking-deposit transaction against the canonical staking contract address for the protocol the user's UI is displaying. If the domain is "lido-stake.com" but the deposit target address does not match Lido's canonical staking contract, the wallet should surface a warning. This is structurally identical to the T2.005 wallet-side address-verification mitigation applied to the staking context.
- **Protocol-side canonical-interface URL publication.** Protocols should publish their canonical interface URLs in a machine-readable format (a `canonical_urls` field in their token-registry entry or a DNS TXT record on their primary domain) that wallets and browsers can verify against. The absence of this standard is a T3.005 surface.
- **Community-side staking-interface domain allowlisting.** Community-maintained domain allowlists (e.g., a community-curated "known staking interfaces" registry) provide a crowd-sourced detection layer that complements automated domain-reputation services. The Lido and Rocket Pool communities maintain informal allowlists in their Discord/Telegram channels; formalising these as a machine-readable registry is a v0.x pipeline item.
- **Search-engine advertisement monitoring.** Monitor search-engine advertisement placements for staking-protocol keywords and flag sponsored-result domains that differ from the legitimate protocol's canonical domain. This is a proactive detection layer that can flag campaigns before user-victim reports arrive.
- **On-chain deposit-flow anomaly detection.** For a given staking contract, monitor the deposit-flow distribution across depositor addresses and flag anomalous patterns — a sudden drop in deposit volume to the canonical contract coincident with an increase in deposits to an unidentified address, or a cluster of small test-deposits followed by a large deposit to a non-canonical address, may indicate an active phishing campaign redirecting user deposits.

## Reference implementations

- MG `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. The off-chain domain-reputation and typosquat-detection primitives are not currently in scope for the Rust-native detector suite. Wallet-side deposit-destination verification is a candidate detector that could be implemented as a wallet-plugin or a transaction-simulation pre-flight check.
- Domain-reputation services: Google Safe Browsing, Cloudflare Radar, PhishTank, OpenPhish — provide partial coverage for domain-typosquat detection but are not staking-protocol-specific and operate at the general-phishing-detection layer.
- Wallet-side: MetaMask's address-verification warnings, Rabby's transaction-preview security scoring, and Phantom's domain-verification banner represent partial wallet-side implementations; none currently cross-references the deposit-destination address against a canonical staking-contract-address registry at the staking-protocol granularity.
- Protocol-side: Lido, Rocket Pool, and Marinade each publish canonical interface URLs in their documentation; none currently publishes a machine-readable canonical-interface registry that wallets can consume programmatically.
- Community-side: Lido and Rocket Pool community Discord/Telegram channels maintain informal domain allowlists; these are not currently surfaced in a machine-readable format.

## Mitigations

- **Protocol-side canonical-interface URL publishing (structural mitigation).** Staking protocols should publish their canonical interface URLs in a machine-readable format — a `canonical_urls` field in the protocol's token-registry entry, a DNS TXT record on the primary domain, or an ENS text record for the protocol's ENS name. Wallets and browsers should verify the domain of any page requesting a staking-deposit transaction against the protocol's published canonical URLs and surface a warning on mismatch.
- **Wallet-side deposit-destination verification (point-of-action mitigation).** Wallet UIs should, at transaction-signing time, compare the deposit-destination address of a staking transaction against the canonical staking contract address for the protocol the user's active domain purports to be. This mitigation closes the T3.005 surface at the moment of user action — the wallet warns before the user signs, rather than detecting the phishing after the deposit confirms.
- **User-side verification of the deposit target contract address.** Users should verify the deposit-destination address displayed in the wallet's transaction-preview screen against the protocol's canonical staking contract address published in its official documentation. This is the user-side fallback when wallet-side verification is absent.
- **Community-side staking-interface domain allowlisting.** Formalise community-maintained staking-interface domain allowlists as a machine-readable registry that wallets and browsers can consume. The Lido and Rocket Pool communities' existing informal allowlists are a v0.x starting point.
- **Search-engine advertisement monitoring and takedown.** Protocols should monitor search-engine advertisement placements for their brand keywords and issue takedown requests for sponsored results serving typosquat domains. This is a reactive mitigation — it closes the phishing surface after the domain is detected — but reduces the campaign's effective window.
- **Cross-comparison with T4.008 and T4.009.** T4.008 mitigations (DEX-frontend domain-verification, swap-destination-address verification) and T4.009 mitigations (pre-token-claim interface verification) are structurally parallel: all three are user-interface phishing where the mitigation primitives are domain verification and destination-address cross-reference. A unified wallet-side "interface-authenticity verification" module covering staking (T3.005), swap (T4.008), and claim (T4.009) interfaces is a v0.x architecture target.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M18, OAK-M21, OAK-M30.

## Discussion

T3.005 closes a structural gap in OAK v0.1's T3 coverage. The existing T3 Techniques address market-manipulation distribution surfaces (sybil-bundled launch, wash-trade volume inflation, pump-and-dump coordination, influencer-amplified promotion-and-dump). T3.005 adds phishing-as-social-engineering-manipulation — the distribution surface is a fake interface that socially engineers the user into depositing to an attacker-controlled address. The social-engineering dimension (the user is manipulated via a fake interface, not via a coordinated market-signal) is why T3.005 is classified under T3 (Social Engineering / Community Manipulation) in addition to T4 (Phishing and User-Deception Operations).

The structural OAK lesson is that **staking-interface phishing targets the deposit action itself**, exploiting users who seek yield on idle ETH/SOL/ATOM. The user's mental model is "I am staking my assets to earn yield"; the attacker's extraction mechanism is "the deposit destination is my wallet, not the protocol's staking contract." The user receives no receipt token, but the absence of the receipt token is only detectable after the transaction confirms — the user has already sent the deposit.

The wallet-side deposit-destination verification mitigation is the highest-leverage point-of-action defence. If wallets compared the `to` address of a staking-deposit transaction against a canonical staking-contract-address registry at signing time, the T3.005 surface would be reduced to the domain-registration-and-certificate layer only — the user might visit the fake frontend, but the wallet would warn before the user signed the transaction. The absence of this verification across major wallet UIs as of OAK v0.1 is the primary structural condition that keeps T3.005 viable.

The protocol-side canonical-interface URL publication mitigation is the upstream structural fix. A machine-readable canonical-interface URL standard, adopted by staking protocols and consumed by wallets and browsers, would close the domain-typosquat surface at the browser layer before the user reaches the transaction-signing step. The standard would also serve the T4.008 and T4.009 surfaces (swap-interface and claim-interface phishing, respectively) and is a candidate cross-Technique mitigation primitive for OAK v0.x.

Promotion to `stable` will follow once (a) wallet-side deposit-destination verification against a canonical staking-contract-address registry is standardised across major wallet UIs, (b) the protocol-side canonical-interface URL publication standard is adopted by major staking protocols and consumed by wallets/browsers, and (c) the per-incident worked-example anchor set expands beyond the current single multi-year cohort file to include standalone named-incident worked examples with phishing-domain registration records and on-chain deposit-to-attacker transaction hashes.
