# OAK-T9.013 — Slippage-Manipulation Sandwich Attack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T5.004 (Sandwich MEV Extraction — T5.004 sandwiches regular swaps; T9.013 exploits the slippage-protection parameter itself as the profit surface)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (primary — Uniswap-style AMMs with user-specified slippage tolerance); Solana (Jupiter DCA / limit-order slippage surface); any chain with an AMM that exposes user-configurable slippage parameters in swap transactions
**First documented:** 2020–2021 (slippage-manipulation attacks characterised alongside the MEV sandwich literature)
**Aliases:** "slippage-tolerance exploitation", "max-slippage sandwich", "sandwich-via-slippage-setting", "slippage-override attack"

## Description

An MEV attack where the attacker exploits the victim's slippage-tolerance parameter — the `amountOutMin` or `sqrtPriceLimitX96` in the swap transaction — to extract value beyond a standard sandwich spread. The victim sets a high slippage tolerance (e.g., 5–10% on Uniswap) to ensure their transaction executes under volatile conditions; the attacker observes the high-slippage swap in the mempool, front-runs it to move the pool price to the victim's worst-acceptable slippage boundary, and back-runs to unwind at the post-victim-swap price. The attacker's profit is not merely the bid-ask spread but the full slippage band the victim authorised — the wider the victim's slippage setting, the larger the attacker's profit.

T9.013 is structurally distinct from T5.004 (Sandwich MEV Extraction): T5.004 profits from the bid-ask spread of the sandwiched trade — the attacker buys before the victim and sells after, capturing the price movement the victim's trade causes. T9.013 profits from the victim's own slippage-tolerance parameter — the attacker front-runs the price to the exact boundary of the victim's `amountOutMin`, the victim's trade executes at the worst-acceptable rate, and the attacker back-runs to unwind at the post-victim-swap price. The victim's slippage protection is the attacker's profit surface: the wider the slippage band, the deeper the attacker can push the price and the larger the extraction.

The structural insight is that the slippage-tolerance parameter — intended as a *protection* against adverse price movement — becomes an *attack surface* when the parameter is set too wide relative to the pool's depth. The attacker does not need to predict the price movement; the attacker *causes* the price movement to the exact boundary the victim authorised.

## Observed indicators

- Victim's swap transaction includes a high slippage-tolerance parameter (>1% for liquid pairs, >3–5% for medium-liquidity pairs) relative to the pool's typical per-block price movement.
- Attacker's front-run trade moves the pool price to the exact boundary of the victim's slippage limit — the victim's `amountOutMin` or `sqrtPriceLimitX96` is precisely hit.
- Attacker's profit per sandwich is unusually high relative to the victim's trade size — the profit includes the authorised slippage band, not merely the spread.
- Victim receives output tokens at the worst-acceptable rate specified in their transaction — the `amountOutMin` is exactly satisfied, never exceeded.
- Attacker's back-run trade unwinds the position at the post-victim-swap price, exiting entirely within the same block or a small number of subsequent blocks.

## Detection signals

- Mempool monitoring for swap transactions with high slippage tolerance relative to the pool's recent per-block price volatility; flag swaps where `amountOutMin` implies a slippage tolerance exceeding a configurable threshold (e.g., >2% for ETH/USDC on Uniswap V3).
- Wallet-side pre-trade simulation: simulate the swap against current pool depth and flag "your slippage tolerance of X% allows up to Y ETH of value to be extracted under a worst-case sandwich — consider reducing."
- MEV-bundle analysis: detect front-run-back-run pairs where the victim's output rate equals their specified `amountOutMin` exactly and the attacker's front-run price movement pushed the pool to that exact boundary.
- Post-trade execution analysis: compare the victim's realised output amount to the output amount that would have been received had the swap executed at the pre-front-run pool price; the difference is the sandwich extraction attributable to the slippage band.

## Mitigations

- **Wallet-side default slippage reduction (highest-leverage):** wallets should suggest default slippage of 0.1–0.5% for liquid pairs and flag any slippage setting above 2% as high-risk. The wallet UI should display the maximum extractable value at the user's chosen slippage tolerance under a worst-case sandwich scenario.
- **Auto-slippage calculation based on current pool depth:** wallets can query the pool's current depth and recent per-block price volatility to compute a recommended slippage tolerance that balances execution-certainty against sandwich-extraction risk, rather than offering a static default.
- **Private transaction relay for large swaps:** submitting swap transactions via Flashbots Protect, MEV-Share, or equivalent private relay prevents mempool observation of the slippage parameter, eliminating the attacker's ability to target the slippage band specifically (though standard sandwich risk remains for the trade itself).
- **User education on slippage-tolerance-as-attack-surface:** wallet UIs and DEX frontends should communicate that the slippage tolerance parameter is not merely an execution-certainty setting but a **value-authorisation ceiling** — the user is authorising up to X% of their trade value to be extracted by any observer who can move the pool price to the boundary.
- **Post-trade slippage-extraction transparency:** DEX frontends should display the realised slippage versus the authorised slippage post-trade; a trade that executed at exactly the `amountOutMin` boundary when the pool's pre-trade price would have yielded a materially better rate is a T9.013 indicator that the user should be warned about.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M09, OAK-M11, OAK-M16.

## Discussion

T9.013 reframes the slippage-tolerance parameter from a *protection* mechanism to an *attack surface*. The user sets slippage tolerance to ensure their transaction executes under volatile conditions — a rational choice when the alternative is a failed transaction and wasted gas. But the same parameter defines the maximum value an MEV searcher can extract from the user's trade: the wider the band, the deeper the attacker can push the price and the larger the extraction. The user's "protection" is the attacker's profit ceiling.

The structural distinction from T5.004 (Sandwich MEV) is the profit mechanism. T5.004 profits from the **spread** — the price movement the victim's own trade causes, captured by front-running before and back-running after. T9.013 profits from the **band** — the price movement the attacker *induces* to reach the victim's authorised slippage boundary. In a T5.004 sandwich, the victim's slippage tolerance is a constraint on the attacker's profit (the attacker cannot push the price beyond the victim's `amountOutMin` without the victim's transaction reverting). In T9.013, the victim's slippage tolerance **is** the profit surface — the attacker deliberately pushes the price to the exact boundary to maximise extraction.

The composition with T5.004 is tight: most operational sandwiches exploit both the spread (T5.004) and the slippage band (T9.013) simultaneously. The attacker front-runs the victim's trade (T5.004), which moves the price in the attacker's favour, and the attacker assesses whether the victim's slippage tolerance permits pushing the price further to the `amountOutMin` boundary for additional extraction (T9.013). The two Techniques are structural siblings that co-occur in practice; the distinguishing signal for T9.013-specific extraction is whether the victim's output amount equals the `amountOutMin` exactly — indicating the attacker pushed the price to the boundary — versus exceeding it marginally — indicating a standard spread-only sandwich.

The `observed` maturity (rather than `emerging`) reflects that T9.013 is an operational reality in the MEV ecosystem with extensive bundle-level empirical evidence. The Technique is observable in EigenPhi's bundle data, Flashbots' relay data, and on-chain transaction traces; the attack mechanism is well-characterised in MEV research. The maturity question is whether the **detection-and-mitigation surface** at the wallet and DEX-frontend layer has matured — wallet-side slippage warnings are inconsistent across vendors, and auto-slippage calculation based on pool depth is not yet standard. The attack is `observed`; the defence is `emerging`.

A cross-chain observation: the slippage-manipulation surface is most acute on chains with transparent public mempools (Ethereum L1, BSC) where the attacker can observe the victim's exact slippage parameter before constructing the sandwich. On Solana, the absence of a public mempool shifts the observation surface to the leader-scheduling layer — the leader who constructs the block can observe incoming transactions and sandwich them, but non-leader searchers cannot. The Solana surface is therefore narrower in the searcher set (limited to the current leader and its allied searchers) but the extraction-per-sandwich is structurally identical — the leader who observes a high-slippage swap can push the price to the `amountOutMin` boundary and capture the full authorised band.

A user-side behavioural observation: wallet UIs that present slippage tolerance as a percentage (e.g., "Slippage: 5%") convey a false sense of modesty — 5% of a $100K trade is $5K, an amount no user would knowingly authorise for extraction. The remediating UI pattern is to display the **absolute value** of the authorised slippage band in the user's base currency: "Your slippage tolerance of 5% authorises up to $5,000 in potential value extraction on this trade." This framing reframes the parameter from an abstract percentage to a concrete value-authorisation ceiling and is the highest-leverage per-user T9.013 mitigation.
