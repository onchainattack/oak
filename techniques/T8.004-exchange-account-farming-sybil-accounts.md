# OAK-T8.004 — Exchange Account Farming / Sybil Account Creation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T8 (Operator Continuity / Attribution Signals)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (exchange-side operational surface)
**First documented:** 2011–2013 (BTC-e era — account-farming infrastructure as systemic exchange-laundering rail); formalised as a distinct detection surface 2017–2025
**Aliases:** "exchange account farming", "Sybil exchange accounts", "synthetic-identity onboarding", "KYC factory", "account-rotation laundering", "verification-farm accounts"

## Description

The operational reuse pattern in which an attacker creates and maintains multiple exchange accounts — each associated with a distinct but synthetic, stolen, or fabricated identity — to circumvent per-account withdrawal limits, KYC thresholds, transaction-monitoring triggers, and per-account velocity checks. T8.004 is structurally distinct from on-chain address clustering (T8.001) and cross-chain operator continuity (T8.002) because the reuse surface is at the **exchange-account identity layer** — the attacker reuses the *account-creation infrastructure* (document templates, identity-vetting scripts, IP/device routing) rather than the on-chain address or operator graph.

The critical operational insight is that T8.004 converts the KYC barrier from a detection surface into a scaling surface: instead of avoiding verification, the attacker uses synthetic or stolen identity documents to create *more* verified accounts than a single natural person could hold, turning the exchange's own compliance infrastructure into a capacity multiplier. Each account individually passes KYC; the operational security failure is the structural pattern of multiple accounts sharing common creation infrastructure — same IP ranges, same device fingerprints, same document-template artefacts, same temporal creation bursts — that reveals the single-operator reality behind the nominally-distinct identities.

Key sub-patterns:

- **Document forgery / synthetic identity creation:** the attacker fabricates identity documents (passport scans, national-ID cards, utility-bill proofs of address) using template-manipulation tooling, generative-image models (post-2022), or purchased stolen-identity datasets. The documents satisfy exchange automated-KYC checks but exhibit template-level artefacts (identical background noise patterns, identical photo-crop boundaries, identical document-issuance metadata) when compared across accounts.
- **Withdrawal-limit circumvention via account rotation:** a single operator controls N verified accounts, each with a per-day withdrawal limit of X. The operator rotates withdrawals across accounts in a pattern designed to stay under per-account velocity triggers while aggregating to N×X daily throughput — the structural definition of the account-farming value proposition.
- **Same-device / same-IP correlation across nominally-distinct KYC identities:** the operator's infrastructure for account creation (device, browser fingerprint, IP-geolocation / VPN exit-node) is reused across multiple account-onboarding sessions. The exchange-side signal is a cluster of accounts with disjoint identity documents but identical or highly-correlated creation-fingerprint metadata.
- **Deposit-source clustering:** multiple nominally-distinct exchange accounts receive deposits from the same on-chain source clusters (same CEX hot wallet, same mixer withdrawal pattern, same bridge-deposit address) — a cross-layer correlation between T8.001 (on-chain funder cluster) and T8.004 (exchange-account identity cluster) that is the highest-signal detection primitive for account farming at exchange scale.
- **Cross-exchange manifestation:** the attacker maintains a "farm" of verified accounts distributed across multiple exchanges, with the same identity-document templates and device fingerprints reused per-exchange. This cross-exchange scaling converts a single-exchange detection problem into a cross-exchange information-sharing problem — the attacker's operational security failure is the persistence of creation-fingerprint reuse across venues that nominally do not share KYC data.

## Observed indicators

- Multiple exchange accounts with disjoint KYC identity documents (different names, different document numbers, different nationalities) but shared creation-fingerprint metadata: same browser-fingerprint hash, same device-ID, same IP range or ASN, same user-agent string, same screen-resolution fingerprint, same timezone-offset pattern.
- Temporal clustering of account creation: N accounts created within a narrow time window (minutes to hours) from the same infrastructure — the "batch-onboarding" signature.
- Document-template artefacts across KYC submissions: identical background-noise patterns, photo-crop boundaries, document-rotation angles, compression artefacts that indicate a common template-generation pipeline rather than independently-photographed physical documents.
- Geographic inconsistency: KYC documents claiming one country of residence (e.g., Germany) while the account-creation IP geolocates to a different region (e.g., Southeast Asia or Eastern Europe), with consistent IP/geo mismatch across multiple accounts in the cluster.
- Withdrawal-pattern rotation: sequential withdrawals from accounts A, B, C, ..., N within a short window, each just under the per-account daily limit, with downstream consolidation at a common destination address or deposit cluster.
- Deposit-source overlap: accounts that nominally belong to different individuals receive deposits from the same on-chain source (same CEX hot wallet, same DeFi protocol withdrawal, same mixer output) — the on-chain identity of the upstream source is the same while the KYC identity of the downstream account is different.
- Account dormancy followed by burst activity: accounts created, KYC-verified, and then left dormant for an extended period (weeks to months), followed by a sudden burst of deposit-and-withdraw activity — the "sleeper account" pattern characteristic of farm-maintained ready-to-use verified accounts.
- Identity-document reuse across exchanges: the same KYC document (or a minimally-altered template variant) appears in verification submissions at Exchange A and Exchange B, filed under different account names — an indicator that only becomes visible at the cross-exchange information-sharing layer.

## Detection signals

- **Device-fingerprint clustering:** compute a browser/device fingerprint per KYC-onboarding session; cluster sessions by fingerprint similarity; flag clusters where >=3 distinct KYC identities share a single device fingerprint. The fingerprint should incorporate browser-canvas hash, WebGL renderer hash, installed-font list, screen-resolution tuple, timezone-offset, and user-agent string — a compound fingerprint is harder to spoof than any single dimension.
- **IP / ASN correlation:** for each KYC-onboarding session, record the IP address and ASN; cluster accounts whose onboarding IPs share an ASN and a /24 subnet (or the same VPN exit-node IP); flag clusters where the IP-geolocated country does not match the claimed residence country in the KYC document for >=2 accounts in the cluster.
- **Document-template artefact detection:** for each KYC document image, compute a perceptual hash (pHash) and a noise-profile fingerprint; cluster documents by pHash distance and noise-profile similarity; flag clusters where documents from distinct claimed identities exhibit template-level artefact similarity above threshold. This primitive requires exchange-side access to the raw KYC document image, which is the canonical detection surface available to the exchange compliance team but not to external observers.
- **Temporal creation clustering:** for each account-creation event, record the timestamp; compute the inter-creation interval distribution per (IP, device-fingerprint) tuple; flag tuples where the inter-creation intervals form a tight cluster (all accounts created within the same hour/day) rather than a random distribution — the batch-onboarding signature.
- **Cross-layer correlation (T8.001 × T8.004):** build a bipartite graph connecting on-chain deposit-source clusters (T8.001) and exchange-account identity clusters (T8.004); flag edges where a single on-chain source cluster deposits to multiple exchange accounts with disjoint KYC identities — the cross-layer correlation that confirms account farming rather than coincidental shared-IP usage. This is the highest-signal detection primitive available to external observers (blockchain forensics vendors, law enforcement) who do not have access to the exchange's internal KYC document store.
- **Account-lifecycle behavioural clustering:** for each verified account, compute a behavioural fingerprint from deposit/withdrawal patterns, trading activity, and login cadence; cluster accounts by behavioural fingerprint; flag clusters where accounts with disjoint KYC identities share a single behavioural fingerprint — the post-onboarding signal that confirms the single-operator reality behind the multi-identity surface.
- **Cross-exchange KYC-document matching:** for exchanges that participate in KYC-information-sharing consortia or that receive law-enforcement-subpoena cross-referencing, compute document-hash matches across KYC submissions at different exchanges; flag identity-document instances that appear at multiple exchanges under different account names — the cross-exchange signal. This primitive is currently limited to law-enforcement-access and regulated-information-sharing frameworks; it is not generally available to exchange compliance teams operating independently.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2017-2025-exchange-account-farming-cohort.md`](../examples/2017-2025-exchange-account-farming-cohort.md) — Cross-exchange account farming cohort, 2017–2025 — covers BTC-e pre-seizure account infrastructure, Hydra market linked exchange accounts, and DPRK IT-worker program (fake identities used to open exchange accounts for laundering). The canonical first-example survey for T8.004.
- [`examples/2019-06-binance-kyc-leak.md`](../examples/2019-06-binance-kyc-leak.md) — Binance KYC data leak via third-party vendor — 2019-06/2019-08. Approximately 60,000 customer KYC records exfiltrated from a third-party KYC processing vendor. The exfiltrated KYC documents, when cross-referenced with account-creation metadata, provide a forensic surface for detecting synthetic-identity clusters — documents that share template artefacts despite nominally belonging to different individuals. Canonical early-exchange illustration of the vendor-as-attack-surface model for operator-side data compromise, with downstream T8.004 implications for synthetic-identity detection.
- **BTC-e account infrastructure (2011–2017).** Per `[dojbtce2017]`, the BTC-e exchange operated with effectively no substantive KYC/AML controls from inception; the platform's user base included a high proportion of accounts registered under fabricated or synthetic identity documents, with account-creation infrastructure concentrated in Eastern European and Russian-language IP ranges. The account-farming pattern at BTC-e was structural rather than incidental — the platform's business model depended on providing a high-volume, low-friction exchange surface that did not distinguish between natural-person and farmed accounts. The 2017-07 domain seizure and FinCEN $110M penalty make BTC-e the canonical account-farming-as-business-model case.
- **Hydra market cashout infrastructure (2015–2022).** Per `[chainalysis2022hydra]` and BKA post-seizure reporting, the Hydra darknet marketplace (Russian-language, 2015–2022; seized by German BKA April 2022) operated a systematic cashout infrastructure that included a network of exchange accounts registered under synthetic or purchased identity documents at Russia-facing and Eastern European exchanges. The Hydra-linked exchange accounts functioned as the off-ramp layer for vendor proceeds, with account rotation used to stay under per-account monitoring thresholds. The April 2022 BKA seizure of Hydra's servers (hosted in Germany despite the service targeting a Russian-language user base — itself a T8.005-class operational security failure) included forensic material documenting the linked exchange-account network.
- **DPRK IT-worker program — exchange account creation (2018–present).** Per `[chainalysis2024dprk]`, `[fbidprkitworker2022]`, and OAK-G04 (DPRK IT-Worker Placement Scheme), DPRK-affiliated IT workers obtaining remote engineering roles at crypto and Web3 firms under fabricated identities have used their employment-derived identity documentation (company-issued email addresses, employment-verification letters, salary-payment bank-account records) to open verified exchange accounts that then serve as laundering rails for DPRK-attributed cyber-operations proceeds. The pattern is a structural T8.004 × OAK-G04 intersection: the IT-worker placement scheme generates legitimate-appearing identity documentation that the regime's laundering infrastructure uses to create verified exchange accounts that pass KYC checks because the underlying employment relationship is real — even though the worker's national identity is fabricated. The May 2022 joint State/Treasury/FBI advisory (`[fbidprkitworker2022]`) explicitly flags the exchange-account-opening step as a scheme indicator.
- **Eastern European cybercriminal network account farming (2017–present).** Per industry forensic reporting (Chainalysis, TRM Labs, Elliptic), Eastern European cybercriminal networks — including ransomware affiliate groups, darknet-market vendor collectives, and standalone laundering services — maintain "account farms" of verified exchange accounts across multiple exchanges, with account creation concentrated in jurisdictions with weaker document-verification infrastructure or through exchanges with legacy KYC processes that do not perform liveness-detection or document-tampering analysis. The account-farming infrastructure is sufficiently commoditised that verified exchange accounts are sold as a service on dark-web forums — a secondary market that is itself a detection signal when forum-listings can be correlated with on-chain deposit-address patterns. The scale of this activity is not precisely quantifiable in the public record but is consistently characterised by forensic vendors as a material fraction of exchange-facing illicit-finance throughput.
- **Garantex / Grinex account layer (2019–2025).** Per `[treasury2025garantexnetwork]` and `[trmlabs2025grinex]`, the Garantex exchange (sanctioned by OFAC April 2022; domain seized March 2025) and its successor Grinex (sanctioned August 2025) operated with account-registration practices that did not substantively verify the link between the claimed identity and the account controller. The persistent counterparty propagation pattern from Garantex to Grinex (see T8.001) has an account-farming dimension: the same underlying user base — including accounts registered under synthetic or borrowed identity documents — migrated from the sanctioned platform to the successor platform, carrying the identity-document templates and device fingerprints with them. The account-layer continuity across the brand rotation is the T8.004 complement to the T8.001 on-chain funder-graph continuity.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. T8.004 detection requires exchange-side access to KYC document images and account-creation session metadata (device fingerprints, IP logs) that is not available from on-chain data alone. The cross-layer correlation primitive (T8.001 × T8.004 bipartite graph) is implementable from on-chain data but produces candidate-flag outputs that require exchange-side confirmation.
- Vendor coverage: Chainalysis, TRM Labs, and Elliptic offer exchange-facing risk-scoring products that incorporate account-level behavioural clustering and deposit-source correlation — the T8.004 detection surface at the vendor layer.
- Exchange-native tooling: exchanges with internal compliance-engineering teams (Coinbase, Binance, Kraken, OKX) implement proprietary device-fingerprinting, IP-geolocation correlation, and document-template analysis as part of their KYC-onboarding pipelines — the primary operational deployment of T8.004 detection.
- Cross-exchange KYC-information-sharing: regulated-information-sharing frameworks (FATF Travel Rule implementations, regional AML information-sharing consortia) provide the legal substrate for cross-exchange KYC-document matching, though operational deployment at scale is a v0.x work-in-progress.

## Mitigations

- **Exchange compliance teams:** implement device-fingerprint clustering at KYC-onboarding time; flag clusters where >=3 distinct KYC identities share a single device fingerprint for manual review. See OAK-M04 (Funder-Graph Clustering) for the analogous on-chain primitive; the exchange-side equivalent applies the same clustering logic to the account-creation metadata surface.
- **Exchange compliance teams:** implement IP/ASN correlation and geo-to-claimed-residence mismatch flagging at onboarding; maintain a watchlist of ASNs and VPN exit-node IPs that exhibit elevated account-farming incidence. See OAK-M07 (Cross-Chain Attribution Graph) for the analogous cross-chain primitive; the exchange-side equivalent operates at the account-identity layer.
- **Exchange compliance teams:** implement document-template artefact detection (pHash clustering, noise-profile fingerprinting) on KYC document images at submission time; flag document clusters with above-threshold artefact similarity across nominally-distinct identities.
- **Exchange compliance teams:** implement post-onboarding behavioural clustering; flag accounts that exhibit behavioural convergence (same deposit/withdrawal patterns, same trading cadence, same login hours) despite nominally belonging to different individuals — the sleeper-account activation signal.
- **Forensic vendors and law enforcement:** deploy the T8.001 × T8.004 cross-layer correlation primitive: build a bipartite graph of on-chain deposit-source clusters and exchange-account identity clusters; flag edges where a single on-chain source deposits to multiple exchange accounts with disjoint KYC identities. This is the highest-signal detection primitive available to external observers.
- **Cross-exchange coordination:** expand KYC-information-sharing frameworks to include document-hash matching across exchanges; flag identity-document instances that appear at multiple exchanges under different account names. This requires legal and regulatory infrastructure that is currently jurisdiction-dependent and incomplete at v0.1.
- **Regulators:** mandate liveness-detection (video-selfie with challenge-response) and document-tampering analysis at the exchange-onboarding layer as part of AML compliance requirements. Exchanges that rely on static-document-upload KYC without liveness-detection are structurally vulnerable to synthetic-identity account farming.

## Discussion

T8.004 occupies a structurally important position in the T8 taxonomy because it is the only T8 Technique that operates entirely at the **exchange-account identity layer** rather than the on-chain address layer. T8.001 (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) clusters on-chain addresses by funder-graph proximity; T8.002 (Cross-Chain Operator Continuity) links per-chain operator profiles across chains; T8.003 (On-Chain Transaction Graph De-Anonymization) attributes on-chain addresses to real-world entities via graph analysis. T8.004 is the complement that covers the case where the attacker's operational security failure is not on-chain address reuse but **off-chain identity-creation infrastructure reuse** — the same device, the same IP, the same document template, the same behavioural fingerprint appearing across accounts that are nominally distinct at the KYC layer.

The defender-side challenge at T8.004 is a data-access asymmetry: the highest-signal detection primitives (device-fingerprint clustering, document-template artefact detection, IP/geo mismatch flagging) require exchange-side access to KYC-onboarding session metadata that is not available to external observers. Forensic vendors and law enforcement are limited to the cross-layer correlation primitive (T8.001 × T8.004 bipartite graph) — a signal that produces candidate flags requiring exchange-side confirmation. Exchange compliance teams have the full detection surface but lack cross-exchange visibility. The structural remediation is cross-exchange KYC-information-sharing, which is currently jurisdiction-dependent and incomplete.

False-positive sources at the T8.004 layer: shared-office or shared-household IP usage (multiple legitimate users behind a single NAT); corporate-account onboarding where multiple employees use the same company-issued device for personal account creation; legitimate users who change devices or IPs between account-creation sessions and coincidentally match another user's fingerprint. The temporal-clustering and document-artefact primitives are higher-precision discriminators than IP or device-fingerprint alone: coincidental shared-IP usage does not produce temporal clustering or document-template artefacts.

The T8.004 detection surface is likely to evolve rapidly post-2025 as generative-AI identity-document fabrication tooling increases the supply of KYC-passing synthetic identity documents. Exchange compliance teams will need to shift from "does this document look real?" (a question that AI-fabricated documents increasingly answer "yes" to) to "does this document look like the other documents submitted from this device/IP?" — a shift from absolute document-veracity assessment to relative document-template clustering. T8.004's detection primitives are designed for the latter regime.

Cross-reference note: T8.004 intersects with the identity-document-reuse concepts described as "KYC Reuse" in informal taxonomy discussions — the structural relationship is that document reuse *across exchanges with the same identity* (the KYC Reuse pattern) and document template reuse *across accounts with different identities* (the T8.004 pattern) are two manifestations of the same underlying exchange-account-identity operational surface. A future v0.x sub-Technique split may separate these two patterns into distinct T8.004 sub-axes if they reach independent characterisation maturity.
