# OAK-T12.002 — Fake-Mint / Counterfeit Collection

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T12 (NFT-Specific Patterns)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM (primary; Ethereum / Polygon), Solana (Magic Eden cohort)
**First documented:** 2021 (early counterfeit cohort on OpenSea); industrial-scale through 2022 Discord-compromise wave
**Aliases:** "fake mint", "counterfeit collection", "copymint", "spoofed collection", "impersonation drop"

## Description

A class of NFT-layer flows in which an attacker deploys a counterfeit ERC-721 / ERC-1155 collection (or a counterfeit Solana NFT collection) that mimics the artefact set of a legitimate collection — name, symbol, off-chain metadata URI, art, sometimes deployer-address-pattern — and routes victim attention to the counterfeit deployment instead of the genuine one. The attacker captures value through one of three sub-patterns:

- **(a) Phishing-mint** — victims sign a `mint` (or `safeMint`) call on a counterfeit-collection contract, paying mint price to an attacker-controlled treasury, in the belief that they are minting from the genuine project. Entry vector is typically a phishing dApp, a compromised X / Discord account, or a malicious mint-page link.
- **(b) Marketplace-listed clones** — the attacker deploys a counterfeit collection and lists it on OpenSea, Blur, or Magic Eden under search-engine-discoverable naming that overlaps with a legitimate collection; victims purchase counterfeit items at floor-price-equivalent prices.
- **(c) Discord-compromise-driven fake mint announcements** — attacker compromises the official Discord (or X account) of a legitimate project and posts a "stealth mint" / "extra mint" / "free claim" announcement that links to a counterfeit-collection mint page. This sub-pattern overlaps in entry vector with `OAK-T4.005` (`setApprovalForAll` NFT Drainer): the same compromised Discord typically distributes both mint-payment scams (T12.002) and approval-grant scams (T4.005) in the same campaign.

From a defender's perspective, T12.002 is the **identity-confusion** failure mode and is structurally distinct from `OAK-T4.005`. T4.005 abuses *authorisation* — the victim grants the attacker operator authority over assets the victim already owns. T12.002 abuses *identity* — the victim transfers value (mint payment, or purchase price) to a counterfeit contract believing it is the genuine collection. The two routinely co-occur in the same campaign because they share the Discord-compromise / phishing-dApp entry vector, but the defender controls live in different layers: T4.005 mitigations sit at the wallet (operator allowlists, approval simulation), while T12.002 mitigations sit at the marketplace and at the verification-badge layer (canonical-contract allowlists, verified-creator badges, image-similarity-driven counterfeit removal).

## Observed indicators

- A newly-deployed ERC-721 / ERC-1155 contract whose `name()`, `symbol()`, and off-chain `tokenURI` metadata closely match those of a legitimate collection but whose deployer address has no funder-graph connection to the genuine project's deployer cluster.
- Marketplace listings whose collection name is a Unicode-lookalike, plural / singular variant, or character-substituted variant of a legitimate collection (e.g., capital-I-vs-lowercase-l, zero-vs-O, added "Official" suffix).
- A spike in mint-page traffic to a non-canonical contract address shortly after a Discord-channel announcement or X-account post from an account historically associated with a legitimate project.
- Mint-page front-end whose visible artefacts (logo, copy, navigation) clone a legitimate project but whose `mint` call routes to a different contract address than the project's previously-published canonical contract.
- Counterfeit contract's `mint` recipient (treasury / withdrawal address) clusters with previously-flagged scam infrastructure rather than the genuine project's treasury cluster.

## Detection signals

- **Per-marketplace counterfeit-collection detection at indexing time** — image-perceptual-hash and metadata-similarity scoring against a registry of verified collections; flag and review newly-indexed collections whose similarity exceeds a calibrated threshold against any verified collection. OpenSea publicly described this approach (image-recognition plus human review) in May 2022.
- **Canonical-contract-allowlist enforcement** — for every legitimate collection, publish (and consume) a canonical contract address; marketplaces, wallets, and aggregators surface a strong "unverified collection" warning when a contract claiming a known name does not match the canonical entry. The Magic Eden January 2023 incident — where an indexer bug allowed unverified NFTs to be listed under verified collection pages — is the canonical failure case for this control.
- **Metadata-similarity scoring** — combine image-perceptual hash, metadata-URI hash, and name / symbol edit-distance into a similarity score; counterfeit collections typically score high on art similarity but have a different deployer-address cluster, treasury cluster, and contract-creation timestamp than the genuine project.
- **Off-chain telemetry** — integrate Discord-server-compromise reports, project-team out-of-band confirmations, and X-account-takeover signals as pre-event indicators for in-progress T12.002 campaigns; the same telemetry feeds T4.005 detection because the entry vector is shared.
- **Mint-payment-cluster heuristic** — for in-progress mints, monitor the recipient address of mint payments; alert when the recipient clusters with previously-flagged scam infrastructure rather than with the project's published treasury.

## Real-world examples

- **OpenSea counterfeit-collection cohort — Ethereum / Polygon — 2021–2022** — OpenSea publicly stated in early 2022 that more than 80% of items created via its free minting tool were plagiarised works, fake collections, or spam. In May 2022 OpenSea launched an image-recognition-plus-human-review system for `copymint` removal and expanded its verification programme. The cohort framing is the canonical reference because per-incident attribution is sparse — counterfeit collections are deployed at scale rather than as named one-shot incidents. Source: `[openseamoderation2022]`.
- **BAYC Discord-compromise fake-mint wave — Ethereum — April–June 2022** — Multiple compromises of the Bored Ape Yacht Club ecosystem's Discord (April 2022 webhook compromise, June 2022 community-manager-account compromise) distributed counterfeit-mint-page links for "stealth mints" and Otherside-related drops. The June 2022 incident drained \~179.9 ETH (\~\$320K) across 32 NFTs (1 BAYC, 2 MAYC, 5 Otherdeeds, 1 BAKC). The campaign mixes T12.002 (counterfeit mint pages capturing purported-mint payments) and T4.005 (`setApprovalForAll` drains routed through the same phishing dApp) — the Discord-compromise entry vector is shared. Sources: `[baycdiscord2022]`, `[fortunebaycjune2022]`.
- **Premint platform compromise — Ethereum — 2022-07-17** — Attackers injected malicious JavaScript into the Premint NFT mint-coordination platform's front end; the injected pop-up solicited a wallet "verification" that, in practice, executed `setApprovalForAll` against a drainer operator. Loss \~\$375K (\~314 NFTs at first count, \~\$421K and 320 NFTs by final tally), including BAYC, Goblintown, and Otherside items. The Premint case is a front-end-side example that overlaps both T12.002 (counterfeit mint-flow framing distributed via a trusted mint-coordination platform) and T4.005 (the technical extraction was an approval-drainer); included here because the user-facing framing was a fake-mint flow. Source: `[certikpremint2022]`.
- **Magic Eden unverified-listing exploit — Solana — 2023-01-04** — A bug in Magic Eden's indexer (Snappy Marketplace / Pro Trade tools) allowed unverified NFTs to appear under verified collection pages for ABC and y00ts; 25 counterfeit NFTs were sold across 4 collections at 20–50 SOL each before Magic Eden patched the indexer and refunded affected buyers. This is the canonical infrastructure-failure example for T12.002 detection sub-control "canonical-contract-allowlist enforcement at the marketplace indexer". Sources: `[magicedeny00ts2023]`, `[chainalysisnftcounterfeit2022]`.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (NFT-marketplace-side moderation is out of MG's operator-behaviour-detection scope).
- **Marketplace-side moderation as primary access path:** OpenSea (image-recognition + human review pipeline since May 2022, verification-badge programme), Magic Eden (verified-collection allowlist; the y00ts incident demonstrates the failure mode when the allowlist is enforced inconsistently across indexer paths), Blur (verified-collection layer; less public documentation of the moderation pipeline as of v0.1).
- **Vendor coverage for off-chain artefact-similarity detection:** Yuga Labs and other large NFT IP holders run trademark-protection workflows that file removal requests against marketplace counterfeit listings; specialised IP-protection vendors (Red Points, MarqVision) operate counterfeit-NFT monitoring services on a commercial basis.

## Mitigations

- **NFT marketplaces (highest-leverage):**
  - Maintain a canonical-contract allowlist per verified collection; refuse to surface listings or mint-pages under a verified collection name from a non-canonical contract.
  - Run image-perceptual-hash and metadata-similarity detection at indexing time; flag newly-listed collections whose similarity to a verified collection exceeds the calibrated threshold.
  - Issue verified-creator badges with strong identity binding (X handle, project domain, deployer-address proof) and surface the badge prominently on collection and item pages; treat absence-of-badge as the dominant warning signal for users.
  - Provide a low-friction trademark-takedown path for IP holders.
- **NFT projects:**
  - Publish the canonical contract address on the project's domain, X bio, and Discord pinned channel; instruct users that any mint-page address divergence from the published canonical address is a counterfeit.
  - Adopt Discord-moderation hardening (admin 2FA enforcement, webhook-creation monitoring, channel-lock procedures around scheduled mints) — this overlaps with T4.005 mitigations because the entry vector is shared.
  - Use out-of-band channels (project domain announcements, signed-message attestations) for high-stakes mint announcements rather than Discord-only / X-only push.
- **Wallets:**
  - Surface the contract address (not only the collection name) at mint-signature time; display "this contract has not been seen before" or "this contract does not match the canonical address for collection X" warnings where canonical-allowlist data is available.
  - Integrate counterfeit-collection feeds from marketplace partners and from on-chain similarity-detection services.
- **Users / collectors:**
  - Verify the contract address against the project's canonical published address before signing a mint transaction; ignore Discord-only / DM-only mint announcements as a categorical rule for high-value collections.

## Discussion

T12.002 is the cleanest "identity-confusion" Technique in OAK v0.1 and is the natural counterpart to `OAK-T4.005` (authorisation-misuse) at the NFT layer. The boundary the T12 Tactic page draws is intentional: a single Discord-compromise campaign typically distributes both T12.002 mint-payment scams and T4.005 approval-drainer flows from the same phishing dApp, but the defender controls differ — T12.002 lives at the marketplace and verification-badge layer, T4.005 lives at the wallet operator-allowlist layer. Contributors writing future T12.002 cases should preserve the T12.002-vs-T4.005 boundary explicitly: classify by *which control would have prevented the loss* (marketplace canonical-contract allowlist → T12.002; wallet operator allowlist + approval simulation → T4.005), not by the entry vector.

Open question for v0.x: whether to split T12.002 into separate sub-Techniques per sub-pattern (a) / (b) / (c). The current single-Technique framing reflects the shared identity-confusion predicate and the largely-overlapping mitigation surface (canonical-contract allowlists protect against all three sub-patterns). A split would be warranted if defender controls diverge meaningfully across sub-patterns in future marketplace architecture (e.g., if mint-page-allowlisting at the wallet layer becomes a distinct control class from marketplace-listing allowlisting).

Marketplace moderation is uneven across venues. OpenSea has the most public documentation of its detection pipeline (image-recognition + human review since May 2022); Magic Eden's January 2023 incident demonstrated that even an explicit verified-collection allowlist can be defeated by indexer bugs that bypass the allowlist check; Blur's counterfeit-detection pipeline is less publicly documented as of v0.1 and is an open contribution target for future versions.
