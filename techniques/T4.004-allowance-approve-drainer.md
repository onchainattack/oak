# OAK-T4.004 — Allowance / Approve-Pattern Drainer

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM (primary)
**First documented:** widespread from approximately 2022; characterised in `[checkpoint2023drainers]`
**Aliases:** "approve drainer", "unlimited allowance phishing", "ERC-20 approve scam"

## Description

A class of phishing flows in which a victim is induced to submit an on-chain `approve(spender, value)` transaction granting an attacker-controlled address the standard ERC-20 allowance (typically `uint256.max` — effectively unlimited) over one or more of the victim's tokens. Once the allowance is granted on-chain, the attacker exercises it via `transferFrom(victim, recipient, value)` to drain the victim's balance of the approved tokens. From a defender's perspective, T4.004 is structurally distinct from T4.001 (Permit2 signature-based authority misuse) because the authority artefact lives **on-chain** as an `Allowance` record rather than as an off-chain signed payload — meaning per-token, per-spender allowance monitoring at the wallet or risk-team layer can detect the granted authority before the `transferFrom` is exercised.

T4.004 is one of the dominant flows in the modern wallet-drainer service ecosystem (`[slowmist2024report]`); per `[checkpoint2023drainers]`, recurring on-chain spender addresses tied to Angel Drainer, Inferno Drainer, and successor service families exhibit the canonical approve-then-transferFrom flow.

## Observed indicators

- ERC-20 `Approval(owner, spender, value)` event with `value = uint256.max` (`0xff…ff`) or large round numbers (`1e36`, `2^96`) naming an attacker-clustered spender, shortly preceded by victim interaction with a phishing site or compromised front-end.
- ABI signature `approve(address,uint256)` (selector `0x095ea7b3`) sent to an ERC-20 with the spender field pointing at a freshly deployed contract with no other inbound legitimate flow.
- Per-spender allowance distribution showing a single spender holding outsized aggregate allowance across many distinct token contracts or distinct victim wallets.
- Attacker-side `transferFrom` (selector `0x23b872dd`) calls executed shortly after the corresponding `Approval` event (single-block to single-day window typical).
- Drainer-router contract addresses on the receiving end matching `[checkpoint2023drainers]` / ScamSniffer / SlowMist threat-feed entries (Angel Drainer routers are publicly enumerated).
- Cohort-funder graph: drainer-router deployers cluster on the funder-graph heuristic (OAK-T8.001), enabling forward attribution of new operator addresses before victim flow accumulates.

## Detection signals

- Real-time on-chain: `Approval` event watcher with threshold rules — alert on `value >= 2^96` to a non-allowlisted spender; cross-reference spender against drainer-cluster allowlist before the attacker exercises.
- Per-EOA approval-velocity outlier detection: a high-balance EOA generating multiple distinct approve grants to non-allowlisted spenders within a short window indicates phishing-context interaction.
- Per-spender, per-chain heat map of held allowances — forward-looking indicator of which addresses are actively phishing.
- Mempool watcher (OAK-M06): pre-block telemetry on `transferFrom` calls targeting known drainer-router addresses provides intervention window for monitored treasury wallets.
- Wallet-side (OAK-M31): surface the user's outstanding allowances at sign time; warn on `approve(uint256.max)` to non-allowlisted spenders; simulate the approve transaction's downstream effect.
- Post-event forensic: cluster victim wallets by common-spender approval to identify the size of the campaign cohort.

## Real-world examples

- **Lawliet Bored Ape phishing — Ethereum — 2022-01-31** — Loss \~\$2.7M across 13 NFTs (7 BAYC, 5 MAYC, 1 Doodle). The collector engaged with a fake "extra mint" announcement posted in the compromised Moschi Mochi Discord, sent 0.49 ETH for 14 scam NFTs, then signed a sequence of `setApprovalForAll` (T4.005) and `approve` calls; the attacker exercised these via `transferFrom` to drain the collection. T4.004/T4.005 chained example. Source: `[theblock2022boredape]`.
- **CoinStats Snap supply-chain compromise — multi-chain — 2024-06** — `[coinstats20240622]`, `[slowmistcoinstats2024]`, `[blocksec_coinstats2024]`, `[peckshieldcoinstats2024]`, `[zachxbtcoinstats2024]` document compromise of CoinStats's portfolio-tracker dApp surface affecting approximately 1,590 wallets; signing prompts authorised attacker-controlled `approve` calls across multiple ERC-20 positions. The compromise sits at the dApp-distribution layer and chains into the canonical T4.004 extraction.
- **DEXX exchange-extension drainer — multi-chain — 2024-10/11** — `[dexxstatements2024]`, `[slowmistdexx2024]`, `[zachxbtdexx2024]`, `[peckshielddexx2024]` document the DEXX trading-extension compromise; signing prompts originating inside the trusted DEXX surface authorised `approve` and `transferFrom` flows across the user base. Cohort-attribution analysis attributes the compromise to operator-infrastructure rather than user-side phishing — same on-chain artefact (T4.004 `Approval` event) with distinct entry vector.
- **Pink Drainer extractions — multi-chain — 2024** — `[scamsniffer2024pink]` documents Pink Drainer's >\$3M cumulative extractions across 2024 including the OpenAI CTO and Orbiter Finance founder cases; `approve` and Permit2-signature flows were used in combination depending on token support. The cohort's October 2024 retirement transferred operations to Angel Drainer per `[scamsniffer2024lineage]`.
- **Inferno Drainer canonical victim cohort — multi-chain — 2023-2024** — Per `[scamsniffer2024lineage]` and `[checkpoint2023drainers]`, Inferno Drainer's recurring spender-address infrastructure produced a sustained T4.004 victim flow across 2023 (\~\$81M from 134K users over nine months per ScamSniffer's 2023 dataset) and continued through 2024 with \~22% market share Q1–Q2 and \~43% Q3 before the October 2024 transfer to Angel Drainer.
- **Wallet-drainer ecosystem aggregate — multi-chain — 2024** — `[slowmist2024report]` documents \~\$494M in user losses across 2024; \~56.7% used permit signatures (T4.001) and the remaining substantial fraction used the on-chain approve-pattern flow described here.
- **OAK-G02 attribution (Drainer-as-a-Service operator family)** — see `actors/OAK-G02-drainer-services.md`. T4.004 is the canonical service-layer Technique alongside T4.001.

## Mitigations

- **OAK-M08 (Per-Spender Approval Audit and Revocation):** revoke unused allowances on a recurring cadence using revoke.cash or comparable tools; never grant `uint256.max` allowance to an unfamiliar spender; treat any allowance grant from a phishing-context interaction as compromised even if no `transferFrom` has yet executed (revoke immediately).
- **OAK-M31 (EIP-712 Permit Display and Signing Risk Heuristics) — extends to plain `approve`:** simulate the approve transaction's downstream effect at sign time (will granting this allowance allow funds to be drained?); warn on `uint256.max` allowance to non-allowlisted spenders; surface outstanding allowances prominently in the wallet UI.
- **OAK-M30 (Per-dApp Domain and App-Store-Package Allowlist):** block `approve` signing prompts originating from non-allowlisted domains.
- **OAK-M19 (Air-Gap Cold Wallet Signing):** for treasury-class wallets, route approve signing through air-gapped device with explicit display of spender, value, and token contract.
- **OAK-M06 (Mempool Pre-Block Telemetry):** organisational watchers for known-drainer-router target addresses to intercept exercise of granted allowances.
- **Token contract designers:** the ERC-20 increase/decrease-allowance pattern reduces the consequence of a stale allowance; new token deployments should consider time-limited allowance designs.

## Discussion

The T4.001 / T4.004 split is partially historical: Permit2 (and EIP-2612 Permit) became dominant precisely because they offered phishing-as-a-service operators a cleaner workflow (signed off-chain payload, no on-chain pre-event signal). T4.004 is the older, on-chain-visible flow. Both remain in active use; some incidents combine both. Contributors writing future T4.001/T4.004 examples should preserve the **on-chain-vs-off-chain authority artefact** distinction explicitly because the corresponding detection and mitigation surfaces differ.

False-positive reasoning: legitimate DEX/aggregator interaction routinely produces `approve(uint256.max)` to canonical router contracts (Uniswap V2 router, 0x ExchangeProxy, 1inch AggregationRouter, CowSwap settlement). The high-precision signal is grantee-clustering against a known-bad allowlist combined with originating-domain provenance. Edge case: legitimate user revocation followed by re-approval to the same legitimate router produces a benign `approve` event that may briefly cluster with phishing-context activity at the per-EOA velocity layer; cohort-funder graph attribution is the disambiguator.

v0.x deferred items: cross-chain approval observability (the same drainer operator deploys functionally identical router contracts on multiple EVM chains; current allowlists are mostly per-chain), and ERC-4337 approval semantics where the `approve` is invoked by the smart account's `executeBatch` — wallet-UX-layer interception requires parsing the inner UserOperation calldata rather than the outer transaction.
