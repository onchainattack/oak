# OAK-T8.002 — Cross-Chain Operator Continuity

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T8 (Operational Reuse)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** cross-chain
**First documented:** systematic 2023 onward as multi-chain operator activity has scaled
**Aliases:** "multi-chain operator profile", "cross-chain attribution", "chain-hop operator"

## Description

A class of OAK-T8 patterns in which a single operator's footprint is observable across multiple chains — same funding-source patterns, same deployment fingerprints, same off-chain artefacts (social handles, hosting infrastructure, code-style fingerprints), or same downstream laundering destinations. Distinct from OAK-T8.001 (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) because T8.001's canonical attribution surface is a *single-chain* funder graph; T8.002 covers the case where the operator's per-chain funder graphs are *individually* coherent but become *jointly* attributable only via cross-chain analysis.

T8.002 is structurally important for OAK-Gnn (Threat Actors) attribution at the modern multi-chain scale. OAK-G01 Lazarus Group activity is increasingly *only* attributable via T8.002 methodology — the same operator deploys across Ethereum, Solana, Bitcoin, Tron, and various L2 environments, and per-chain attribution alone undersells the operator profile.

## Observed indicators

- Cross-chain funding-source overlap: the same upstream addresses fund operator-controlled deployments on multiple chains, often via a centralised exchange withdrawal or a bridge-deposit hop.
- Cross-chain deployment-fingerprint reuse: identical compiler metadata, constructor parameter shapes, role mappings, salt patterns, or tooling-fingerprints across chains.
- Cross-chain laundering-destination overlap: proceeds from operator-controlled extractions on multiple chains converge at the same off-ramp infrastructure (CEX deposit clusters, mixer services, cross-chain swap protocols — THORChain, LI.FI, Across).
- Off-chain operator continuity: shared social handles, shared hosting infrastructure, shared code-style fingerprints across per-chain operator deployments.
- Cross-chain victim-profiling reuse: drainer-kit operators reuse victim-classification logic and target-list infrastructure across per-chain affiliate streams.

## Detection signals

- **Cross-chain funder-graph extension:** extend per-chain funder-graph computation to a cross-chain graph by ingesting bridge-deposit / CEX-withdrawal events as cross-chain edges; identify connected components whose addresses span multiple chains.
- **Per-fingerprint cross-chain match:** maintain per-deployment-fingerprint indexes across chains; alert on fingerprint matches across operator-clustered addresses on different chains (Dune-style: `select fingerprint, count(distinct chain) as chains from deployments group by fingerprint having chains > 1`).
- **Laundering-destination convergence:** per-cross-chain-laundering-destination flow analysis; identify destinations receiving inflows attributed to multiple per-chain operator clusters within a window.
- **Off-chain CTI integration:** cross-reference off-chain operator profiles (social handles, hosting fingerprints) against on-chain attribution clusters.
- **Cross-chain swap-protocol monitor:** subscribe to THORChain / LI.FI / Across transaction streams; on inflow from a known per-chain operator cluster, flag the outbound chain-and-address for downstream propagation.

## Real-world examples

- **OAK-G01 Lazarus / Bybit → THORChain → multi-chain — Feb–Mar 2025.** Per `[chainalysisbybitthorchain]`, `[coindeskthorchainlazarus2025]`, and `[chainalysis2024laundering]`, Lazarus laundered ~$1.4B of Bybit-extracted ETH through THORChain in approximately 10 days post-extraction; ~83% of stolen ETH was converted to BTC, with ~72% of those funds moving through THORChain; node operators earned ~$12M in fees from the operation. Subsequent industry tracking (Elliptic flagged 11,000+ wallets across chains potentially connected to the heist; FBI's sanctioned-wallet list held only 51) is the canonical T8.002 worked case at v0.1 — per-chain analysis alone substantially undersold the operator profile, and cross-chain joint attribution was load-bearing for the public attribution outcome. <!-- TODO: cite https://www.bitdefender.com/en-us/blog/hotforsecurity/lazarus-group-shows-no-signs-of-stopping-after-moving-almost-three-quarters-of-bybit-stolen-funds -->
- **OAK-G01 multi-chain operations — 2022 onward.** The post-2022 OAK-G01 operational pattern is canonically multi-chain: extractions on Ethereum (Ronin, Harmony, WazirX), Solana, Bitcoin, and various L2s; laundering via cross-chain swap protocols. Per `[chainalysis2024dprk]`, the cross-chain attribution methodology is the dominant approach for modern OAK-G01 attribution. The trilateral U.S.-Japan-ROK statement of January 14, 2025 (`[trilateraldprkstatement2025]`) attributed 2024 thefts including DMM Bitcoin (~$308M), Upbit (~$50M), Rain Management (~$16.13M), WazirX (~$235M), and Radiant Capital (~$50M) to the DPRK — a multi-chain incident set whose joint attribution surface is the T8.002 instance.
- **OAK-G02 Drainer-as-a-Service multi-chain operations — 2023 onward.** Per `[slowmist2024report]` and `[scamsniffer2024lineage]`, the named drainer-service families (Inferno → Angel handover Oct 2024; Pink, Vanilla, Chick successors) operate across multiple chains and across multiple wallet-vendor surfaces. Cross-chain victim-profiling reuse (target-list infrastructure shared across per-chain affiliate streams) is the cleanest non-Lazarus T8.002 case in the public record. T8.002 attribution at the service-infrastructure level is more stable than per-named-operator attribution.
- **OAK-G10 ALPHV/BlackCat ransom-payment cross-chain laundering — Feb–Mar 2024.** The Change Healthcare ~$22M Bitcoin payment and the operator-side exit-scam laundering exhibit cross-chain conversion (BTC → stablecoin / Monero) at downstream venues; the cross-asset laundering profile is the relevant T8.002 instance at the RaaS-payment layer per `[chainalysis2025ransomware]`.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T8.002 detection requires cross-chain forensic graph that is out of MG single-chain scope).
- Vendor coverage: cross-chain attribution providers (Chainalysis Reactor, TRM Forensics, Elliptic Investigator) are the canonical access path.
- Open-data: bridge-aggregator transaction streams (THORChain explorer; LI.FI API; Across explorer) provide the ingest surface for community-built cross-chain graphs.

## Mitigations

- **Risk teams / cross-chain forensic providers:** maintain cross-chain attribution graphs with per-chain funder-graph extensions; integrate off-chain operator profile data into the attribution layer; publish per-operator-cluster watchlists at the cross-chain level. See OAK-M07 (Cross-Chain Attribution Graph).
- **Receiving venues (CEXes, bridges):** integrate cross-chain attribution-provider screening at deposit time; treat downstream off-ramp inflows from cross-chain-attributed operator clusters as elevated risk.
- **Cross-chain swap protocols (THORChain, LI.FI, Across):** the Bybit / THORChain case demonstrates that swap-protocol-level pre-execution screening of inflow addresses is the highest-leverage layer; protocol-design discussions on cross-chain compliance gates are an open v0.x area.
- **Standardisation:** cross-chain attribution-graph formats are not standardised at v0.1; vendor-specific access methodology is the canonical path. v0.x considerations include whether OAK should publish a normalised cross-chain attribution schema.

## Discussion

T8.002 is structurally important to OAK because it operationalises the modern multi-chain attribution reality: per-chain analysis alone undersells operator profiles for the largest categories (notably OAK-G01). Contributors writing future T8.002 examples should make the **single-chain-coherent + cross-chain-jointly-attributable** distinction explicit — T8.002 is what makes a multi-chain operator profile readable as a single Group, even when no individual chain's evidence is conclusive.

A live design question is whether T8.002 should split into per-axis sub-Techniques (cross-chain funder graph; cross-chain deployment fingerprint; cross-chain laundering convergence; off-chain operator-profile continuity). v0.x updates may revisit the granularity if specific sub-axes reach independent characterisation maturity. The Bybit / THORChain case is the most-defender-relevant calibration anchor at v0.1 because the cross-chain laundering-convergence axis is the most-load-bearing for that incident.

False-positive sources at the cross-chain layer are different from the single-chain layer: shared cross-chain swap-protocol routers, shared bridge-deposit clusters, and shared CEX hot-wallet infrastructure all produce cross-chain edges that are not operator-attributable. Discriminators include matched-amount / matched-timing across the cross-chain edge (operator-controlled flows preserve amount and tight timing; protocol-mediated flows do not) and persistent-counterparty propagation at downstream venues.
