# OAK-T7.005 — Privacy-Chain Hops

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T7 (Laundering)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Bitcoin, cross-chain, Monero, Zcash
**First documented:** systematic from approximately 2017 onward (Monero gained darknet-market dominance in 2016–2017); enforcement-relevant attention 2020+
**Aliases:** "privacy-coin hop", "XMR hop", "Monero off-ramp", "shielded-pool hop"

## Description

A class of laundering flows in which proceeds are routed through a privacy-oriented blockchain (Monero, Zcash, Dash, Pirate Chain, etc.) — typically by depositing into a centralised exchange that supports both the source chain and the privacy chain and withdrawing on the privacy-chain side, or by using an instant-swap service that performs the same conversion without a custodial account. The on-chain attribution trail does not just become harder to follow on the privacy-chain side; at the chain-architecture level it is broken entirely. Monero's combination of ring signatures, RingCT confidential transactions, and stealch-address-derived one-time outputs means that the sender, recipient, and amount of every transaction are concealed by construction; Zcash shielded (z-) addresses provide comparable guarantees within the shielded pool, although the majority of historical ZEC volume has settled on its transparent (t-) side. From a defender's perspective T7.005 is a *terminal* obfuscation rail: once funds enter the privacy chain, defender capability is reduced to off-chain attribution (CEX deposit-side records, withdrawal-side patterns on the source chain, and post-privacy-chain re-emergence inference) rather than on-chain forensics.

T7.005 is structurally distinct from T7.001 (Mixer-Routed Hop): T7.001 obfuscates within a transparent chain via a smart-contract or service-level mixer whose deposit and withdrawal events remain publicly observable and statistically associable; T7.005 obfuscates by jumping to a chain whose architecture itself defeats on-chain attribution, so the equivalent statistical-association attack does not apply on the privacy-chain leg. T7.005 is also distinct from T7.003 (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering): T7.003 adds attribution-graph hops between transparent chains and is defeated (probabilistically) by cross-chain forensic graphs; T7.005's destination chain is itself opaque, so adding more cross-chain forensic capacity does not close the gap. T7.005 is the third orthogonal laundering rail alongside T7.001 and T7.003.

The T7.005 venue surface has narrowed since 2023. Most major Western-regulated CEXes have delisted Monero or scheduled it for delisting; per `[binancexmrdelist2024]`, Binance announced XMR delisting on 2024-02-06 (effective 2024-02-20), continuing a multi-year delisting trend (Bittrex, OKX, others). The off-ramp surface for the source-chain → CEX → XMR pattern has consequently shifted toward a smaller set of remaining exchanges and toward instant-swap services that do not require account-level KYC. Per `[chainalysisprivacychain2024]` and the Chainalysis 2025 Crypto Crime Report cohort findings, vendor solicitation of Monero and instant-exchanger-mediated XMR conversion have continued to grow even as exchange-mediated XMR access has contracted.

## Observed indicators

- Source-chain side: outflow from a known illicit cluster into deposit addresses associated with a CEX that supports both the source asset and a privacy chain; per-deposit values may or may not be threshold-structured per T7.002.
- Source-chain side: outflow into instant-swap services (e.g., FixedFloat, ChangeNOW-type services) followed by no observable downstream on-chain trail consistent with the deposited amount.
- Withdrawal-side (source chain): no further movement of the deposited funds on the source chain in a pattern consistent with the operator's prior on-chain footprint — the trail terminates at the venue.
- Re-emergence side (post-XMR / post-ZEC): inflows on a *different* transparent chain at a later date, consistent in amount-class and timing with the original deposit, into addresses whose subsequent behavioural pattern matches the upstream operator (T8.001-style cluster-continuation across the opaque hop).
- Venue-side: a CEX with both source-chain deposit support and privacy-chain withdrawal support shows recurring same-customer deposit-then-privacy-chain-withdraw flows whose source-side cluster aggregates into a known illicit-cluster watchlist.

## Detection signals

- Per-CEX deposit-address attribution: forensic providers (Chainalysis, TRM, Elliptic) maintain CEX deposit-address-cluster attribution; the source-chain leg is detectable using the same machinery as T7.002 even if the privacy-chain leg is not.
- Instant-swap-service inflow tracking: inflows into known instant-swap-service deposit addresses from illicit-cluster watchlists, with no proportionate downstream source-chain trail, are an inferential T7.005 indicator (the funds have most likely exited the transparent ledger).
- Post-privacy-chain re-emergence heuristics: probabilistic linkage between source-side terminal-deposit events and later same-amount-class re-emergence on a different transparent chain, scored at stated confidence with explicit acknowledgement that on-chain certainty is unattainable across the opaque hop.
- Volume-shape signals: per-venue ratio of privacy-chain withdrawals to non-privacy-chain withdrawals over a rolling window, segmented by deposit-source-cluster risk score, as a venue-compliance-posture metric.
- Cluster continuation: T8.001-style operator-cluster continuity inferred across the privacy-chain hop from re-emergence patterns rather than direct on-chain linkage.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2025-04-exch-shutdown.md`](../examples/2025-04-exch-shutdown.md) — eXch instant-exchange shutdown (April–May 2025). eXch was a non-KYC instant cryptocurrency exchange that supported XMR conversion alongside BTC / ETH / USDT / USDC; its 2014–2025 operating window straddles the post-Binance-XMR-delisting (2024-02-20) venue-surface contraction that drove laundering-cohort migration toward instant-swap services. The case is the cleanest 2024–2025 anchor for the **instant-swap-service-mediated T7.005** sub-pattern: source-chain → instant-swap-service → privacy-chain conversion without account-level KYC, with a confirmed-strength sovereign-side enforcement closure (German BKA / Frankfurt Public Prosecutor's Office seizure 2025-04-30, U.S. DOJ whistleblower track) plus residual-infrastructure persistence (TRM Labs follow-up reporting). T7.005 chains alongside T7.001 (mixer-equivalent role) and T7.003 (cross-chain-swap role) at the same venue.
- [`examples/2024-02-change-healthcare-ransom.md`](../examples/2024-02-change-healthcare-ransom.md) — Change Healthcare ALPHV/BlackCat ransom, February–March 2024. The Bitcoin-to-Monero conversion leg in the operator-side off-ramp chain is the canonical post-2023 RaaS privacy-chain hop pattern: the ALPHV operator-side share (\~\$22M ransom paid in BTC) was routed through a Bitcoin → XMR conversion leg that defeated on-chain attribution for the operator's terminal share, documented by Chainalysis and TRM Labs in near-real-time forensic reconstruction. The case is the strongest-confirmed T7.005 anchor at the ransom-operator cohort layer.
- **Aggregate cohort context — multi-chain — 2024** — Per `[chainalysis2024laundering]` and `[chainalysisprivacychain2024]`, privacy-chain conversion (predominantly XMR) is the dominant rail for a specific subclass of illicit cohorts (notably darknet-market vendors and CSAM vendors), with vendor solicitation of Monero and instant-exchanger-mediated XMR conversion continuing to grow through 2023–2024. The headline-magnitude theft cohort (Lazarus-class extractions) is *not* the primary T7.005 user-base at v0.1 cadence — that cohort is dominantly T7.003 (cross-chain bridge laundering); T7.005 dominates lower-magnitude high-volume vendor-class flows.
- **Lazarus historical XMR usage** — DPRK-attributed laundering has historically included Monero conversion at smaller-value layers (per `[chainalysis2024dprk]` and industry retrospectives), but at v0.1 the documented dominant Lazarus laundering rail post Tornado Cash sanctions is T7.003 cross-chain swap (THORChain etc.), not T7.005. Specific incident-level XMR attribution to Lazarus is sparse in the public forensic record precisely because the privacy-chain leg defeats on-chain confirmation; OAK does not assert specific incident attributions it cannot verify.
- **Binance XMR delisting — 2024-02-20** — Per `[binancexmrdelist2024]`, Binance's delisting of XMR (announced 2024-02-06, effective 2024-02-20) is the single largest venue-surface contraction event for source-chain → CEX → XMR off-ramping in the OAK-tracked period. The delisting is a regulatory artefact rather than an incident; for OAK's purposes it bounds the time period during which Binance was a viable XMR off-ramp venue and provides a stable reference for the venue-surface-shrinkage trend that has shaped T7.005 operator behaviour through 2024–2025.
- [`examples/2024-01-dprk-chain-hop-laundering.md`](../examples/2024-01-dprk-chain-hop-laundering.md) — DPRK-attributed multi-stage chain-hop laundering via privacy-chain conversions — cross-chain — 2024. Documents the post-Tornado-Cash-sanctions mature Lazarus laundering chain: T7.003 cross-chain bridge hops (volume-movement layer) + T7.005 privacy-chain terminal conversion (obfuscation layer). Demonstrates the structural defender asymmetry between the T7.003 layer (probabilistically attributable with forensic investment) and the T7.005 layer (not attributable regardless of investment — the privacy-chain opacity is by design).
- [`examples/2026-bybit-hack-laundering-continuation-thorchain-dprk.md`](../examples/2026-bybit-hack-laundering-continuation-thorchain-dprk.md) — Bybit $1.5B Hack Laundering Continuation into 2026 — DPRK / Lazarus — T7.001 + T7.002 + T7.005 + T8.001; Lazarus Group routed remaining ETH proceeds through Tornado Cash analogues, privacy pools, and DEX-based mixing patterns through mid-2026

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1 (T7.005 is fundamentally cross-chain forensic + venue-compliance-side; the privacy-chain leg is out of reach for any on-chain detector regardless of implementation effort, and the source-chain leg is covered by the T7.002 surface rather than a dedicated T7.005 detector).
- Vendor coverage: Chainalysis Reactor, TRM Forensics, Elliptic Investigator — source-chain deposit-side attribution into privacy-chain-supporting venues; instant-swap-service inflow tracking. The privacy-chain leg itself is not on-chain-traceable; vendor coverage is therefore inferential / re-emergence-based rather than direct.
- Venue-side compliance: per-CEX privacy-chain-withdrawal-support policy and per-CEX KYC posture on privacy-chain-paired customer flows.

## Mitigations

- **CEX compliance (highest-leverage):** integrate compliance-provider screening at deposit time on the source-chain side; apply elevated review for customer accounts whose deposit-then-privacy-chain-withdraw flow pattern correlates with watchlisted source-clusters; venues that retain XMR/ZEC support carry disproportionate T7.005 mitigation responsibility.
- **CEX delisting policy:** delisting privacy-chain pairs (the documented industry trend through 2020–2024) is a structural T7.005 mitigation. OAK documents the trend without taking a position on whether further delisting is the correct policy response — the policy debate is out of OAK's scope (see Discussion).
- **Travel-rule enforcement:** FATF Travel Rule-style cross-venue-message infrastructure improves the off-chain attribution layer that T7.005 forces defenders into; jurisdictions with stronger Travel Rule enforcement narrow the off-ramp surface for the post-privacy-chain re-emergence leg.
- **Instant-swap service compliance:** integrate compliance-provider screening on inflows; instant-swap services are at v0.1 a comparatively under-mitigated surface relative to CEXes and represent a high-leverage future mitigation target.
- **Risk teams (organisational):** treat post-incident-time-window flows into privacy-chain-supporting venues and instant-swap services as elevated risk; maintain re-emergence watchlists for amount-class-matching inflows on alternative transparent chains following an unresolved source-chain terminal-deposit event.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M07, OAK-M27.

## Discussion

T7.005 sits at the intersection of a documentable laundering technique and a contested public-policy question. The OAK position is that the framework documents the laundering use of privacy chains at the technique level — observed indicators, detection signals, and mitigations — without opining on the broader privacy-versus-surveillance policy debate. Privacy chains have legitimate non-laundering use-cases (financial privacy as an end in itself, protection of commercial counterparties, protection from chain-analytic targeting of ordinary users); OAK does not characterise privacy-chain use as inherently illicit and does not advocate for a particular regulatory outcome. The documented venue-surface contraction (delistings) is reported as an empirical fact that shapes operator behaviour, not as an OAK policy endorsement.

A live structural observation, separate from the policy debate: T7.005 is the laundering rail with the *highest defender asymmetry* of the T7 sub-techniques. T7.001 and T7.003 are probabilistically attackable through sufficient forensic investment; T7.005 is not — the chain-architecture-level opacity is by design and is not closable through better tooling on the privacy-chain side. Defender capability against T7.005 is therefore concentrated entirely in the off-chain (venue compliance, Travel Rule, instant-swap-service compliance) and inferential (re-emergence heuristics) layers. Contributors writing future T7.005 examples should preserve this asymmetry and resist the temptation to overclaim on-chain attribution against the privacy-chain leg.
