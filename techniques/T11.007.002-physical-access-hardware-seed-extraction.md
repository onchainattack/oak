# OAK-T11.007.002 — Physical-Access Hardware-Side Seed Extraction

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure); sub-Technique of OAK-T11.007 (Hardware-wallet Supply-chain / Physical-access Compromise)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (physical-access attack against the hardware wallet's microcontroller; downstream extraction across all chains the wallet supports)
**First documented:** 2020-01-31 (Trezor One / Model T RDP-downgrade voltage-glitch attack, Kraken Security Labs disclosure)
**Aliases:** "voltage-glitch seed extraction", "side-channel hardware wallet attack", "RDP-downgrade attack", "chip-tampering seed extraction"

## Description

Voltage-glitching, side-channel, or chip-tampering attacks against a legitimate hardware wallet's microcontroller or secure element that extract the encrypted seed or private-key material. The attack requires **physical access** to the device for a period of minutes to hours, plus modest hardware-hacking equipment (oscilloscope, voltage-glitcher, JTAG/SWD debug probe — typically under $500 in 2020 equipment cost for the Kraken-demonstrated attack).

The canonical capability anchor is the Kraken Security Labs disclosure (January 31, 2020) of a voltage-glitch attack against the Trezor One (STM32F205) and Trezor Model T (STM32F427): the microcontroller's Read Protection (RDP) feature could be downgraded from RDP2 to RDP1 by voltage-glitching the MCU's VDD pin during boot-up, exposing SRAM contents — including the encrypted seed — over the ARM SWD (Serial Wire Debug) debugging interface. The physical-access requirement is approximately 15 minutes. The vulnerability is **structural** — it is a property of the STM32F2/F4 microcontroller family and cannot be patched by firmware update.

The BIP39 passphrase is the defender-side mitigation primitive: an attacker who successfully extracts the seed obtains an encrypted seed that, without the passphrase, opens an empty decoy wallet. Passphrase strength bounds the realised loss rather than preventing the extraction.

The structural distinction from sibling sub-Techniques:

- **T11.007.002** exploits a structural microcontroller vulnerability against a **legitimate device**. Physical access is the binding constraint. The device is genuine; the attack is against the chip.
- **T11.007.001** (Counterfeit-Hardware Substitution) substitutes the **device itself**. No physical-access expertise is required; the device is structurally compromised at manufacture.
- **T11.007.003** (Brand-Trust-Leveraged Active Phishing) requires no device access at all; the attack is against the user's trust in the vendor communication channel.

## Observed indicators

- Hardware wallet based on STM32F205, STM32F427, or similar microcontrollers without a dedicated EAL5+/EAL6+ certified secure element. Trezor One and Model T are the canonical affected devices; Ledger Nano series uses a dedicated secure element and is not vulnerable to the STM32-class voltage-glitch attack.
- Attacker requires physical access to the device (lost, stolen, seized, or briefly accessed — hotel safe, checked luggage, border crossing).
- Post-extraction: the seed is encrypted; the BIP39 passphrase gates access to the real wallet. Without the passphrase, the attacker opens an empty decoy. With the passphrase (or no passphrase), the attacker drains all associated wallets.

## Detection signals

- **Secure-element-equipped hardware selection (preventive):** for new deployments where physical-access threat model is non-trivial, select hardware with a dedicated EAL5+/EAL6+ certified secure element (Ledger Nano series, Trezor Safe 3 / Safe 5, or equivalent) rather than general-purpose microcontroller-based hardware.
- **BIP39 passphrase enforcement (mitigative):** for users on STM32-based hardware, BIP39 passphrase usage must be treated as mandatory, not optional. The passphrase converts a physical-access seed-extraction from "wallet drainable" to "empty-decoy-revealing" outcome.
- **Physical-access threat model evaluation:** users who travel internationally, cross borders, or store hardware wallets in hotel safes / checked luggage should evaluate their threat model against a 15-minute physical-access attack before selecting hardware.

## Mitigations

- **Hardware selection for physical-access threat model:** prefer secure-element-equipped hardware (Ledger Nano series, Trezor Safe 3/5, or equivalent with Common Criteria EAL5+/EAL6+ certified secure element) over general-purpose microcontroller-based hardware.
- **BIP39 passphrase as mandatory mitigation on STM32-class hardware:** users with non-trivial holdings on Trezor One / Model T or any hardware whose microcontroller is known-vulnerable to physical-access seed-extraction must use a BIP39 passphrase (strong, unique, not stored with the device). The passphrase is the load-bearing mitigation that converts extraction from "wallet drainable" to "empty-decoy-revealing."
- **Physical-access operational security:** do not leave hardware wallets in hotel safes, checked luggage, or border-crossing inspection where 15-minute unsupervised access is possible; treat the device as a bearer instrument.

## Discussion

T11.007.002's structural OAK lesson: **the physical-access threat model is operationally distinct from the software-attack threat model, and hardware-wallet selection must be evaluated against both independently.** A wallet whose firmware is secure against remote attack may be structurally vulnerable to a 15-minute physical-access attack with commodity hardware-hacking equipment.

The BIP39 passphrase is the load-bearing mitigation primitive — it converts a successful seed extraction from "wallet drainable" to "empty-decoy-revealing." The passphrase does not prevent the extraction; it bounds the outcome. The passphrase must be strong (high entropy), unique (not reused), and not stored with the device or in any third-party service. A weak or absent passphrase on STM32-class hardware is a standing T11.007.002 surface.

Newer hardware (Trezor Safe 3 / Safe 5 with EAL6+ secure element, 2023–2024) addresses the structural vulnerability at the hardware layer. Defender guidance for existing Trezor One / Model T users with non-trivial holdings whose physical-access threat model is non-trivial should recommend migration to secure-element-equipped hardware plus BIP39 passphrase enforcement.
