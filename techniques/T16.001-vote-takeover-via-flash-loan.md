# OAK-T16.001 — Vote Takeover via Flash-Loan

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T16 (Governance / Voting Manipulation)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T9.003 (generic Governance Attack class — preserved on every T16.001 worked example), OAK-T9.002 (Flash-Loan-Enabled Exploit — the precondition without which T16.001 does not scale)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (canonical anchors); cross-chain governance contracts inherit the surface where flash-loan liquidity exists in the same execution context as the voting-power-eligibility check
**First documented:** 2022 (Beanstalk April 2022 as the canonical anchor)
**Aliases:** "flash-loan governance attack", "same-block flash-borrow vote", "BIP attack", "voting-power flash-borrow", "governance flash-loan"

## Description

Same-block flash-borrow of governance tokens (or of stablecoins / collateral that can be converted to governance tokens within the same execution context) used to acquire majority voting power on a proposal whose execution payload directs treasury or privileged-action surfaces into the attacker's pocket, then repaid in the same block once the malicious proposal has executed. The defining shape is **acquire-vote-execute-repay-exit, all within atomicity**: the attacker never holds the voting tokens at any time outside the single block in which the malicious proposal passes and executes.

T16.001 is the operationally-tightest sub-class of T9.003 (Governance Attack) — Beanstalk April 2022 is its canonical anchor and the textbook chain T9.002 + T9.003 + T16.001 + T7.001 + T5.005. The mitigation surface is well-understood and has been broadly adopted by post-2022 governance contracts: voting power computed from snapshotted (checkpointed) balances at proposal-creation time over a multi-block window (Compound Governor Bravo's checkpoint pattern), mandatory minimum delay between proposal submission and execution (timelock), and removal of `emergencyCommit`-class expedited-execution paths that bypass the normal proposal lifecycle. As of 2024-2025, large-scale T16.001 incidents at top-tier DeFi protocols are rare precisely because of this design shift; smaller-protocol T16.001 incidents continue to fire and are typically not published forensically.

T16.001 is **structurally distinct from T16.002 (Hostile-Vote Treasury Drain)**. T16.002 covers the case where the attacker accumulates governance tokens through legitimate market means (or via T9.001 oracle-inflation that operates upstream of the vote) over a window that may extend across multiple blocks or weeks; the voting position is held throughout the vote and the resulting extraction. T16.001 is the *single-block atomicity* sub-class — the voting position exists only inside the flash-loan window and the attacker is never economically exposed to the position outside that window. The mitigation differs in shape: T16.001 is closed by snapshot-based voting power at proposal-creation time; T16.002 is closed at the upstream oracle layer (T9.001 mitigation) or at the governance-process layer (cohort-surveillance, counter-proposal capability).

T16.001 is also **structurally distinct from T16.003 (Delegation-Cluster Vote Takeover)**. T16.003 attackers acquire voting power via delegation pull from a cluster of compromised / controlled wallets, often over a multi-week accumulation window. T16.001 is the same-block flash-borrow case where no delegation graph is involved — the attacker holds the tokens directly for the duration of the block.

The T16.001 surface is gated by two design conditions: (a) governance-token voting-weight is computed against spot balances at proposal-execution time rather than against snapshotted balances at proposal-creation time, and (b) the proposal lifecycle either has no minimum execution delay (Beanstalk's `emergencyCommit`) or has a delay short enough to be cleared within the flash-loan window. Either condition alone is insufficient to enable T16.001; both must hold. Removing either closes the surface.

## Observed indicators

- **At-event (single-block signature):** flash-loan event sourcing governance-token-equivalent collateral (DAI / USDC / WBTC / governance-token directly) in the same transaction as the proposal-execution event; the proposal's voting-eligibility check resolves against spot balances in the same block; the malicious proposal payload directs treasury or mints privileged roles to attacker-controlled addresses; the flash loan is repaid in the same block; the attacker's net position outside the flash-loan window is the resulting extraction minus the flash-loan fee.
- **Pre-event (governance-design layer):** voting power computed from spot balances rather than from snapshotted / time-averaged balances; absence of minimum proposal-to-execution delay; presence of `emergencyCommit`-class expedited-execution paths; voting-quorum threshold below the flash-loanable supply of the governance token.
- **On-chain (proposal-payload layer):** proposal calldata directing treasury egress, mint of privileged role, modification of upgrade slot, or `delegatecall` to an address that has not been a governance target in the protocol's history; treasury-affecting calls in the executed proposal payload directing funds to attacker-controlled addresses.
- **Forensic record:** the single transaction containing flash-loan, vote, execution, and repayment; the attacker's net-profit calculation post-repayment; the laundering follow-on (typically Tornado Cash for pre-2022-08 events, Tornado Cash + cross-chain bridges for post-sanction events).

## Detection signals

- **Real-time governance-quorum monitor:** monitor governance-token transfer events for sudden large acquisitions correlated with active proposals; alert specifically on flash-loan-funded acquisitions (the flash-loan event and the voting-weight-acquisition event in the same transaction); cross-reference voted weight against a snapshotted distribution from N blocks before proposal creation.
- **Pre-deployment governance-design review:** governance-contract audit for emergency-execution paths, quorum-without-timelock voting, and voting-power computed from spot balances rather than from time-averaged or snapshotted balances. The Beanstalk `emergencyCommit` is the canonical anti-pattern.
- **Per-proposal simulation:** replay-style simulation of any proposal whose execution payload touches treasury or privileged-action surfaces; particular attention to proposals that pass quorum within a single block of submission.
- **Flash-loan-resistance check at voting-eligibility computation:** voting weight computed at proposal-creation time over a multi-block window (Compound Governor Bravo checkpoint pattern) defeats T16.001 by construction. Detection here is at the design-review layer, not at the runtime layer.

## Real-world examples

- **Beanstalk Farms — Ethereum — 2022-04-17** — Loss \~\$182M (\~\$76M attacker net profit after flash-loan repayment). Canonical T16.001 case. The attacker borrowed approximately \$1B via Aave flash loans (in DAI, USDC, USDT), used the borrowed stablecoins to acquire approximately 67% of STALK voting power, used Beanstalk's `emergencyCommit` function to bypass the normal proposal lifecycle and immediately execute a malicious BIP-18 that drained treasury funds to attacker-controlled addresses, repaid the flash loans in the same transaction, and laundered the proceeds through Tornado Cash. The textbook T9.002 (flash-loan precondition) + T9.003 (governance-attack class) + T16.001 (operational sub-pattern) chain. See [`examples/2022-04-beanstalk.md`](../examples/2022-04-beanstalk.md).
- **Curio DAO — Ethereum — 2024-03-23** — Loss \~\$16M nominal / \~\$1M realised. The attacker used a flash loan to acquire approximately 1% of CGT voting power (sufficient to reach quorum given the MakerDAO-fork-governance economic-threshold-of-attack), then exploited a storage-layout collision in the MakerDAO-fork governance proxy to elevate the 1% voting position into administrative privileges. The case is dual-mapped: T16.001 (flash-loan-funded voting-power acquisition) + T16.005 (storage-collision-mediated authority capture as the malicious-proposal-snowballing mechanism). See [`examples/2024-03-curio.md`](../examples/2024-03-curio.md).
- **Shibarium PoS-bridge — Ethereum ↔ Shibarium — 2025-09-12 (T16.001 applied at the bridge-validator-stake-eligibility layer; dual-mapped with T10.001)** — Loss \~\$2.3M-\$4.1M across 17 tokens. The attacker flash-borrowed 4.6M BONE in the same execution context as a malicious bridge checkpoint to *amplify* staked-BONE-derived validator voting weight past the 2/3 super-majority threshold. Demonstrates that the Beanstalk lesson (snapshot-based voting power at proposal-creation time) did not propagate from the governance-contract sub-domain into bridge-validator-set-eligibility designs even 41 months later — i.e., T16.001 is operationally viable at the *validator-stake* layer wherever the validator-set-eligibility-computation primitive resolves against same-block staked balances rather than against snapshotted balances. Composes with T10.001 partial validator-key compromise: with fewer than the super-majority threshold of compromised keys, T16.001 stake-amplification provides the missing margin. See [`examples/2025-09-shibarium-bridge.md`](../examples/2025-09-shibarium-bridge.md).
- **Cohort context** — `[zhou2023sok]` documents flash-loan-enabled governance-attack incidents as a recurring class through 2018-2022; smaller-protocol governance-attacks continue through 2023-2025 but rarely surface forensically at scale because the dollar-loss is typically bounded by the smaller protocol's treasury.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. T16.001 detection is feasible at the runtime layer (a single transaction containing flash-loan + governance-token-acquisition + proposal-execution events is a high-confidence signature) but the canonical mitigation lives at the governance-design layer (snapshot-based voting power) rather than at the runtime layer.
- Vendor coverage: Forta governance-monitoring agents (real-time flash-loan + governance-event correlation); Tally / Boardroom analytics dashboards (post-event governance-side observation); audit firms with explicit governance-design review practice (Halborn, Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin, ConsenSys Diligence).

## Mitigations

- **Protocols (OAK-M17 — time-locked governance + multi-block quorum):** highest-leverage layer. Voting power computed from snapshotted (checkpointed) balances at proposal-creation time over a multi-block window defeats T16.001 by construction. Compound Governor Bravo's checkpoint pattern is the canonical reference design; post-2022 governance contracts at top-tier DeFi protocols broadly adopted this pattern.
- **Protocols — minimum proposal-to-execution delay (timelock):** mandatory delay between vote-passage and execution opens a defender review window. The Beanstalk `emergencyCommit` anti-pattern (no delay) is the canonical T16.001 enabler. Removing all expedited-execution paths from the governance contract closes the bypass.
- **Protocols (OAK-M02 — proposal-payload static analysis):** before any governance proposal reaches its execution window, decode and statically analyse the calldata; alert on payloads that touch treasury, mint privileged roles, or perform `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets.
- **Protocols (OAK-M16 — pre-deployment audit + formal verification):** governance contract audit and formal verification specifically targeting upgrade paths, emergency branches, and voting-power-computation primitives.
- **Risk teams:** for governance-token holdings, monitor proposal queues for any proposal whose execution payload affects treasury; maintain per-protocol governance-design inventories.

## Discussion

T16.001's structural lesson is that **flash-loan-resistance at the voting-power-eligibility-computation layer is the load-bearing mitigation**, not flash-loan availability itself. Banning flash loans does not retire T16.001; only fixing the voting-power-computation primitive does. As with Cream October 2021 (T9.002 + T9.001) and Euler March 2023 (T9.002 + T9.004), the flash loan is the precondition; the cause is the design that allowed spot-balance voting weight inside the flash-loan-atomicity window.

The cohort is structurally distinct from T16.002 (hostile-vote treasury drain) by the *atomicity window*: T16.001 attackers are exposed to the voting position only inside a single block; T16.002 attackers hold the position throughout the vote and into the resulting extraction. The cohort is structurally distinct from T16.003 (delegation-cluster takeover) by the *acquisition mechanism*: T16.001 acquires via flash-loan; T16.003 acquires via delegation pull from a cluster of wallets. The mitigation surfaces partition cleanly along the same boundaries.

Attribution-strength notes: Beanstalk is `pseudonymous` (fully on-chain, attacker known to forensic vendors but not publicly named); Curio is `pseudonymous-unattributed` with partial recovery via negotiated 10% bug-bounty. T16.001 attackers historically prefer Tornado Cash for laundering (Beanstalk pre-2022-08 sanctions); post-sanction T16.001 attackers more often route through cross-chain bridges to privacy chains (T7.003 + T7.005).

False-positive considerations: legitimate governance-token holders routinely vote on proposals using flash-loanable collateral as the underlying funding source. The detection signal is not "flash-loan correlated with voting" but specifically "flash-loan-funded voting-weight acquired and exhausted within the same transaction as the proposal execution that benefits the borrower." Cohort-level analysis confirms that the legitimate use-case for "flash-borrow governance tokens to vote" essentially does not exist outside attacker behaviour.

v0.x deferred items: a structured rubric for distinguishing technical-T16.001 from social-T16.001 cases (e.g., a flash-loan-funded vote on a proposal whose treasury direction was eventually argued to be community-aligned); cohort-level metrics on the flash-loanable-supply-vs-quorum-threshold ratio across DeFi governance contracts as a forward-looking T16.001 surface estimate.
