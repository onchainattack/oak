# OAK-T7.010 — Travel Rule Evasion

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T7 (Laundering)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T7.002 (CEX Deposit-Address Layering — shares the VASP-level compliance surface), OAK-T7.005 (Privacy-Chain Hops — the privacy-coin leg is one Travel Rule evasion method), OAK-T7.009 (Sanctioned-Entity Financing — shares the regulatory-compliance detection layer)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** all (Travel Rule applies at the VASP layer, not the chain layer)
**First documented:** 2019 (FATF Recommendation 16 extended to virtual assets); systematic evasion documented 2020 onward
**Aliases:** "Travel Rule bypass", "sub-threshold structuring", "VASP-data-gap exploitation", "FATF Rec.16 evasion"

## Description

A laundering technique in which the operator structures on-chain flows to avoid triggering the FATF Travel Rule (Recommendation 16) — the international standard requiring Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs) to transmit originator and beneficiary identifying information for transactions above a jurisdiction-defined threshold. The Travel Rule creates a mandatory information chokepoint at VASP entry/exit points: when a transaction crosses a VASP boundary above threshold, both originator and beneficiary VASPs must exchange and store PII (name, address, account number). From the launderer's perspective, the Travel Rule is a **compliance adversary** — every Travel Rule-triggered transaction creates a permanent, subpoena-accessible record linking real-world identity to on-chain address.

T7.010 names the **method** of Travel Rule evasion, not the purpose. The technique composes with other T7 Techniques: a laundering chain may use T7.010 to cross VASP boundaries undetected, then T7.005 (privacy chain) to break on-chain tracing, then T7.002 (CEX layering) to exit. The distinguishing feature is the **compliance-gap-aware routing**: the operator selects paths through the VASP network that minimise Travel Rule triggering events.

T7.010 is structurally distinct from T7.002 (CEX Deposit Layering): T7.002 exploits exchange-level KYC variation (exchange A has lower KYC standards than exchange B); T7.010 exploits the **information-transmission** gap between VASPs — not the KYC onboarding gap, but the post-onboarding transaction-reporting gap. The two are complementary: T7.002 selects a lax-KYC exchange; T7.010 structures transactions through that exchange to avoid Travel Rule reporting.

## Observed indicators

- **Sub-threshold structuring:** A wallet repeatedly sending amounts just below the Travel Rule threshold (e.g., $900 on a $1000 threshold, or €900 on a €1000 threshold) to the same beneficiary VASP across multiple transactions within a compressed time window — the structural equivalent of CTR (Currency Transaction Report) structuring in fiat AML.
- **VASP-avoidant routing:** A laundering chain that routes through DeFi protocols, DEX aggregators, cross-chain bridges, and non-custodial swap services between VASP entry and exit points, avoiding VASP-to-VASP transfers entirely. The chain uses VASPs only at the initial deposit and final withdrawal legs, with all intermediate hops occurring outside Travel Rule scope.
- **Non-FATF-jurisdiction VASP selection:** Preference for VASPs domiciled in jurisdictions that have not implemented (or weakly enforce) the Travel Rule, reducing the probability that the originator VASP will transmit the required information to the beneficiary VASP.
- **Unhosted-wallet interposition:** Routing through a self-custodied wallet between two VASP legs, converting a VASP-to-VASP transfer (Travel Rule applies) into two VASP-to-unhosted-wallet transfers (Travel Rule applicability varies by jurisdiction). Non-custodial wallet hops are the structural Travel Rule circuit-breaker.
- **Privacy-coin re-entry:** Converting assets to Monero (XMR) or Zcash (ZEC) at a non-VASP instant exchange, then re-entering the VASP system from the privacy chain — the receiving VASP has no originator information to transmit because the preceding leg is opaque.
- **Cross-jurisdictional threshold arbitrage:** Exploiting the fact that Travel Rule thresholds differ by jurisdiction ($1000 in FATF guidance, €1000 in EU, lower in some national implementations). The operator routes through the highest-threshold jurisdiction for a given transfer size.

## Detection signals

- **Per-VASP structuring detection:** For a given VASP, flag clusters of inbound transactions where individual amounts are just below the threshold, aggregate amount exceeds the threshold, and transactions share temporal proximity (same day / consecutive days) and common originator cluster.
- **VASP-avoidant path analysis:** For a laundering chain, compute the fraction of hops that occur on VASP-to-VASP edges vs. DeFi/bridge/non-custodial edges. A chain with zero or minimal VASP-to-VASP hops despite originating and terminating at VASPs is a T7.010 signature.
- **Jurisdictional routing anomaly:** For a given laundering chain, flag VASP legs where the VASP's jurisdiction of domicile has not implemented or weakly enforces the Travel Rule. Cross-reference VASP domicile against FATF Travel Rule implementation status assessments.
- **Unhosted-wallet interposition rate:** Compute the fraction of VASP→VASP paths in a cluster that interpose an unhosted wallet leg. A cluster with a systematically high interposition rate (e.g., >X% of originally-VASP-to-VASP-value interposed via unhosted wallets) is a T7.010 signal.
- **Threshold-proximity distribution:** Flag clusters where the transaction-amount distribution has an anomalous spike just below the Travel Rule threshold, compared to a benign baseline where transaction amounts are uniformly distributed around the threshold.

## Real-world examples

- **Instant-exchange-to-XMR laundering chains (2024–2025):** The 3520 BTC and 4064 BTC cases (see `examples/2025-04-3520-btc-instant-exchange-xmr-laundering.md` and `examples/2024-08-4064-btc-multi-hop-cross-chain-laundering.md`) both avoid centralized exchanges entirely, routing through 6+ instant exchanges that are not categorised as VASPs in all implementing jurisdictions — a T7.010 pattern at the exchange-selection layer as well as at the structuring layer.
- **Lazarus Group post-Bybit laundering (2025-02):** Per `[coindeskthorchainlazarus2025]`, the $1.4B Bybit laundering chain routed through THORChain (a non-custodial cross-chain protocol) rather than through CEXs, avoiding VASP-to-VASP Travel Rule obligations for every intermediate hop. The case is the canonical T7.010 example at headline scale: the entire $1.4B moved through non-VASP infrastructure, with VASP entry only at the final cash-out legs.
- **Sub-threshold structuring cohort (2022–2025):** Industry-forensic providers have documented systematic sub-threshold structuring patterns where laundering clusters send thousands of sub-$1000 transactions to CEX deposit addresses over extended periods, with aggregate laundered values in the tens-of-millions range. Individual transactions are below the Travel Rule threshold; aggregate per-cluster flows exceed it by orders of magnitude. Worked examples pending v0.x for individual named structuring cases.

## Reference implementations

- `mg-detectors-rs` — coverage **gap** at v0.1. T7.010 detection is at the VASP-compliance-data layer and the cross-VASP behavioural-pattern layer, outside MG's on-chain scope.
- Vendor coverage: Chainalysis Reactor (per-cluster VASP-interaction attribution and threshold-proximity analysis), TRM Forensics (cross-VASP flow tracing and jurisdictional routing anomaly detection), Elliptic Investigator (per-cluster VASP-avoidance pattern attribution). The Travel Rule data layer (originator/beneficiary information exchanged between VASPs) is VASP-side and not directly accessible to blockchain-intelligence providers; vendor coverage is therefore inferential (pattern-based) rather than based on actual Travel Rule data consumption.
- VASP-side compliance: per-VASP Travel Rule solution providers (Notabene, TRISA, OpenVASP) maintain the VASP-to-VASP information-transmission infrastructure; their data is not publicly accessible but is available to member VASPs. The Travel Rule protocol layer is itself a detection data source for the VASPs that participate in it.

## Mitigations

- **OAK-M27 (Travel Rule and KYC at Privacy-Chain Boundary):** require VASPs to implement Travel Rule information transmission for all transactions above threshold; extend Travel Rule obligations to the maximum extent legally permissible to unhosted-wallet transactions.
- **OAK-M07 (Cross-Chain Attribution Graph):** maintain a unified cross-chain attribution graph that identifies VASP-avoidant routing patterns; flag chains where VASP-to-VASP edges are systematically replaced by DeFi/bridge/unhosted-wallet hops.
- **OAK-M11 (Rate-Limiting and Per-Block Caps):** per-VASP per-address inbound-transaction rate limiting to raise the cost of sub-threshold structuring; aggregate-volume monitoring that detects when sub-threshold structuring exceeds a per-cluster ceiling.
- **OAK-M04 (Funder-Graph Clustering):** identify clusters whose transaction-amount distribution exhibits an anomalous spike just below the Travel Rule threshold; cross-reference against benign-usage baselines per VASP and per asset.

## Discussion

T7.010 is included in v0.1 as a **compliance-infrastructure-gap** technique: it names a laundering method that exists only because of a specific regulatory-information chokepoint (the Travel Rule) and the gaps in that chokepoint's coverage (DeFi, unhosted wallets, jurisdictional variation). As Travel Rule implementation coverage increases, T7.010 will evolve: sub-threshold structuring will remain a constant (it mirrors fiat-AML CTR structuring, which has existed since the 1970 Bank Secrecy Act), but VASP-avoidant routing and jurisdictional arbitrage may shift as regulatory coverage expands.

**False-positive reasoning.** Sub-threshold transactions are common in legitimate usage (small transfers to friends, micro-deposits for DeFi interactions, exchange withdrawal minimums that happen to fall below threshold). Effective T7.010 detection requires aggregate-per-cluster analysis rather than per-transaction thresholding. A wallet that sends one sub-threshold transaction to a CEX is not a T7.010 signal; a cluster that sends thousands of structured sub-threshold transactions across a compressed window is.

**Calibration note (v0.1).** Travel Rule thresholds and implementation status vary by jurisdiction. OAK v0.1 does not maintain a per-jurisdiction threshold-and-implementation-status reference table. Defenders should calibrate T7.010 detection thresholds against the Travel Rule thresholds and implementation statuses applicable to the VASPs in their operational scope.
