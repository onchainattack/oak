# OAK-T5.007 — Third-party Brand-impersonation Custodial Soft-rug

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T5 (Value Extraction)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (Polygon-resident Polymarket-branded canonical case at v0.1; cross-platform analogues across any high-trust platform whose brand can be impersonated by off-platform services — Polymarket, Hyperliquid, dYdX, Pump.fun, GMX, prediction-market and trader-tooling ecosystems)
**First documented:** 2026-01 (Polycule trading bot — Polymarket-branded but unaffiliated, ~$230K, exit-via-"hack" announcement followed by communication blackout)
**Aliases:** "third-party brand-impersonation soft rug", "Polymarket-branded bot exit", "fake-affiliated trading-bot rug", "custodial soft-rug exit-as-hack"

## Description

An **off-platform service brands itself in a legitimate platform's name without contractual basis**, accumulates user deposits in operator-controlled custody, and exits citing an unverifiable "hack." The substrate of extraction is *user funds voluntarily deposited into operator-controlled custody* on the assumption of platform-affiliation that does not exist. The operator never had a fiduciary or contractual relationship with the platform whose brand they used.

T5.007 is **structurally distinct** from sibling T5 Value-Extraction Techniques and adjacent classes:

- **T5.001 (Hard LP Drain) / T5.002 (Slow LP Trickle Removal)** — token-level liquidity extraction via AMM pool manipulation. T5.007 has no token, no LP, no AMM pool; the substrate is a custodial deposit address.
- **T5.005 (Treasury-Management Exit)** — operator-side exit on a *real* DeFi platform where the operator had a fiduciary relationship with the protocol. T5.007's operator never had such a relationship; the brand is exploited rather than the contractual relationship.
- **T11.001 (Third-Party Signing-Vendor Compromise)** — third-party signing-vendor relationship at the organisational-customer layer. T5.007's "third party" is a brand-impersonating operator with no signing-vendor relationship; the operator *is* the custodian.
- **T11.005 (Operator-side Fake-Platform Fraud)** — closest adjacent class. T11.005 covers end-to-end operator-controlled platform fraud where the platform itself is the vehicle of theft (fake-CEX / pig-butchering / fake-custodian / fake-asset-manager). T5.007 differs in the brand-substrate dimension: T11.005's operator constructs an apparently-original platform brand (BeurAx-class fake exchanges; OneCoin / HyperVerse fake yield products); T5.007's operator brands itself in a *legitimate platform's* name without contractual basis, exploiting the legitimate platform's existing trust to accumulate deposits.
- **T4 phishing** — phishing campaigns acquire on-chain authority on a real platform without user-deposit. T5.007 users deposit *willingly* to the operator-controlled wallet on the assumption of platform-affiliation.

The structural feature is **brand-as-asset**: the operator extracts the legitimate platform's brand-trust without contractual basis, accumulates deposits on that exploited trust, runs custodial flow, and exits citing a "hack" that cannot be independently verified.

The exit-as-hack pattern is the **canonical T5.007 exit primitive**: the operator announces an external compromise (a "hack" of the operator-controlled wallet) which is structurally indistinguishable from an exit-scam without independent forensic verification. The on-chain outflow pattern often surfaces a single coordinated transfer (consistent with operator exit) rather than the dispersion pattern characteristic of an external attacker exfiltrating quickly under time pressure.

## Observed indicators

- **Off-platform service** branding itself with a legitimate platform's name without official partnership announcement; absence from the legitimate platform's authorised-third-party-service registry (where one exists).
- **Custodial deposit flow:** users deposit USDC / ETH / BTC into a service-controlled wallet rather than connecting their own wallet to a smart-contract integration.
- **"Hack" announcement on Telegram / Discord / X** characterising an external compromise of the operator-controlled wallet, typically without independent forensic disclosure or on-chain attacker-attribution analysis.
- **Communication blackout post-announcement:** absence of follow-up updates, no public operator identification, no observable recovery effort, no engagement with industry-press or community-forensic analysts.
- **On-chain exit-pattern fingerprint:** single coordinated outflow transfer pattern from the operator wallet (consistent with operator exit) rather than the dispersion / fragmented-extraction pattern characteristic of external attacker exfiltration.
- **Pre-event operating window:** the service operated publicly through some period (months to years) with no formal partnership announced; users assumed an affiliation that did not exist.

## Detection signals

- **Platform-side authoritative-third-party-service registry publication:** the legitimate platform publishes a vetted registry of authorised third-party services. Absence from the registry is the load-bearing user-side detection signal.
- **Brand-impersonation surveillance:** trademark monitoring; domain monitoring (typosquat / brand-association domain registration); Telegram-handle / X-handle brand-impersonation monitoring; legitimate-platform-side cease-and-desist outreach to clearly-impersonating services.
- **Industry-press cohort framing:** secondary-coverage of T5.007 cases (KuCoin and Cryptorank coverage of the Polycule case characterised it as fitting the soft-rug pattern) provides cohort-level detection at the framework layer.
- **On-chain exit-pattern analysis:** post-announcement on-chain analysis of the outflow pattern to distinguish operator-exit from external-attacker exfiltration; coordinated single-transfer pattern is the operator-exit fingerprint.
- **Operator-attribution surface:** post-announcement attempts to identify the operators publicly; communication blackout in response to attribution attempts is itself a T5.007 fingerprint.

## Real-world examples

- **Polycule trading bot — third-party brand-impersonation soft rug — 2026-01 (canonical T5.007 case)** — ~$230,000 in user funds frozen / withdrawn by operator. The Telegram-distributed "Polycule trading bot" branded itself as a Polymarket-integrated copy-trading service and operated a custodial wallet that users deposited USDC into. On January 13, 2026, the Polycule operators announced that the bot's wallet had been "hacked" and ~$230,000 in user funds had been drained. After the announcement, the operators went into communication blackout — no follow-up updates, no public operator identification, no observable recovery effort. The KuCoin and Cryptorank coverage characterised the event as fitting the soft-rug pattern: a custodial third-party tool brands itself with a legitimate platform's name, accumulates user deposits, and exits citing an external compromise that cannot be independently verified. Polycule occupies a category that v0.1 OAK does not cleanly cover at the existing T5 / T11 sub-Technique level: not a token-level rug (T5.001 / T2.002); not an operator-side treasury-management exit (T5.005 — the operator was never contractually associated with Polymarket); not phishing (T4 — users deposited willingly); not a custodial-vendor compromise (T11.001 — there was no third-party signing-vendor relationship; the Polycule operators *were* the custodian). Attribution: `inferred-strong`. The Polycule operators were never associated with Polymarket; the platform clarified it had no relationship with the bot. Operator identity was not publicly resolved as of v0.1; the disappearance pattern (communication blackout following the "hack" announcement, no recovery effort, no public operator identification) is consistent with an exit-scam rather than a third-party intrusion. See [`examples/2026-01-polymarket-polycule-bot.md`](../examples/2026-01-polymarket-polycule-bot.md).

## Mitigations

- **Platform-side authoritative-third-party-service registry (highest-leverage):** legitimate platforms with material third-party tooling ecosystems (Polymarket, Hyperliquid, Pump.fun, prediction-market and trader-tooling ecosystems generally) should publish authoritative registries of authorised partners. The registry is the load-bearing user-side detection primitive — users can verify a service's authorisation by registry lookup before depositing.
- **Platform-side brand-impersonation surveillance:** trademark / domain / Telegram-handle / X-handle monitoring + cease-and-desist outreach to clearly-impersonating services. The platform-side surveillance burden is non-trivial but the alternative (relying on user-side identification of impersonation) imposes the burden on individual users.
- **User-side custodial-deposit invariant:** treat any deposit to an off-platform service as a custodial deposit to the service operator, regardless of brand affiliation claimed; prefer self-custody-with-smart-contract-integration over operator-controlled-wallet deposit flows.
- **Industry-press cohort framing:** secondary-coverage outlets that recognise T5.007 cases as a structural class (KuCoin and Cryptorank coverage of the Polycule case) accelerate cohort-level user awareness across competing platforms.
- **On-chain exit-pattern analysis as evidence-of-exit-scam:** post-announcement on-chain forensic analysis (single coordinated transfer pattern vs. dispersion pattern) provides the technical evidence to distinguish operator-exit from external-attacker exfiltration. Public-record exit-pattern analysis disciplines future T5.007 attempts by raising attribution-resolution probability.

OAK Mitigation cross-references at v0.1 are a **gap** — no existing OAK-M* entry directly maps to platform-side brand-impersonation surveillance or authoritative-third-party-service-registry publication. v0.x candidate Mitigation entries: "OAK-M-x platform-side authoritative-third-party-service-registry"; "OAK-M-x platform-side brand-impersonation surveillance"; "OAK-M-x on-chain exit-pattern analysis as exit-scam evidence".

## Discussion

T5.007 closes a structural gap in OAK v0.1's T5 coverage. The dominant v0.1 T5 sub-Techniques (T5.001-T5.006) all operate within a real platform's contractual / on-chain primitive surface — hard LP drain, slow LP trickle, hidden mint, sandwich MEV, treasury-management exit, vesting-cliff dump. T5.007 introduces an extraction class that operates *off-platform* with the legitimate platform's brand as the exploited asset.

The structural OAK lesson is that **brand-trust is itself an exploitable asset** for value-extraction operators, and platform-side authoritative-third-party-service registries are a load-bearing user-side mitigation primitive that legitimate platforms should publish for any tooling ecosystem with non-trivial deposit volume.

The T11.005-vs-T5.007 distinction is structurally important and worth preserving in future contributor guidance:

- **T11.005** covers end-to-end operator-controlled platform fraud where the platform itself is the vehicle of theft. The operator constructs an apparently-original platform brand (BeurAx-class fake exchanges; OneCoin / HyperVerse fake yield products) and the brand has no legitimate counterpart.
- **T5.007** covers off-platform service that brands itself in a *legitimate platform's* name without contractual basis. The brand has a legitimate counterpart whose trust is exploited; the legitimate platform is a third-party victim of the brand-impersonation alongside the depositing users.

The exit-as-hack pattern is the **canonical T5.007 exit primitive**. The operator announces an external compromise of the operator-controlled wallet — a claim that is structurally indistinguishable from an exit-scam without independent forensic verification. The on-chain outflow pattern often surfaces a single coordinated transfer (consistent with operator exit) rather than the dispersion pattern characteristic of an external attacker exfiltrating quickly under time pressure. Post-announcement communication blackout reinforces the operator-exit interpretation.

The structural similarity to the **Inferno Drainer / DaaS pattern** at the brand-as-exploited-asset layer is informative but not identical: DaaS phishing captures keys at signing time; T5.007 captures funds at *deposit* time. Users send funds voluntarily, expecting custodial-but-honest service. Defender practice should treat any deposit to an off-platform brand-impersonating service as a custodial deposit to the *service operator's identity* rather than to the impersonated platform's brand.

The Polymarket cohort is structurally significant for T5.007 because the platform's prediction-market tooling ecosystem has a high density of off-platform third-party services (copy-trading bots, analytics tools, trading agents) and the brand-impersonation surface is correspondingly broad. Pipeline anchor cases for v0.x include additional Polymarket-branded soft-rug events; Hyperliquid-branded soft-rug events; Pump.fun-branded soft-rug events; and trader-tooling-ecosystem-branded soft-rug events across the broader DeFi space.

Promotion to `stable` will follow once additional cohort cases land and once at least one case reaches `confirmed` attribution strength (named-individual operator attribution via DOJ / regulator action or via ZachXBT-style public-forensic identification with corroborating documentation). The class anchor at v0.1 (Polycule case) is sufficient for `emerging` status but the multi-anchor pattern that warrants `stable` is still forming.
