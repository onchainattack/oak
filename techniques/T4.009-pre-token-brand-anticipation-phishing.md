# OAK-T4.009 — Pre-token Brand-Anticipation Phishing

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T4 (Access Acquisition)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (primary); Solana (secondary; EigenLayer / zkSync anticipation cohort has cross-chain analogues on Solana-based protocol token-anticipation campaigns)
**First documented:** ~2023-10 (zkSync airdrop-anticipation phishing cohort); class-level scaling accelerated through EigenLayer anticipation (early 2024) and Polymarket POLY anticipation (post-October-2025 CMO confirmation)
**Aliases:** "pre-token phishing", "airdrop-anticipation scam", "future-token typosquat", "brand-ambiguity phishing", "pre-launch anticipation drainer"

## Description

Phishing campaigns that exploit a **structural information gap**: a platform has publicly signalled future token plans (CEO statements, blog posts, community calls, roadmap references) but has not yet launched a token. The attacker constructs typosquat / near-miss domains and dApps that purport to be the platform's official "airdrop claim" / "token launch" / "early-access allocation" portal. The deceptive signal is the platform's own public signalling — the platform created the ambiguity about a future token event; the attacker supplies the claim-surface the ambiguity implies.

T4.009 is **structurally distinct** from sibling T4 Techniques at the *lure-substrate* level:

- **T4.002 (Compromised Front-End Permit Solicitation)** — the attacker compromises a *real platform's existing* user-facing surface. T4.009 targets a platform where **no token or token-claim surface has ever existed**; the entire lure is built on the platform's own future-event signalling.
- **T4.008 (Fake-DEX Clone-Frontend Phishing)** — the attacker deploys a counterfeit clone of an *existing* DEX UI. T4.009's target platform has no deployed token contract, no claim portal, and no on-chain distribution surface — the attacker is constructing a UI for a future event that the platform itself has only signalled.
- **T6.002 (Fake-Audit Claim)** — the attacker lies about an audit. T4.009's attacker does not claim a false audit — the ambiguity is around a *future event* that the platform itself has encouraged users to anticipate.
- **T4.001 / T4.004 / T4.005** — define the on-chain extraction primitive (Permit2 misuse, allowance-drainer, setApprovalForAll-drainer). T4.009 chains into the same drainer-kit substrate after the user connects their wallet to the fake claim portal.

The novel structural feature is the **pre-token information gap** as the lure substrate. The platform's own marketing creates the expectation that a token will exist; the attacker supplies the concrete claim-surface to satisfy that expectation before the platform does. The detection-and-mitigation surface lives at the **platform-side communication-and-domain-registration layer** (the platform should defensively register typosquat domains and pre-announce the canonical claim URL before any token launch) rather than at the ad-platform enforcement layer that resolves T4.008.

Three distinguishable cohort anchors:

- **Polymarket POLY anticipation cohort (canonical)** — Polymarket's CMO publicly confirmed future token plans in October 2025. Within weeks, attacker-operated typosquat domains and dApps claiming "POLY airdrop claim" / "Polymarket token launch" appeared, targeting Polymarket's user base with wallet-drainer backends. Calibration anchor: the platform's own CMO-issued public confirmation of future token plans created the ambiguity the attacker exploited. No POLY token had been deployed on-chain at the time the phishing campaigns were active.
- **EigenLayer token anticipation cohort (early 2024)** — EigenLayer publicly signalled an intention to launch a token (later launched as EIGEN). During the pre-launch window, attacker-operated "EigenLayer airdrop claim" sites targeted restakers who had deposited into EigenLayer's protocol, exploiting the ambiguity of "you deposited into the protocol → you're entitled to an airdrop → here's the claim portal." EIGEN did not yet exist on-chain during the most active phishing window.
- **zkSync airdrop anticipation cohort (2023–2024)** — zkSync publicly signalled an intention to launch a governance token (later launched as ZK in June 2024). During the 12+ month pre-launch window, attacker-operated "zkSync airdrop claim" sites targeted zkSync bridge users and ecosystem participants. The extended pre-launch window created a protracted exposure surface: users had months of "airdrop coming" signalling to internalise, making the fake claim portal more credible.

## Observed indicators

- **Domain-side fingerprints:** typosquat / near-miss domains combining the platform name with "airdrop," "claim," "token," or "launch" (`polymarket-claim.com`, `eigenlayer-airdrop.io`, `zksync-token.app`); recent domain registration dates post-dating the platform's public token-plan signalling.
- **Platform-side signalling fingerprints:** the platform's own public statements (CEO interviews, blog posts, roadmap references, community AMA answers) that create the expectation of a future token event. The attacker's domain registration window tracks the platform's public-signalling timeline.
- **On-chain absence fingerprint:** the claimed token either (a) has no on-chain contract deployment, (b) has no official claim portal address published by the platform, or (c) has a publicly-signalled-but-not-executed launch event. The attacker's claim portal is live before the platform's.
- **Drainer-backend fingerprints:** the same drainer-kit substrate observed in T4.008 (Inferno / Angel / AngelFerno; MS Drainer; PhishLab) at the on-chain extraction layer.

## Detection signals

- **Platform-side pre-launch defensive registration (highest-leverage):** platforms signalling future token plans should defensively register typosquat / near-miss domains covering the `[platform]-airdrop`, `[platform]-claim`, `[platform]-token`, and `[platform]-launch` patterns *before* the public signalling creates the ambiguity window.
- **Platform-side canonical-URL pre-announcement:** platforms should publish the canonical token-claim URL (even if the claim portal itself is not yet live) at the moment of public signalling, so users have a ground-truth reference before attacker domains appear.
- **Domain-reputation services:** ScamSniffer / Web3 Antivirus / SEAL ISAC drainer-website blocklists should ingest pre-token-anticipation domains as a discrete phishing sub-class; domain-reputation scoring should penalise domains whose registration date post-dates the platform's token-plan signalling but whose content claims to be the platform's official claim portal.
- **Wallet-side pre-trade simulation:** MetaMask / Rabby / Phantom / OKX Wallet pre-trade simulation surfaces should flag domains combining platform-name + air-drop/claim/token/launch keywords when the claimed token has no on-chain deployment or no known official claim contract address.
- **Community-side amplification monitoring:** monitoring of platform-community channels (Discord, Telegram, X/Twitter) for links to non-canonical claim portals; community moderators should maintain a pinned list of known phishing domains during the pre-launch window.

## Mitigations

- **Platform-side pre-launch defensive domain registration (highest-leverage):** platforms that signal future token plans should preemptively register typosquat / near-miss domains covering `[platform]-airdrop`, `[platform]-claim`, `[platform]-token`, `[platform]-launch` before public signalling creates the ambiguity window. This is a platform-operational-cost mitigation rather than a protocol-level mitigation.
- **Platform-side canonical-claim-URL pre-announcement:** publish the single official URL (`claim.[platform].com` or equivalent) at the moment of public token-plan signalling, even if the claim portal is not yet live. Users should be trained to expect *one* domain and reject all others.
- **Platform-side communication discipline:** platforms should communicate token plans with explicit language about the non-existence of any current claim portal, the expected launch timeline, and the canonical domain. Ambiguous "airdrop coming soon" language creates the structural ambiguity T4.009 exploits.
- **Domain-reputation ingestion of pre-token-anticipation patterns:** phishing-domain blocklists should flag domains combining platform-name + airdrop/claim/token/launch when the registration date post-dates the platform's public token-plan signalling and the claimed token has no on-chain deployment.
- **Wallet-side pre-trade simulation:** wallet UIs should flag domains matching pre-token-anticipation patterns and surface a warning inline with the connection prompt when the claimed token is not deployed on-chain.
- **Community-side domain-allowlist pinning:** platform community moderators (Discord, Telegram, X/Twitter) should maintain a pinned channel listing the *single* canonical claim domain and known phishing domains during the pre-launch window, updated as new phishing domains appear.

OAK Mitigation cross-references at v0.1 are a **gap** — no existing OAK-M* entry directly maps to platform-side pre-launch defensive domain registration or canonical-claim-URL pre-announcement. v0.x candidate Mitigation entries: "OAK-M-x pre-launch defensive domain registration"; "OAK-M-x canonical-claim-URL pre-announcement"; "OAK-M-x platform-side token-plan communication discipline".

## Discussion

T4.009 addresses a structurally novel phishing-lure substrate that is not covered by any existing T4 Technique. T4.002 (Compromised Frontend) requires a *real platform surface to compromise*; T4.008 (Fake-DEX Clone) requires an *existing DEX to clone*. T4.009's target platform has no token, no claim portal, and no on-chain distribution surface — the entire lure is built on the platform's own future-event signalling. The platform creates the demand; the attacker fills it.

The structural OAK lesson is that **platform-side pre-launch communication is a first-class attack-surface dimension**. A platform's public signalling about future token plans is not merely marketing — it is a load-bearing component of the defender's mental model, and ambiguity in that signalling is exploitable. The *absence* of a token is structurally more dangerous than the existence of one because the platform cannot point to a canonical deployment to anchor user expectations.

The EigenLayer and zkSync cohorts are instructive as calibration anchors because both platforms had technically sophisticated user bases (restakers; L2 bridge users) who were nonetheless successfully targeted. The phishing surface is not gated by user sophistication — it is gated by the platform's own signalling ambiguity. The extended pre-launch window in the zkSync case (12+ months) demonstrates that the exposure window scales with the platform's signalling timeline: the longer the platform signals "token coming" without deploying one, the more attacker-operated claim portals look chronologically credible.

The class is included at v0.1 with `emerging` maturity rather than `stable` because (a) the per-incident anchor cohort is still forming — the Polymarket POLY cohort is live at v0.1 and incident-specific anchors are not yet independently published, (b) the platform-side mitigation surface (pre-launch defensive registration) is not yet operationalised by any major pre-token platform, and (c) the drainer-kit operator-cluster attribution depth is partial. Promotion to `stable` will follow once per-incident worked-example anchors land for the Polymarket, EigenLayer, and zkSync cohorts and at least one major pre-token platform operationalises the pre-launch defensive-domain-registration mitigation.

The class structurally generalises beyond EVM/Solana — any chain where a protocol signals future token plans without a deployed token has the same pre-token-anticipation phishing surface. The structural condition is: (1) a platform signals a future token event, (2) no token is deployed on-chain, (3) the time-window between signalling and deployment is long enough for attacker infrastructure to be deployed and for users to internalise the expectation. These conditions are chain-agnostic.
