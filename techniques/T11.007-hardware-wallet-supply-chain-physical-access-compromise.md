# OAK-T11.007 — Hardware-wallet Supply-chain / Physical-access Compromise

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (the substrate-of-extraction is the BIP39 seed phrase / hardware-wallet-controlled private-key material; downstream extraction occurs across whichever chains the affected wallet supports)
**First documented:** 2017 (early Ledger Nano S inserts cohort) at the cohort-shape layer; 2020-01-31 (Trezor One / Model T RDP-downgrade Kraken disclosure) for the physical-access capability anchor; 2025 counterfeit Ledger Nano S Plus cohort for the deployed-attack anchor; 2023–2026 fake-firmware-update / recovery-app phishing cohort for the active-phishing sub-pattern
**Aliases:** "counterfeit hardware wallet", "fake Ledger / fake Trezor", "pre-seeded recovery card", "hardware-wallet phishing", "physical-access seed extraction"

## Description

Brand-impersonation-driven hardware substitution, pre-seeded-recovery-card scams, physical-access seed-extraction attacks against hardware-wallet microcontrollers, and brand-trust-leveraged phishing targeting hardware-wallet users for seed-phrase exfiltration. The structural feature is *brand-impersonation-driven trust transferred to a structurally-compromised hardware / channel surface* — the legitimate-vendor's brand provides the social-engineering substrate that produces user-side trust in a hardware unit, communication, or companion-app that the legitimate vendor did not produce.

T11.007 has **three structurally-distinct sub-patterns**:

- **T11.007.001 — Counterfeit-hardware substitution.** Counterfeit devices distributed through informal retail channels (Chinese marketplaces — AliExpress / Taobao third-party sellers; Telegram-based resellers; in-person counterfeit-product markets; second-hand purchases without packaging) that brand-impersonate legitimate Ledger / Trezor / similar products. The counterfeit unit either pre-seeds the recovery phrase (the user funds a wallet whose seed the operator already knows) or replaces the secure element with an attacker-controlled microcontroller storing seeds in plaintext flash with embedded radio for exfiltration. Canonical (deployed-attack anchor): 2025 counterfeit Ledger Nano S Plus cohort (~$9.5M+ across ~50+ victims, 20+ blockchain ecosystems; ESP32-S3 microcontroller substitution with chip markings physically scraped off; antenna trace on PCB for 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi / BLE radio exfiltration; legitimate Ledger Nano S Plus is air-gapped by design); see [`examples/2025-counterfeit-ledger-nano-s-plus-cohort.md`](../examples/2025-counterfeit-ledger-nano-s-plus-cohort.md). Adjacent: 2021 mailed-fake-hardware-wallet replacement campaign leveraging the December 2020 Ledger customer-data leak.

- **T11.007.002 — Physical-access hardware-side seed extraction.** Voltage-glitching, side-channel, or chip-tampering attacks against the wallet's microcontroller / secure element, requiring physical access to the device. Canonical (capability anchor): 2020-01-31 Trezor One / Model T RDP-downgrade voltage-glitch attack disclosed by Kraken Security Labs; the underlying STM32F205 / STM32F427 microcontroller flaw is structural rather than patchable; vendor-acknowledged structural vulnerability. The BIP39 passphrase feature is the defender-side mitigation primitive that bounds realised loss to passphrase strength rather than preventing the attack outright (an attacker who extracts the seed obtains a seed that, without the passphrase, opens an empty decoy wallet). Newer Trezor products (Trezor Safe 3 / Safe 5, 2023–2024) ship with a dedicated EAL6+ certified secure element addressing the structural vulnerability at the hardware layer. See [`examples/2020-01-trezor-kraken-rdp-downgrade.md`](../examples/2020-01-trezor-kraken-rdp-downgrade.md).

- **T11.007.003 — Brand-trust-leveraged active phishing for seed-phrase exfiltration.** Phishing campaigns impersonating legitimate hardware-wallet vendor communications via email, physical mail, in-app prompts (via trojanised companion apps), and unsolicited mailed-hardware-replacement letters. The December 2020 Ledger customer-data breach (which leaked email + physical-address + phone-number data of ~273,000 customers) provides the operational substrate for personalisation data, increasing per-recipient success rates across the broader cohort. Sub-campaigns at v0.1: Ledger-data-breach-leveraged email phishing (2020 onward); Kaspersky / ESET 2023 spring cohort (85,000+ scam emails detected in a single quarter); Trezor-impersonating physical-mail-letter campaign (2025–2026; QR codes leading to fake `trezor.authentication-check.io` / `ledger.setuptransactioncheck.com` domains); trojanised-Ledger-Live / trojanised-Trezor-Suite companion-app cohort (2023 onward, infostealer-driven). See [`examples/2023-2026-fake-firmware-update-phishing-cohort.md`](../examples/2023-2026-fake-firmware-update-phishing-cohort.md).

T11.007 is **structurally distinct** from adjacent T11 sub-Techniques:

- **T11.002** (Wallet-Software Distribution Compromise) — substitutes the *software* the user runs on a legitimate device. T11.007.001 substitutes the *hardware itself*; T11.007.003 substitutes the *vendor communication channel* or the *companion-app code path*.
- **T11.006** (Cold-storage Seed-phrase Exfiltration at Rest) — third-party storage-service-at-rest failure surface (LastPass / iCloud-MetaMask). T11.007.003 is *active phishing-driven* seed-phrase solicitation rather than passive storage compromise.
- **T4 phishing** — the substrate-of-extraction in T4 is an on-chain authority grant (Permit2, allowance, setApprovalForAll). T11.007.003's substrate-of-extraction is the seed phrase itself.

## Observed indicators

- **Purchase channel:** hardware purchased through informal channels (Chinese-marketplace third-party sellers, Telegram resellers, in-person counterfeit-product markets, second-hand without packaging) rather than the vendor's authorised retail channels.
- **Tamper-evidence:** packaging without the vendor's tamper-evident seal (Ledger's tamper-evident packaging; Trezor's holographic seal); device behaviour during initialisation that does not match the vendor's documented onboarding flow.
- **Hardware fingerprints:** chip markings physically scraped off; antenna traces on a PCB for a device whose legitimate variant is air-gapped; visible solder rework or piggyback components.
- **Vendor-impersonation communications:** unsolicited emails / physical mail / in-app prompts requesting the user to "verify" / "update firmware" / "complete security upgrade" / "replace compromised device" by entering their seed phrase; QR codes leading to typosquat / near-miss domains; phone calls or chat-support requests soliciting the seed phrase.
- **Companion-app behaviour:** trojanised Ledger Live / Trezor Suite UI fakes an error and initiates a "recovery process" prompting for the seed phrase; the legitimate companion app does *not* request the seed phrase.

## Detection signals

- **User-side purchase verification:** authorised-retailer-purchase verification; refusal of any informal-channel purchase regardless of how legitimate the device appears.
- **Vendor-side authenticity check:** vendor-side device-genuineness check (Ledger Live's authenticity verification; Trezor Suite's parallel check) executed on first connection of any new device.
- **Tamper-evident packaging inspection:** vendor-supplied tamper-evident seal verification at unboxing; refusal to use a device whose packaging shows tampering.
- **Vendor invariant enforcement:** *legitimate vendor never asks for the seed phrase across any communication channel* — invariant published by Ledger, Trezor, and the broader hardware-wallet category in writing through user-onboarding documentation, customer-support pages, and recurring security advisories. User-side enforcement of the invariant is the binding mitigation for T11.007.003.
- **Vendor-side continuous-update threat-intelligence:** Ledger's "Ongoing phishing campaigns" page (continuous-update tracking of active campaigns); SatoshiLabs / Trezor parallel security-advisory feed; Kaspersky / ESET / Microsoft / Group-IB threat-intelligence on hardware-wallet-targeting phishing cohorts.
- **BIP39 passphrase as physical-access mitigation:** for T11.007.002 specifically, the BIP39 passphrase is the structural defender-side primitive that bounds realised extraction outcome to passphrase strength rather than preventing the extraction itself.

## Real-world examples

- **Counterfeit Ledger Nano S Plus hardware-wallet supply-chain compromise cohort — multi-chain — 2025 (canonical T11.007.001 deployed-attack anchor)** — Publicly-reported confirmed losses ~$9.5M+ across ~50+ documented victims, spanning 20+ blockchain ecosystems, per the Brazilian-cybersecurity-researcher disclosure that surfaced the cohort in 2025. Counterfeit operation: ESP32-S3 microcontroller substitution with chip markings scraped off; plaintext seed-phrase storage in flash; antenna trace on PCB for 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi / BLE radio exfiltration; visual-fidelity brand impersonation of legitimate Ledger Nano S Plus. Distribution channel: AliExpress / Taobao third-party sellers, Telegram-based resellers, in-person counterfeit-product markets. Multi-platform malware infrastructure: trojanised companion apps for Android, Windows, macOS, and iOS (via Apple TestFlight). Attribution: `inferred-strong` at the cohort-operator-level (multi-platform / multi-vector / cross-blockchain coordination indicates a structured operator-cluster); `pseudonymous` at the per-incident named-individual level. See [`examples/2025-counterfeit-ledger-nano-s-plus-cohort.md`](../examples/2025-counterfeit-ledger-nano-s-plus-cohort.md).
- **Trezor One / Model T RDP-downgrade voltage-glitch seed extraction (Kraken Security Labs disclosure) — hardware wallets — 2020-01-31 (canonical T11.007.002 capability anchor)** — $0 realised on-chain loss; coordinated-vulnerability disclosure from Kraken Security Labs to SatoshiLabs (responsibly disclosed 2019-10-30; public disclosure 2020-01-31). The STM32 microcontroller's Read Protection feature could be downgraded from RDP2 to RDP1 by voltage-glitching during boot-up, exposing SRAM contents (including the encrypted seed) over the ARM SWD debugging interface. Approximately 15 minutes of physical access plus modest hardware-hacking equipment required. Vendor-acknowledged structural vulnerability; not patchable by firmware update. BIP39 passphrase published as the defender-side mitigation primitive. Trezor Safe 3 (2023) and Trezor Safe 5 (2024) ship with a dedicated EAL6+ secure element addressing the structural vulnerability. See [`examples/2020-01-trezor-kraken-rdp-downgrade.md`](../examples/2020-01-trezor-kraken-rdp-downgrade.md).
- **Fake hardware-wallet firmware-update / recovery-app phishing cohort — Ledger / Trezor user base — 2023 onward (canonical T11.007.003 active-phishing anchor)** — Cohort-level cumulative loss not centrally tabulated; documented per-incident cases through 2023–2026 in the high-five-figure to low-six-figure range. Kaspersky's spring 2023 cohort tabulation reported "over 85,000 scam emails" detected in a single quarter. Sub-campaigns: Ledger-data-breach-leveraged email phishing (using the December 2020 Ledger customer-data leak — ~273,000 customer email + physical-address + phone-number records — for personalisation); Trezor-impersonating physical-mail-letter campaign (2025–2026, QR codes to typosquat domains); trojanised companion-app cohort (Kaspersky 2023 disclosure: infostealers swap fragments of legitimate Ledger Live / Trezor Suite code with trojanised code that fakes an error and initiates a "recovery process"). Attribution: `inferred-strong` at the cohort-operator-level; `pseudonymous` at the per-incident named-individual level for most sub-campaigns. See [`examples/2023-2026-fake-firmware-update-phishing-cohort.md`](../examples/2023-2026-fake-firmware-update-phishing-cohort.md).

## Mitigations

- **Authorised-retailer-purchase invariant (highest-leverage for T11.007.001):** any user with non-trivial holdings should purchase hardware exclusively through the vendor's authorised retail channels (vendor official store; named authorised retailers); informal-channel purchases (Chinese marketplaces, Telegram resellers, in-person counterfeit markets, second-hand without packaging) should be treated as **structurally compromised regardless of how legitimate the device appears**.
- **Tamper-evident packaging inspection:** vendor-supplied tamper-evident seal verification at unboxing; refusal to initialise a device whose packaging shows tampering.
- **Vendor-side device-genuineness check:** execute the vendor's authenticity verification (Ledger Live's check; Trezor Suite's parallel) on first connection of any new device. Reject devices that fail the check.
- **BIP39 passphrase as physical-access mitigation (T11.007.002):** for users with non-trivial holdings on Trezor One / Model T or any hardware whose underlying microcontroller is known-vulnerable to physical-access seed-extraction, BIP39 passphrase usage should be treated as **mandatory** (not optional). For new deployments where the physical-access threat model is non-trivial, prefer secure-element-equipped hardware (Trezor Safe series, Ledger Nano series, or equivalent with Common-Criteria-certified secure element).
- **Legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase invariant (highest-leverage for T11.007.003):** user-side enforcement that the legitimate vendor *never* requests the seed phrase across email, physical mail, in-app prompts, phone calls, chat support, or firmware-update flows. Refuse to enter the seed phrase into any surface other than the legitimate device's own physical input mechanism.
- **Vendor-side continuous-update threat-intelligence subscription:** subscribe to vendor-side phishing-campaign tracking (Ledger's "Ongoing phishing campaigns" page; Trezor's parallel advisories) and treat any unsolicited communication purporting to be from the vendor as a phishing candidate until verified through the vendor's authenticated channels.
- **Companion-app integrity:** download Ledger Live / Trezor Suite exclusively from the vendor's official site or through an authenticated app-store entry; verify cryptographic checksums where the vendor publishes them; treat companion-app prompts soliciting the seed phrase as definitively malicious.

OAK Mitigation cross-references at v0.1 are a **gap** — no existing OAK-M* entry directly maps to the hardware-wallet supply-chain authenticity surface or the brand-impersonation phishing surface. v0.x candidate Mitigation entries: "OAK-M-x authorised-retailer-purchase invariant"; "OAK-M-x device-genuineness check execution"; "OAK-M-x BIP39 passphrase invariant for known-vulnerable hardware"; "OAK-M-x legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase invariant".

## Discussion

T11.007 closes a structural gap in OAK v0.1's T11 coverage. The dominant v0.1 T11 worked examples (Bybit / Safe{Wallet} for T11.001, Atomic Wallet for T11.002, WazirX for T11.003) all share an *organisational-customer-or-vendor-side compromise* shape; T11.007 introduces a **brand-impersonation-and-supply-chain** failure surface that is structurally distinct.

The structural OAK lesson is that **a hardware wallet purchased outside authorised retail channels is structurally indistinguishable from a deliberate seed-exfiltration device**, and the legitimate-vendor's brand impersonation is the social-engineering surface that produces user-side trust. The wallet's cryptographic correctness (secure element architecture, firmware integrity, BIP39 implementation) is meaningful only if the device the user holds is the device the legitimate vendor produced. Hardware-wallet purchase-channel verification is a first-class user-side T11 mitigation surface.

The three sub-patterns differ at the **substrate** layer:

- **T11.007.001** substitutes the hardware unit itself; the brand-impersonation provides social-engineering trust at purchase time.
- **T11.007.002** exploits a structural microcontroller vulnerability against a legitimate device; physical access is the binding constraint.
- **T11.007.003** exploits the vendor's communication channel as a brand-impersonation surface; the device is legitimate but the *channel* is counterfeit.

The 2020 Ledger customer-data leak is structural to the cohort: ~273,000 customer email + physical-address + phone-number records provide ongoing personalisation data that produces apparent legitimacy in T11.007.003 phishing campaigns. The user-side mental model "this email / letter / app is from Ledger because it has their branding, addresses me by name, and references my purchase history" is structurally false because the operational substrate is the leaked customer database.

The **vendor-side commitment + user-side enforcement** of the *legitimate-vendor-never-asks-for-seed-phrase* invariant is the load-bearing mitigation primitive for T11.007.003. The vendor commits in writing through user-onboarding documentation, customer-support pages, and recurring security advisories; the user enforces the invariant by refusing to enter the seed phrase into any surface other than the legitimate device's own physical input mechanism. The invariant is simple but operationally fragile because phishing campaigns continuously refine their personalisation surface.

For T11.007.002, the BIP39 passphrase converts physical-access seed-extraction capability from a "wallet drainable" outcome to a "wallet-revealing-empty-decoy" outcome. The structural OAK lesson is that **the physical-access threat model is operationally distinct from the software-attack threat model**, and hardware-wallet selection must be evaluated against both threat models independently.

Promotion to `stable` will follow once additional per-incident worked-example anchors land — additional counterfeit-hardware cohorts beyond the 2025 Ledger Nano S Plus cohort, additional physical-access deployed-attack cases beyond the Trezor RDP-downgrade capability anchor, and additional sub-campaign anchors within the active-phishing cohort.
