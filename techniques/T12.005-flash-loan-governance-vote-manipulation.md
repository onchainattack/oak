# OAK-T12.005 — Flash-Loan Governance Vote Manipulation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T12 (Governance / Protocol Management)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (primary); any chain with flash-loan-enabled DEXs and on-chain governance token voting without a minimum-holding-period requirement
**First documented:** 2022 (Fortress Protocol, Beanstalk Farms, Elephant Money — all within a six-week window in April–May 2022)
**Aliases:** "flash-loan governance attack", "flash-loan vote capture", "single-block governance takeover", "flash-loan proposal passage", "uncollateralized governance-weight acquisition"

## Description

A governance-attack primitive in which the attacker acquires governance voting power via uncollateralized flash-loan borrowing rather than through market purchase, delegation, or long-term accumulation. The attacker borrows a controlling quantity of governance tokens from DEX liquidity pools through a flash loan, uses the temporary voting power to create, vote on, and execute a malicious governance proposal (treasury drain, mint-authority grant, contract upgrade, or parameter change), and repays the flash loan within the same transaction or across a minimal number of blocks. The governance contract has no snapshot mechanism that measures voting power at a prior block height, and no minimum holding-period requirement — making flash-loan-acquired voting power indistinguishable from long-term-holder voting power at the point of proposal execution.

T12.005 is structurally distinct from several adjacent OAK Techniques along the following axes:

- **vs T9.003 (Governance Attack — broad).** T9.003 is the parent-class Technique covering governance capture generally: multisig compromise, vote-buying, proposal-creation manipulation, delegation abuse, and flash-loan voting as one sub-pattern. T12.005 is the specific flash-loan-as-voting-power-primitive: the temporary, uncollateralized acquisition of governance weight via a DeFi lending primitive, used to pass proposals that benefit the attacker at protocol expense. T12.005 is the operational sub-Technique that describes *how* the voting power was acquired, while T9.003 describes *that* governance was captured. An incident involving flash-loan governance manipulation is tagged with both T12.005 (the acquisition primitive) and T9.003 (the governance-capture outcome). The mitigation ladder for T12.005 (snapshot voting power at proposal-creation time; enforce a minimum governance-token holding period) is narrower and more specific than the T9.003 mitigation ladder (which includes multisig hardening, quorum requirements, timelocks, and delegation controls).

- **vs T12.004 (Timelock-Free Protocol Upgrade Execution).** T12.004 covers the absence of a mandatory timelock between governance approval and on-chain execution — a governance-design decision that determines the blast radius of any governance compromise. T12.005 covers the acquisition of voting power itself — a governance-attack execution primitive that can be deployed whether or not a timelock exists. A flash-loan governance attack on a timelock-protected protocol queues a malicious proposal that executes after the timelock delay (giving users an exit window); the same attack on a timelock-free protocol executes instantly. T12.004 amplifies the extraction payoff of T12.005 by eliminating the user-exit window, but T12.005 is the load-bearing attack primitive regardless. A protocol can be vulnerable to T12.005 even if T12.004 is fully mitigated (a timelock exists) — the attacker can still pass the malicious proposal via flash-loaned votes; the timelock merely converts the extraction from instant to delayed.

- **vs T16.003 (Delegation Cluster Vote Takeover).** T16.003 covers vote-power acquisition through delegation — the attacker accumulates voting power by receiving delegations from a cluster of token holders (via social engineering, incentive programmes, or delegation-market mechanisms). T12.005 covers acquisition through flash loans — the attacker temporarily borrows tokens and returns them within the same transaction. The two Techniques produce the same outcome (attacker-controlled voting weight sufficient to pass a proposal) but through structurally different acquisition primitives: T16.003 requires off-chain coordination and delegation-graph manipulation; T12.005 requires only on-chain capital availability (the flash-loan fee) and a DEX with sufficient governance-token liquidity. The detection signals differ correspondingly: T16.003 detection runs on the delegation graph; T12.005 detection runs on same-block balance-spike and proposal-execution event correlation.

- **vs T9.002 (Flash-Loan-Enabled Exploit — broad).** T9.002 is the parent-class Technique for any exploit whose capital base is provided by a flash loan: oracle manipulation, liquidation attacks, arbitrage-extraction, and governance-vote acquisition. T12.005 is the governance-vote-specific sub-class of T9.002. An incident involving flash-loan governance manipulation is tagged with both T12.005 (the governance-specific acquisition primitive) and T9.002 (the flash-loan-as-capital-base precondition). The T9.002 surface is the capital-availability precondition; the T12.005 surface is the governance-design vulnerability that makes that capital politically consequential.

The canonical vulnerable governance design — the structural precondition for T12.005 — is a governance contract that measures voting power at the block height of proposal execution (or proposal creation, but without a minimum holding-period requirement) and accepts any token balance present at that block as valid voting power. If governance tokens can be acquired at block height N and voting power is measured at block height N, flash-loan-acquired tokens confer legitimate voting power. The fix is to measure voting power at a prior block height (a "snapshot" block) that precedes the proposal-creation block by a window longer than the flash-loan duration — typically one block prior to proposal submission, or a configurable snapshot block set at proposal-creation time. Any governance system where voting power is measured at the same block height at which tokens can be acquired (via flash loan) is structurally vulnerable to T12.005.

## Observed indicators

- A single transaction (or tightly clustered set of transactions within the same block) in which: (a) a large quantity of governance tokens is acquired from DEX liquidity pools, (b) a governance proposal is created and/or voted on, and (c) the governance tokens are returned to the DEX pools — all within atomic execution or across a minimal block span.
- Governance-token balance of the attacker address spikes from near-zero to a controlling percentage of total supply within a single block, coincident with proposal-creation and/or proposal-execution events from the governance contract.
- Same-block flash-loan borrow event (from a lending protocol or DEX pair) and governance-proposal execution event — the canonical T12.005 on-chain signature.
- Governance contract has no `getPriorVotes` (or equivalent snapshot-balance) function — voting power is measured at `block.number` rather than at a prior snapshot block height.
- Governance contract's proposal-creation function accepts a snapshot block parameter that can be set to the current block — the snapshot mechanism exists but is not load-bearing because it can be set to the block in which tokens are flash-loaned.

## Detection signals

- **Pre-deployment governance audit — snapshot mechanism verification.** Verify that the governance contract measures voting power at a block height that precedes the proposal-creation block by a window longer than one block (minimum: one block prior). Confirm that the snapshot block parameter, if configurable, cannot be set to the current block or to a block within the same transaction.
- **Pre-deployment governance audit — minimum holding-period verification.** Verify that the governance contract enforces a minimum holding period for governance tokens before they confer voting power (e.g., tokens must be held for N blocks before they can be used to vote, or tokens acquired in the same block as proposal creation do not confer voting power). This is a stronger guarantee than snapshot-block alone — a minimum holding period eliminates flash-loan voting even if the snapshot mechanism is misconfigured.
- **On-chain same-block flash-loan + governance-event correlation.** Monitor for transactions (or same-block transaction clusters) in which a flash-loan borrow event (e.g., Aave `FlashLoan` event, Uniswap V2 `Swap` event with near-identical input/output amounts, or a DEX-specific flash-loan event) coincides with a governance `ProposalCreated` or `ProposalExecuted` event where the proposer address matches the flash-loan recipient. This is the canonical T12.005 runtime detection signature.
- **Governance-token balance-spike monitoring.** Monitor governance-token `Transfer` events for addresses that receive a controlling percentage of total supply within a single block and then transfer the same quantity out within the same block or the immediately following block — the flash-loan borrow-and-repay pattern applied to governance tokens.
- **Governance-contract static analysis.** Verify that the governance contract's voting-power computation function calls a snapshot-balance function (e.g., `getPriorVotes(tokenHolder, snapshotBlock)`) rather than a current-balance function (e.g., `balanceOf(tokenHolder)`). Flag governance contracts that use `balanceOf` (or equivalent current-balance functions) for voting-power computation as critically vulnerable to T12.005.

## Real-world examples

- [`examples/2022-05-fortress-protocol-flash-loan-governance-attack.md`](../examples/2022-05-fortress-protocol-flash-loan-governance-attack.md) — Fortress Protocol flash-loan governance attack — BNB Chain — 2022-05-08 — ~$3M; attacker flash-loaned FTS governance tokens, created and passed a malicious proposal granting mint authority, minted tokens, and drained the protocol. The canonical T12.005 case on BNB Chain: a governance system where voting power was measured at the proposal-execution block, with no snapshot mechanism and no minimum holding period.
- **Beanstalk Farms — Ethereum — 2022-04-17 — ~$182M total (~$76M attacker net).** The Beanstalk attacker used ~$1B in Aave flash-loaned stablecoins to acquire BEAN/ETH LP tokens (which conferred STALK governance voting power), gaining ~67% of voting power, and exploited the `emergencyCommit` function to bypass the normal proposal lifecycle and execute a malicious proposal (BIP-18) that drained the protocol treasury. Beanstalk is the largest T12.005 incident by notional loss, though the acquisition primitive was flash-loaned stablecoins used to purchase LP tokens (a two-hop acquisition) rather than a direct flash loan of the governance token itself. The case is tagged T12.005 + T9.002 + T9.003. See [`examples/2022-04-beanstalk.md`](../examples/2022-04-beanstalk.md).
- **Elephant Money — BNB Chain — 2022-04-12 — ~$11M.** The Elephant Money attacker flash-loaned BNB, used it to acquire ELEPHANT governance tokens from PancakeSwap, created a malicious proposal granting mint authority, and drained the treasury. The on-chain signature is the canonical T12.005 pattern: same-transaction flash-loan borrow, governance-token acquisition, proposal execution, governance-token repayment. Maturity on BNB Chain in April–May 2022 suggests a copycat dynamic following the Beanstalk incident on Ethereum.

## Mitigations

- **Protocols — OAK-M11 (Governance Snapshot Voting Power).** Governance voting power MUST be measured at a block height that precedes the proposal-creation block by a minimum window longer than one block. The canonical implementation is Compound GovernorBravo's `getPriorVotes(tokenHolder, snapshotBlock)` pattern, where the snapshot block is set at proposal-creation time and is at least one block prior. The snapshot block parameter MUST NOT be configurable to the current block or to a block within the same transaction as proposal creation.
- **Protocols — OAK-M12 (Minimum Governance-Token Holding Period).** Governance tokens MUST be held for a minimum number of blocks before they confer voting power. Tokens acquired in the same block as proposal creation or proposal voting MUST NOT confer voting power. A minimum holding period of 1 block is sufficient to eliminate flash-loan voting (flash loans cannot span multiple blocks by definition). A longer holding period (e.g., 6,500 blocks / ~1 day on Ethereum) provides additional defense-in-depth against short-duration token-borrowing attacks that extend beyond flash loans (e.g., lending-protocol borrows repaid within one day).
- **Protocols — governance-token liquidity monitoring.** Monitor the DEX liquidity depth of the protocol's governance token relative to the quorum requirement. If the governance token's DEX liquidity (the maximum flash-loan-able quantity) exceeds the quorum threshold, the protocol is structurally vulnerable to T12.005 even with a snapshot mechanism — the attacker can acquire quorum-level voting power via flash loan, create a proposal with a snapshot block set to one block prior (when the attacker did not hold the tokens), and the proposal will fail because the attacker lacked voting power at the snapshot block. However, if the governance contract measures voting power at the execution block rather than a snapshot block, DEX liquidity depth above quorum is a standing T12.005 vulnerability.
- **Protocols — quorum threshold above flash-loan-able liquidity.** Set the governance quorum threshold above the maximum flash-loan-able quantity of governance tokens (the DEX liquidity depth). A quorum threshold that exceeds the total DEX liquidity of the governance token makes flash-loan quorum acquisition impossible — the attacker cannot borrow enough tokens to reach quorum because the tokens do not exist in DEX pools. This is a defense-in-depth measure that operates independently of the snapshot mechanism: even if the snapshot mechanism is misconfigured, the quorum threshold above DEX liquidity prevents flash-loan governance capture.
- **Users / integrators — monitor governance proposals for flash-loan voting patterns.** Monitor the governance-token balance of proposal creators and voters at the proposal-creation block; flag proposals where the creator's governance-token balance spiked from near-zero to a controlling percentage within the same block as proposal creation. This is a post-hoc detection signal rather than a prevention measure, but it provides a detection capability for protocols that have not implemented snapshot-based voting power.
- **Audit firms — flag flash-loan-votable governance designs as critical findings.** Any governance contract that measures voting power via `balanceOf` (or equivalent current-balance function) without a snapshot mechanism and without a minimum holding period should be flagged as critically vulnerable to T12.005, with the canonical mitigations (snapshot-block voting power, minimum holding period, quorum above DEX liquidity) recommended.

## Discussion

T12.005 is the governance-specific flash-loan primitive — the technique by which an attacker converts DeFi capital efficiency (uncollateralized lending) into political power (governance voting weight) within a single transaction. The technique was demonstrated at scale during a six-week window in April–May 2022 (Beanstalk, Elephant Money, Fortress Protocol), after which the governance-design standard shifted to incorporate snapshot-based voting power and minimum holding periods. Post-2022, large-scale T12.005 incidents at top-tier DeFi protocols with audited governance contracts are rarer precisely because the design standard improved — but the technique remains viable against any governance contract that measures voting power at the current block without a snapshot mechanism or minimum holding period.

The structural relationship between T12.005 and T12.004 is an amplification relationship: T12.004 (absence of a timelock) determines the blast radius of a successful T12.005 attack. A flash-loan governance attack on a timelock-protected protocol results in a queued malicious proposal that executes after the timelock delay, giving users an exit window. The same attack on a timelock-free protocol executes instantly. The Compound Timelock's 48-hour standard, combined with GovernorBravo's snapshot-based voting power, provides defense-in-depth against T12.005: even if an attacker could acquire flash-loaned voting power (prevented by the snapshot mechanism), the 48-hour timelock would give users an exit window (prevented only if the timelock is absent — T12.004).

The DEX-liquidity-depth-vs-quorum-threshold relationship is an underappreciated T12.005 surface. A protocol whose governance token has deep DEX liquidity relative to its quorum threshold is structurally more vulnerable to flash-loan governance capture than a protocol with shallow DEX liquidity — the attacker can borrow a larger fraction of quorum. This relationship is independent of the snapshot mechanism: if the snapshot mechanism is correctly implemented, flash-loaned tokens do not confer voting power regardless of DEX liquidity depth; but if the snapshot mechanism is misconfigured or absent, DEX liquidity depth above quorum is a standing T12.005 vulnerability. Protocols should model this relationship at governance-parameter design time and set quorum thresholds conservatively relative to DEX liquidity.

False-positive considerations: a same-block flash-loan event and governance-proposal-execution event correlation is a high-signal T12.005 indicator, but legitimate governance operations can also involve flash-loan-adjacent patterns (e.g., a protocol's own treasury management using flash loans for rebalancing, coincident with a scheduled governance proposal execution). The detection signal should be interpreted in the context of the governance contract's documented snapshot mechanism: a same-block flash-loan + proposal-execution event pair on a governance contract that uses `balanceOf` for voting power is a strong T12.005 signal; the same event pair on a governance contract with a properly-implemented snapshot mechanism is likely a coincidence or a legitimate operation.

T12.005 sits at the intersection of the T9 (Capital-Base / Flash Loan) and T12 (Governance / Protocol Management) Tactic families. The technique is the governance-specific operationalization of the T9.002 flash-loan primitive: the same uncollateralized-lending capital base that enables oracle-manipulation attacks (T9.001) and liquidation attacks (T9.003-family) can be weaponized against governance systems that do not distinguish between long-term-holder capital and flash-loaned capital. The governance-design lesson is that voting power is a political primitive, not a financial one — tokens that can be acquired and returned within a single transaction do not represent governance alignment — and the governance contract must enforce this distinction at the protocol layer.
