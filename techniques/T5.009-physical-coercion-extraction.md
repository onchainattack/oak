# OAK-T5.009 — Physical-Coercion Extraction (Wrench Attack)

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T5 (Value Extraction)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (any self-custodied or credential-accessible asset; the coercion is off-chain, the extraction transaction is on any chain the victim holds)
**First documented:** physical robbery of Bitcoin holders appears from the mid-2010s; the pattern scaled with crypto-wealth concentration into kidnapping-for-ransom by the early 2020s (TeufeurS, 2023), produced a concentrated France/Europe wave of kidnappings and home invasions in 2024–2025 (the Ledger co-founder Balland case, 2025-01), and became a CertiK-tracked global surge in 2026 (dozens of verified attacks; documented shift toward targeting victims' families)
**Aliases:** "wrench attack", "$5 wrench attack" (after xkcd #538), "physical extortion", "crypto kidnapping / ransom", "home-invasion crypto robbery", "rubber-hose extraction"

## Description

A class of OAK-T5 patterns in which an attacker uses **physical violence, the credible threat of it, or the abduction of the holder or an associate able to pay**, to compel a cryptocurrency transfer or the surrender of the credentials that authorise one. The Technique captures the **coerced on-chain transfer** — its downstream laundering is T7 — and treats the coercion itself as an off-chain predicate act whose on-chain manifestation is the compelled transaction.

From a defender's perspective, T5.009 is the pattern that **bypasses the entire cryptographic security model by attacking the person rather than the key.** The chain validates a signature; it cannot ask whether the hand that produced it was free. A hardware wallet, cold storage, multi-signature scheme, or exchange 2FA all defend the *key* against remote or software adversaries; none of them defends a *keyholder* who is physically compelled to sign, or whose associate is compelled to pay on their behalf. The extraction is therefore **cryptographically authorised by the victim's own key under duress** — an authentic transaction indistinguishable, on-chain, from a voluntary one.

The enabling precondition is **targeting**: the attacker must identify and locate a holder worth attacking. That is frequently an on-chain and social problem — linkable large holdings, doxxed or self-disclosed identity↔address associations, publicly known wealth — which is why T5.009 pairs tightly with T8.005 (Operational-Security Procedural Failure) at the victim level (see the *targeting surface* below).

T5.009 is structurally distinct from:

- **T5.008 (Ransomware Extortion Payment):** the closest neighbour and explicitly *not* the same. T5.008's leverage is a cyber act — data encryption or threatened data leakage — with no physical contact; T5.009's leverage is physical violence or the threat of it against a person. Both share the "victim voluntarily-under-coercion sends the payment" shape, which is why the corpus previously mapped physical-extortion events to T5 generically; T5.009 gives that class its own Technique and separates it from ransomware by the nature of the coercion.
- **T4 family (Phishing / Approval Solicitation):** T4 deceives a victim into signing; T5.009 compels a victim who is not deceived — the victim knows exactly what they are signing and does it under threat.
- **T9 family (Smart-Contract Exploit):** no contract vulnerability is used; the extraction is human-mediated.
- **T8.005 (OpSec Procedural Failure):** T8.005 is the *targeting/attribution* surface (how the holder was identified and located, or how the attacker was later caught); T5.009 is the *extraction* act. They co-occur — targeting exposure enables the wrench attack — but describe different links in the chain.

The lifecycle chains T8.005 (targeting exposure — identify/locate the holder) → **T5.009 (coerced extraction)** → T7 (laundering). The recovery surface is the same as for any on-chain theft: the compelled transfer is public and therefore traceable and sometimes freezable if reported fast enough — the one property that distinguishes a crypto ransom from a cash ransom.

## Observed indicators

- A large-value transfer from a self-custodied wallet or a coerced exchange withdrawal, correlated in time with a reported abduction, home invasion, or assault of the holder (or of an associate contacted for payment).
- Rapid consolidation of the extracted assets followed by high-value-mixer routing (Tornado Cash and equivalents) — the T7 signature typical of attribution-sensitive proceeds.
- A demand denominated in cryptocurrency communicated to the holder or an associate, frequently accompanied by proof-of-life / proof-of-harm (as in the Balland case, where a severed finger was sent to force payment).
- A target who is publicly identifiable as a high-balance holder — through on-chain doxxing, self-disclosure of wealth, conference/industry prominence, or a leaked customer database that maps home addresses to crypto ownership (e.g., the 2020 Ledger e-commerce breach of ~272,000 postal addresses).
- Geographic and temporal clustering indicating organised, repeatable operations (e.g., the 2024–2026 France/Europe wave; CertiK's finding of ~82% of early-2026 cases in Europe and an escalation to targeting family members).

## Sub-patterns / variants

- **Direct coercion of the holder** — the holder is physically compelled to sign/transfer on the spot (home invasion, "grab", express-style forced transfer).
- **Coercion via an associate able to pay** — the holder or their family is abducted and a *third party* (co-founder, business partner, family member) is contacted for the ransom, as in the Balland case. The attack surface includes anyone who can move the funds.
- **Kidnapping-for-ransom** — abduction with a ransom demand denominated in crypto (TeufeurS, Balland).
- **Coordinated physical + credential compromise** — a physical operation combined with wallet/credential compromise (the 2026 Kraken/Coinbase-linked case).

## Mitigations

T5.009 is the T5 sub-Technique for which the cryptographic perimeter offers no defence; the relevant mitigation class is **duress-resistant custody controls plus physical OPSEC**:

- **Duress-resistant controls (remove the ability to satisfy coercion *instantly*):** time-locked withdrawals; geographically-distributed multi-signature requiring a remote co-signer who is not present and not reachable in the moment; exchange withdrawal delays; **decoy / duress wallets** holding a small, plausible, surrenderable balance. These convert the attacker's only asset — *now* — into insufficiency.
- **Targeting-surface reduction (physical OPSEC):** minimise the public linkage between *identity*, *holdings*, and *physical location*. Do not self-disclose balances; segregate a doxxable identity from large wallets; treat any public association of a real-world identity with a high-value address as a targeting risk; for public figures, executive-protection and family-security measures.
- **Post-event recovery:** report immediately and get the ransom/extraction address to receiving exchanges *fast* — the on-chain trail is the only recovery surface, and freezes have worked on a clock of hours (TeufeurS: ~$800K of a $2M ransom frozen).

Candidate OAK-M mappings: a duress-resistant-custody mitigation and a targeting-exposure-reduction mitigation; both are user/holder-behavioural and are not covered by the vendor-side custody-UX mitigation class. See the retail-user mitigation-class gap noted in the corpus's mitigation backlog.

## Discussion

T5.009 exists because the corpus repeatedly hit the same gap: physical-extortion-to-crypto events have a real on-chain extraction and laundering leg, but no covering Technique — T5.008 (ransomware) was the nearest and did not fit, its leverage being data rather than violence. The three anchor cases (Balland, TeufeurS, Kraken/Coinbase) span the confidence range (confirmed → confirmed → reported) and the variant range (associate-coercion, kidnapping-ransom, coordinated physical+credential), and each independently flagged the missing Technique.

The reason a physical crime belongs in an on-chain-attack taxonomy is threefold: the **extraction** is an on-chain transfer (T5); the **laundering/recovery** is on-chain (T7); and the **targeting** that enables it is frequently an on-chain and social exposure problem (T8.005). What T5.009 records that no purely-technical Technique can is the structural fact that **the security model of self-custody terminates at the human being** — the perimeter is physical, the defence is OPSEC and duress-resistant design, and the crypto layer's only favourable property is that the ransom, unlike cash, leaves a trail.
