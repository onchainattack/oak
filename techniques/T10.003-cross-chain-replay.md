# OAK-T10.003 — Cross-Chain Replay

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T10 (Bridge and Cross-Chain)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM, cross-chain
**First documented:** 2022 (concept characterised in academic literature; recurring across smaller incidents)
**Aliases:** "bridge replay attack", "chain-ID replay", "message-replay across chains"

## Description

A class of bridge incidents in which a valid authorisation message generated on one chain (or for one bridge instance) is replayed on a different chain (or against a different bridge instance) where it should not have been accepted. The attack surface is the **scope of the authorisation message** — specifically, whether the message includes sufficient binding (chain ID, instance address, nonce) to prevent reuse outside its intended scope.

T10.003 is less commonly the *primary* failure mode in headline bridge incidents than T10.001 or T10.002, but it appears as a contributing factor in several incidents and is well-characterised in bridge-security literature as a recurring class. OAK includes it as a standalone Technique because its detection signal (pre-deployment review of message-binding completeness) and mitigation (canonical inclusion of chain ID + instance address + nonce in every signed cross-chain message) are distinct from the other T10 Techniques.

## Observed indicators

- A bridge transaction whose message payload, when reconstructed off-chain, lacks chain-ID or instance-address binding.
- Two bridge instances in the same operator family that share signing keys and accept structurally-similar message formats.
- Post-deployment additions of chain-ID or instance-address binding (suggests a previous lack — a defensive signal for risk teams reviewing recent commits).

## Detection signals

- Pre-deployment: audit message-construction logic to verify every signed cross-chain message includes chain ID, instance address (the deployed bridge contract address), and a per-instance nonce.
- Per-bridge architecture review: identify operator-family deployments that share signing infrastructure; require explicit per-instance binding in such cases.
- Runtime (where applicable): per-bridge-instance monitoring for messages whose payload structure suggests cross-instance forgery (rare; mostly relevant during incident response).

## Mitigations

- **Bridge architects:** include chain ID, deployed-contract address, and per-instance nonce in every signed cross-chain message, by design; document the message-binding scheme as part of the bridge specification.
- **Operators with multi-instance deployments:** never share signing keys across instances without explicit per-instance binding; treat any cross-instance signing-key reuse as a standing T10.003 surface.
- **Risk teams:** when reviewing a new bridge instance from an existing operator family, verify per-instance binding before treating the new instance as having the same risk profile as the audited original.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M07, OAK-M12, OAK-M16.

## Discussion

T10.003 is included in v0.1 partly as a **completeness move** for the Bridge Tactic: a Bridge Tactic that covers only validator compromise and message-verification-bypass would leave a structurally-distinct attack class uncatalogued, even though dollar-loss attribution to T10.003 specifically is small relative to T10.001 and T10.002. As bridge architecture standardises around well-binding-by-default designs, T10.003 may become primarily a "historical" and "near-miss" Technique. Contributors writing future T10.003 entries should not feel obliged to find a comparable-scale named incident; the Technique is documented because the failure mode is real, the mitigation is canonical, and a defender or auditor reviewing a bridge specification needs to ask the relevant question regardless of whether a famous incident matches.
