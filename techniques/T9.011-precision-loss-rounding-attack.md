# OAK-T9.011 — Precision-Loss Rounding Attack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM (primary); any chain whose arithmetic model uses integer division with floor/truncation rounding
**First documented:** 2021–2022 (ERC-4626 vault share-price manipulation via donation + rounding formally characterised by the Yearn, Solmate, and OpenZeppelin communities; the first large-loss operational anchors follow in 2023)
**Aliases:** "rounding-error exploit", "donation attack", "inflation attack", "vault-share-price manipulation", "dust-accumulation attack", "share-price front-running via donation"

## Description

A class of smart-contract exploits that weaponises rounding direction in integer-division-based token accounting to extract value that accumulates across many transactions. The attacker manipulates the token accounting's numerator or denominator such that the rounding error consistently favours the attacker — typically by donating a small amount of tokens to a vault/share contract to skew the share-price calculation, making subsequent deposits round down to zero shares (attacker captures the deposited value) or round in the attacker's favour. The canonical surface is ERC-4626 vault share-price manipulation: the vault's `convertToShares` formula computes `(assets * totalSupply) / totalAssets` using integer division; an attacker who front-runs a large deposit by donating assets directly to the vault inflates `totalAssets`, causing the victim's shares to round down, while the attacker's own position captures the victim's deposited value through the inflated `totalAssets`.

T9.011 is structurally distinct from T9.001 (Oracle Price Manipulation): T9.001 manipulates an external price feed; T9.011 manipulates the **accounting math within the protocol itself** — no oracle is involved. The victim's loss is not from a bad price but from the protocol's own rounding arithmetic producing fewer shares than the deposited assets would warrant under fair-division math. T9.011 is distinct from T9.005 (Reentrancy): there is no re-entry, no stale state — the rounding error is a property of integer arithmetic applied to a state that the attacker has legitimately (via donation) altered. T9.011 is distinct from T9.002 (Flash-Loan-Enabled Exploit): while flash loans are frequently used to acquire the donation capital (the two techniques compose), the exploit primitive is the rounding error, not the flash loan — a well-capitalised attacker can execute T9.011 with their own capital.

The technique is "stable" rather than "emerging" because (a) the class is well-characterised in formal literature (EIP-4626 documentation explicitly discusses the inflation attack), (b) multiple named large-loss incidents exist across 2023–2025, and (c) a mature mitigation pattern (virtual shares / dead shares minted at vault initialisation) is widely deployed.

## Observed indicators

- A vault/share contract initialised with zero or near-zero total supply that accepts an immediate first deposit — the attacker seeds the vault with a minimal position to capture the initial share supply.
- A small-value token donation directly to the vault contract (bypassing the deposit entrypoint, using `transfer` directly to the vault address) immediately preceding a large deposit — the donation inflates `totalAssets` without minting corresponding shares, skewing the share-price ratio.
- A deposit by a victim that receives zero shares (or proportionally fewer shares than the deposited assets would warrant under a fair-exchange computation) — the victim's deposit value is distributed across existing shareholders (the attacker).
- Repeated pattern across multiple vaults from the same attacker address: the attacker seeds vaults, waits for deposits, then withdraws at a profit proportional to the rounding-error capture.
- The vault's `totalAssets` / `totalSupply` ratio diverges from the underlying asset's per-share redemption value by a margin that exceeds gas-cost noise — the divergence is the attacker's accumulated rounding-capture.

## Detection signals

- Pre-deployment: static analysis of vault/share contracts for integer division whose rounding direction is not explicitly controlled (e.g., `assets * totalSupply / totalAssets` without a rounding-up or virtual-shares correction).
- Pre-deployment: verify that the vault mints a non-zero initial share supply (virtual shares / dead shares) at deployment to prevent the zero-totalSupply inflation vector. OpenZeppelin's ERC-4626 implementation includes a `_decimalsOffset` to mitigate the surface; Covalent/Yearn vaults mint dead shares to the zero address.
- Per-transaction monitoring at deployed vaults: alert on `transfer` events directly to the vault contract address (donation without deposit) followed by a `Deposit` event within a configurable window; this is the structural T9.011 signal.
- Per-vault share-price monitoring: track the per-share redemption value (`totalAssets / totalSupply`) over time; alert on sudden increases not explained by yield — these are consistent with donation events that skew the ratio.
- Victim-side signal: alert on `Deposit` events where the `sharesMinted` is zero or is proportionally smaller than the deposit amount relative to the pre-deposit share price by more than a configurable margin.

## Real-world examples

- **Hundred Finance — Gnosis Chain — 2023-04-15.** Loss ~$7M. The attacker exploited an empty-market rounding error in Hundred Finance's Compound V2 fork on Gnosis Chain: by donating a large amount of WBTC to an empty hWBTC market, the attacker manipulated the exchange-rate calculation such that subsequent deposits received zero hTokens, and the attacker's existing hTokens captured the deposited value. The case is canonical for the empty-market / zero-totalSupply sub-pattern of T9.011. See `[hundredfinance2023postmortem]`.
- **Sonne Finance — Optimism — 2024-05-14.** Loss ~$20M. The attacker exploited a precision-loss rounding error in Sonne Finance's Compound V2-fork markets on Optimism. Two markets (soVELO, soWETH) were exploited via donation + rounding: the attacker donated collateral to empty markets, inflated the exchange rate, and extracted victim deposits through the rounding-down mechanism. The case exemplifies the "donation into an empty or near-empty market" T9.011 sub-pattern.
- **Onyx Protocol — Ethereum — 2024-09-26.** Loss ~$3.8M. The attacker exploited an empty-market rounding error in Onyx Protocol's Compound V2-fork lending market. The donation-of-underlying inflated the exchange rate; subsequent deposits rounded to zero oTokens; the attacker's existing oTokens captured the deposited value. The case demonstrates T9.011 applied to a lending-market (cToken) architecture rather than a vault architecture.
- **zkLend — StarkNet — 2025-02-12.** Loss ~$9.5M. The attacker exploited an empty-market rounding error in zkLend's lending market on StarkNet, demonstrating T9.011's applicability beyond EVM — the rounding error is a property of integer arithmetic, not of the execution environment.
- **ERC-4626 vault cohort — 2023–2025.** Multiple ERC-4626 vaults (across Yearn, StakeWise, Resupply Finance, and others) have been the subject of audit findings and, in some cases, operational exploits where the attacker donated assets to inflate the share price. The ERC-4626 standard's own documentation describes the "Inflation Attack" vector explicitly; OpenZeppelin, Solmate, and Yearn each provide mitigation variants (virtual shares, dead shares, decimal-offset).

## Mitigations

- **Protocols (OAK-M01 — virtual shares / dead-shares initialisation):** mint a non-zero initial share supply at vault deployment, either to the zero address (dead shares) or as virtual shares embedded in the share-price formula. This is the canonical mitigation and is included in OpenZeppelin's ERC-4626 implementation (`_decimalsOffset`) and Yearn V3 vaults (dead shares). A vault that deploys with zero `totalSupply` and accepts deposits at that state is a standing T9.011 surface regardless of other audit findings.
- **Protocols (OAK-M02 — minimum deposit / share-rounding-up enforcement):** enforce a minimum deposit that bounds the rounding error to a configurable fraction of deposited value; revert deposits whose computed shares round to zero. This closes the "victim gets zero shares" arm of the attack even if the share price has been manipulated.
- **Protocols (pre-deployment audit):** treat the rounding direction of every integer-division-based accounting formula as a load-bearing security property; require audit-firm verification that rounding errors favour the protocol (or are symmetric) under all reachable vault states.
- **Risk teams:** maintain per-vault inventories of (a) whether the vault deployed with zero initial supply, (b) whether a minimum-deposit guard is enforced, and (c) whether a donation event has been observed at the vault address. Treat zero-initial-supply vaults as elevated T9.011 risk.

## Discussion

T9.011 is one of the more precisely characterisable T9 sub-techniques because the enabling primitive — integer division with floor rounding — is deterministic and the mitigation pattern (virtual shares / dead shares) is well-tested. The residual T9.011 surface at v0.1 consists of (a) vaults deployed with zero initial supply that have not been retrofitted with a dead-shares mint, (b) vaults that deployed with dead shares but whose dead-shares amount is too small relative to achievable donation magnitude (the donated amount can overwhelm the virtual-share cushion), and (c) non-vault token-accounting contracts (bonding curves, liquidity-mining pools, veToken wrappers) that use integer division without explicit rounding-direction control.

The class is marked stable rather than emerging because the pattern is mature — the ERC-4626 standard itself acknowledges the vector, multiple mitigation libraries exist, and audit firms treat it as a standard checklist item. However, the exploit incident rate has not declined materially from 2023 to 2025, suggesting that adoption of the mitigation pattern lags the deployment of vulnerable vaults. This is primarily a protocol-operations gap (zero-initial-supply vaults continue to be deployed, particularly by forks and smaller projects) rather than a research gap.

A structural observation: T9.011 is most severe when the vault is empty or near-empty, because the attacker's donation-to-totalAssets ratio is highest — a donation of 1 wei into a vault with 1e18 totalAssets produces negligible rounding error, but a donation of 1e18 into a vault with 0 totalAssets produces an arbitrarily large ratio distortion. This is why the empty-vault sub-pattern (donation into a freshly deployed, unfunded vault) is both the most common and the highest-loss variant. The virtual-shares mitigation directly addresses this by making the vault never truly empty.

False-positive considerations: not every donation-to-vault is T9.011. Legitimate users may send tokens directly to a vault contract address (rather than through the deposit entrypoint) out of ignorance of the vault's interface — this is a user error, not an attack. The distinguishing signal is the sequence: donation directly to the vault address, followed by a deposit from a different address whose shares round to zero or are proportionally depressed, followed by the donor withdrawing at a profit. The donation alone is insufficient; the donation + victim-deposit + donor-withdrawal sequence is the structural T9.011 signal. Additionally, vaults that have already accumulated meaningful totalAssets (well above the donation amount) are not practically exploitable — a donation of 1e6 into a vault with 1e18 totalAssets produces a share-price distortion of 0.0001%, which is within gas-cost noise. T9.011 is specifically the empty-or-near-empty-vault attack.

Attribution-strength considerations: T9.011 attribution is typically at the `pseudonymous-attacker` level — the attacker's address that executed the donation, the victim's deposit that rounded to zero, and the attacker's subsequent withdrawal are all on-chain attributable. The attack sequence is reproducible from event logs alone; the attacker's profit is computable from the withdrawal event. This makes T9.011 one of the more forensically tractable T9 Techniques at the on-chain attribution layer.
