# OAK-T12.001 — NFT Wash-Trade Volume Inflation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T12 (NFT-Specific Patterns)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T7.004 (same on-chain artefact, different motive — T12.001 inflates marketplace metrics to attract real buyers; T7.004 launders illicit proceeds through a self-financed art sale)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (primary; Ethereum / Polygon), Solana (Magic Eden, Tensor)
**First documented:** 2021–2022 (Chainalysis NFT retrospective; LooksRare incentive-wash episode)
**Aliases:** "NFT volume wash", "collection-rank inflation", "floor-price wash", "marketplace-incentive wash"

## Description

A class of NFT-marketplace trading patterns in which a small set of related addresses repeatedly transact the same NFTs (or NFTs from the same collection) against each other to inflate the **collection-level volume metric**, the **per-token sale history**, and the **floor-price-trend signal** that marketplaces and aggregators surface to prospective buyers. The operator goal is **price-discovery distortion**: the inflated metrics push the collection up marketplace leaderboards (top-volume, trending, top-gainers), draw third-party attention, and let the operator exit holdings into real demand at prices anchored by the manufactured trade history.

From a defender's perspective, T12.001 is structurally distinct from OAK-T3.002 (fungible Wash-Trade Volume Inflation) and OAK-T7.004 (NFT Wash-Laundering) along two axes:

- **vs OAK-T3.002 (different on-chain artefacts).** T3.002 detection runs on **DEX swap events on per-pair counterparty graphs** and looks for cycles in the trade-counterparty graph for a fungible token pair. T12.001 detection runs at the **NFT-marketplace layer** (OpenSea, Blur, Magic Eden, LooksRare, X2Y2, Tensor) on **per-collection same-buyer-same-seller patterns** and **per-token-id sale-history reconstruction** — the unit of analysis is the collection and the individual token, not the trading pair. The same operator goal (inflate volume to attract real buyers) maps to a different on-chain trace and therefore a different detector.
- **vs OAK-T7.004 (same on-chain pattern, different operator goal).** T7.004 self-financed NFT trades launder illicit funds through "art purchase" income; T12.001 self-financed NFT trades manipulate marketplace metrics to attract real buyers. The on-chain pattern is similar — self-financed sales between related addresses on an NFT marketplace — but the downstream behaviour differs (T7.004 typically retains the asset or routes proceeds out; T12.001 typically follows the wash episode with a real-buyer exit) and the appropriate marketplace-side mitigation differs accordingly. This is the same goal-discrimination problem as T3.002 vs T7.004 in the fungible space.

A third sub-motivation is worth naming explicitly: **marketplace-incentive-driven wash**, in which the operator washes trades to farm marketplace token rewards (LooksRare LOOKS, X2Y2 X2Y2, Blur BLUR airdrops and seasons). This sub-motivation overlaps with T12.001 on the on-chain artefact and with T3.002-family incentive-farming on the goal; OAK v0.1 treats it as a sub-class of T12.001 because the artefact is NFT-marketplace-specific, but the mitigation set differs (incentive-wash is fixed by changing the reward formula, not by per-collection metric adjustment).

## Observed indicators

- High collection volume relative to unique-holder count, social footprint, or unique-buyer count over the same window.
- A small number of token-ids accounting for a disproportionate share of collection sale count, with each token-id transacting between a small set of related wallets.
- Repeated round-trips of the same token-id between two or more addresses in a short window, often at increasing nominal prices that drift the recorded floor / last-sale upward.
- Per-collection same-buyer-same-seller pairs accounting for a measurable fraction of trade count.
- Sudden collection-leaderboard appearances (top-volume, trending) without commensurate growth in unique buyers or external mentions.

## Detection signals

- **Per-collection counterparty graph:** for each collection, build the buyer–seller graph weighted by trade count and notional volume; flag collections where a small connected component accounts for a disproportionate share of edges or volume.
- **Per-token-id sale-history reconstruction:** for each token-id with N > k sales in a window, check whether the buyer/seller sets across those sales reduce to a small cluster under a self-financed-cluster heuristic.
- **Self-financed-cluster signature:** trace the buyer-side funding for high-impact sales (sales that reset the floor or top a per-token-id sale history) back N hops; alert when the funding originates from the seller-side cluster directly or indirectly.
- **Floor-price-resilience metric:** compute a wash-adjusted floor by excluding sales between addresses in the same self-financed cluster; flag collections where the wash-adjusted floor diverges sharply from the marketplace-published floor.
- **Leaderboard-entry validation:** when a collection enters a top-N leaderboard, recompute its volume excluding self-financed-cluster trades and report the gap.
- **Goal-discrimination heuristic (T12.001 vs T7.004):** T12.001 episodes typically end with a real-buyer exit at the inflated price; T7.004 episodes typically do not need a real buyer and the asset may be retained or routed onward. Window-the-cluster behaviour after the wash burst and check for a non-cluster buyer absorbing inventory.

## Real-world examples

- **Aggregate cohort — multi-marketplace — 2021.** Per `[chainalysis2022nft]`, 262 users were identified as selling NFTs to self-financed addresses more than 25 times in 2021; 110 of them collectively netted \~\$8.9M in profit. The cohort-level metric does not separate T12.001 (price-discovery-distortion) from T7.004 (laundering) motivation per-user, but the profit-realisation pattern (the 110 profitable users) is more consistent with T12.001 than with T7.004 because realised profit implies a real-buyer exit.
- **Marketplace-level wash-trade rates — 2022–2023.** Industry and academic studies have documented per-platform wash-trade rates reaching \~94.5% on LooksRare and \~84.2% on X2Y2 in 2022–2023. These rates reflect a mix of marketplace-incentive-driven wash (LOOKS / X2Y2 reward farming) and T12.001-style price-discovery-distortion wash on the same on-chain trace; per-trade goal-attribution at this scale remains a gap in the public forensic record.

- [`examples/2022-01-looksrare-wash-trading-launch.md`](../examples/2022-01-looksrare-wash-trading-launch.md) — LooksRare launch wash-trading wave (January 2022); ~95%+ of reported launch-day volume identified as wash-traded via token-reward-incentive mechanism.

## Mitigations

- **NFT marketplaces (highest-leverage):**
  - Compute and enforce a **per-cluster wash-trade-rate cap** (e.g., a wallet whose 30-day wash-trade rate exceeds a calibrated threshold has its trades excluded from leaderboard volume and from the published floor).
  - Publish a **wash-adjusted volume** metric and a **floor-price-resilience** metric alongside raw volume; the resilience metric is the wash-adjusted floor's divergence from the raw floor, surfaced on collection pages.
  - Exclude self-financed-cluster trades from leaderboard ranking and from "trending" surfaces.
  - For incentive programs (token rewards, airdrop seasons), apply the same cluster-level exclusion to reward-eligible volume to neutralise the marketplace-incentive sub-motivation at the source.
- **NFT aggregators / analytics:** publish per-marketplace and per-collection wash-trade-rate metrics on a recurring cadence; let downstream UIs default to wash-adjusted figures.
- **Traders / collectors:** discount published collection volume and floor by an estimated wash factor; weight unique-buyer growth and external-mention count over raw volume; treat sudden leaderboard entries as a signal for wash investigation, not for buying interest.
- **Risk teams:** for high-value NFT exposure, run per-collection counterparty-graph and per-token-id sale-history checks before underwriting price.

**Canonical mitigations:** OAK-M26.

## Discussion

T12.001 exists as a standalone Technique under T12 — rather than as a sub-class of T3.002 or T7.004 — because the **detection surface is different**. T3.002 detectors run on DEX-swap counterparty graphs at per-pair granularity; they cannot consume NFT-marketplace events without a separate ingest pipeline. T7.004 detectors share the NFT-marketplace ingest with T12.001 but discriminate on the **post-wash behaviour** (asset retention vs real-buyer exit) and target a different mitigation owner (compliance and KYC tiers vs marketplace ranking and floor-display logic). Treating T12.001 separately surfaces the marketplace-side mitigation set — per-cluster wash-trade-rate caps, wash-adjusted volume, floor-price-resilience metrics, leaderboard exclusion — which is the highest-leverage layer for this Technique and which is owned by a different party than the T7.004 mitigation set.

The **marketplace-incentive-driven wash** sub-motivation deserves its own framing in a future v0.x update. It shares the on-chain artefact with T12.001 but the goal is to extract token rewards from the marketplace itself, not to attract third-party buyers. The mitigation is at the reward-formula level (e.g., the LooksRare and X2Y2 token-emission designs that made wash-farming profitable in 2022 are the underlying issue, not the wash trades themselves). OAK v0.1 keeps it under T12.001 because the artefact-level detection is shared; v0.2 may promote it to a sibling sub-Technique (T12.00X) once a clean operator-goal discriminator is published.

This Technique sits at the centre of the new T12 NFT-Specific Patterns Tactic and is expected to anchor the NFT pillar's detection guidance as the v0.x updates expand T12 coverage.
