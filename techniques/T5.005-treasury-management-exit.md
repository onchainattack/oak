# OAK-T5.005 — Treasury-Management Exit

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T5 (Value Extraction), OAK-T6 (Defense Evasion)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Solana
**First documented:** 2021 (Polywhale-class incidents); regulatory record 2023 (SafeMoon federal complaint)
**Aliases:** "soft rug via treasury", "treasury draw exit", "team-unlock dump", "salary-rug"

## Description

A class of OAK-T5 patterns in which a team-controlled multisig or treasury wallet executes outflows to founder, team, or "investor" addresses that are framed publicly as legitimate operating expenditure — "treasury management", "salary draws", "marketing spend", "team token unlocks", "OTC investor distributions" — but whose downstream pattern (size, cadence, destination clustering, lack of corresponding off-chain deliverables) is inconsistent with the stated rationale and consistent with operator exit. From a defender's perspective, T5.005 is the canonical pattern of **substantive misuse of legitimate authority**: the team multisig has standing authority at the contract and governance layer, the transfers are not unauthorised at the smart-contract level, and surface-level static analysis returns clean. The failure mode is *substantive* — the outflows do not match the publicly-stated purpose of the treasury.

T5.005 is structurally distinct from OAK-T5.001 (Hard LP Drain) because the authority being exercised is *legitimately retained*: the team multisig is publicly disclosed as the treasury custodian, holders entered with notice that it would execute outflows, and no covert backdoor is involved. It is structurally distinct from OAK-T1.003 (Renounced-but-not-really) because the team is not falsely claiming to have given up authority — the question is whether the publicly-retained authority is being exercised in good faith. T5.005 is therefore not a defect in authority *structure* but a defect in authority *use*, which is why it is the hardest T5 sub-Technique for purely on-chain detection and the one that most often requires an off-chain "stated-purpose" reference to score.

T5.005 frequently chains downstream of OAK-T2.002 (Locked-Liquidity Spoof) when the off-chain claim about how treasury funds are stewarded is itself the misrepresentation, and upstream of OAK-T7.001 / T7.002 (Mixer-Routed Hop / CEX Deposit Layering) when the operator subsequently launders the proceeds.

## Observed indicators

- Team-controlled multisig executing recurring or large outflows to founder / team / "investor" cluster addresses, framed in announcements as treasury management, salary, marketing, or team-unlock distributions.
- Outflow magnitude or cadence that diverges materially from the project's published budget, tokenomics, or vesting schedule.
- Outflow destinations that route directly (or after one-hop) to centralised-exchange deposit addresses, mixer entry points, or freshly-funded laundering clusters rather than to vendor / payroll / operational counterparties.
- Public communications that justify outflows in vague aggregate terms ("operations", "growth", "strategic partners") without itemised expenditure reports or counterparty disclosure.
- Treasury-balance trajectory inconsistent with continued protocol operation — for example, sustained net-outflow at a rate that exhausts runway long before the project's own published roadmap.
- Multisig signer set concentrated within a single operator cluster (founder + close associates), with no independent stakeholder seat and no timelock between proposal and execution.

## Detection signals

- Treasury-balance time series per disclosed multisig, with cumulative net-outflow scored against the project's published budget where available.
- Destination-cluster classification on multisig outflows: flag outflows that terminate at CEX deposits, mixers, or operator-cluster wallets within N hops; flag outflows whose destinations match clusters previously surfaced under OAK-T8.001 (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) or OAK-T8.002 (Cross-Chain Operator Continuity).
- Stated-purpose reconciliation: cross-reference treasury outflow destinations with the project's published vendor / counterparty list; flag outflows to undeclared destinations.
- Multisig governance posture: enumerate signers; flag signer sets that are operator-cluster-internal, lack timelock, or lack on-chain governance gating between proposal and execution.
- Cross-token cluster signal: deployer cluster previously operated treasuries that exhibited the same signature.

T5.005 detection is necessarily probabilistic — the on-chain artefact alone (a multisig outflow to a fresh address) is not conclusive without the off-chain stated-purpose reference. The Technique's detection methodology therefore lives at the junction of on-chain treasury-flow analysis and off-chain budget-document ingestion, and is the canonical case for OAK's "operator-behaviour-detector + governance-document-ingestion" composite-detector pattern.

## Real-world examples

- **SushiSwap Chef Nomi dev-fund — Ethereum — 2020-09-05 to 2020-09-11** — Loss \~\$14M nominal (\~38,000 ETH) extracted from the SushiSwap developer-fund SUSHI allocation by pseudonymous founder Chef Nomi via on-chain swap of dev-allocated SUSHI into ETH; **the entire \$14M was voluntarily returned to the SushiSwap multisig treasury six days later** following public-pressure escalation and an interim-stewardship handover to FTX-then-CEO Sam Bankman-Fried. The cleanest T5.005-with-recovery worked example on the public record; the canonical 2020 anchor for the *vampire-attack* token-genesis sub-shape (T1 + T2.001 + T5.005 launch-mechanic chain); and the v0.1 OAK reference for the *public-pressure-mediated voluntary-return* recovery channel distinct from regulator-mediated (SafeMoon SEC) and operator-funded-reimbursement (Vee Finance) channels. Pseudonymous attribution; SUSHI mark-to-market drawdown of \~73% during the post-dump window represented the realised user-side loss even after the fund return. See [`examples/2020-09-sushiswap-chef-nomi.md`](../examples/2020-09-sushiswap-chef-nomi.md).
- **SafeMoon (SFM) — BNB Chain — federal action 2023-11-01** — The U.S. SEC complaint specifically alleges that SafeMoon executives misappropriated approximately \$200M from purportedly-locked liquidity for personal expenditures, with the regulatory filing identifying purchases of luxury vehicles ("McLaren cars"), real estate, and travel as among the misappropriated uses. The case is OAK's canonical regulatory-record example of T5.005 because the federal complaint documents both (a) the public framing under which the funds were said to be stewarded ("locked liquidity" treasury) and (b) the substantively-divergent actual use of the funds — exactly the public-frame-vs-substantive-use mismatch that T5.005 names. The case is mapped primarily to OAK-T2.002 (Locked-Liquidity Spoof) under OAK's parsimony rule (the federal complaint's central allegation is the misrepresentation about locked liquidity) and is documented in `examples/2023-11-safemoon-charges.md`; SafeMoon is referenced here as the canonical T2.002 + T5.005 chained case for contributors writing future T5.005 examples. Source: `[secsafemoon2023]`.
- **Cohort context — soft-rug-via-treasury class** — The "soft rug" or "slow rug via team treasury" cohort is documented in industry retrospectives as a non-trivial fraction of overall rug-pull losses; the canonical worked example in the public record is the Polywhale Finance incident (2021), in which the team was found to have sold millions of tokens from the team-treasury wallet over a multi-week window while maintaining public communications consistent with continued protocol operation, sources: `[fullycryptosoftrug]`. Aggregate cohort context: `[chainalysis2025rug]`. As of v0.1, OAK does not name single incidents from this cohort beyond SafeMoon and the Polywhale reference because the public forensic record for individual T5.005 cases is thinner than for T5.001-class hard-rug cases — the substantive-misuse failure mode is harder to attribute publicly without either a regulatory filing or a community-led forensic investigation. Contributors with public forensic write-ups of named T5.005 incidents are encouraged to submit them via PR.

## Mitigations

The canonical defensive stack for T5.005 is **governance-gating + treasury-budget-publication + multi-stakeholder approval**, applied at the protocol-design layer rather than at the per-trade detector layer:

- **Protocols / DAOs (design-time):** require on-chain timelock between treasury-outflow proposal and execution; require multi-stakeholder signer set on the treasury multisig (independent seats from at least one non-founder party — auditor, foundation, large-holder representative); publish a quarterly treasury-budget report with itemised expenditure categories and counterparty disclosure; vest team-unlock allocations through publicly-verifiable on-chain vesting contracts rather than through ad-hoc multisig draws.
- **Risk teams / traders (pre-entry):** treat absence of any of the above (no timelock, operator-cluster-internal signer set, no published budget) as a T5.005 elevated-risk signal and a precondition for sizing-down or avoiding exposure.
- **Detection engineers:** maintain a per-protocol treasury-outflow ledger; alert on cumulative net-outflow above a fraction of the published budget, on outflows to undeclared destinations, and on outflows whose destinations terminate at CEX deposits / mixers within N hops.
- **Wallet-vendor / launchpad pre-trade UX:** surface treasury-multisig governance posture (signer set, timelock, budget-publication cadence) in pre-entry risk warnings.

T5.005 is not preventable by smart-contract-level controls alone — the failure mode is in the interpretation of authority, not in the authority's existence. The mitigation surface is therefore protocol-governance design and pre-entry diligence, not transaction-level static analysis.

## Discussion

T5.005 is the cleanest worked example in the OAK taxonomy of **substantive misuse of legitimate authority**, and is the canonical teaching point for distinguishing structural-defect Techniques (where the contract or authority *structure* itself is malicious — T1.001, T1.003, T2.002) from substantive-defect Techniques (where the *exercise* of an otherwise-legitimate authority is the failure). This distinction matters because the two classes require structurally different defensive stacks: structural-defect Techniques are addressable at the static-analysis-and-listing-precondition layer, whereas substantive-defect Techniques like T5.005 are addressable only at the protocol-governance-design and pre-entry-diligence layer, and require off-chain stated-purpose references to score reliably.

T5.005 is also the Technique most often confused with OAK-T5.001 (Hard LP Drain) by contributors writing their first OAK-T5 example: any case in which "team funds disappeared" superficially looks like both. The distinguishing question is whether the authority exercised was (a) the LP-control authority, executed in a single substantial event, against the public lock claim (T5.001), or (b) the publicly-disclosed treasury-management authority, executed in a stated-purpose-divergent manner (T5.005). SafeMoon is mapped primarily to T2.002 in `examples/2023-11-safemoon-charges.md` because the federal complaint's central allegation is the locked-liquidity misrepresentation rather than a stated-purpose-divergent treasury draw; the case is referenced here for the *secondary* T5.005 framing of the misappropriated proceeds, illustrating how a single incident can chain across multiple Techniques.

Future OAK versions are expected to split T5.005 into sub-Techniques along the stated-purpose-category axis (T5.005.NN — salary-draw, marketing-spend, team-unlock, OTC-investor-distribution), once the public forensic record is rich enough to characterise per-sub-pattern detection methodology distinctly. v0.1 publishes T5.005 as a single umbrella Technique and documents the substantive-misuse-of-legitimate-authority framing as the key teaching point.
