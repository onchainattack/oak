# OAK-T7.003 — Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T7 (Laundering)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Solana, Bitcoin, cross-chain
**First documented:** systematic from approximately 2022 onward; emerged as the dominant Lazarus laundering rail post Tornado Cash sanctions
**Aliases:** "chain hopping", "bridge laundering", "THORChain laundering" (the dominant rail)

## Description

A class of laundering flows in which proceeds are routed across multiple chains via cross-chain swap and bridge protocols, breaking the single-chain trail that traditional forensic methodology relies on. The typical pattern is: extract on chain A, swap to a bridgeable asset on chain A (e.g., ETH), bridge to chain B (often Bitcoin), swap to a settlement asset on chain B (often DAI or USDC re-minted on the destination chain), then exit via T7.002 CEX off-ramp or further chain hops. From a defender's perspective, T7.003 is the post-Tornado-Cash-sanctions evolution of `[ofac2022tornado]`-era laundering: when single-chain mixers became regulatorily costly, operators shifted to cross-chain swap infrastructure that achieves comparable obfuscation through chain-jumping rather than through mixing on a single chain.

T7.003 has become the dominant Lazarus Group / OAK-G01 laundering rail through 2024-2025. Per `[coindeskthorchainlazarus2025]`, THORChain emerged as the central protocol post Tornado Cash sanctions. After the February 2025 Bybit hack, Lazarus laundered the full $1.4B in approximately 10 days through THORChain — and THORChain node operators collectively earned at least $12M in fees from the Bybit heist alone. The fee accrual to the bridge operator is itself a T7.003 detection signal at the protocol-level: a sudden spike in fees against an active illicit-cluster-attributed flow is a structural indicator.

## Observed indicators

- Source-side: large outflow from a known illicit cluster into a cross-chain swap or bridge protocol within hours of an extraction event.
- Destination-side: large inflow to a "settlement asset" (DAI, USDC, BTC) on the destination chain consistent with the source-side timing and amount.
- Bridge-protocol-side: per-protocol fee spike correlated with an active illicit-cluster attribution (e.g., the ~$12M THORChain fee accrual post-Bybit).
- "Chain-hop" pattern: assets move ETH -> BTC -> DAI (or similar canonical paths) within a short window through multiple cross-chain protocols rather than a single direct swap.
- Liquidity-pool flow correlation: a step-function increase in inbound liquidity to a specific cross-chain swap pool (e.g., THORChain ETH.ETH / BTC.BTC pools) within hours of an attributed extraction event.
- Native-asset preference: the modern T7.003 rail uses native-asset cross-chain swaps (THORChain, Maya) rather than wrapped-asset bridges to avoid the canonical "lock-and-mint" attribution that Chainalysis / TRM / Elliptic graph-traversal heuristics most reliably catch.

## Detection signals

- Per-protocol attribution: monitor cross-chain swap and bridge protocols (THORChain, Maya, LI.FI, Across, Stargate, Synapse, etc.) for inflows from known illicit-cluster watchlists; produce per-protocol illicit-cluster inflow metrics analogous to T7.001 mixer-flow metrics.
- Per-incident speed: alert on the speed-from-extraction-to-bridge-deposit metric; the canonical Lazarus pattern is hours-to-days, not weeks.
- Fee-accrual-correlated-with-attribution: at the protocol-economics layer, sudden fee spikes that correlate with illicit-cluster inflow are a structural indicator that the protocol is being used as a laundering rail at scale.
- Cross-chain attribution graphs (OAK-M07): forensic providers (Chainalysis Reactor, TRM Forensics, Elliptic Investigator) maintain cross-chain attribution graphs that follow funds across hops; alert on flows that traverse N>=3 chain hops within a short window.
- Bridge-side runtime: cross-chain protocol watcher networks (related to OAK-M39) that publish per-protocol illicit-inflow metrics on a recurring cadence.

## Real-world examples

- **Bybit aftermath — Ethereum -> THORChain -> Bitcoin / DAI — 2025-02-21 onward** — Per `[coindeskthorchainlazarus2025]` and `[chainalysis2024laundering]`, the OAK-G01 Lazarus Group laundered ~$1.4B in Bybit-extracted ETH through THORChain in approximately 10 days post-extraction. THORChain node operators collectively earned at least $12M in fees connected to this single laundering operation. The case is the canonical T7.003 + G01 example at the headline-magnitude scale; the speed (10 days for the entire $1.4B) demonstrates the operational maturity of the modern Lazarus laundering rail. See worked example at `examples/2025-02-bybit-thorchain-laundering.md`.
- **Ronin Bridge -> cross-chain hops — Ethereum / Bitcoin — 2022-04 onwards** — Per `[ellipticronin2022]`, the post-extraction laundering chain for the March 2022 Ronin Bridge theft (~$625M, OAK-G01) included Tornado Cash (T7.001) for the initial obfuscation followed by progressive chain-hopping through bridges and cross-chain swap services as the Tornado-era substrate became regulatorily costly. The Ronin case is the canonical pre-THORChain-dominance T7.003 reference flow.
- **Harmony Horizon -> Railgun -> cross-chain — Ethereum / Bitcoin — 2022-2023** — Per `[fbiharmony2023]` and `[ellipticharmony2022]`, the June 2022 Harmony Horizon theft (~$100M, OAK-G01) saw multi-stage post-extraction routing including the documented January 2023 >$60M Railgun continuation and subsequent chain-hopping activity. Harmony is one of the cleanest public examples of the T7.001 -> T7.003 chain.
- **Multichain anySwap T7.003 surface — multi-chain — 2023-07** — Per `[chainalysismultichain2023]` and `[halbornmultichain2023]`, the July 2023 Multichain incident (`[dlnewsmultichain2023]`) created post-extraction T7.003 surface as the operator distributed assets across the Multichain-supported chain set; the case illustrates that T7.003 is not exclusively a Lazarus pattern but a generic post-extraction substrate available to any operator with cross-chain extraction footprint.
- **General Lazarus laundering profile — 2022-2025** — Per `[chainalysis2024laundering]`, the volume of stolen-funds inflows to cross-chain bridges increased substantially in 2024 vs prior years, primarily attributed to Lazarus-cluster activity. The shift mirrors the post-Tornado-Cash-sanctions repositioning catalogued in industry reporting.

## Mitigations

- **Cross-chain bridge protocols (highest-leverage):** integrate compliance-provider screening at deposit time; pause or freeze deposits from sanctioned addresses; publish per-protocol illicit-cluster inflow metrics on a recurring cadence. See OAK-M07 (cross-chain attribution graph) and OAK-M39 (cross-protocol watcher network).
- **Risk teams:** maintain cross-chain attribution graphs; treat post-incident-time-window cross-chain flows as elevated risk for inbound deposits at downstream venues. See OAK-M22 (rotate-on-disclosure) for venues that discover historical exposure to a now-attributed cross-chain laundering counterparty.
- **Regulators / law enforcement:** the cross-chain attribution methodology is mature enough at v0.1 that asset-freeze actions can be coordinated across multiple chains within days of a major extraction event; this requires policy infrastructure beyond OAK's scope.

## Discussion

T7.003 is structurally important to OAK because it operationalises the post-Tornado-Cash-sanctions reality: single-chain mixers are no longer the dominant laundering rail for headline-scale extractions; cross-chain swap and bridge protocols have replaced them. The OAK-T7.001 (Mixer-Routed Hop) Technique remains relevant but has shifted from "the dominant Lazarus laundering rail" to "one of multiple available rails", with T7.003 increasingly dominant for the headline-scale cases.

**False-positive reasoning.** Cross-chain swap protocols have substantial legitimate volume — DeFi-native portfolio rebalancing, cross-chain yield farming, and arbitrage flows. Per-protocol inflow alerts must be coupled with upstream illicit-cluster attribution rather than treating cross-chain inflow as evidence on its own. The fee-accrual-correlated-with-attribution signal (e.g., the $12M THORChain fee figure during the Bybit window) is most useful as an *aggregate-level* indicator that a protocol has become a laundering rail at scale, not as a per-transaction screening criterion.

**Privacy-chain substitute risk.** As cross-chain bridge laundering attracts compliance-provider attention, operators have begun to combine T7.003 with privacy-chain detours (Monero, shielded Zcash, Penumbra) at the mid-flow point, defeating the wrapped-asset attribution graph entirely between hops. Per `[chainalysisprivacychain2024]` and `[binancexmrdelist2024]`, the privacy-chain off-ramp is constrained by venue-side compliance posture but the mid-flow obfuscation remains.

A live policy question is whether the T7.003 mitigation responsibility lives at the cross-chain bridge protocol layer or at the receiving-venue layer. OAK does not opine; it documents that both layers offer real defensive surface and that the dollar-loss-prevented metric is most sensitive to whichever layer moves first. v0.x deferred work: a per-protocol T7.003 cohort-attribution table linking each major cross-chain swap protocol to its dominant illicit-flow share, calibrated against the `[chainalysis2024laundering]` 2024 metrics.
