# OAK-T12.003 — Royalty Bypass / Marketplace Manipulation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T12 (NFT-Specific Patterns)
**Maturity:** observed
**Chains:** EVM (primary; Ethereum / Polygon / other ERC-721-bearing chains)
**First documented:** 2022 (Blur launch and the LooksRare / X2Y2 royalty-optional shift); structural problem ongoing
**Aliases:** "royalty stripping", "royalty-optional trading", "marketplace royalty disregard"

## Description

A class of NFT marketplace and transfer-flow patterns in which trades settle without paying the creator's stated royalty rate, despite the collection's on-chain metadata or marketplace listing claiming a royalty. From a defender's perspective — particularly a creator-side defender — T12.003 is the structural consequence of a **standard-vs-enforcement gap**: the canonical NFT royalty interface (ERC-2981) is a metadata standard that lets a contract advertise a royalty recipient and basis-points rate, but it does not bind the taker side of a trade to actually pay that royalty. Enforcement is delegated to the marketplace contract that mediates the trade, and marketplaces have made divergent policy choices.

T12.003 is broader than a single "marketplace policy mismatch" event. The Technique covers the cluster of structural and tactical patterns that together produce the gap:

- **Marketplace-policy bypass at the contract layer** — marketplace contracts whose default order-execution path does not query a royalty registry, or queries it and routes a customizable (zero-permissible) fraction to the creator. Blur's late-2022 launch is the canonical instance: the marketplace shipped with a recommended-minimum royalty (0.5% on most collections, with collection-specific overrides such as the higher BAYC default) and a buyer-customizable royalty input that, on collections without enforcement coupling, can be reduced to zero.
- **Allowlist-bypass via direct transfers or alternative routers** — creator-side enforcement attempts that block listing on non-paying marketplaces (notably OpenSea's Operator Filter Registry, ERC-721C-style enforcement contracts) can be bypassed by transferring NFTs through paths that do not trigger the gating predicate, or by marketplaces that deliberately do not interact with the gating registry.
- **Standard-side under-specification** — ERC-2981 deliberately scopes itself to royalty *information* and explicitly leaves payment-binding outside its scope; this is a design choice that creators relying on royalty income may not understand at deployment time.

T12.003 is structurally more "industry policy problem" than "exploit", and the OAK position is non-partisan: a marketplace that ships zero-royalty trading is not necessarily an attacker (many argue it is a feature for traders, and the legal status of off-chain royalty obligations is contested). T12.003 is in OAK because (a) creators rely on royalty income as a primary revenue model, (b) the bypass patterns are well-documented and exploited at scale, and (c) the defensive question — *how should creators protect royalty income on-chain* — is a real, ongoing engineering problem.

## Observed indicators

- Per-collection effective royalty rate trending toward zero across the volume mix as a non-trivial share of trades migrates to royalty-optional venues.
- Trade execution through marketplace contracts whose order-execution path does not contain an `onERC721Received`-time or pre-transfer royalty-query call against the collection's ERC-2981 entry point.
- Direct `transferFrom` / `safeTransferFrom` paths between non-marketplace addresses that follow a privately-matched off-marketplace deal, with no associated royalty payment.
- Marketplace router contracts that interpose between the user's wallet and the canonical marketplace contract in a way that bypasses an Operator-Filter-Registry-class gating check.

## Detection signals

- **Per-collection royalty-payment-rate metric** — for a defender or analyst tracking a single collection, compute realized-royalty / nominal-royalty across a rolling window of trades, broken down by marketplace. The drop from \~100% (full enforcement, OpenSea pre-Blur) to materially lower rates as volume migrates to royalty-optional venues is the load-bearing collection-level signal.
- **Cross-marketplace volume distribution** — track the share of a collection's trade volume settling through each marketplace contract. Migration of a high share to zero-royalty or customizable-royalty marketplaces is the primary cohort-level antecedent of revenue loss for the creator.
- **Operator Filter Registry status** — for collections that opted into the Operator Filter Registry, monitor the registry state for the collection (filtered operators, registrant updates, post-Aug-2023 enforcement-mode change as the registry was deprecated by OpenSea).
- **Direct-transfer-to-trade ratio** — at the per-collection level, an unusual share of `transferFrom` calls without an associated marketplace-contract context can indicate off-marketplace settlement, although false-positive rate is high (legitimate gifting, custodial moves).

## Real-world examples

- **Blur — Ethereum — launched 2022-10-19** — Blur shipped as a pro-trader-focused NFT marketplace with a recommended-minimum royalty of 0.5% on most collections (with higher per-collection defaults — for example BAYC at 8%) and a buyer-customizable royalty input. A non-trivial share of Ethereum NFT volume migrated to Blur in the months following launch, and the effective realized royalty rate at the cohort level dropped materially relative to OpenSea's pre-2022 enforcement baseline. Sources: Blur public documentation; cohort-scale framing per `[chainalysis2022nft]`.
- **OpenSea Operator Filter Registry — Ethereum — Nov 2022 to Feb 2024** — OpenSea introduced the Operator Filter Registry in November 2022 as a creator-side enforcement mechanism: collections that opted in could block listing on non-royalty-enforcing marketplaces. Ownership of the registry was transferred to a CORI-controlled multisig in early 2023. On 2023-08-17, OpenSea announced sunset of its enforcement of the Operator Filter starting 2023-08-31; a grace period continued enforcement for already-opted-in collections (and non-Ethereum chains) through 2024-02-29, after which Operator-Filter-Registry-style enforcement on the OpenSea side ended. Sources: `[openseaoperatorfilter2022]`.
- **ERC-2981 standard** — finalized as Final in July 2021. The standard is deliberately scoped to royalty *information* (a `royaltyInfo(tokenId, salePrice)` query interface) and explicitly does not bind taker-side payment; the standard text notes that compliance is voluntary on the part of the entity executing the sale. Source: `[eip2981]`.
- [`examples/2022-11-x2y2-looksrare-royalty-optional.md`](../examples/2022-11-x2y2-looksrare-royalty-optional.md) — X2Y2 + LooksRare coordinated royalty-optional shift; canonical marketplace-level T12.003 case.
- [`examples/2023-08-opensea-operator-filter-sunset.md`](../examples/2023-08-opensea-operator-filter-sunset.md) — OpenSea Operator Filter Registry sunset; documents the end of platform-side royalty-enforcement leverage.

## Reference implementations

T12.003's defensive surface is primarily marketplace-side rather than detector-side. `mg-detectors-rs` does not currently surface T12.003 as a detection class — coverage **gap** at v0.1, and the Technique is structurally a poor fit for an operator-behaviour detector because the "bypass" is a marketplace contract executing exactly as designed, not a deviation from expected behaviour.

The relevant reference-implementation surface is on the enforcement side: ERC-721C (LimitBreak), the Operator Filter Registry contracts (CORI multisig as of v0.1), and creator-side royalty-routing patterns (split contracts, payment-binding wrappers). NFT analytics vendors (e.g., Nansen, Chainalysis, dappradar) publish per-collection royalty-payment-rate metrics that operationalize the detection-signal layer.

## Mitigations

- **Creators (collection deployers):** at deployment, decide explicitly whether the collection's royalty model assumes voluntary marketplace cooperation (ERC-2981 metadata only) or requires enforcement coupling (Operator-Filter-Registry-style allowlist, ERC-721C-style enforcement, or a successor standard such as the EIP-7572-class contract-level metadata that a marketplace can read for policy hints). Track Operator Filter Registry status for the collection and budget for the policy-shift risk demonstrated by the OpenSea sunset.
- **Marketplaces (highest-leverage layer):** the policy choice is genuine and contested; OAK does not prescribe a position. Marketplaces that implement royalty enforcement should publish their enforcement methodology (registry queried, gating predicate, fallback behaviour); marketplaces that ship royalty-optional trading should publish that policy clearly so creators deciding on a deployment can make an informed choice.
- **NFT analytics platforms:** publish per-collection effective-royalty-rate metrics on a recurring cadence; the cohort-scale framing in `[chainalysis2022nft]` and per-marketplace volume share are well-established and creators rely on these to monitor revenue.
- **Standards bodies:** future standards work on royalty-binding (a successor to ERC-2981 that constrains taker-side payment, or contract-level metadata standards such as EIP-7572 that improve marketplace-side policy interpretation) is the structural path forward; OAK tracks proposals as the public record matures.

## Discussion

T12.003 is the most policy-ambiguous Technique in the T12 Tactic, and OAK takes a deliberately non-partisan editorial position. Many marketplaces that ship zero-royalty or royalty-optional trading argue that the on-chain enforceability gap is a feature, not a bypass — creators were never on-chain-guaranteed royalty income; the ERC-2981 standard text itself acknowledges this; and the migration of trader volume to royalty-optional venues is the market resolving an ambiguous prior policy. The opposing view is that creator royalty income was a load-bearing assumption of the 2021 NFT cohort and that marketplaces deliberately routing around the established enforcement mechanism (the Operator Filter Registry) are exploiting a coordination problem at creators' expense.

OAK documents T12.003 because the **defensive question is real** regardless of the policy ambiguity: a creator deploying a new collection in 2026 needs to make an informed decision about which enforcement model their revenue assumptions depend on, and a defender evaluating a collection's revenue durability needs to track per-collection effective-royalty-rate. Both surfaces are well-established in the public record; OAK's role is to document the technique class and the detection signals without prescribing the underlying policy answer.

T12.003 sits adjacent to but distinct from T7.004 (NFT Wash-Laundering) and T12.001 (NFT Wash-Trade Volume Inflation): all three involve marketplace-flow analysis, but T12.003's load-bearing observation is the standard-vs-enforcement gap rather than the self-financed trade pattern. Contributors writing future T12.003 examples should preserve the policy-vs-attack framing rather than collapsing the Technique into a "zero-royalty-marketplaces-are-bad" narrative.
