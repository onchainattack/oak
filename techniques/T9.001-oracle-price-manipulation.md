# OAK-T9.001 — Oracle Price Manipulation

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T9 (Smart-Contract Exploit), OAK-T17 (Market Manipulation)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** EVM, Solana
**First documented:** 2020 (early cases on Compound and bZx); systematic 2022+
**Aliases:** "oracle attack", "price-feed manipulation", "single-block oracle exploit"

## Description

A class of exploits in which an attacker drives the price reported by a protocol's price oracle to an artificial value, then uses that artificial value as collateral or as a settlement reference in the same protocol. From a defender's perspective, T9.001 is identifiable through two complementary signals: the *oracle-side* signal (sudden price moves on the oracle's input venues that are not reflected in deeper-liquidity venues elsewhere) and the *protocol-side* signal (a single account taking actions whose outcomes depend on the manipulated price).

T9.001 is frequently chained with T9.002 (flash loans provide the capital required to move thin-liquidity oracle inputs); the two are documented separately because the underlying defensive control is different — T9.001 is mitigated at the oracle-resilience layer (TWAP windows, multi-venue feeds, deviation circuit breakers — see OAK-M09), while T9.002 is mitigated at the contract-design layer (flash-loan-resistant accounting, single-block-action prohibitions).

Sub-patterns observed in the wild:

- **Spot-feed manipulation** — single-venue spot price (often a thin AMM pool) drives a protocol's collateral or settlement valuation; canonical pattern in Mango, BonqDAO, Vee Finance.
- **Book-value / yield-bearing-token manipulation** — the oracle reads the share price of a yield vault rather than the underlying spot price; a single donation to the vault's reserves mints book-value upward (Cream Oct 2021, Rari/Fuse Apr 2022 cohort).
- **Reporter-pull oracle manipulation** — pull-based oracles (Tellor, UMA-style) where attackers post a manipulated value and ride out the dispute window with stake (BonqDAO).

## Observed indicators

- Sudden oracle price movement on a token whose primary trading venue is the oracle's only input.
- Same-block sequence of "manipulate price on input venue → consume manipulated price in target protocol".
- Disproportionate borrow / settlement / liquidation outcome relative to the protocol's normal activity profile in the surrounding window.
- Per-vault `pricePerShare` or `getPricePerFullShare` jump within a single block of >1% on a vault whose underlying assets did not move comparably on reference venues.
- Pull-oracle `submitValue` from a newly-funded reporter address whose stake barely exceeds the dispute-bond minimum.
- Oracle-deviation thresholds: spot-vs-TWAP > 3-5% over a 30-block window, or oracle-vs-CEX-mid > 2% sustained for ≥1 block on assets with deep CEX liquidity.

## Detection signals

- **Oracle-deviation circuit-breaker (OAK-M09):** compare each oracle's reported price against a wider-liquidity reference (CEX mid, multi-venue TWAP) and pause consumption beyond a calibrated deviation; standard mitigation is multi-venue TWAP plus deviation halt.
- **Cross-action correlation:** detect single-block sequences in which the same actor (or a tightly-related cluster — see OAK-M04 funder-graph clustering) both moves price on an oracle input venue and consumes the resulting price in the target protocol.
- **Vault-share invariant monitor:** alert on `pricePerShare` movements that exceed the underlying asset's reference-price movement by a calibrated margin.
- **Pull-oracle dispute-window monitor:** for Tellor / UMA-style oracles consumed by lending protocols, alert on `submitValue` reports whose deviation from a multi-venue reference price exceeds a threshold within the dispute window.
- **Post-event:** per-protocol replay of the exploit transaction against a price feed that includes a circuit-breaker; quantifies the loss avoided.

## Real-world examples

- **Mango Markets (MNGO) — Solana — 2022-10-11** — Loss \~\$110M. Avraham Eisenberg created offsetting long and short MNGO perpetuals positions, then purchased large quantities of MNGO on the venues feeding Mango's oracle (FTX, AscendEX, Serum). The reported MNGO price moved approximately 13× over a 30-minute window, inflating his collateral position, against which he then borrowed approximately \$110M. The CFTC charged Eisenberg with manipulative and deceptive scheme; criminal convictions were vacated in 2025 on venue / materiality grounds. See worked example at `examples/2022-10-mango-markets.md`. Source: `[cftcmango2023]`.
- **Cream Finance — Ethereum — 2021-10-27** — Loss \~\$130M. The attacker manipulated the price of `yUSDVault` (a Yearn vault share token) used by Cream as collateral by donating ~\$1B in yUSD-DAI to the vault via a flash-loan-funded sequence; the vault's `getPricePerFullShare` consequently doubled, and the attacker borrowed against the inflated collateral and repeated the cycle. The case is the canonical "book-value / share-price oracle" sub-pattern. See worked example at `examples/2021-10-cream-finance.md`. Sources: `[creamfinance2021postmortem]`, `[halborncream2021oct]`, `[muditgupta2021cream]`, `[immunefikream2021]`.
- **BonqDAO — Polygon — 2023-02-01** — Loss \~\$120M (notional; actual liquid recovery far smaller due to market depth on the protocol's stablecoin). The attacker exploited BonqDAO's reliance on Tellor as a pull-oracle without sufficient dispute-window safeguards: a Tellor reporter (the attacker) submitted a manipulated WALBT price, the value propagated to BonqDAO before the dispute window expired, and the attacker minted BEUR against artificially-revalued collateral and triggered liquidations of others. See worked example at `examples/2023-02-bonqdao.md`. Sources: `[bonqpostmortem2023]`, `[blocksecbonq2023]`, `[halbornbonq2023]`, `[peckshieldbonq2023]`, `[tellorbonq2023]`.
- **Vee Finance — Avalanche — 2021-09-21** — Loss \~\$35M. Vee Finance used Pangolin AMM spot prices for leveraged-trading settlement; the attacker manipulated thin-liquidity Pangolin pools (notably for ETH/USDT and BTC.b/USDT) to open and immediately close highly-leveraged positions at an artificial reference price, extracting protocol reserves. Sources: `[veefinancepostmortem2021]`, `[halbornveefinance2021]`, `[blocksecveefinance2021]`, `[slowmistveefinance2021]`, `[rektveefinance2021]`.
- **Rari Capital / Fuse Pool — Ethereum — 2022-04-30** — Loss \~\$80M across multiple Fuse pools. The attacker chained reentrancy in Rari's Fuse implementation with manipulation of pool-level price feeds to drain assets from several Rari Fuse pools simultaneously. The case is included as a cohort example of the "oracle as one of multiple chained flaws" pattern; the primary failure mode in some forensic write-ups is classified as reentrancy / T9.005, with oracle-feed manipulation as a contributing factor. <!-- TODO: cite URL — Rari Fuse Apr 2022 forensic write-up -->

- Vendor coverage outside MG: Chainalysis, TRM Labs, and protocol-specific monitoring services (Forta, OpenZeppelin Defender, BlockSec PhalconHQ) provide detection in this class with varying methodologies; pre-deployment audit firms (Halborn, Trail of Bits, OpenZeppelin) catch most missing-TWAP / single-venue patterns at review.

## Mitigations

- **Protocols (OAK-M09):** use TWAP-windowed oracles with deviation circuit-breakers; require multi-venue inputs with quorum; block in-block consumption of newly-updated oracle prices for sensitive actions; for pull oracles, enforce dispute-window respecting consumption (do not act on values within the active dispute interval).
- **Protocols (OAK-M16):** pre-deployment audit + formal verification of any oracle-consumption surface; explicit invariant that protocol actions cannot be solvent against a single-venue manipulated price.
- **Protocols (OAK-M11):** rate-limiting and per-block caps on actions whose outcome depends on the oracle (borrow caps per block, liquidation throughput limits) to bound the exploit payoff even when manipulation succeeds.
- **Risk teams:** maintain per-protocol oracle-architecture inventories; treat protocols whose oracle has a single input venue or no TWAP window as elevated risk; flag yield-vault-share oracles as a distinct risk sub-class from spot-price oracles.

## Discussion

The Mango Markets case is unusual in that the attacker publicly identified and litigated; most T9.001 incidents are pseudonymous. The 2025 vacatur of the criminal convictions on venue and materiality grounds left the underlying facts undisputed but opened a regulatory question about how "code is law" arguments interact with traditional market-manipulation statutes — a question OAK does not opine on, but contributors writing T9.001 examples should note where regulatory or judicial findings are still in flux.

**Sub-class structure (deferred to v0.x).** The five examples cohort splits cleanly into three sub-Techniques that may be promoted in a future revision:

- T9.001.001 — Spot-AMM-feed manipulation (Mango, Vee Finance) — single-venue thin-liquidity move.
- T9.001.002 — Yield-vault-share / book-value manipulation (Cream Oct 2021, partial Rari) — donation-based `pricePerShare` inflation.
- T9.001.003 — Pull-reporter-oracle manipulation (BonqDAO) — Tellor / UMA-style stake-and-report dispute-window bypass.

Each sub-class has a distinct mitigation surface (TWAP+multi-venue for .001; vault-share invariant for .002; dispute-window-respecting consumption for .003); v0.1 keeps them under the umbrella because the dollar-loss attribution and worked-example cohort are still building. Attribution-strength notes: Mango (high — public actor, court record), Cream / Vee / BonqDAO (medium — pseudonymous attackers, well-documented mechanics), Rari / Fuse (medium — mechanics public, primary-vs-contributing-factor classification disputed across forensic vendors).
