# OAK-T16.005 — Malicious Proposal Snowballing

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T16 (Governance / Voting Manipulation)
**Adjacent Techniques:** OAK-T9.003 (generic Governance Attack class — preserved on every T16.005 worked example), OAK-T9.004 (Access-Control Misconfiguration — frequently the post-passage privilege-escalation mechanism in T16.005 chains), OAK-T6.001 (Source-Verification Mismatch — adjacent class for the proposal-text-vs-deployed-bytecode divergence that T16.005 exploits)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** EVM (canonical anchors at Tornado Cash, Audius, Curio); cross-chain governance contracts inherit the surface where proposal payloads can contain `delegatecall` or storage-collision-mediated authority capture
**First documented:** 2022 (Audius July 2022 storage-collision case as the earliest canonical anchor); refined by Tornado Cash governance May 2023 self-modifying-contract case
**Aliases:** "hidden malicious proposal", "self-modifying proposal", "storage-collision governance attack", "delegatecall governance attack", "proposal-payload-as-attack-vector", "Tornado-class governance attack", "proposal text-vs-execution divergence"

## Description

Submission of a governance proposal whose **on-chain execution-payload semantics diverge from the proposal-text-as-displayed-by-the-front-end-UI**, such that the proposal passes on legitimate vote weight (community votes based on the front-end-displayed text) but executes a hidden malicious effect at on-chain execution time. The defining shape is **proposal-text-vs-payload divergence**: the front-end-displayed proposal text describes a benign or routine action, but the deployed proposal contract (or the proposal calldata interpreted by the governance contract) contains a hidden `delegatecall` to attacker-controlled code, a `selfdestruct` + redeploy with attacker-controlled implementation, a storage-layout collision that elevates attacker privilege, or a `setup()`-style function that grants attacker voting power or admin authority on execution.

T16.005 is the canonical Tornado Cash governance May 2023 pattern. The case is the textbook chain: the attacker submitted Proposal 20 with front-end-displayed text describing a routine governance-treasury-management proposal; the community voted on Proposal 20 based on the front-end-displayed text and the proposal received sufficient legitimate votes to pass; at execution time the hidden `setup()` function ran and granted the proposer 1.2M voting power — sufficient to reach quorum on subsequent arbitrary proposals unilaterally; the attacker then submitted and self-passed Proposal 21 (transferring the governance treasury balance to the attacker), drained approximately \$1M of treasury TORN and ETH, and exited.

T16.005 is **structurally distinct from T16.001 / T16.002 / T16.003** in *how voting power is acquired*. T16.001 / T16.002 / T16.003 attackers acquire voting power through the protocol's normal voting-power-acquisition mechanisms (flash-loan, market accumulation, delegation pull) before submitting the malicious proposal. T16.005 attackers do **not** need to acquire voting power before the attack — the attack itself is the mechanism by which voting power is acquired (or by which the malicious effect is delivered without needing majority voting power). The mitigation surface is therefore structurally different: snapshot-based voting power (T16.001 mitigation), cohort-surveillance (T16.003 mitigation), and timelock-gated execution paired with counter-proposal capability (T16.001-003 mitigation) all leave T16.005 fully open. Closing T16.005 requires reading the **actual deployed contract bytecode at proposal-creation time** rather than trusting the front-end-displayed text — a discipline-in-practice not consistently implemented across DAO front-end implementations even at v0.1 cutoff.

T16.005 covers four structurally adjacent sub-shapes:

- **Self-modifying-contract proposal.** Proposal contract appears to perform one function (per the front-end UI / proposal description), but the deployed bytecode includes a hidden `setup()` / `init()` / `selfdestruct` + `CREATE2` redeploy primitive that grants the proposer voting power or admin authority on execution. Canonical anchor: Tornado Cash governance May 2023 (Proposal 20 → 1.2M voting power grant → Proposal 21 treasury drain → \~\$1M extracted, \~\$890k subsequently returned).
- **Hidden delegatecall to attacker-controlled code.** Proposal calldata contains a `delegatecall` to an address that has not been a governance target in the protocol's history; the `delegatecall` runs in the governance contract's storage context and rewrites privileged storage slots to attacker-controlled values. Mitigation: execution-target allowlist + `delegatecall`-target denylist at proposal-payload static-analysis time.
- **Storage-layout collision proposal.** Proposal exploits a storage-layout collision between the governance contract and a downstream contract (e.g., MakerDAO-fork governance proxy whose storage layout collides with a CGT-token contract's admin-role storage slot). The proposal's execution payload writes to a benign-looking storage slot in the governance contract that, due to the collision, also writes to a privileged storage slot in the downstream contract. Canonical anchors: Audius July 2022 (storage-collision proposer-eligibility bypass — \~\$6M); Curio DAO March 2024 (storage-collision in MakerDAO-fork governance proxy elevating 1% voting-position into CGT admin authority — \~\$16M nominal / \~\$1M realised).
- **Multisig-to-governance pivot.** Attacker who has already compromised a privileged multisig (T11.x upstream) submits a malicious proposal that the multisig itself signs and executes, converting a custody-compromise into a governance-compromise without going through the normal voting lifecycle. Boundary case against T11.x; the T16.005 mapping captures the proposal-payload-as-attack-vector dimension while T11.x captures the upstream custody compromise.

The T16.005 surface is gated by two design conditions: (a) the governance contract executes proposals as deployed contract code (rather than executing only a fixed set of allowlisted action types) OR the proposal calldata can perform `delegatecall` / arbitrary-storage-write operations, and (b) the front-end UI displays the proposal text from a source distinct from the actual deployed contract bytecode. Both conditions hold across most modern Governor-Bravo-pattern DAOs and across most DAO front-ends; T16.005 is therefore a standing surface from the day the governance contract is deployed.

## Observed indicators

- **Pre-event (governance-design layer):** governance contract executes proposals as arbitrary deployed contract code OR proposal calldata supports `delegatecall` / arbitrary-storage-write operations; absence of execution-target allowlist; absence of proposal-payload static-analysis at proposal-creation time; front-end UI displays proposal text from a source distinct from the deployed bytecode.
- **At-event (proposal-payload-vs-text divergence):** proposal text describes a benign or routine action; proposal calldata contains `delegatecall` to a non-canonical target, `selfdestruct` + redeploy primitive, `setup()` / `init()` function with privilege-granting effect, or storage-write to a slot whose layout collides with a privileged slot in a downstream contract.
- **At-event (post-execution privilege-grant):** attacker's voting power, role assignment, or admin authority increases as a result of proposal execution; the increase is structurally inconsistent with the proposal text and the legitimate-vote-weight that passed the proposal.
- **At-event (follow-on proposal):** attacker submits a follow-on proposal (Proposal N+1 in the Tornado Cash pattern) using the newly-granted voting power / admin authority to direct treasury / privileged-action surfaces; the follow-on proposal's voting weight is concentrated at the attacker.
- **Forensic record:** the deployed proposal contract bytecode (which is NOT the source-verified bytecode the front-end UI assumed); the diff between the front-end-displayed proposal text and the actual on-chain proposal effect; the storage-layout collision evidence (where applicable); the post-execution storage-state showing the attacker's privilege grant.

## Detection signals

- **Proposal-payload static analysis at proposal-creation time (OAK-M02):** before any governance proposal reaches its execution window, decode and statically analyse the calldata; alert on payloads that contain `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets, `selfdestruct` + `CREATE2` primitives, `setup()` / `init()` functions, or storage-writes to slots whose layout overlaps privileged slots in downstream contracts.
- **Execution-target allowlist:** governance-contract design-layer enforcement that proposal payloads can only call into an allowlisted set of contracts and functions; defeats hidden-`delegatecall` and self-modifying-contract sub-shapes by construction.
- **Source-verified-bytecode-vs-deployed-bytecode comparison (OAK-M01 + OAK-M03):** at proposal-creation time, verify that the deployed proposal contract's bytecode matches a source-verified reference; reject proposals where the front-end-UI-displayed text references one source but the deployed bytecode is different. Adjacent to T6.001 mitigation surface.
- **Storage-layout collision audit:** at governance-contract design and audit time, formal verification of storage-layout against all downstream contracts that share storage context (proxy patterns, inherited contracts, MakerDAO-fork governance proxies). Particularly load-bearing for fork-substrate DAOs whose governance contract inherits layout from an upstream reference (MakerDAO-fork case).
- **Per-proposal simulation:** replay-style simulation of any proposal whose execution payload is non-trivially structured; explicit check that post-execution storage-state matches the proposal-text-implied effect, with deviation flagged for community review.
- **Post-execution privilege-grant monitoring:** monitor governance-contract storage for unexpected role / admin / voting-power state changes following proposal execution; alert on changes that are structurally inconsistent with the executed proposal's text.

## Real-world examples

- **Tornado Cash governance — Ethereum — 2023-05-20** — Loss \~\$1M nominal (\~\$890k subsequently returned by the attacker via Proposal 21-class follow-up; governance-control implications outsized relative to dollar loss). Canonical T16.005 anchor for the **self-modifying-contract sub-shape**. Attacker submitted Proposal 20 with front-end-displayed text describing a routine governance-treasury-management proposal; deployed proposal contract included a hidden `setup()`-style function granting the proposer 1.2M voting power on execution; the attacker then submitted and self-passed Proposal 21 transferring governance treasury balance to attacker-controlled addresses; subsequently returned \~\$890k of extracted ETH and TORN with an "I was demonstrating a vulnerability" framing. The case is the canonical T16.005 + T9.003 + T9.004 chain. See [`examples/2023-05-tornado-cash-governance.md`](../examples/2023-05-tornado-cash-governance.md).
- **Audius — Ethereum — 2022-07-23** — Loss \~\$6M. Canonical T16.005 anchor for the **storage-collision sub-shape**. Attacker exploited a storage-layout collision in Audius's governance proxy contract to bypass the proposer-eligibility / role-assignment guard, submit a malicious proposal with the resulting "eligibility," and direct treasury egress through the proposal payload. The case is the canonical T16.005 + T9.003 + T9.004 storage-collision chain. See [`examples/2022-07-audius.md`](../examples/2022-07-audius.md).
- **Curio DAO — Ethereum + multi-chain — 2024-03-23** — Loss \~\$16M nominal / \~\$1M realised. Storage-collision sub-shape against a MakerDAO-fork governance proxy. Attacker used a flash loan to acquire approximately 1% of CGT voting power (T16.001 component of the chain), exploited a storage-layout collision between the MakerDAO-fork governance proxy and the CGT token contract to elevate the 1% voting-position into administrative privileges over the CGT contract (T16.005 component), and minted approximately 1 billion CGT tokens (\~16x pre-event supply). Realised loss bounded by thin CGT-to-USD market liquidity and by Curio's operator-side response (contract pause within \~3 hours of mint). The case anchors **MakerDAO-fork-substrate vulnerability** as a recurring class. See [`examples/2024-03-curio.md`](../examples/2024-03-curio.md).
- **Unleash Protocol — Story Protocol — 2025-12-30** — Loss \~\$3.9M. Anchor for the *unauthorised-upgrade-payload via multisig-admin-boundary failure* sub-shape, structurally distinct from the storage-collision and self-modifying-contract sub-shapes. An externally-owned address acquired administrative control over Unleash's multi-signature governance system through a weakness in admin-permission enforcement, then pushed a contract upgrade through the multisig that had not been approved through normal core-team review; the upgrade opened the path for direct asset withdrawal outside approved governance. The case chains primarily with T9.004 (access-control misconfiguration in the multisig admin model) rather than with T16.001-class flash-loan vote acquisition; the load-bearing mitigation surface is per-signer custody hardening, off-chain destination verification at sign-time, and admin-permission delta monitoring rather than proposal-payload review. Proceeds laundered via cross-chain bridge to Ethereum and Tornado Cash deposit (1,337.1 ETH) within hours of extraction. See [`examples/2025-12-unleash-protocol.md`](../examples/2025-12-unleash-protocol.md).
- **Cohort context** — `[zhou2023sok]` documents proposal-payload-as-attack-vector incidents as a recurring sub-class through 2018-2022; the storage-collision and self-modifying-contract sub-shapes have been broadly documented in audit-firm and governance-research literature since 2022. Smaller-DAO T16.005 incidents continue through 2023-2026 and are typically not published forensically because the dollar-loss is bounded by the smaller protocol's treasury.

## Mitigations

- **Protocols (OAK-M02 — proposal-payload static analysis):** highest-leverage layer. Decode and statically analyse proposal calldata at proposal-creation time; alert on payloads containing `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets, `selfdestruct` + redeploy primitives, `setup()` / `init()` functions, or storage-writes whose layout collides with privileged slots in downstream contracts.
- **Protocols — execution-target allowlist:** governance-contract design-layer enforcement that proposal payloads can only call into an allowlisted set of contracts and functions. Defeats hidden-`delegatecall` and self-modifying-contract sub-shapes by construction. The canonical post-Tornado-Cash-2023 mitigation.
- **Protocols (OAK-M16 — pre-deployment audit + formal verification):** governance contract audit and formal verification specifically targeting upgrade paths, emergency branches, storage-layout against all downstream contracts that share storage context, and proposal-execution semantics. Particularly load-bearing for fork-substrate DAOs (MakerDAO-fork, Compound-v2-fork).
- **Protocols — proposal-payload-bytecode-vs-source-verified-reference matching (adjacent to OAK-M01 / T6.001):** at proposal-creation time, verify that the deployed proposal contract's bytecode matches a source-verified reference; reject proposals where the front-end-UI-displayed text references one source but the deployed bytecode is different.
- **DAO-process layer — community proposal-bytecode review:** discoverable governance-process discipline that any non-trivially-structured proposal opens a multi-day pre-vote bytecode-review window where independent reviewers verify the deployed bytecode against the proposal text. Tornado Cash 2023 is the canonical case where this discipline was absent.
- **Risk teams:** for governance-token holdings, monitor proposal queues for any proposal whose deployed bytecode is not source-verified, contains `delegatecall` to non-canonical targets, or modifies privileged storage state; maintain per-protocol governance-contract storage-layout inventories.

## Discussion

T16.005's structural lesson is that the **proposal-text-as-displayed and the proposal-execution-as-deployed are different artefacts**, and that voters who trust the front-end UI without independently verifying the deployed bytecode are vulnerable to a class of attacks where the voting weight is legitimately distributed and the vote is legitimately cast on legitimate signal — but the resulting on-chain effect diverges from the social commitment the vote represents. The mitigation surface is therefore structurally different from T16.001 / T16.002 / T16.003: voting-power-acquisition-resistance does not close T16.005 (the voting power can be entirely legitimate); cohort-surveillance does not close T16.005 (the proposing actor may be a legitimate community member submitting a malicious payload); timelock-gated execution paired with counter-proposal capability is necessary but operationally insufficient unless paired with proposal-payload-bytecode review during the timelock window.

The cohort is structurally distinct from T16.001 / T16.002 / T16.003 by the *voting-power-acquisition-vs-payload-as-attack-vector* axis. T16.001-003 all attack the voting-power-acquisition layer; T16.005 attacks the proposal-payload layer. The mitigation surfaces partition cleanly along the same boundary: T16.001-003 mitigation lives at voting-power-eligibility-computation; T16.005 mitigation lives at proposal-payload-static-analysis + execution-target allowlist + storage-layout audit.

Attribution-strength notes: Tornado Cash 2023 is `pseudonymous-unattributed` (the attacker subsequently published Proposal 21 returning \~\$890k and reverting the attack-acquired voting power, framed as "demonstrating a vulnerability" — complicating intent attribution); Audius 2022 is `pseudonymous`; Curio 2024 is `pseudonymous-unattributed` with partial recovery via negotiated 10% bug-bounty. T16.005 attackers historically prefer "demonstrating a vulnerability" framing as a partial-attribution-defence — a pattern OAK does not opine on but which contributors should preserve in the worked-example record.

False-positive considerations: legitimate non-trivially-structured proposals (proxy upgrades, multi-contract treasury operations, parameter-batch updates) routinely contain `delegatecall` and arbitrary-storage-write operations. The detection signal is not "the payload is non-trivially structured" but specifically "the deployed proposal bytecode is not source-verified, OR the bytecode-derived effect diverges from the proposal-text-implied effect, OR the post-execution storage-state changes outside the proposal-text-implied surface." Cohort-level analysis confirms that the legitimate use-case for "non-source-verified proposal contract whose effect diverges from its text" essentially does not exist outside attacker behaviour.

v0.x deferred items: the multisig-to-governance pivot sub-shape (where an upstream T11.x compromise produces a downstream T16.005 effect) lacks a clean canonical anchor at v0.1 and should be anchored once a public-record case lands; treatment of cross-fork storage-layout-vulnerability classes (MakerDAO-fork, Compound-v2-fork, Uniswap-V2-fork) as a distinct sub-shape that may deserve its own sub-Technique once the cohort-record matures; the boundary against T6.001 (Source-Verification Mismatch) which is structurally adjacent — T6.001 covers the generic source-verified-vs-deployed-bytecode divergence class; T16.005 covers the governance-proposal-specific instance of that class. Future contributors should reference both Techniques on T16.005 worked examples to preserve the boundary.
