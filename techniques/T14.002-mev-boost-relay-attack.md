# OAK-T14.002 — MEV-Boost Relay Attack

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T14 (Validator / Staking / Restaking Attacks)
**Maturity:** stable
**Chains:** Ethereum (mainnet PoS; secondarily any EVM L1 running an MEV-Boost-compatible PBS sidecar — Holesky/Hoodi testnets, Gnosis Chain, etc.)
**First documented:** 2023 (April 3rd unbundling incident; concurrent vendor disclosures of equivocation-class timing issues)
**Aliases:** "relay unbundling", "MEV-Boost timing exploit", "block equivocation attack", "low-carb-crusader attack"

## Description

A class of attacks against the **MEV-Boost relay layer** — the off-chain network of relay servers that sits between block builders and consensus-layer proposers (validators) under Ethereum's out-of-protocol Proposer–Builder Separation (PBS). The relay's defining role is to hold the executed block body in escrow: the proposer sees only the header, signs it, and the relay releases the body to the network only after the proposer's signature has been published. T14.002 covers attacks that **break this escrow assumption** in either direction — a malicious proposer who induces the relay to reveal the body without a valid publication, or a malicious relay that abuses its privileged read of builder bid streams. T14.002 is the validator-stack analogue of T13.002 (Bundler MEV): both concern a privileged neutral-aggregator role (relay / bundler) being used as an MEV extraction surface, but T14.002 sits at the consensus-layer proposer interface rather than the AA EntryPoint.

Sub-patterns:

- **(a) Relay timing / unbundling exploit.** A proposer (or proposer-colluding actor) sends a signed-but-invalid `SignedBlindedBeaconBlock` to the relay. A vulnerable relay implementation validates only the proposer's signature on the header, returns the unblinded block body via `getPayload`, and the proposer then re-orders or replaces the bundle's transactions and proposes a *different* block to the network in the same slot. The escrow guarantee — body-only-after-publication — is violated because the relay's release path did not require the released block to be the one actually canonicalised. This is the canonical April 2023 incident.
- **(b) Censoring relay.** A relay refuses to propagate bundles or transactions that interact with OFAC-sanctioned addresses (or any other policy-defined exclusion set). Validators connected only to censoring relays propose blocks from a filtered orderflow, and the censored transactions are forced onto non-censoring paths at higher cost or longer latency. Overlaps with T13.002 sub-pattern (c) at the orderflow layer; the relay-specific shape is that the censorship is enforced at the *bid-acceptance* boundary, not at block construction.
- **(c) Relay-data exfiltration.** A relay extracts MEV-relevant intelligence from the inbound builder bid stream (top-of-block content, profitability deltas, builder identity per slot) and either (i) trades on that intelligence directly, or (ii) leaks it to a colluding builder before the slot resolves. The bid stream is privileged because builders are required to submit full execution payloads to the relay for header-only proposer view.
- **(d) Relay–builder vertical collusion.** A relay and a specific block builder coordinate so that the relay's `getHeader` response is biased toward the colluding builder's bids — through preferential bid acceptance, simulation latency manipulation, or optimistic-relay trust extension — capturing MEV that competing builders' validators would have realised. Vertical integration of relay + builder is a cohort-level concern in MEV-Boost as of 2023–2024 and the standard structural prerequisite for (d).

The defender's leverage point differs across sub-patterns. (a) is closed by relay code (do not release the body unless the published block is the relay's body, and add a publication-time guard); (b) and (d) are closed by **validator-side relay diversification** — connecting to multiple non-censoring, non-vertically-integrated relays — because no single relay can be forced to be neutral; (c) is closed by builder-side bid-stream protections (commit-reveal on bid contents, threshold-encrypted bids) which are an active research area rather than a deployed mitigation.

## Observed indicators

- Two distinct blocks signed by the same proposer in the same slot, where one was sent to a relay and the other was actually proposed to the network — the on-chain signature of (a). Detectable post-hoc via consensus-layer block-equivocation monitoring.
- A relay's `getPayload` log line shows a successful unblinding immediately followed by a `missing block` / `block not on canonical chain` outcome from the beacon node — the relay-side signature of (a).
- A specific relay's accepted-bundle distribution shows zero inclusion of transactions touching an externally-defined sanctions list while peer relays show non-zero inclusion in the same slot windows — signature of (b).
- A specific (relay, builder) pair captures a sustained share of `getHeader`-winning slots that materially exceeds the builder's fair share computed from independent bid-volume metrics — signature of (d).

## Detection signals

- **Equivocation monitoring at the consensus layer.** For every slot, cross-reference the relay's published `getPayload` block-hash against the canonical beacon-chain block-hash. A mismatch on a non-missed slot is a strong signal of (a). Several public relay operators (Flashbots, Aestus) ship slashable-equivocation telemetry as standard since the April 2023 mitigations.
- **Relay-side body-release audit.** For every `getPayload` call, log (i) the `SignedBlindedBeaconBlock` validity at the time of release, (ii) whether the unblinded block was subsequently observed on the canonical chain, and (iii) the time delta between release and canonical observation. Sub-pattern (a) materialises as releases on signed-but-invalid blocks, or releases without subsequent canonicalisation.
- **Cross-relay censorship comparison.** For a known sanctions / exclusion list, compute per-relay inclusion rate of matching transactions per epoch; flag relays with statistically-significant exclusion against the cohort baseline (the methodology used by the public MEV Watch dashboard).
- **Bid-stream leakage signal.** For relays that operate or co-operate with a builder, compute (a) the fraction of slots in which the affiliated builder's bid is the relay's `getHeader`-winning bid and (b) the fraction of slots in which the affiliated builder appears in the top-N of bids; sustained outlier values against cohort-multi-relay baselines are the structural signal of (c)/(d).
- **Simulation-bypass / optimistic-relay outliers.** Optimistic relays trust builder-supplied execution and skip pre-`getHeader` simulation; correlate a relay's optimistic-mode usage with that relay's invalid-block rate to catch (a)-class issues amplified by optimistic mode.

## Real-world examples

- **April 3rd, 2023 — Ethereum mainnet — relay unbundling / "low-carb-crusader" incident.** A malicious validator at slot 6137846 sent a signed-but-invalid `SignedBlindedBeaconBlock` to the Flashbots `mev-boost-relay` (the implementation also operated by Aestus, Bloxroute, Eden, BloXroute Max-Profit, Manifold and others). The vulnerable `PublishBlock()` path validated the proposer signature and returned the unblinded body even though the block was not canonicalised; the validator used the revealed body to construct a competing block in which sandwich-MEV bundles were re-ordered into a back-run-only configuration, draining ~\$25M from five sandwich operators in a single slot. Subsequent disclosure (April 21st 2023) attributed the operation to actors self-styling as "low-carb-crusader" / "sandwich the ripper"; the U.S. DOJ later (May 2024) indicted Anton and James Peraire-Bueno for the operation. Five patches were shipped to the `mev-boost` and `mev-boost-relay` ecosystem in the following days, including a publication-gated body-release path and a slot-bound timing guard, and a brief network-instability window with elevated reorgs followed on April 6th. The Flashbots, Aestus, and Bloxroute post-mortems frame the relay codebase change as the load-bearing mitigation. Sources: `[flashbotsmevboost2023]`, `[bloxroutemevboost2023]`, `[blocksecmevboost2023]`, `[paradigmpbstime2023]`, `[dojmevbros2024]`.
- **Equivocation-class disclosure, 2023.** Independent of the April 3rd incident, a separate equivocation-strategy class — in which a proposer who has called `getPayload` early can publish a *different* block while still receiving the relay-released body — was disclosed and mitigated through coordinated relay updates. Source: `[flashbotsequivocation2023]`, `[chainlightpbs2023]`.
- **Cohort framing — censorship and vertical integration, 2022–2024.** Public dashboards (MEV Watch, Etherscan's PBS tracker) document that a non-trivial fraction of MEV-Boost relays apply OFAC-style transaction filters; relay–builder vertical integration is documented for several operators, with neutrality-only relays (Aestus, Ultra Sound, Agnostic Gnosis at various points) explicitly positioning themselves against this concentration. These conditions are the structural prerequisites for sub-patterns (b) and (d). Sources: `[mevwatch2024]`, `[aestusverticalintegration2023]`.

## Mitigations

- **Relay operators:** keep the `mev-boost-relay` codebase patched at the post-April-3rd revisions; gate body release on successful network publication of the relay's specific block hash; ship slot-bound timing guards on `getPayload`; publish equivocation telemetry as a public dashboard.
- **Validators / staking operators:** connect to a diversified set of non-censoring, non-vertically-integrated relays (Aestus, Ultra Sound, Agnostic Gnosis as v0.1 cohort exemplars); never operate with a single relay as a single point of trust; reject optimistic-mode relays for high-stake validators where the latency win is not load-bearing.
- **Block builders:** prefer relays that publish bid-stream-handling commitments (commit-reveal, encrypted bids) over relays that do not; treat bid-stream privacy as a relay-selection criterion, not a default.
- **Protocols / dApps:** route MEV-sensitive orderflow (oracle updates, large swaps, liquidations) via private builder channels with cryptographic submission guarantees rather than public mempool, so that a censoring or exfiltrating relay cannot reach the orderflow.
- **Ecosystem / protocol research:** in-protocol PBS (ePBS), inclusion lists, single-slot finality, and threshold-encrypted bid streams are the structural fixes that retire T14.002 as a Technique class — they remove the off-protocol relay's privileged role rather than patching it. Tracked by EIP-7732 and successor proposals.

## Discussion

T14.002 is rated **stable** because the canonical incident (April 3rd 2023) is exhaustively documented, the relay-side mitigation has been shipped and adopted across the major implementations, and the cohort-level framings for (b) and (d) are continuously instrumented by public dashboards. The Technique nonetheless remains live: the relay's privileged role is structural to out-of-protocol PBS, and only an in-protocol PBS (ePBS, EIP-7732 family) retires the surface — every patch to `mev-boost-relay` between April 2023 and v0.1 has been a containment, not a cure. Defenders treating T14.002 as a 2023 episode rather than a steady-state surface miss that sub-patterns (b)/(c)/(d) are operating *now*, in production, at the cohort level, and that the validator-side mitigation (relay diversification + neutrality selection) is a per-operator choice that is not enforced by the protocol. T14.002 should be re-rated only when in-protocol PBS lands on mainnet.
