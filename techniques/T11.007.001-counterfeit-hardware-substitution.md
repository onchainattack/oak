# OAK-T11.007.001 — Counterfeit-Hardware Substitution

**Parent Tactics:** OAK-T11 (Custody and Signing Infrastructure); sub-Technique of OAK-T11.007 (Hardware-wallet Supply-chain / Physical-access Compromise)
**Maturity:** emerging
**Chains:** chain-agnostic (counterfeit device substitutes the legitimate hardware wallet; downstream extraction across all chains the affected wallet supports)
**First documented:** 2017 (early Ledger Nano S inserts cohort) at the cohort-shape layer; 2025 counterfeit Ledger Nano S Plus cohort for the deployed-attack anchor
**Aliases:** "counterfeit hardware wallet", "fake Ledger / fake Trezor", "pre-seeded recovery card", "hardware-wallet supply-chain substitution"

## Description

Counterfeit hardware-wallet devices distributed through informal retail channels (Chinese marketplaces — AliExpress / Taobao third-party sellers; Telegram-based resellers; in-person counterfeit-product markets; second-hand purchases without packaging) that brand-impersonate legitimate Ledger / Trezor / similar products. The counterfeit device either:

- **Pre-seeds the recovery phrase:** the device ships with a pre-printed recovery card whose seed the operator already knows. The user funds the wallet; the operator drains it.
- **Replaces the secure element:** the legitimate secure element is substituted with an attacker-controlled microcontroller (ESP32-S3 or similar) that stores seeds in plaintext flash and includes an embedded radio (Wi-Fi / BLE) for exfiltration, defeating the air-gap that the legitimate device provides.

The structural feature distinguishing T11.007.001 from sibling sub-Techniques:

- **T11.007.001** substitutes the **hardware unit itself**: the device in the user's hand is not the device the legitimate vendor produced. Brand-impersonation provides the social-engineering substrate at purchase time.
- **T11.007.002** (Physical-Access Hardware-Side Seed Extraction) exploits a structural microcontroller vulnerability against a legitimate device; physical access is the binding constraint.
- **T11.007.003** (Brand-Trust-Leveraged Active Phishing) exploits the vendor's communication channel as a brand-impersonation surface; the device itself is legitimate.

The canonical deployed-attack anchor is the 2025 counterfeit Ledger Nano S Plus cohort: ESP32-S3 microcontroller substitution with chip markings physically scraped off, antenna trace on PCB for 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi / BLE radio exfiltration, legitimate Ledger Nano S Plus is air-gapped by design. ~$9.5M+ across ~50+ victims spanning 20+ blockchain ecosystems.

## Observed indicators

- **Purchase channel:** hardware purchased through informal channels (Chinese-marketplace third-party sellers, Telegram resellers, in-person counterfeit markets, second-hand without packaging) rather than the vendor's authorised retail channels.
- **Tamper-evidence:** packaging without vendor's tamper-evident seal; device behaviour during initialisation that does not match vendor's documented onboarding flow.
- **Hardware fingerprints:** chip markings physically scraped off; antenna traces on a PCB for a device whose legitimate variant is air-gapped; visible solder rework or piggyback components.
- **Pre-seeded recovery card:** the device ships with a recovery phrase card already filled in. Legitimate hardware wallets never ship with a pre-filled recovery card.

## Detection signals

- **Authorised-retailer-purchase invariant:** any user with non-trivial holdings must purchase hardware exclusively through the vendor's authorised retail channels. Informal-channel purchases should be treated as structurally compromised.
- **Vendor-side device-genuineness check:** execute the vendor's authenticity verification (Ledger Live's check; Trezor Suite's parallel) on first connection of any new device. Reject devices that fail.
- **Tamper-evident packaging inspection:** vendor-supplied tamper-evident seal verification at unboxing; refuse to initialise a device whose packaging shows tampering.
- **Pre-seeded-recovery-card detection:** any hardware wallet that ships with a pre-filled recovery card is definitively a T11.007.001 surface. Legitimate vendors never pre-fill recovery cards.

## Mitigations

- **Authorised-retailer-purchase invariant (highest-leverage):** purchase hardware exclusively through vendor-authorised channels. Informal-channel purchases are structurally indistinguishable from deliberate seed-exfiltration devices.
- **Vendor-side device-genuineness check:** execute on first connection. Reject devices that fail.
- **Tamper-evident packaging inspection:** verify vendor's seal at unboxing.
- **Pre-seeded-recovery-card invariant:** never use a hardware wallet that shipped with a pre-filled recovery card or recovery phrase.

## Discussion

T11.007.001's structural OAK lesson: **a hardware wallet purchased outside authorised retail channels is structurally indistinguishable from a deliberate seed-exfiltration device.** The legitimate vendor's brand is the social-engineering surface that produces user-side trust in a unit the vendor never produced. The wallet's cryptographic correctness — secure element architecture, firmware integrity, BIP39 implementation — is meaningful only if the device the user holds is the device the vendor manufactured. Purchase-channel verification is a first-class T11 user-side mitigation surface.

The ESP32-S3 substitution pattern with embedded radio exfiltration is particularly insidious because it defeats the air-gap that is a core selling proposition of hardware wallets. Users who purchase a counterfeit "air-gapped" device receive a device that actively exfiltrates seeds over Wi-Fi / BLE — the opposite of the security property they paid for.
