# CoinEx hot-wallet theft — multi-chain — 2023-09-12

**Loss:** approximately \$54M across Ethereum, BNB Chain (BSC), Polygon, Tron, Bitcoin, Ripple, Bitcoin Cash, and Solana hot wallets at CoinEx, a Hong Kong–based centralised exchange. Initial loss estimates from on-chain forensic providers ranged from \~\$27M (PeckShield's first-pass figure on the Ethereum / Tron / Polygon legs) to \~\$54M after BTC, XRP, BCH, and SOL outflows were enumerated; CoinEx's own internal accounting put the figure at up to \~\$70M. The \~\$54M figure published by SlowMist, Elliptic, and Match Systems is the consensus on-chain-observable loss and is the figure used in the OAK corpus. **Recovery:** none publicly disclosed at scale; CoinEx pledged 100% user reimbursement from its own reserves and rebuilt its hot-wallet system from scratch.
**Attribution:** **inferred-strong** — see OAK-Gnn line below for attribution detail.
**OAK Techniques observed:** **no exact OAK v0.1 entry-vector match** — the entry vector was operator-internal hot-wallet private-key compromise, not a third-party signing vendor (T11.001), not wallet-software distribution (T11.002), and not in-use multisig manipulation (T11.003). Closest sibling Technique class is the broader T11 custody-and-signing family; the CoinEx case sits in the same operator-internal-key-compromise gap as the KuCoin (2020), Coincheck (2018), and Stake.com (2023-09-04) worked examples, and is the **third 2023 worked example** of this pattern in the OAK corpus. Downstream Techniques observed on-chain: **OAK-T7.001** (Mixer-Routed Hop — Sinbad on the Bitcoin side; later Yomix as Sinbad was seized in late 2023), **OAK-T7.003** (Cross-Chain Bridge Laundering — bridging across Ethereum / BSC / Tron / Bitcoin to consolidate into BTC), and **OAK-T8.001** (Common-Funder Cluster Reuse) which is the on-chain attribution surface that forensic providers used to link the CoinEx attacker cluster to the prior Stake.com and Alphapo clusters.

**OAK-Gnn:** [OAK-G01 Lazarus Group / DPRK-attributed](../actors/OAK-G01-lazarus.md). Attribution status: **inferred-strong** — there is no FBI press release that publicly names CoinEx as a Lazarus / DPRK-attributed event in the same way the 2023-09-07 release named Stake.com, Atomic Wallet, and Alphapo / CoinsPaid. Attribution rests on independent forensic work by SlowMist, ZachXBT, Elliptic, and Match Systems, all of whom published Lazarus attribution within \~72 hours of the incident on the basis of attacker-cluster reuse — specifically, an address that the CoinEx attacker shared with the Stake.com attacker cluster (FBI-named on 2023-09-07) and the Alphapo / CoinsPaid attacker cluster (FBI-named on the same release). By OAK convention this is `inferred-strong`-grade attribution: forensic-provider consensus plus on-chain cluster overlap with FBI-named clusters, but no CoinEx-specific FBI public statement. The attribution would be upgradeable to `confirmed` if a future FBI / Treasury release names CoinEx specifically.

## Summary

On September 12, 2023, CoinEx — a Hong Kong–based centralised exchange — saw unauthorised outbound transactions from its hot wallets across Ethereum, BNB Chain, Polygon, Tron, Bitcoin, Ripple, Bitcoin Cash, and Solana, beginning around 18:00 UTC. CoinEx's internal Risk Control System detected the anomalous withdrawals and the operations team paused all deposits and withdrawals, performed an emergency shutdown of the affected hot-wallet servers, and migrated remaining balances to cold storage. The cumulative on-chain-observable loss was approximately \$54M; CoinEx's own internal accounting put the figure at up to \~\$70M after accounting for unconfirmed and pending transactions.

The proximate cause was compromise of the platform's hot-wallet private keys. CoinEx's public post-mortem and subsequent updates explicitly attributed the breach to **a private-key leak from the hot-wallet system**, not to a smart-contract exploit, a bridge exploit, or a third-party-vendor compromise. The exchange's public framing of the entry vector matches the on-chain forensic consensus from SlowMist, Halborn, and Elliptic. The exact off-chain access path (employee social engineering, internal-IT compromise, or insider misuse) has not been publicly disclosed by CoinEx and has not been pinned down in the public forensic record; what is on the public record is that the attacker held key material with `transfer()` authority over the CoinEx hot-wallet addresses across all eight affected chains.

Lazarus / DPRK attribution was published by ZachXBT, SlowMist, and Elliptic within \~72 hours of the incident on the basis of **attacker-cluster reuse**: the CoinEx attacker reused at least one address that had appeared in the Stake.com attacker cluster (FBI-named on 2023-09-07, just five days earlier) and in the Alphapo / CoinsPaid attacker cluster (FBI-named on the same 2023-09-07 release). The cluster-reuse signal is the canonical OAK-T8.001 attribution surface; it is the same mechanism that produced the Atomic Wallet and Alphapo / CoinsPaid attributions earlier in 2023. There is, as of the OAK v0.1 cutoff, no CoinEx-specific FBI / Treasury public statement, which is why this case carries `inferred-strong` rather than `confirmed`-grade attribution.

For OAK's purposes, the CoinEx case is the **third canonical worked example** of operator-internal hot-wallet key compromise in the 2023 OAK-G01 wave, after Atomic Wallet (2023-06) and Stake.com (2023-09-04). It is also the **second 2023 case in eight days** — Stake.com hit on 2023-09-04, CoinEx on 2023-09-12 — which is one of the strongest pieces of evidence in the OAK corpus that OAK-G01 was running multiple parallel custody-compromise operations in fall 2023. Cluster-tracking and defender-side blocklist reasoning should reflect this temporal density.

## What defenders observed and learned

- **The 2023 OAK-G01 custody-compromise wave was running at temporal density measured in days, not months.** Atomic Wallet (June 2023), Alphapo / CoinsPaid (July 2023), Stake.com (2023-09-04), and CoinEx (2023-09-12) are four custody-side OAK-G01 events in \~14 weeks; the Stake.com–CoinEx gap is **eight days**. Defender risk teams treating "Lazarus custody compromise" as a once-or-twice-a-year tail event will miss the operational reality of fall 2023, which was a parallel-operations campaign. Cluster-tracking should reflect that OAK-G01 maintained multiple distinct attacker clusters running concurrently — the Stake.com and CoinEx clusters overlap on at least one address but are otherwise separately operationalised.
- **Cluster-reuse (T8.001) is the modal attribution surface for the 2023 wave, not FBI public statements.** Stake.com is the exception, not the rule: it carries `confirmed`-grade FBI attribution because the FBI's 2023-09-07 release named it explicitly. CoinEx, like Atomic Wallet, carries `inferred-strong`-grade attribution from forensic-provider consensus on cluster overlap. Defender risk teams that depend on FBI public statements as the trigger for blocklist updates will be 72 hours behind the forensic-provider community; defender risk teams that subscribe to SlowMist / Elliptic / TRM Labs cluster feeds will be at the 72-hour-after-incident attribution surface. The Stake.com timeline is the optimistic case; the CoinEx timeline is the modal case.
- **Operator-internal hot-wallet key compromise is the dominant 2023 OAK-G01 vector against CEXes; smart-contract exploits are not.** None of the four 2023 OAK-G01 custody-side cases (Atomic Wallet, Alphapo / CoinsPaid, Stake.com, CoinEx) involved a smart-contract exploit, a bridge exploit, or a validator-key compromise. All four involved operator-internal key compromise — through varying off-chain access paths (wallet-software distribution for Atomic Wallet, employee social engineering for Stake.com, undisclosed for Alphapo / CoinsPaid and CoinEx). For CEX defender runbooks, the 2023 wave establishes operator-internal-key compromise as the primary OAK-G01 threat model; smart-contract hardening alone does not address it.
- **CoinEx's "rebuild the hot-wallet system from scratch" response is structurally different from the Stake.com response.** Stake.com paused withdrawals for \~4 hours, disabled the malicious-transaction surface, and resumed operations on the same key infrastructure. CoinEx instead announced a full hot-wallet system rebuild and migrated to a new wallet address set. The contrast suggests CoinEx assessed the compromised key surface as broader than a single-address-set leak — consistent with an internal-IT or build-pipeline compromise rather than a single-employee social-engineering compromise — though CoinEx has not publicly confirmed the off-chain access path. For defender-side post-incident decision-making, the rebuild-vs-rotate choice is a function of how much of the operator's key-management surface the attacker is assessed to hold; CoinEx's choice is the conservative one.

## What this example tells contributors writing future Technique pages

- **The operator-internal hot-wallet key compromise gap is now documented across four worked examples (KuCoin 2020, Stake.com 2023-09-04, CoinEx 2023-09-12, and the Coincheck 2018 case) and is unambiguously a recurring pattern, not a one-off.** A future v0.x update should add a T11.x sub-Technique for operator-internal key compromise, with sub-sub-Techniques distinguishing (a) social-engineered employee access (Stake.com, Atomic Wallet candidate), (b) internal-IT compromise via malware (KuCoin candidate, CoinEx candidate), and (c) insider misuse. CoinEx specifically does not let the contributor pin the access path without speculation; the worked example documents the on-chain-observable surface and notes the off-chain gap.
- **`inferred-strong` attribution from cluster overlap (T8.001) with FBI-named clusters is the right marker for CoinEx, not `confirmed`.** Contributors writing 2023 OAK-G01 wave cases should default to `inferred-strong` unless an FBI / Treasury statement explicitly names the case. The Stake.com `confirmed` grade is the exception driven by the 2023-09-07 FBI press release; without an analogous CoinEx-specific public statement, the OAK convention is to record the attribution at the strength the public record supports.
- **Temporal-density signals belong in the worked example.** The Stake.com–CoinEx eight-day gap is itself an OAK-corpus-level finding: it is evidence that OAK-G01 was running parallel custody-compromise operations rather than a single-track campaign. Contributors writing future OAK-G01 cases should explicitly cross-reference temporally adjacent cases in the same actor's portfolio; the wave structure is part of the threat model and should not be left implicit.

## Discussion

CoinEx is the OAK example of an **operator-internal hot-wallet key compromise that arrived eight days after the highest-profile companion case in the same OAK-G01 wave**. The structural counterpart is Stake.com (2023-09-04): the two cases share the same entry-vector class (operator-side key compromise, no exact OAK v0.1 Technique match), the same OAK-G01 attribution (Stake.com `confirmed`, CoinEx `inferred-strong`), and the same downstream laundering pattern (cross-chain bridging plus Bitcoin-side mixers — Sinbad until its November 2023 seizure, then Yomix). They diverge on (a) attribution evidentiary strength, (b) operator-side response (Stake.com rotated keys; CoinEx rebuilt the hot-wallet system from scratch), and (c) public disclosure depth on the off-chain access path (Stake.com via TRM Labs / FBI: employee social engineering; CoinEx: no public disclosure).

Together, **CoinEx and Stake.com demonstrate that OAK-G01 was running multiple parallel custody-compromise operations in fall 2023**. The eight-day gap between two distinct CEX-side custody compromises with overlapping but separately-operationalised attacker clusters is materially different from a single-track campaign hitting one target at a time. Cluster-tracking infrastructure on the defender side should reflect this temporal density: a single OAK-G01 cluster being burned in an FBI public statement (Stake.com on 2023-09-07) does not exhaust the contemporaneous OAK-G01 surface, because parallel clusters are still operational against still-uncompromised targets. The CoinEx case landed in the five-day window between the FBI's Stake.com attribution and the CoinEx incident; this is the operational reality the OAK-G01 page's actor-level tradecraft section should reflect.

The CoinEx case also extends the **2023 OAK-G01 custody-compromise wave** that the FBI's 2023-09-07 press release explicitly grouped (Atomic Wallet, Alphapo / CoinsPaid, Stake.com) by adding a fourth case that the FBI did not name but that the forensic-provider consensus places in the same wave. This is a useful calibration point for the OAK corpus: the FBI's named-case list is a lower bound on the wave size, not the full wave. Contributors writing future OAK-G01 wave cases should expect that the FBI's public-attribution surface lags the forensic-provider attribution surface by 72 hours to indefinitely, and should record cases at the attribution grade the public record supports rather than waiting for FBI naming as a precondition for inclusion.

The contrast with the smart-contract-exploit OAK-G01 cases (Ronin Bridge 2022, Harmony Horizon 2022) is the same as for Stake.com: the 2022 wave compromised on-chain control surfaces; the 2023 wave compromised off-chain operator surfaces. CoinEx is one more data point that the post-2022 hardening of on-chain control surfaces (multisig threshold increases, validator-key segregation) pushed OAK-G01 toward the operator's off-chain surface — which OAK v0.1 does not cover at the Technique level and should add coverage for in a future v0.x update.
