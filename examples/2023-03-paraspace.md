# ParaSpace whitehat reentrancy rescue by BlockSec — Ethereum — 2023-03-17

**Loss:** zero net loss. Approximately \$5M of user funds were *at imminent risk* on ParaSpace's lending protocol on Ethereum on 2023-03-17 due to a reentrancy vulnerability identified during third-party code review. **Recovery:** **near-total**. BlockSec engineers identified the vulnerability during ParaSpace code review, executed a *pre-emptive front-run rescue* extracting the at-risk funds before the vulnerability could be exploited by a malicious actor, and returned the rescued funds to a ParaSpace-controlled multisig within hours. This is one of the cleanest 2023 cases of a whitehat *defensive-MEV* rescue executed in the *pre-exploit* window — distinct from the Euler 2023 *post-exploit recovery-negotiation* and the Ronin 2024 *in-mempool front-run-of-active-exploit* cases.
**OAK Techniques observed:** **OAK-T9.005** (Smart-Contract Exploit) in the *reentrancy* sub-class — the underlying vulnerability that BlockSec identified during code review; the structural surface that made the rescue necessary. **OAK-T5.004** (Sandwich / MEV Extraction) in the **defensive sense** — the rescue was executed by leveraging MEV-style transaction-ordering primitives to extract the at-risk funds before a malicious actor could; T5.004 is observed here as the defensive-MEV outcome that became a whitehat rescue, rather than as an extraction-from-users mechanism.
**Attribution:** **confirmed**. BlockSec engineers publicly attributed the rescue to themselves through their corporate blog, X / Twitter posts, and engagement with industry coverage; this is one of the few OAK cases where defender attribution is direct and confirmed. ParaSpace publicly confirmed the rescue and the return-to-multisig in their own communications. There is no attacker side (the rescue was executed before any malicious-actor exploit transaction was submitted to the public mempool).
**Key teaching point:** **defensive-MEV in the pre-exploit window is a real, distinct rescue primitive that requires (a) third-party security-research discovery of the vulnerability before adversaries discover it, (b) operator-side incentive alignment for the researcher to act, and (c) operator-side preparation to receive rescued funds at a discoverable trusted address.** ParaSpace 2023 is the canonical OAK worked example for this primitive in the *pre-exploit* window. The Ronin Bridge August 2024 rescue at `/examples/2024-08-ronin-bridge-rescue.md` is the canonical worked example for the analogous primitive in the *in-mempool* window (front-running an active exploit transaction). The Euler Finance March 2023 case at `/examples/2023-03-euler-finance.md` is the canonical worked example for the *post-exploit recovery-negotiation* primitive. The three together span the recovery-primitive distribution by intervention window.

## Summary

ParaSpace operated as an NFT lending / hybrid lending protocol on Ethereum, allowing users to borrow against NFT and ERC-20 collateral. In March 2023 the protocol was undergoing third-party security review by BlockSec engineers in preparation for a launch / upgrade.

During the review, BlockSec engineers identified a reentrancy vulnerability in ParaSpace's deployed contracts that would have allowed an attacker to drain approximately \$5M of user funds from the protocol's lending pools. The vulnerability was discoverable during a sufficiently rigorous code review; the structural shape was a callback re-entry pattern that allowed the attacker to extract funds twice across a single transaction context, similar in shape to the canonical 2016 DAO reentrancy class.

BlockSec faced an operational decision: disclose the vulnerability privately to ParaSpace and rely on the protocol team to deploy a fix before any malicious actor independently discovered the same vulnerability, or execute a pre-emptive whitehat rescue extracting the at-risk funds before a malicious actor could. The rescue route was chosen on the basis that the vulnerability was sufficiently discoverable that the time-to-malicious-exploit window was unbounded but plausibly short.

BlockSec engineers executed the rescue: they constructed a transaction that exercised the same reentrancy pathway a malicious actor would have exercised, extracted approximately \$5M from the at-risk pools to a BlockSec-controlled address, and immediately coordinated with ParaSpace to return the rescued funds to a ParaSpace multisig. The rescue was executed in a single operational window measured in hours; ParaSpace publicly acknowledged the rescue and confirmed the return to multisig.

For OAK's purposes the case is a clean T9.005 (reentrancy sub-class) *latent vulnerability* that did not extract because of the T5.004 (defensive-MEV in pre-exploit window) rescue primitive. The case is the canonical 2023 worked example for the pre-exploit defensive-MEV primitive and is one of the few OAK cases with confirmed defender attribution.

## What defenders observed

- **The pre-exploit window is operationally distinct from the in-mempool and post-exploit recovery windows.** The Euler Finance March 2023 recovery-negotiation primitive operated in the *post-exploit* window: the extraction had completed, the attacker held the funds off-protocol, the operator engaged via on-chain message channel after the fact. The Ronin Bridge August 2024 rescue at `/examples/2024-08-ronin-bridge-rescue.md` operated in the *in-mempool* window: an exploit transaction was already in the public mempool, and a defensive front-run preempted it. ParaSpace operated in the *pre-exploit* window: no malicious exploit transaction had been submitted to the mempool, and the defensive-MEV primitive exercised the vulnerability before any adversary did. The three windows have distinct activation conditions and distinct operational requirements; worked examples should preserve the window distinction.
- **The defensive-MEV primitive in the pre-exploit window requires third-party security-research discovery as the trigger.** The activation condition for the ParaSpace rescue was that BlockSec discovered the vulnerability during a paid / structured code review and chose to act on it via rescue rather than via private disclosure. The primitive does not activate without third-party discovery; it does not activate if the discoverer chooses private disclosure; it does not activate without operator-side preparation to receive rescued funds at a discoverable trusted address. None of these conditions are guaranteed for a future incident. Worked examples should treat the primitive as conditional and document the activation conditions explicitly.
- **Operator-side preparation made the rescue cleanly bounded.** ParaSpace was operationally positioned to receive the rescued funds at a multisig address, to publicly acknowledge the rescue with appropriate framing, and to coordinate the no-prosecution / whitehat-protection authority chain in the time window the primitive required. The operator-side preparation surface is similar to the surface required for the in-mempool rescue primitive (Ronin 2024) and the post-exploit recovery-negotiation primitive (Euler 2023, Sentiment 2023) — pre-drafted statement, designated multisig, no-prosecution authority chain — but is exercised in a distinct intervention window.
- **Confirmed defender attribution is one of the structurally distinguishing features of the case.** Most OAK cases have pseudonymous attacker attribution and unattributed-or-pseudonymous-defender attribution. ParaSpace 2023 is one of the few cases with confirmed defender attribution: BlockSec engineers publicly attributed the rescue to themselves and engaged with industry coverage to document the operational decision. The confirmation is operationally important because it makes the worked example useful for documenting the activation conditions for the primitive at function-level detail.
- **Detection latency was the entire activation timeline; recovery latency was operator-acceptable.** The rescue was executed during the active code review window; recovery to the ParaSpace multisig completed within hours. The overall operational shape — discovery, rescue, return — ran in a single business day. The base-rate expectation for T9.005 incidents that reach exploitation is total loss; ParaSpace did not reach exploitation because the pre-exploit defensive-MEV primitive activated.

## What this example tells contributors writing future Technique pages

- **T5.004 has multiple defensive variants in distinct intervention windows.** The standard T5.004 framing is sandwich / MEV extraction *against users*. The Ronin Bridge 2024 rescue case at `/examples/2024-08-ronin-bridge-rescue.md` documents the *in-mempool defensive-MEV variant*. The ParaSpace 2023 rescue case documents the *pre-exploit defensive-MEV variant*. Both are T5.004 in the defensive sense; both deliver near-total recovery; both depend on operator-side preparation. Contributors writing the T5.004 technique page should consider a "Defensive Variants" section enumerating the intervention-window sub-cases.
- **Recovery primitives are an operationally consequential dimension that should be documented across all four canonical sub-cases.** The four recovery primitives visible in the OAK 2022–2025 corpus are (a) post-exploit recovery-negotiation with attacker self-identification (Euler 2023, Sentiment 2023, Allbridge 2023), (b) in-mempool defensive-MEV rescue (Ronin 2024), (c) pre-exploit defensive-MEV rescue (ParaSpace 2023), and (d) validator-coordinated freeze on permissioned-validator-set L1s (Cetus 2025 at `/examples/2025-05-cetus.md`). Contributors writing the Mitigations layer should consider a cross-cutting Recovery-Primitives entry with all four sub-cases as the canonical worked examples, framed honestly as conditional primitives with prerequisites that may or may not hold per incident.
- **Confirmed defender attribution should be reported as a structural feature when present.** The OAK attribution taxonomy currently emphasises attacker-side attribution (confirmed, inferred-strong, inferred-weak, pseudonymous). Defender-side attribution should be a parallel dimension: who executed the rescue, with what authority, under what disclosure framework. ParaSpace 2023 is the canonical worked example for confirmed-defender-attribution in the pre-exploit window; the Ronin 2024 rescue is canonical for self-identified-whitehat-defender attribution in the in-mempool window. Worked examples should document defender-side attribution honestly and resist collapsing all rescue cases into a single category.
- **Third-party security-research discovery as a defensive primitive is structurally adjacent to formal audits but operationally distinct.** Audits surface vulnerabilities in a private-disclosure framework; the disclosed vulnerabilities are then patched by the protocol team in a controlled timeline. Pre-exploit defensive-MEV rescue surfaces vulnerabilities and *acts* on them via on-chain rescue rather than via private disclosure. The two primitives are not substitutes; they activate under different time-pressure conditions and different operator / researcher relationships. Worked examples in the rescue-primitive class should preserve this distinction and not collapse pre-exploit rescue into "another form of audit."

## Discussion

ParaSpace 2023 is OAK's canonical worked example for the *pre-exploit defensive-MEV rescue* primitive — the structural sub-case in which a third-party security researcher discovers a vulnerability before any adversary, faces the disclose-vs-rescue operational decision, chooses rescue, and returns the funds to the operator multisig. The case is unusual in the OAK corpus because (a) the defender attribution is confirmed at corporate-entity granularity (BlockSec), (b) the intervention window is the pre-exploit window rather than the more common post-exploit or in-mempool windows, and (c) the recovery is near-total despite the size of the at-risk surface (\~\$5M).

The cross-window cohort framing pairs the ParaSpace case with the Euler Finance March 2023 post-exploit recovery-negotiation case (same month, different primitive) and the Ronin Bridge August 2024 in-mempool rescue case (different chain, different primitive). The three together span the canonical recovery-primitive distribution by intervention window: pre-exploit (ParaSpace), in-mempool (Ronin), post-exploit (Euler). The Cetus May 2025 validator-coordinated freeze case at `/examples/2025-05-cetus.md` is the fourth canonical primitive — operating in the post-extraction window on a permissioned-validator-set L1 — and is the structural complement to the EVM-mainnet-window primitives.

The activation conditions for the pre-exploit defensive-MEV rescue primitive are stricter than for the in-mempool and post-exploit primitives. The pre-exploit primitive requires third-party security-research discovery as the trigger; the discovery must be by a researcher operationally able to execute on-chain rescue rather than only private disclosure; the operator must be operationally positioned to receive rescued funds at a discoverable trusted address. The in-mempool primitive (Ronin 2024) requires public mempool visibility of the exploit transaction; the post-exploit primitive (Euler 2023, Sentiment 2023) requires attacker incentive alignment for return-with-bounty. The pre-exploit primitive's stricter activation conditions are why the OAK corpus contains only ParaSpace 2023 as a clean canonical worked example as of v0.1; future contributors should add cases as they emerge.

The confirmed-defender-attribution dimension is operationally important for the OAK Mitigations layer. Worked examples that document the operational decision-making of the defender at function-level detail are more useful for downstream defender practice than examples that report only the on-chain outcome. ParaSpace 2023 is the canonical OAK worked example with confirmed defender attribution at the operational-decision-making layer; future cases in the rescue-primitive class should document defender-side attribution at similar granularity where the defender is willing to engage publicly.
