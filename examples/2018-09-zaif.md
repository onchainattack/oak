# Zaif exchange compromise — multi-asset (BTC + BCH + MONA) — 2018-09-14

**Loss:** approximately ¥6.7 billion (\~\$60M USD at-the-time) across three principal assets — approximately 5,966 BTC (~\$45M at-the-time), approximately 6,236 BCH (~\$3M at-the-time), and approximately 42,327 MONA (Monacoin, the Japan-prevalent altcoin; ~\$5M at-the-time, with the MONA loss representing a substantial fraction of the asset's then-circulating exchange-held supply). Per Zaif's parent-operator Tech Bureau Inc.'s 2018-09-20 disclosure, approximately ¥4.5B of the loss represented customer-asset balances and approximately ¥2.2B represented Tech Bureau / Zaif corporate balances. **Recovery:** structurally distinctive on the public record. (1) **Operator-funded restitution via tokenised-restitution scheme**: Tech Bureau announced an emergency-financing arrangement with Fisco Cryptocurrency Exchange Inc. (subsidiary of Fisco Ltd., a Tokyo-listed financial-information company) under which Fisco would inject ¥5B of capital and assume operational responsibility for the Zaif platform; Tech Bureau and Fisco co-operated on a tokenised-customer-restitution scheme structurally similar to (though distinct in mechanics from) the Bitfinex 2016 BFX-token model. (2) **FSA Japan business-improvement order**: the Japanese Financial Services Agency issued business-improvement orders against Tech Bureau / Zaif in 2018-09 and 2018-10 in response to the disclosed compromise and the operator-side control deficiencies the FSA's on-site inspection identified. (3) **Operational absorption**: Zaif platform was subsequently absorbed by Fisco Group's exchange operations; Tech Bureau Inc. transitioned out of exchange-operating responsibility. (4) **No on-chain recovery directly attributable**: the stolen BTC, BCH, and MONA were laundered through chain-hopping and mixer routing in the months following the event; the MONA-side laundering activity was particularly visible because MONA's smaller circulating supply made the attacker-cluster activity disproportionately observable on-chain.
**OAK Techniques observed:** OAK-T11 broadly construed (custody-side compromise — operator-side hot-wallet key compromise) producing authorised withdrawals across BTC, BCH, and MONA hot-wallet inventories. Adjacent: OAK-T11.002 (operator-endpoint compromise) entry-vector class.
**Attribution:** **pseudonymous** at the OAK v0.1 cutoff — no public-record indictment, attribution-cluster claim, or law-enforcement disposition has named the Zaif 2018 attacker. The case sits ambiguously near the 2017–2019 OAK-G01 cohort but the public-record evidence does not support an `inferred-strong` G01 attribution.

**Key teaching point:** Zaif 2018 is the OAK record's canonical illustration of the *operator-funded tokenised-restitution* recovery mechanism at the 2018-era loss magnitude — structurally similar in spirit to Bitfinex 2016's BFX-token mechanism but with an explicit external-investor (Fisco Group) capital-injection-and-operational-absorption component. The case is also a canonical illustration of how the FSA Japan regulatory surface produced post-event business-improvement orders and an effective transition of exchange-operating responsibility from a compromised operator (Tech Bureau Inc.) to an external acquirer (Fisco Group), without resorting to insolvency proceedings — a distinctive Japanese-jurisdiction recovery shape that contributors writing jurisdiction-comparison material should preserve.

## Summary

Zaif was a Japanese cryptocurrency exchange operated by Tech Bureau Inc., headquartered in Osaka, and was at the time of the event one of the larger licensed Japanese exchanges (Tech Bureau held an FSA-issued exchange operating licence under the Payment Services Act regime that came into effect in Japan in April 2017). The exchange was operationally distinctive within the Japanese exchange surface for its substantial Monacoin (MONA) trading volume — Monacoin being a Japan-originated and Japan-prevalent altcoin whose principal trading volume historically concentrated on Japanese exchanges, with Zaif holding a meaningful fraction of the asset's then-circulating exchange-held supply.

On 2018-09-14 (Japan Standard Time), Zaif's hot-wallet infrastructure suffered an unauthorised drain across BTC, BCH, and MONA inventories. Tech Bureau detected the compromise on 2018-09-17 (KST: detection occurred approximately three days after the extraction event), suspended deposit-and-withdrawal operations, and disclosed the breach publicly on 2018-09-20. Tech Bureau's disclosure quantified the loss at approximately ¥6.7B (\~\$60M USD at-the-time), partitioned across approximately ¥4.5B of customer-asset balances and approximately ¥2.2B of Tech Bureau / Zaif corporate balances.

Tech Bureau's response is the case's most operationally distinctive feature for OAK's recovery-mechanism documentation. Within days of the breach disclosure, Tech Bureau announced an emergency-financing-and-operational-absorption arrangement with Fisco Cryptocurrency Exchange Inc. (subsidiary of Fisco Ltd., a Tokyo-listed financial-information company), under which Fisco would inject ¥5B of capital and assume operational responsibility for the Zaif platform. The Japanese Financial Services Agency (FSA) issued business-improvement orders against Tech Bureau / Zaif in 2018-09 and 2018-10 in response to the disclosed compromise and the on-site-inspection findings on operator-side control deficiencies. The Zaif platform was subsequently absorbed by Fisco Group's exchange operations across late-2018 / early-2019; Tech Bureau Inc. transitioned out of exchange-operating responsibility.

The customer-restitution mechanism that emerged from the Tech Bureau / Fisco arrangement is the case's structurally distinctive feature on OAK's recovery-mechanism axis. The mechanism was *not* a direct cash repayment (Tech Bureau did not have the corporate balance-sheet capacity to fund a ¥6.7B repayment from internal resources) and was *not* a creditor-claim civil-rehabilitation proceeding (Tech Bureau did not enter insolvency); the mechanism was a tokenised-restitution scheme funded by the Fisco capital-injection-and-operational-absorption arrangement, structurally similar in spirit to Bitfinex 2016's BFX-token mechanism but with an explicit external-investor (Fisco Group) capital-injection component that distinguishes it from the operator-internal Bitfinex precedent.

For OAK's purposes the Zaif 2018 case is the canonical reference for two structurally distinctive features. First, it is the canonical *operator-funded tokenised-restitution with external-investor capital-injection* recovery mechanism on the OAK record, distinct from the operator-internal Bitfinex 2016 precedent and from the operator-internal-earnings NiceHash 2017 / Coincheck 2018 precedents. Second, it is a canonical illustration of the FSA Japan regulatory surface producing an effective transition of exchange-operating responsibility from a compromised operator to an external acquirer through business-improvement orders rather than through insolvency proceedings — a distinctive Japanese-jurisdiction recovery shape that sits alongside the Mt. Gox civil-rehabilitation precedent on the Japanese-jurisdiction surface.

## What defenders observed and learned

- **Pre-event:** the load-bearing failure was inadequate operator-side controls on hot-wallet management infrastructure, per the FSA's on-site-inspection findings. Tech Bureau / Zaif held BTC, BCH, and MONA hot-wallet inventories under signing-authority surfaces that were compromised at scale across all three assets in a single extraction event — meaning the signing-authority segregation across asset classes that the post-2020 industry baseline calls for was not operational at Zaif in September 2018. The defender lesson is that *signing-authority segregation across asset classes* is itself a control surface; an exchange holding multiple-asset hot-wallet inventory under a single signing-authority surface produces a multi-asset drain when that authority is compromised, and the post-2020 baseline of segregating signing authority across asset classes is partly retro-engineered against this multi-asset-extraction shape.
- **At-event (detection delay):** detection occurred approximately three days after the extraction event — substantially later than the same-day detection that NiceHash 2017 and Coincheck 2018 achieved, and indicative of an absent-or-inadequate continuous-balance-reconciliation control on the Zaif side. The defender lesson is that the post-2020 industry baseline of continuous cryptographic-balance reconciliation between hot-wallet on-chain state and exchange-side internal accounting — verified at minutes-scale rather than at end-of-day batch granularity — is the load-bearing detection layer, and that a three-day detection lag in 2018 represented a material gap relative to the contemporaneous Japanese-exchange peer baseline.
- **Post-event (recovery — external-investor capital-injection):** the Tech Bureau / Fisco arrangement is the case's most operationally distinctive feature for OAK's recovery-mechanism documentation. The mechanism — external investor (Fisco Group) injects capital, assumes operational responsibility for the platform, funds a tokenised-customer-restitution scheme, and effectively absorbs the compromised operator's exchange-operating function across a multi-quarter transition window — is structurally distinct from the four canonical exchange-recovery patterns OAK documents (operator-funded full repayment from earnings, socialised-loss-with-equity-token, cold-storage-funded reimbursement, civil-rehabilitation creditor-claim) and deserves its own line in the recovery-mechanism taxonomy.
- **Post-event (FSA regulatory surface):** the FSA Japan business-improvement orders against Tech Bureau / Zaif in 2018-09 / 2018-10 are part of the broader 2018-Q3–Q4 wave of FSA regulatory action against Japanese exchanges following the Coincheck 2018-01 incident. The Japanese-jurisdiction recovery shape — FSA business-improvement orders producing operational transitions to external acquirers without resorting to insolvency proceedings — is distinctive, and contributors writing jurisdiction-comparison material should preserve it as a recovery-mechanism axis distinct from the New Zealand / Cryptopia property-status precedent and the US / FTX Chapter 11 precedent.
- **Post-event (attribution):** no public-record indictment, attribution-cluster claim, or law-enforcement disposition has named the Zaif 2018 attacker at the OAK v0.1 cutoff. The case sits ambiguously near the 2017–2019 OAK-G01 cohort but the public-record evidence does not support an `inferred-strong` G01 attribution. Contributors writing operator-accountability or attribution-axis material should preserve the `pseudonymous-unattributed` notation rather than retro-fitting the case to the cohort.

## What this example tells contributors writing future Technique pages

- **Zaif 2018 is the canonical OAK-record illustration of the *operator-funded tokenised-restitution with external-investor capital-injection* recovery mechanism.** The four canonical exchange-recovery patterns OAK previously documented — corporate-funded reimbursement from earnings (Bitstamp 2015, NiceHash 2017, Coincheck 2018), socialised-loss-with-equity-token (Bitfinex 2016), cold-storage-funded reimbursement (KuCoin 2020 with insurance fund supplementing recovered fraction), creditor-claim civil-rehabilitation (Mt. Gox 2014–2025) — do not include the Zaif / Fisco mechanism. The Zaif case adds a fifth canonical pattern: external-investor capital-injection-and-operational-absorption funding a tokenised-restitution scheme, with the compromised operator transitioning out of exchange-operating responsibility through the absorption arrangement rather than through insolvency. Contributors writing future exchange-hack worked examples in which the recovery is non-trivially structured should describe the funding source, instrument, and conversion mechanics with the same care that on-chain extraction mechanics get, and should reference Zaif 2018 as the canonical reference for the external-investor-capital-injection pattern.
- **The FSA Japan regulatory surface deserves its own line in any jurisdiction-comparison material.** The Japanese-jurisdiction recovery shape — FSA business-improvement orders producing operational transitions to external acquirers without resorting to insolvency proceedings — is distinctive on OAK's jurisdiction-comparison axis and contributors should reference Zaif 2018 alongside Mt. Gox 2014–2025 (civil-rehabilitation in Japan) and Coincheck 2018 (FSA business-improvement order without operational absorption) as the three Japanese-jurisdiction reference cases for the post-2017 Japanese exchange-regulatory-and-recovery surface.
- **`pseudonymous-unattributed` is the right attribution-strength marker for Zaif 2018.** No public-record indictment, attribution-cluster claim, or law-enforcement disposition has named the attacker. Contributors writing the OAK-G01 actor page should *not* retro-fit Zaif to the 2017–2019 G01 cohort despite the geographic-and-operational adjacency to Coincheck 2018-01; the public-record evidence does not support the `inferred-strong` notation that the cohort otherwise warrants. The case is one of the cleaner illustrations available of how the 2018-era East-Asian-exchange compromise surface includes both G01-cohort cases and genuinely-unattributed cases, and contributors should preserve the distinction.
- **The MONA-side laundering activity is its own analytic surface.** The MONA loss (~42,327 MONA at-the-time, representing a substantial fraction of the asset's then-circulating exchange-held supply) is operationally distinctive because MONA's smaller circulating supply made the attacker-cluster activity disproportionately observable on-chain. Contributors writing T7-class laundering Technique pages should treat the Zaif MONA-side laundering as an example of how *asset-supply-relative laundering visibility* affects the post-event on-chain forensics surface — an attacker laundering a substantial fraction of an asset's exchange-held supply produces correspondingly visible on-chain footprint, regardless of the chain-hopping or mixer routing that follows.

## Discussion

Zaif 2018 is the OAK record's canonical illustration of the operator-funded tokenised-restitution with external-investor capital-injection recovery mechanism, and a canonical illustration of the FSA Japan regulatory surface producing an effective transition of exchange-operating responsibility from a compromised operator to an external acquirer through business-improvement orders rather than through insolvency proceedings. The case sits structurally between the canonical 2017–2018 OAK-G01 cohort cases (NiceHash 2017, Coincheck 2018, Coinrail 2018) on the one hand and the genuinely-unattributed cases (Cryptopia 2019) on the other, with an attribution surface that contributors should preserve as `pseudonymous-unattributed` despite the geographic-and-operational adjacency to the cohort.

The recovery-mechanism distinction matters most for OAK's mitigation-axis documentation. The Tech Bureau / Fisco arrangement adds a fifth canonical pattern to the four canonical exchange-recovery patterns OAK previously documented: external-investor capital-injection-and-operational-absorption funding a tokenised-restitution scheme, with the compromised operator transitioning out of exchange-operating responsibility through the absorption arrangement rather than through insolvency. Contributors writing future exchange-hack worked examples in which the recovery is non-trivially structured should reference Zaif 2018 alongside Bitfinex 2016 (BFX-token, operator-internal), NiceHash 2017 (operator-funded full repayment from earnings), Coincheck 2018 (corporate-and-owner-funded direct repayment), KuCoin 2020 (cold-storage-and-insurance-fund-supplemented recovery), and Mt. Gox 2014–2025 (civil-rehabilitation creditor-claim with in-kind distribution).

The FSA Japan regulatory surface is the case's structurally significant jurisdiction-comparison feature. The Japanese-jurisdiction recovery shape — FSA business-improvement orders producing operational transitions to external acquirers without resorting to insolvency proceedings — is distinctive, and contributors writing jurisdiction-comparison pages should treat Zaif 2018 alongside Mt. Gox 2014–2025 (civil-rehabilitation in Japan) and Coincheck 2018 (FSA business-improvement order without operational absorption) as the three Japanese-jurisdiction reference cases on the post-2017 Japanese exchange-regulatory-and-recovery surface.

Finally, the attribution-axis discipline matters. The case sits ambiguously near the 2017–2019 OAK-G01 cohort on geographic-and-operational adjacency grounds (Japanese exchange, 2018-era hot-wallet compromise, multi-asset extraction), but the public-record evidence does not support an `inferred-strong` G01 attribution. Contributors writing the OAK-G01 actor page should preserve the `pseudonymous-unattributed` notation for Zaif 2018 rather than retro-fitting the case to the cohort, and should treat the case as one of the cleaner illustrations available of how the 2018-era East-Asian-exchange compromise surface includes both G01-cohort cases and genuinely-unattributed cases. The OAK record's value depends on accurate attribution-strength notation, and Zaif 2018 is one of the cleanest available cases for the genuinely-unattributed end of the attribution-strength spectrum within the broader 2018-era East-Asian-exchange surface.
